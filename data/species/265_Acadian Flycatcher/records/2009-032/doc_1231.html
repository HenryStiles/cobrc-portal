Description of the Bird

A greenish backed empidonax flycatcher, with thinish complete yellowish eyering, very bright white throat, long and wide bill, with orange lower maniable, blackish wings, and two whitish wing bars, whitish underparts, long primary projection.

Story from my blog:
On the way to do a bird survey in eastern Prowers County on Tuesday morning, May 12, 2009, I was trying to decide if I wanted to stop at Van's grove in Bent County to look to see if there were any migrant birds around, or just head to Lamar and look around there. I was listening to KLMR (the Lamar, Colorado, radio station), and they were saying that it was clear and sunny there. Since, it wasn't clear and sunny where I was 30 miles to the west, I decided to stop, since maybe the overcast skies would be better for migrants to be around. It was a little after 7am, when I got to Van's grove. This is a small grove of trees that seems to produce rare birds during spring and fall migration. I walked around the trees on the west side of the grove and found an interesting looking empidonax flycatcher fairly quickly. It was up in the canopy of the green leaves of the elms. I noticed a green back on the bird, and bright yellow lower mandible on the bill, and how long and wide the bill seemed to be. Also, I noted the white throat, blackish wings, and whitish wing bars, with small complete eye ring. I thought, this bird looks interesting and could be an Acadian Flycatcher (a species that hasn't been found in Colorado before), though, if it doesn't make some noise, it will be hard to know for sure what it is. I also, noted a male Northern Parula in the grove as well. Around 730am or so, the bird made a very distinctive noise, a loud one noted partial song which it continued to make for 5 minutes. I then knew for sure the bird was in-fact an ACADIAN FLYCATCHER, since I have heard the species quite a few times in the southeastern U.S. I quickly reached local birders on the phone, and left messages on other cell phones of birders who might be able to get the word out about this bird, and then I finally reached someone, Bill Maynard, who was able to post to cobirds about the sighting right away. The birders who live close, Duane Nelson (Las Animas) and Stan Oswald (Rocky Ford) were able to arrive at the scene within about 30-45 minutes, and were able to see the bird. While waiting for them, I took as many photos as possible, since the species isn't on the Colorado State List, and photos are needed for sure. Since I had to get to my bird survey area in eastern Prowers County, I wasn't able to wait for any other birders that would be coming in the next hour or two. The bird was seen by several other birders until 7pm, which included Bill Maynard (Colorado Springs), Norm Erthal (Denver area), Andrew Spencer (Denver area), Paul Differding (Denver area), Dave Leatherman (Fort Collins), Joey Kellner (Denver area), Joe Roller (Denver area), and Mark Peterson (Colorado Springs). Several of them were also able to photograph the bird. I did return to the grove at 430pm, and Dave Leatherman was there looking at it. I was able to study it through the spotting scope, photograph, and take video of the bird the rest of the afternoon and evening. It looks like everyone agrees that the bird was indeed an Acadian Flycatcher!! This species is normal in the southeastern United States and is rarely seen in the west. It was thrilling to find a first state record, and it was also great that others were able to see it, and agree with the identification. Empidonax flycatchers are very hard to identify, though this bird was very distinctive, and hearing it clinched the identification. Please enjoy the photos I took of the bird above, and the video I took below.
Behaviors: First seen up high in the green elm tree leaves. Also, seen lower as well.
Call: a loud one noted partial song. Very explosive sound. The Eastern Stokes CD has this on it.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Alder Flycatcher - kind of similar, though bill not as long and not as wide, different calls/song.

Yellow-bellied Flycatcher - yellow throat

Cordilleran and Pacific Slope Flycatcher - yellow throat, more of a tear drop eye ring

None of the other empidonax are as green on the back.
