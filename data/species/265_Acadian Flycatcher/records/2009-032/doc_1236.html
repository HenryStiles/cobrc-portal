Description of the Bird

Skipping right to the Empidonax flycatchers, there are three birds that need to be considered, the 3 big guys, Willow, Alder, and Acadian, and maybe a fourth one too, Gray. This is based entirely on observations over a 30 minute period with some gaps when I lost track of it, so maybe 15 minutes overall.

I recently read and studied "Indentifying Empidonax Flycatchers: The Ration Approach in the March 2009 issue of Birding. I now know Acadian, Gray, and Alder have the longest wings of teh U.S. empid group. Acadian has the longest wings, averaging 1.5mm longer than Alder Fly. This gives those three Emipd wing tips what newer field guides describe as being sword-like. In my dark photo that i am sending, the ACFL with only its back being seen, you should see the long wings and its long primary projection (PP). Don't look there for much else but wait for other photos.

Primary projection is difficult to evaluate if other birds aren't there for comparison. I make myself look at PP on all Empids and I at least try to say if the PP is long or short. ACFL has, by far, the longest PP, aveaging 17.3 mm, HAFL 16.0, and Alder only 13.4 mm, and WIFL even shorter at 12.9 average. The comparative PP length is important, therefore, to try to evaluate.

For me, the most important feature was the length and width of the bill. In comparison to ACFL, Gray has the longest bill averaging 10.1 mm, followed by ACFO at 9.8, WIFL at 9.5, and ALFL at 8.6 mm.

Becasue the Bent Empid often perched above me, it was fairly east to look at the bill width, which to me looked very wide, especially since I had been recently looking at HAFL bills which are very thin.

ACFL also has the widest average bill width, 5.8 mm, flollowed by WIFL at 5.6, ALFL at 5.4mm. Although tenths of a mm is probably not discernable n the filed, the combination of a long and very wide bill probably is. The Bent bird (see photos) has what appeared to me in field and in photos to be very long and very wide.

Becasue WIFLs have either no eye ring, or a broken one, and have brownish backs, and have contrasty white throats, I eliminate WIFLY as a possiblilty. ALFL comes closest in many aspects to ACFL, both have a greenish back, longish wings, but their bills are relatively short on ALFL, and their PPs average almost 4 mm less than ACFLs, so I ruled out ALFL, based on the Bent bird's seemingly long PP.

The Bent bird has an olive-green head and back. The ventral view was of a lght gray chin, throat and belly, but I mostly had dorsal views. I loked for a contrast between throat and breast, but didn't

did not notice any; no white throat look. The tail appeared greenish at times and grayish at times when in low light. The leg color was dusky gray. Its olive-green head was peaked with a long slope down to the base of the bill. The eye was dark and surrounded by a dull yellowish narrow eyering.


In conclusion, the Bent bird was green-backed with contrasty long darkish brown wings. It had a narrow, but complete, yellowish eye ring, it had a light unmarked very long and very broad lower mandible and its primary projection appeared long. All of these field marks point to ACFL and away from ALFL and WIFL. GRFL was easily eliminated by the greenish back color and the lack of downward pumping tail. Other traits of ACFL that were noted and seen and can be seen in Brandon’s ACFL #9 is the peaked crown that gradually slopes down to the bill. The yellowish edges to the tertials and secondaries can be seen in my photos, but are not exclusive to ACFL.
Behaviors: Flycatching low to mid height.
