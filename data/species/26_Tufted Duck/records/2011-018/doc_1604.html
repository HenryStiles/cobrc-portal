Description of the Bird

The bird was a diving duck, mostly similar in shape and size to the female Redhead that it was hanging out with, but slightly smaller in direct comparative views. It had a prominent tuft that stuck out from the back of the peak of its crown, perhaps up to an inch from the rest of the head. The tuft was nearly always visible except when the bird would sleek its head feathers in the two or three seconds immediately preceding a dive. The bird's head was a solid rich dark brown, and its eye was yellow. Its bill was bluish-gray except for a paler silvery subterminal band right before the tip, which was extensively black -- not just a black bill knob like on a scaup, but black from edge to edge in a head-on view. The shape of the bill was quite similar to that of the female Redhead nearby, except that in profile view the outer 1/3 of the bill narrowed a little more quickly to the tip. The bird's back was a medium dark brown; its breast was roughly the same color, but slightly mottled. The sides / flanks were also mottled brown, but much paler, with some of the palest patches approaching white in color. There was notable contrast between the pale flanks and the dark back. To the rear, the pale flanks were sharply delimited by a dark ventral region, but the transition between darker breast and paler flanks was much more gradual. The belly (visible only when the bird rolled to preen in the water or rose to flap its wings) was much paler, nearly white, but still mottled. The tail feathers were uniformly dark, stiff, and pointed. The undertail had some white mottling. When the bird rose to flap, I several times saw its extensively (almost exclusively) bright white wing linings. When it preened, I was able to get a decent look at the wingstrip, which was bright white at least from the body most of the way into the primaries.
Behaviors: The bird was on a small section of open water in an otherwise frozen pond. It was not particularly skittish, but kept away from the observers with the other ducks. It dove very frequently. At other times it preened extensively and rose halfway out of the water several times to flap its wings.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

The prominent tuft handily ruled out any other duck species. All that remained was to rule out hybrids. The bird did not appear to have any characteristics of other birds -- all its field marks appeared "pure," from white underwing coverts to white wingstripe to pale blue-banded bill.

Resources Used

Before leaving to chase the bird, I looked at numerous photos of immature and female Tufted Ducks on the internet. In the field I made extensive use of Sibley and the Collins guide while watching the bird.

Previous Experience

I have seen a number of Tufted Ducks in Europe and one in California, but this was my first in ten years and the first one I have really studied.
