Habitat

Narrowleaf cottonwood riparian corridor along Fourmile Creek; widened area with several springs, seeps, and old beaver ponds. Riparian habitat interfaces with Gambel oak, pinyon-juniper, and sagebrush habitats. Agricultural pastures are present where the topography is flat.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  8x42B Leica Binoculars; Bausch & Lomb 45x60 -15 zoom spotting scope
Distance:  As close as 35 feet.
Light:  12/13 overcast; 12/14 partly cloudy, bird west of the road, sun behind my back.

Description of the Bird

Dark brown head, all black bill-upper and lower. Dark eye with NO EYE RING. Dark head contrasted with a lighter dark brown back. Tail and wings dark brown, no WING BARS and no white observed in the tail feathers. First day of observation was overcast and the underside of the bird from below the bill to and including the under tail coverts were white. A slight was of gren-gray merged out from the top of the wings on top of the chest. Light was better on 12/14 and the white under parts appeared to have a light was of yellow. A Song Sparrow perched next to the bird and gave good looks for size comparison. The Phoebe was slightly larger looking, but similar in size.
Behaviors: The bird used open conspicuous perches at the shrub vegetation height (~4'). Posture was erect when perched; bill angle was tilted slightly upward. Bird constantly pumped its' tail downward. It often gave a call note from the perch. It flew down often to feed on mats of watercress for long periods of time with Song Sparrows.
Call: The bird was "talkative"; it never gave a primary song but called often. The call was a single, loud. clear "CHIP". The call was clearly audible at 50 meters or more. It was a crisp, single not repeated often on both days.
Plumage: basic

Similar Species Discussion

Black Phoebe is reported from Colorado. This bird was white under the chin, on the throat, and all the way to the under tail covers, unlike the Black Phoebe which is black under the chin, on the throat and part way down the breast.

Say's Phoebe is not white or yellow underneath but strongly buff to gray in color. I am very familiar with all calls of the Say's Phoebe and it does not have a 'CHIP' call. Say's Phoebe call has more than one syllable and sometimes uses the 'come heerre' call; very different than the distinct 'CHIP' call of the Eastern Phoebe.

The bird was similar in size to a Song Sparrow - too large for Empidonax flycatcher. The bird had NO EYE RING and NO WING BARS, also distinguishing it from the Empidonax flycatchers.

Resources Used

National Geographic Field Guide during observations. Consulted Audio Guide: Peterson's Western Birds, it had all flycatchers and phoebe songs and calls. Confirmed Eastern Phoebe ID.

Previous Experience

20 October 1996; one Eastern Phoebe was observed (and reported) at Vega Reservoir in Mesa County during fall migration. This bird was identified by plumage only it never vocalized. I grew up in the Midwest and saw many Eastern Phoebes. I am familiar with all three American Phoebes. I have Say's Phoebes on my BBS routes and have identified them on point-count transects. I have experience with Black Phoebes in Colorad0 and have observed them on their winter territories in Nevada. During the 1990's I spent 2-4 weeks each winter in Florida where Eastern Phoebes winter. On several occasions I tracked down the strong call note of this bird. This birds behavior was similar to winter behavior of birds I've seen in Florida-giving low call often-never singing the 'song', usually perched on a tall conspicuous forb or low shrub.
