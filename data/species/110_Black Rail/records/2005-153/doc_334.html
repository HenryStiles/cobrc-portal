Description of the Bird

Bird was not seen. I had received permission to enter this large cattail marsh on private property in order to establish a wetlands transect there for RMBO. During the course of the transect I performed the Secretive Marshbird Survey, which involved playing tapes for various secretive marshbirds including Black Rail. A male Black Rail immediately responded to the tape playback with frequent renditions of the "kee-kee-doo" song. I was able to document the bird with my sound recording equipment, even though it was very distant and many other species were vocalizing at closer range.
Behaviors: Bird responded twice to tape playback--the first time while I was deep in the marsh, doing the marshbird survey, and then again twenty minutes later when I had returned to my car for my recording equipment and played the tape again from the edge of the marsh in order to document the bird. Each time the bird responded almost immediately and kept vocalizing for several minutes, but did not seem to come any closer.
Call: Primary song: "kee-kee-doo," given continuously by male in response to tape playback.
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

The "kee-kee-doo" song of Black Rail is distinctive in both tone and pattern and nothing in marshes in the United States could be confused with it. (For argument's sake, it is somewhat similar to the vocalizations of certain Synallaxis spinetails from Central and South America.)
