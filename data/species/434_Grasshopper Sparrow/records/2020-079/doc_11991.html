Description of the Bird

 In particular, this sparrow's facial pattern (eye ring and orangish lores) as well as its underside (buffy and clean except for a breast band of narrow streaks) led me to this ID.

I noticed this bird perched a few feet off the ground in a shrub at the edge of the Nature Park fields. It was clearly an interesting sparrow. This was during a sort of fallout after last September's big cold front, and the park was filled with birds, including sparrows.

This bird immediately struck me as an Ammodramus sparrow and probably a Grasshopper Sparrow — different than the many other sparrows present. It was warm brown overall with a distinct eyering and, most notably, some yellow near the lores. The bird did not vocalize and sat perched in the bush before flying off to the east, following some other sparrows. It seemed to be associating loosely with the myriad other sparrows present that day.

Similar Species Discussion

Cassin's Sparrow is the most similar species based on coloration alone, particularly the pattern on the head — eyering and orange/yellow near the lores. However, Cassin's has a more rounded head, more dull brown (as opposed to warm brown) coloration overall, and a longer tail than this bird had. In addition, the eBird reviewer accepted is as GRSP, which I take to be additional confirmation.

No other Ammodramus sparrows, which are similar in shape, match the plumage of GRSP.

Resources Used

I used my Sibley field guide app to narrow down the sparrow options to a choice between Cassin's and Grasshopper. I then used online resources (including eBird and All About Birds) to compare photos of Cassin's and Grasshopper Sparrows to come up with my ID.

Previous Experience

I have seen both Cassin's Sparrow and Grasshopper Sparrow in other contexts (including Southeastern Colorado). In addition, I had grown to become quite framiliar with the many expected sparrow species of the Western Slope, which is why I took a photo of this bird (I knew it was different).

My thought at the time was that this was clearly an Ammodramus, perhaps a Grasshopper Sparrow, and that in any case, I'd feel more confident in my ID if I was able to use photos to confirm. I was at the time unaware that this species had been hitherto unrecorded in eBird in Garfield County.

 I have been birding for about 20 years while living in a variety of places (PA, CA, DC, CO) and have good experience with sparrows and other passerines in the US (not that this makes me immune to any mis-IDs).
