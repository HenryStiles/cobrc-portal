Description of the Bird

While doing a nocturnal survey run of Oak Creek Grade, I was stopping every quarter mile or so to listen for owls and whatever else I could hear. As I was listening to the calling Poorwills on the rocky mountain side opposite me, I heard a Whip-poor-will. Due to the distance away, I could not discern any difference in the call from the Eastern Whip-poor-will which I am more familiar with. I heard it call maybe 3 calls and immediately began struggling with my cell phone to attempt a recording. It called 3 or so more times and then stopped calling. After listening for awhile, I moved on to another location and heard it again further down the canyon (toward Canon City). This happened at my 3rd stop near the bridge as well. I heard it call a few times and then go silent. That's the extent of the sighting. I heard it calling probably no more than 20 individual times in 3 different locations (assuming it was the same bird).

The option to upload the audio files is not working for me. The ebird checklist containing the audio records can be found here: https://ebird.org/checklist/S68529064

Returned to the location on the evening of 5/7, meeting up with Mark and Wyatt Peterson. At 8:35 pm the bird cleared its throat with an initial short "whit" and then proceeded to call its complete call for perhaps 25 or 30 seconds. The bird was much closer this time and I was able to hear it much better, despite the wind. Assuming the sound of both the Mexican and Eastern birds is "whip-poor-will", you could clearly hear the burry quality of the call, particularly in the initial "whip". In the eastern species, that's a very pure note compared to the Mexican species. Once again, after the initial sound-off, the bird made its way further down the canyon, stopping to sing in several other locations, each further away from us.

Similar Species Discussion

Eastern Whip-poor-will. I struggled with this as to me, the call sounded similar enough to the Eastern Whip that I'm more familiar with to figure it was that species. My experience for whip-poor-wills have been simply going by range and never had to differentiate (nor ever expected to) between the two species. Upon hearing the bird, I barely heard it well enough to call it a whip-poor-will before I began to struggle with my cell phone to record it. Yet my recording seemed adequate to get a consensus by birders more familiar with the calls than myself that it was indeed a Mexican Whip-poor-will. Habitat, particularly on that rocky slope seemed similar to habitat I've heard Mexican Whip-poor-will calling in southern Arizona and being that the Wet Mountains are basically an extension of mountains coming out of New Mexico, Mexican Whip does make more sense.

On 5/7, was able to clearly hear the burry quality of the call to eliminate any doubt in my mind as to the correct identification.

Resources Used

The recording allowed me to share the observation with others much more experienced than myself in differentiating the various species of whip-poor-wills. Their experience and the habitat the bird was located in are the primary resources utilized.

Previous Experience

Originally from Wisconsin, I've heard Eastern Whip-poor-will countless times and Chuck-will's-widow often enough to be able to differentiate those two species quickly enough. I've heard Mexican Whip-poor-will on perhaps 3 or 4 trips to southeast Arizona but in all honesty, never listened well enough to how their call differed from the Eastern species. I just identified them by range unfortunately never thinking that it might be worth learning the difference.
