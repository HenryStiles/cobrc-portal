Habitat

The general area was severly burned in the Missionary Ridge Fire in 2002. Regrowth of aspen and Gambel's Oak surrounds the barren habitat where the bird was seen. The specific location is a wide turn-around at the end of a road. This clearing was 75 feet or more in diameter. and had mostly bare soil. In addition, there was some low, purplish ground cover and scattered clumps of grasses. Gambel's Oak (3 or 4 feet in height) surrounded the clearing.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski 8.5 X 42 binoculars.
Distance:  Approximately 60 feet
Light:  Light conditions were ideal. The day was clear and the sun was directly behind us.

Description of the Bird

The longspur was foraging with other sparrows and quickly stood out as slightly larger than Chipping Sparrows, but smaller that the White-crowned Sparrow. The bird had an obvious, white eye ring and unmarked lores. The supercilium was cream-colored, and uninterrupted with any streaking. The crown had brown streaking. There was no mid-crown stripe. The auricular patch was a warm, light brown with fairly even color throughout - not showing a heavily outlined edge to it. The submoustachial line was white. That white of the submoustachial line continued as a line of the same width behind the auricular patch. The malar stripe was brown and very thin. The breast was buffy and unstreaked. The chin and throat were white. The belly and undertail were white. The back was a beige color with wide dark, brown streaks. The wing was brown and had two beige wing bars. The dark, brown color of the tertials stood out. The central tail feathers were a dark brown. From the side, we noticed that the outer edges of the tail were white. When seeing the upperside of the tail, that white area was wide. The legs were pink. The bill was conical and a pale pink. The bird was slightly larger than the Chipping Sparrows and much bulkier. In comparison to the other sparrows, the bird's body was much closer to the ground as it foraged. I didn't take note of the primary extension. I suspect that wing tips on the standing bird were often positioned in front of the tail. Since the dark primary tips were dark in color as was the brown in the tail, the primary extension would have been difficult to see without specifically focusing on that detail.
Behaviors: The bird was foraging on mostly barren ground with some very low, purplish creeping ground cover and some clumps of grass. The longspur was with Chipping Sparrows, White-crowned Sparrows, Dark-eyed Juncos and one Vesper Sparrow in this clearing. When the flock perceived some danger,such as a Red-tailed Hawk soaring, our movement or a gust of wind, all the birds would flush into the perimeter of this barren ground to seek shelter within the shrubs.
Call: No vocalizations were heard.
Plumage: first winter

Similar Species Discussion

The face of the bird was delicately marked with the auricular patch and eye ring reminding us slightly of a Brewer's Sparrow facial characteristics. The longspur had a wide, cream-colored supercilium that was much more prominent than the supercilium of a Brewer's Sparrows. The shape of the longspur was much bulkier than a Brewer's Sparrow. A Brewer's Sparrow looks much more long and slender than the longspur proportionally. The extensive white in the tail supported a McCown's longspur.
A Vesper Sparrow wasn't really considered. However, the white line of the malar and continuation of that line to frame the auricular patch was a feature this longspur had in common with a Vesper Sparrow. Vesper Sparrow has streaking on the underparts, whereas the longspur did not have any streaking on the underparts. Other longspurs were ruled out because of the delicately marked face, prominent supercilium and unstreaked underparts of this longspur.

Resources Used

Both Peter and I went back to our vehicle to check the ID in the Sibley Guide to Birds. Without discussing our thoughts until we each had sufficient time to consult our books, we independently reached the same conclusion. Immediately afterward, we walked back and viewed the longspur some more, concentrating mostly on the tail markings and also the buffy color of the chest. A day after the sighting, I jotted down all the markings that I had seen. After listing all the key details, I consulted The Sparrows of the United States and Canada by James Rising.

Previous Experience

I've seen a fair number of McCown's Longspurs, but don't recall seeing this plumage before. I am very familiar with all the sparrows regularly seen in our area, such as Vesper Sparrow, Brewer's Sparrows, Chipping Sparrows, etc. Quite frankly, I enjoy sparrows!
