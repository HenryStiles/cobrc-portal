Habitat

Recovering burn (Missionary Ridge Fire of 2002). Scrub oak and thick shrubbery mixed with weeds and wildflowers and remnant stumps and snags of the former Ponderosa forest surrounding. The specific area for observation was within the flat, circular dirt-paved turn-around upon which grew a purplish "prostrate" weed, the seed of which attracted all these "sparrows."

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  8X 42 Elites and 8 X 42 Swarovskys

Description of the Bird

Susan Allerton and I discovered this bird among a small flock of 6 or 8 Dark-eyed Juncos, 4 or 5 White-crowned, 3 or 4 Chipping and one Vesper Sparrow feeding on ground-cover weed seed at the circular terminus of CR 852 just off the road that parallels the east shore of Vallecito Reservoir in La Plata County.
Overall it was a paler buff than any of these species; smaller than the WCSPs and a shade larger than the CHSPs.
The first thing that struck us was the broad, buffy supercilia, buffy eye-rings, the plain brown sides of the face and the broad white mustachial. The throat area was white and the breast and underparts unstreaked and tinged buff. Back streaked brown, legs and feet pinkish, bill pale.
Never having seen this species on the Western Slope, we considered some unusual plumage of Brewer's or Clay-colored Sparrows. Confused, we returned to our vehicle some 200 yards away and agreed each to examine our two copies of Sibley independently, and after "finding" what we observed, shared our conclusions. Independently, we each showed the other the illustration of a female McCown's.
We returned slowly to the site where this jumpy flock ventured out into the open to resume feeding; it blew back into the brush with every passing shadow or distant gunshot from hunters. For the next half hour we examined the bird each time it emerged, noting carefully each of the field marks. We each noted white edges to the longish folded tail and a "dark chocolate" coloring to the center of the folded tail, a finely streaked dark crown, darker tips to the folded wings, the impression of squatting on its feet, so low to the ground did it keep as well as all the marks noted above. In all we viewed the bird off and for an hour and were completely convinced of our identification.
Behaviors: Flocking with sparrows and juncos and feeding warily on the seeds of a purplish flattened weed that grows on the barren surface of a turn-around at the end of this spur road into the burn area. Low scrub-oaks and other brush, along with charred stubs and trunks of the remnant forest decimated by the Missionary Ridge fire, ring this turn-around. It was certainly moving with this little flock, first creeping out of protective cover into the open, then flying low and directly for cover with every perceived danger such as the circling Red-tail's shadow or the rorts of distant firearms. We watched the same action, creeping out of cover and flitting back, repeated another three or four times until we were completely satisfied on all points of identification. A bright, sunny, windless day. Viewed from a distance of no more than 15-20 meters.
Call: Silent
Plumage: 1st winter female

Similar Species Discussion

We considered such sparrows as Brewer's and Clay-colored, but this longspur simply did not closely resemble any species we normally encounter in the Four Corners.

Resources Used

Two Sibley editions, the Birds of Western North America and the full-sized Guide to Birds at the time of sighting. At home checked out the National Geo and Peterson guides too.

Previous Experience

I only know this species from spring and early summer visits to the Pawnee Grassland. I have been birding worldwide for 38 years. Susan Allerton is a well-known Colorado birder, but, as a gentleman, it would be improper to even venture a guess as to years of experience.
