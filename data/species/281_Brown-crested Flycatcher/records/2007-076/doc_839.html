Description of the Bird

The bird was immediately recognizable as a large flycatcher about the size of a kingbird but appearing slimmer, perhaps because its tail was a bit longer. Its upper parts were brown and rust, its underparts yellow and light gray. My other early impression was that it was big-headed and somewhat crested. The bill was mostly black, with the basal 1/3 of the lower mandible being pinkish. In addition, this pink coloration extended along the very top edge of the lower mandible from its base half way to the tip. The upper mandible was tipped with a sharp, fine hook, visible only at close range. The base of the upper mandible was gray. I do not know if the mandible was actually this color or if it came from feathering in this area. The bill was long, basically straight and appeared to be thick, both in depth and width at its base. The side of the face, a broad swath defined above by the level of the middle of the eye and below by the level of the gape, extending from the lores thru the auriculars to the front of the nape, was medium gray. In good light the side of the face contrasted with the forecrown, supercillium, crown, nape and mantle, all of which were a uniform gray-brown. The forecrown sloped gently to the peak of the crown, which was well behind the eye. The rear of the crown was steep and distinctly shaggy or ragged. The malar region was gray, a shade lighter than the side of the face. The throat was a shade lighter gray than the malar region, appearing almost white in poor light. The upper breast was light gray, similar to the malar region. The lower breast, belly, flanks, and undertail coverts were evenly pale yellow. The transition from the light gray of the upper breast to the pale yellow of the lower breast was gradual. The scapulars were the same brown-gray as the mantle and broadly, diffusely edged in light gray. The centers of the median coverts were slightly darker than the mantle and diffusely edged in light gray, the tips of these feathers being broadly pale and forming the upper wingbar. The greater covert centers were dark gray-brown and broadly edged with light gray, the tips being pale and forming the lower wingbar. The lower wingbar was not as prominent as the upper wingbar due to comparatively wider pale tips on the median coverts. The secondary centers were a shade of gray-brown similar to the centers of the greater coverts, and were likewise broadly, diffusely edged in light gray (see similar species section for a discussion of this character). The primaries appeared to be dark gray-brown from base to tip on one side of the mid-shaft line and bright rusty brown on the other side, but I could not get a clear idea of which half of each feather was which color. The overall impression this gave, however, was bright rusty primaries, so perhaps this confirms it was the outer halves of the primaries that were this color. The primaries got gradually somewhat darker toward the tips. Two, perhaps three, feathers (primary coverts?) showed at the base of the outer primary. The upper one (or two) showed a medium brown center edged broadly and diffusely in light gray, while the lower one had a rusty-brown center with pale edging. The primary extension was what I would call short. The tail was generally rusty brown but I did not get a look at it, particularly from behind (above or below). (See photos submitted for a poor view of the upper tail surface.) It was apparent, however, that the outer tail feathers were very narrowly edged with tan or light yellow. The tail was fairly long and rounded and appeared to be an even width throughout, perhaps a bit wider at the tip. This "apparent "flare" might have been due to the tail being concave thru its upper portion and somewhat flattened at the tip. The legs and feet were dark gray, appearing black in the field.
Behaviors: The bird was initially found sitting quietly in the interior of a medium sized, open grown Russian-olive. Also in the tree were 4-5 American Robins. Presumably both species were feeding on the olives, although I did not witness this for the Myiarchus flycatcher. All the Myiarchus flycatchers regular in North America are reported in the Birds of North America accounts to feed on berries and other tree fruits, particularly in migration and winter. Either because of my approach or on its own, the bird left the first Russian-olive and flew 10 meters to the east down to a low limb of another Russian-olive only inches above the grass (west side of the tree). There it sat quietly for a time and then flew down into the grass, presumably in pursuit of an insect or perhaps fallen olives. It remained out of sight for at least a minute, returned to the lower limb, and then flew around the tree to the north and east. I next discovered it sitting just inside the outer edge of the crown about 15 feet off the ground. The way it silently, inconspicuously moved around the tree crowns, often back inside the tree crowns, reminded me of a cuckoo. My pursuit of photos apparently forced it to fly back around to the northwest side of the tree, where it sat for a time. Unprovoked, it calmly flew out of this tree to the northwest toward the fenced grazing exclosure area planted decades ago by the Boy Scouts. I did not pursue the bird further. The total length of this episode was 24 minutes according to my camera data. The bird was silent the entire time.
Call: the bird was silent
Plumage: unknown, presumably adult basic

Similar Species Discussion

This bird was clearly within the flycatcher genus Myiarchus. Given that it was too large and big-billed for Dusky-capped and too yellow for LaSagra's, the birds to consider here are Ash-throated, Brown-crested, and Great Crested.

Ash-throated apparently can overlap in all characters with Brown-crested, particularly the Texas subspecies cooperi. However, Cardiff in his Birds of NA account states that considering the whole suite of characters, no individual bird has MULTIPLE characters considered within the range typical of the other species. In other words an Ash-throated with a longer bill than, say, a smallish cooperi Brown-crested would not also be yellower underneath. At no time after the first few seconds did Ash-throated seem a possibility for the ID of this bird at Crow Valley. It was just too big-billed and too yellow underneath.

Thus, it seems the question of identity falls within the twosome of Great Crested and Brown-crested. The bill of the bird seemed too big for Great Crested, not so much in length as bulk, especially the depth and width of the base (consistent with the Arizona subspecies (M.t. magister). The bill tip was more hooked than Great Crested should have, consistent with Brown-crested. Initially the pale color of the lower mandible base seemed to match what Sibley shows for Great Crested, but in looking at photos and talking with others, this character is not all that helpful, especially at this time of year. The contrast between the gray side of the head (see Description section) and the crown (browner) was right for Brown-crested, with Great Crested being similarly gray for both portions of the head. The mantle was brown-gray for this bird and would be more olive in Great Crested. The throat of this bird was very light gray, appearing almost white in poor light, and should be dark gray for Great Crested. The breast was lighter gray than what a typical Great Crested should be. The shade of yellow on the belly and bordering underparts was "in between" what would be typical of Great Crested and Ash-throated. The amount of gray on the chest (that is, how far down it comes before changing to yellow) is apparently variable and overlaps for both species. The field guides are not particularly good on this character, usually showing the gray on Brown-crested being more extensive than Great Crested. My checks of study skins at DMNS did NOT show this difference to the degree one would be able to use it in the field. Perhaps the single best feature of separation for Great Crested and Brown-crested (other than voice, which is of no help with a silent bird) is the extent, sharpness, and brightness of light edges to the innermost three secondaries ("tertials"). In this bird, the innermost secondary appeared to have a brighter edge than the bordering two secondaries, but the sharpness, brightness, and extent of this pale edge was within that of Brown-crested and would be even more conspicuous on Great Crested. Great Crested is a long-distance migrant, and like most passerine species with this habit, has a long primary extension. Brown-crested does not typically travel long distances within its known range and has a comparatively short primary extension. This bird had a short primary extension. I did not see, or at least note, the color of the mouth lining, which Pyle describes as "flesh to buffy yellow" for Brown-crested and "yellow-orange" in Great Crested. I have been unable to find any mention of behavioral differences between these two species (other than habitat preference, which is mostly irrelevant in this case). But as stated above, the sluggish, "gentle" demeanor of this bird seemed in constrast to the rather wary and hyper activity I have experienced trying to photograph Great Cresteds in southeastern CO. Also, I have never seen a Great Crested perch within a foot of the ground and go down in the grass for prey, but must admit that I have not seen a lot of "out-of-context" late migrant Great Cresteds away from tall trees.

Resources Used

All the standard field guides, including Sibley, NG, and Kaufman. Also extensively Pyle (ID Guide to NA Birds). I also referred heavily to Steven Cardiff and Donna Dittman's species account for the Birds of NA series. I "googled" on-line images and found those of Greg Lasley from Texas particularly helpful. Previous Experience

I have seen dozens of Ash-throated Flycatchers (mostly CO), Great Crested Flycatchers (mostly Ohio, NC, and CO), and Brown-crested Flycatchers (mostly AZ and TX). These were all in context. Never have I studied a bird, of any species, in the field and afterward, as I have this individual.
