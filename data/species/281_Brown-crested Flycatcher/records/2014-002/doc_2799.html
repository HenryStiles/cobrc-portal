Description of the Bird

At 3pm on Saturday 29th June 2013 I came across a Myiarchus Flycatcher. Having only seen Ash-throated Flycatcher here at Zapata I watched it for a while and took some photos. The bird was fairly mobile but gave excellent views on occasion. At the time I began to the think there were some structural features that were obviously not right for Ash-throated Flycatcher. The bird in question looked more robust, especially about the head and bill, it looked overall more imposing than Ash-throated Flycatcher. Although Ash-throated is a large flycatcher it looks quiet nicely proportioned to me, somewhat elegant at times. In addition this bird’s coloration differed from Ash-throated too, with the under parts being more saturated with lemon yellow. It didn’t have the overall silky appearance of Ash-throated Flycatchers subtle hues.

Returning home (I live on site) I loaded the photos on to my Mac and sat with them for a while. I had begun to look at the possibility of it being Brown-crested Flycatcher. I also started to muse on the possibility of it being a Great-crested Flycatcher but I couldn’t reconcile the bird’s structure with that species. My memory of that species from time spent living in Cape May, NJ. was one of a very colorful bird in spring, with highly contrasting flight feathers, tertial edges etc. My Bird looked somewhat muted in comparison. I resorted to Google images (being mindful there are lot of mislabeled bird photos on there!) to reference GCFL. From every angel I couldn’t find a shot in which GCFL’s bill matched the size and width of the bird in question. In fact in some photo’s my bird’s bill looked grotesquely large! Also I became aware of perhaps a less reliable feature of GCFL showing a pale base to the lower mandible, which my bird didn’t show.

I sent the shots onto Bill Maynard with my suspicions of its identity. I was pretty shocked when his reply came “It looks like a Brown-crested, you realize it would be the second record for Colorado?!”

So from that point the photos were circulated to some very eminent US birders (including Paul Lehman) and all were in unanimous agreement with the initial identification of Brown-crested Flycatcher.

Unfortunately the bird was not seen beyond 7pm on the 29th.

Plumage and structural features made the bird immediately recognizable as a Myiarchus Flycatcher. The bird was approx. 8.5 inches in length.

 

In terms of Myiarchus flycatchers, the bird had quite an imposing character, the head looking large, the bill particularly robust and very broad based, it appeared relatively short winged & the tail proportionally long. Legs appeared strong and sturdy.

 

Head:

Crown, ear coverts & nape were a neutral, light brown color, with the slightest olive infusion. This contrasted with a light and “cleaner” pale grey throat and upper breast giving clear tonal demarcation between the head and throat, isolating the ear coverts. The crown appeared slightly darker at some angles and postures.

 

Mantle & Scapulars:

This was same neutral brown as the nape, crown and ear coverts.

 

Wings:

Tertials, median & greater coverts were all darker centered then the mantle color and all having pale edges contrasting with the darker brown centers. The pale tips and edges median coverts were the brightest. The edges to the greater coverts and tertials (I think American birders refer to these feathers as “inner secondary’s”?) were similar in contrast. The edges of the tertials were consistent in width along each edge but fading towards the tips. The tertial edges could not be described as “bright” or “whitish”.

On the closed wing the primaries displayed warm orange-brown fringed on the outer webs. This warmth could also be seen on the folded marginal coverts. The underwing coverts were a similar yellow to the underparts.

 

Tail: When closed the upper tail was slightly warmer than the flight feathers, but not as bright as the primary edges. When spread in active flight or perusing prey it appeared brighter orange-brown than when closed.

 

Under parts:

The upper breast was the same color as the throat, that being pale light grey. At about the same point as the marginal coverts, there was a subtle transition to a muted lemon yellow color which continued on to the belly & under tail covers.

 

Bill:

All dark.

 

Legs:

All dark.

 

Eye:

Dark.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Similar Species Discussion

All 5 Myiarchus flycatcher in the Sibley guide were considered. Although I was fairly confident with the ID of the bird (& being on holiday from the UK) I circulated the images amongst other US birders for confirmation.
The birds structure comprehesively rules out Dusky-capped, Ash-throated & La Sagra's. Leaving two options, Great-crested and Brown-crested. The proportions of the birds bill rules out the possibility of the smaller billed Great-crested leaving only one conclusion, the bird was a Brown-crested Flycatcher. Other "pro" Brown-crested features were less staturated plumage and an absence of a pale base to the bill.

Previous Experience

I have seen Great-crested Flycatchers in the eastern region of the US and Ash-throated in the west. I had not encountered Brown-crested before this date.
