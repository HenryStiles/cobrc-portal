Description of the Bird

Seen in flight high above ground as it entered the park from the southeast (corner of 17th Avenue and Colorado Boulevard)

The bird was in a flat/horizontal and completely stretched-out flying posture, with somewhat slow and deliberate wingbeats, flying directly west/northwest toward the park's Ferril Lake (again, high aboveground and not descending), then circling above the lake about 3 times in an arc of no more than 50-75 feet across, then turning directly west/northwest again and continuing out of sight beyond trees that obstructed my further view

Similar Species Discussion

My first thought, believe it or not, was Anhinga -- only because the rare individual female bird that spent several weeks in Boulder County had just one week ago departed that location and had not been sighted again. Coincidentally, I had recently seen a photo of an Anhinga in flight in a what's-this-bird quiz in one of the birding magazines, and the bird's bearing in that photo somewhat resembled what I had just glimpsed through my windshield as I drove slowly south on the interior park road to the vehicle parking-turnaround loop in the southeast corner of City Park, over which the Wood Stork was flying.

In a matter of seconds, I also dismissed Great Blue Heron (far different flight profile) and ibis (much smaller) and realized, even before considering Sandhill Crane, that it was a Wood Stork. Coloration and size were the main triggers for my ID (I have previously seen them in flight during winter visits to Puerto Vallarta).

By then I had my camera up and was trying to focus/shoot through the windshield, then quickly reaching a parking spot, exiting the vehicle and taking a few more photos, which would confirm my tentative (and of course, dumbfounded) ID.

 

Previous Experience

As previously mentioned, I've seen Wood Storks -- both as individuals soaring and groups of up to 12 flying straight, viewed from well below -- during winter vacation trips to Puerto Vallarta.

I've also seen cranes, ibis, herons and egrets in flight and was generally familiar with differences in size and flight profile. Once focused on this bird and not puzzling it out, it looked exactly as I would expect a Wood Stork in flight to look.
