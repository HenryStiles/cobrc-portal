Description of the Bird

I.D. is not the issue.  Adult Variable Hawk from photos, formerly considered 2 species, this one the lower elevation Red-backed Hawk, called for some reason by some, Red-backed Buzzard even though it was a Buteo from South America where the term buzzard is not used. The bird was following a tractor that was cutting alfalfa when we watched it.

Similar Species Discussion

The former taxon called Puna Hawk is similar but it has been lumped with Red-backed Hawk to become Variable Hawk and taken out of the genus Buteo.  Both have multiple color morphs, one described from higher elevations and the other from lower elevations. 

Resources Used

In 1987 I used the 2 volume Eagles, Hawks, and Falcons of the World by Leslie Brown and Dean Amadon.  This bird was also convincingly identified by Brian K. Wheeler in early September, 1987. 

Previous Experience

I have seen about 10 individuals in various countries of South America.  Below is the new information aboout Variable Hawk inserted here.

I propose to add Variable Hawk (Geranoaetus polyosoma) to the Colorado Bird Checklist.  Red-backed Hawk (Buteo polyosoma), now lumped with the higher elevation forms once thought to be a separate species, Puna Hawk (Buteo poecilochrous) now called Variable Hawk with a new scientific name and removal from the genus Buteo, and assigned the scientific name (Geranoaetus polyosoma) was not accepted to the Colorado Bird Checklist after a vote in 1989.  From the Colorado Breeding Bird Atlas, Hugh Kingery editor, pp. 549, “Red-backed Hawk” was first found by Mark Daily on 31 August 1987 on the Allen Ranch north of Gunnison.  

The species account author, Frank J. Hein, claims the Colorado Variable (Red-backed) Hawk was present for 8 consecutive summers, from 1987-1994.   It mated one year with a Swainson’s Hawk during the 1988 season but it was seen with a Swainson’s Hawk the other 7 years.  One offspring from the mated mixed pair was banded and it was reported to look much like a Swainson’s.

Below, I include the specific reasons given not to accept this species on the Colorado Bird Checklist in 1989, and I will present new information that was not available when the Committee voted in the 1989.

In Vol. 25, No.4 C.F.O. Journal Fall 1991, THE C.F.O. RECORDS COMMITTEE REPORT FOR 1989, Duane Nelson summarized the Colorado Variable Hawk multi-year sightings.

The members who voted on these records include Bill Brockner (Evergreen), Coen Dexter (Clifton), William Howe (Laurel, Maryland), Mark Janos (Pueblo), David Silverman (Rye), Van Truan (Pueblo) and Nick Watmough (Norfolk, UK).

Part 3. Reported Species Not Added to The State List.

“Red-backed Buzzard (Buteo polyosorna) 10-87 -45. No individual bird in Colorado (and perhaps in the U.S.) has been at the center of so much controversy as this bird, first, reported in 1987 in the Gunnison Valley, Gunnison County. Reams of notes, many photographs and much conversation has centered, not on its identity, as much as how, or why, it continues to return yearly to the banks of' Ohio Creek. The committee members, while wanting to believe that the bird somehow confused its species affinities with wintering Swainson's Hawks on the Pampas of South America and that it migrates north with them in spring, feel that adding the species to the North American list would be in error. The Red-backed Buzzard is essentially non-migratory - but new information shows this statement is false and would probably not be able to undertake long-distance migrations (if for no other reason than an inability to put on fat for a long journey without eating). While we believe that anything is possible, it seems presumptuous for us to leap from the possible level to saying with certainty it arrived here on its own. THIS STATEMENT IS NOW KNOWN TO BE INCORRECT SINCE YOUNG BIRDS IN THE SOUTHERN PARTS OF THEIR RANGE CAN MIGRATE 1500 MILES.

The information presented above from C.F.O. Journal, now Colorado Birds, shows how committee members thought at the time about the Colorado Variable Hawk.  Steve N.G. Howell in his 2014 book, Rare Birds of North America writes “…an adult female in Gunnison Co., CO, Aug-Oct 1987 (AB 42:112) apparently returned in subsequent years (IT DID, my emphasis) and paired with a Swainson’s Hawk (Nelson 1991; AOU 1998).  The species is a partial migrant within S America, and some birds (mainly 1st-years) withdraw n. in the austral fall up to about 3000 km (1800 miles), as from s. to nw. Argentina (Juhant 2011).”  [Juhant, M. A. 2011. Where to watch raptor migration in South America. Neotropical Birding 9:8–16.]

Whether a Variable Hawk would/could associate with Swainson’s Hawks is anyone’s guess.  In the absence of any other vagrant n. records, the species was not accepted by the state committee, based in part on the misconception that the species is highly sedentary (Allen 1988; Nelson 1991).”

Susan Allen, a professional bird guide, wrote an article titled “Some thoughts on the identification of Gunnison’s Red-backed Hawk (Buteo polyosoma) and why it’s not a natural vagrant” C.F.O. Journal 22:9-14 basically saying it is a ridiculous to think that a “non-migratory Red-backed Hawk would join migratory Swainson’s and fly to Colorado. She also stated it was ridiculous to think, as had been suggested, that the CO female Red-backed Hawk would mate with a male Swainson’s, which in fact it did.  In the same issue of C.F.O. Journal 22:5-8 Brian Wheeler (later authoring Raptors of Western North America and Raptors of Eastern North America) authored “A Red-backed Hawk” in Colorado stating his reasons for believing in natural vagrancy for the Colorado Variable Hawk.

I searched the Internet for a list of raptors used by falconers in the world.  There were a lot of raptor species listed but I found no reference for Red-backed, Puna, or Variable Hawk being used in falconry.  We also know that “ship assisted” is unlikely for this bird.

After looking in Birds of Northern South America (Restall et al., Vol. 2, 2006) and in Handbook of the Birds of the World, Vol. 2, it becomes apparent that Variable Hawk is extending its range northward in Colombia contra Birds of Colombia (Hilty et al., 1986) where Hilty considered Variable Hawk only a rare migrant to Colombia (but it now breeds there).  In Colombia, Variable Hawk is found in intermountain valleys, not unlike the large open valley near Gunnison where the Colorado Variable Hawk summered and nested.  Looking at Variable (Red-backed) Hawk on eBird shows many sightings from Colombia and admittedly none for Mexico or for countries in Central America. 

Howell, on page 12 of Rare Birds of North America, shows a map of Piratic Flycatcher wintering in Colombia and undergoing a “reverse migration” that he suggests as the reason three have been found in Arizona, two have been found in New Mexico, ? in Texas and in 2015 one discovered in Kansas. If that line was extended into Colorado it would intersect the area where the Variable Hawk summered.  This hypothesis on my part assumes that it was a Variable Hawk from Colombia that made its way to Colorado for 8 summers via reverse migration.

IMO, if the Colorado Variable Hawk had been a one year phenomenon, I would not be submitting this record a second time, but since the same bird returned multiple times and against conventional wisdom mated with a migrant Swainson's Hawk, likely one wintering in southern South America, in addition to the Variable Hawk's withdrawal from the Colorado each fall, I think this record should be reconsidered even though geolocaters were not available at the time of the sightings which would have solved at least one part of the mystery.

In response to Duane Nelson’s summary…“ While we believe that anything is possible, it seems presumptuous for us to leap from the possible level to saying with certainty it arrived here on its own” we now know that Variable Hawk is migratory in at least part of its range and it is impossible to know with certainty the origin of all the extralimital records of birds in North America.
