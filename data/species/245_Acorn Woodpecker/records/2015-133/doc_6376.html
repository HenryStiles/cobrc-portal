Description of the Bird

This is presumably the same individual first seen and reported by me (to Audubon's CBC, ebird, cobirds, and CBRC) in Dec. 2012 on the Colorado Springs CBC; it stayed into March 2013. Though we failed to locate it in the Dec. 2013 CBC, the property owner at 1804 Cheyenne Blvd says it was around that winter; we did again locate it on the Dec. 2014 CBC, and now again in Dec. 2015 for the 4th consecutive year of its winter vacations up north in Colorado Springs. 

Roughly the size of a Red-headed or Hairy Woodpecker, mostly black above but with a red cap on back of the head that meets a black headband on its "forehead," which in turn meets a light yellow patch above the bill, it has striking yellow eyes with black pupils, and a light yellow "bib" across its throat and cheeks--giving it a clownface appearance as described in books. Its underside is black in the breast area, white below streaked with black spreading down from the breast. In flight it flashes white in the wings. This bird often announces its arrival with a medium-soft, throaty sort of rattling laugh. 

Typically after its forays elsewhere around the neighborhood (on both north and south sides of Cheyenne Blvd) it lands first in one of two large, bare deciduous trees on the outer NE edge of Willow Circle, then after a time drops down into the yard of Mr. Finlay, often into the lower branches of a large spruce--and then to one of the feeders. Mr. Finlay reports that it appears to be caching whole peanuts, in addition to feeding directly at the suet & peanut butter feeders. Another neighbor on Willow Circle behind Finlay's yard (where the two large staging trees are) reports that "Ruby," as the neighborhood has named her, has excavated a roosting hole in the westernmost of the two trees. Mr. Finlay says it generally has been around the neighborhood from December to March every year.

Similar Species Discussion

As reported to CBRC after the first Dec. 2012 sighting, a colleague on the CBC first wondered if he was seeing, from the window of the coffee shop on the south side of Cheyenne Blvd., a Red-headed Woodpecker clinging to a roof edge of a house on the north side of the street. After looking myself, I knew immediately it wasn't that, as it did not have an entirely red head, nor large wing patches; it was very different from a Hairy Woodpecker or any other woodpecker or sapsucker I'd seen in the East or West. We ran outside to get a better look, and could see the smaller but still distinctive red patch on its head, and lots of pale yellow in its face. Having never seen this species before, after getting the best look I could from our position, I dug out Sibley's Field Guide to Birds of Western N. Amer. and saw that it could only be an Acorn Woodpecker. It took further and closer looking to determine that it was a female. 

In subsequent sightings (after Dec. 2012) no other species were considered.

Resources Used

Binoculars, Sibley's Field Guide to Birds of Western North America, Nikon D90 camera.

Previous Experience

I've seen it on two previous CBC's now (2012 and 2014), and also in La Plata County CO on May 18, 2013 with a CFO Convention field trip led by John Drummond. I'm very familiar with most woodpeckers typically found in Teller and El Paso Counties in CO, and with most found east of the Mississippi, where I began birding with the Clark County Audubon Society of Ohio around 1966.
