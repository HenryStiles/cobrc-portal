Description of the Bird

Clearly it was a woodpecker. Roughly the size of a red-headed woodpecker, which one of my companions (who first sighted the bird) on the CBC at first thought it might be, with the prominent red on its head. But the red was confined to the crown, or back of crown, and there wasn't prominent white on the back. The solid black back also eliminated hairy woodpecker and the various sapsuckers. When it flew and gave a call in flight, we could see a lot of white in the wings and rump.
Behaviors: It was perched on the lower edge of a roof, and seemed to be lapping at prey or water on the edge and underside of the roof (though the temperature was around 30-34, surely too cold for either insect prey or dripping water). It persisted in this behavior, clinging to the edge and reaching under with its beak, while shifting position and moving further away from us along the roof edge. Then it flew off at such an angle (to the left) that we only briefly saw it from the side as it flew--and let loose its call.

About two hours later I came back and refound it in a tree in the yard of the same house whose roof it had been feeding on. Curiously enough, a Northern flicker was doing the exact same feeding behavior on the roof edge that the Acorn Woodpecker had been doing earlier.

On this second sighting, it was working up & down & around the tree's main branches and trunk much more like a typical woodpecker. While starting out high and silouetted against the bright but cloudy sky, and then seeming to move to the opposite sides of the branches & trunk from where I'd be moving to see it better, it then suddenly flew down to eye level, right in front of me (ten feet or so away), allowing me to get a number of photos.
Call: In flight, a sort of softly nasal, chattery laugh. Heard only once.
Plumage: adult
