Habitat

The bird was in the trees on the edge of a small meadow near the lodge that serves as its lawn. The trees in the park are mostly Ponderosa Pine. The bird seemed to be favoring a large dead tree.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Canon SX50 superzoom camera
Distance:  Approximately 50 feet
Light:  Partly cloudy, but relatively good light. The bird could be clearly seen, especially when it landed on the second branch.

Description of the Bird

A medium sized bird with a red cap on a black and white head. There was a black ring around the bill and the bird had a pale eye. The upper breast was black and streaked. There were white spots on the wings that were clearly visible when the bird flew. The bird's rump was white.
Behavior: The bird landed on a dead tree that had numerous holes drilled into it. It pecked at the tree several times and then flew to a lower brach where it perched again and then flew off.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

The plumage on the head and the solid black back eliminated the possibility that the bird was a Downey, Hairy, or Ladder-backed woodpecker or any of the Sapsuckers that are found in the area. The position of the red coloration on the head and the distinct black stripe at the base of the bill also eliminated the more common speciesfound in the area and the Red-headed Woodpecker as well.

Previous Experience

I have seen numerous Downey and Hairy Woodpeckers as well as Northern Flickers. This is the first Acorn Woodpecker I have ever seen, but I am confident in my abilities to identify birds due to the training I received in taxonomy while a Biological Technician at the Smithsonian Environmental Research Center and while I earned my Master's degree in Biology from Western Washington University
