Description of the Bird

This female Acorn Woodpecker had been seen in the same place the previous year and found again a few days before our sighting. An Acorn Woodpecker of unknown gender
had been seen about 3 years previously at a private home within 2 miles of here, possibly the same individual. 
This bird flew in over our heads and perched on the side of a dead Ponderosa Pine, about 20 feet above the ground. It was directly compared to another Melanerpes woodpecker, Red-headed Woodpecker (M. erythrocephalus) and had the same shape, indicating the same genus.
The bird was about the same size as the RHWO and was highly patterned, nothing subtle about it. The back was jet black, with small white wing patches at the bases of 
the primaries, best seen in flight.The rump was white. The black of the back continued to the nape of the neck. PRoceeding rostrally, there was a small red spot on the back of the crown, and 
then a black patch on the forecrown, indicating female, which continued on the side of the face, enclosing the eye like a mask,  then a white forehead, & pale iris. The area around teh beak was also black, bill black. The area below the eye was white, continuing to the throat. This pattern resembled the "Bulls-eye" of an archery target, with 
black around the beak, surrounded by a ring of white, then a ring of black. This pattern accounts for the bird's nickname, "The clown-faced woodpecker."
The upper chest was black, with a few small white spots, the lower chest and belly white, with a black-streaked interface between the two. 
I did not hear any calls.
The bird interacted with the Red-headed Woodpecker, with the ACWO being displaced by the RHWO. They did stay near each other. This affinity was compatible with gregarious nature of the birds, but also could be interpreted as pair interaction.

Similar Species Discussion

Other woodpeckers, especially genus Melanerpes, were considered.
Consideration did not last a considerable length of time, as the plumage patterns are so different among these birds. 
The nearby Red-headed Woodpecker had an all red head, showed no black on the chest, but rather was all white below.
The RHWO wing patches were larger and closer to the body.

White-headed Woodpecker is superficially similar, with a patterned white and black head, with a small spot of red on the crown,
but in contrast to the bird we saw, 2/3 of the head is white. That species does not stray from its range, two states to the west, 
whereas ACWO's usual range is near to Colorado in New Mexico and there are a dozen or so previous locations in CO
where ACWO's have been documented.

Lewis's Woodpecker is a much darker bird overall, with only a little white on the neck, none in the wings or rump, with wider wings.
They are found in the part of Jefferson County and we saw a few that day for direct comparison.
Sapsuckers, Ladder-backed Woodpeckers are also patterned black, white and red, but the patterns are considerably different from the 
bird we saw and with the close look we had, were easily eliminated.
