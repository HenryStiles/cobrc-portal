Description of the Bird

The bird initially was facing away from me and gave me good looks at the back side which showed the streaking on the back (of course this does not rule out everything else). It was somewhat grayish/tan in color on the back with "blackish" streaks. The tail appeared a yellow-green color. The fringes of the wings were white with the base wing color seeming to be black in color.

The bird then moved in the tree and gave me more of a frontal view, showing the small black throat area and black at the base of the bill just into the eyes. The bill had the size and shape of a Bullock's Oriole. There was a yellow-orange coloring on the head and breast down towards the belly but it did seem to fade out near the base of the belly. I did not note the undertail coverts color. The yellow-orange color was much more "orangy" around the immediate face area. Becoming almost a orange-red color in the "cheek" area or just around the black throat patch.

The size of the bird was very similar to a "Northern" Oriole. But dimensions seemed different with the tail seeming longer. See similar species sections for the tail length description. The overall coloring of the bird was that of a Streak-backed Oriole and not that of a "Northern" Oriole. Not sure how to explain that but if you are familiar with Streak-backed birds in this plumage you know that the coloring just is not that of a "Northern" or any other species of Oriole that i have seen. Young Altamira Orioles might look similar in color but see similar species section for that.

I am speculating here on the age and sex of the bird, 1st year male. If I got the sex correct then I think I got the age correct. If the bird was a female then I assume it was an adult as I don't believe a 1st year female would show the black throat and even if it did I don't think it would show quite as bright orange around the face. The other reason I think it was a first year bird is how the color on the belly diffused to almost gray and I think an adult female would have been bright all the way down. But I am only speculating.
Behaviors: No real behaviors in supporting the ID other than general jizz of the bird being that of an oriole. The bird caught my attention as I was talking on the phone it flew up from either the ground, low bush or possibly the sewage "pond." It sat in a leafless elm at first looking away from me and bent over. It sat up and then "flitted" up just a bit in the tree. and faced me and then moved again down a bit and gave more of a profile look before finally flying almost straight over me into some thickets. Not to be seen again.

Similar Species Discussion

Hooded - Bill shape on Hooded is curved and the subject bird appeared to have a straight bill. The throat patch on this bird did not seem extensive enough to be hooded. Winter adult males do have similar backs but the pattern of "spotting" doesn't really form "streaks." I also don't believe the coloring of Hooded matches with the subject bird. I base this on the many Hooded Orioles I have seen.

Bullocks - Both immatures and adult female types can show a very similar back but typically very light gray streaks/spots on the back versus the "blackish" that I observed on the subject bird. Bullock's has a much shorter tail. Not that I could tail the length of the tail but I will explain at the bottom why I feel this bird had a rather long tail. Immature males, 2nd calendar year males and adult females can all show a very similar throat patch but the immature and adult would not show the orange and bright orange around the face. The 2nd calendar year male is what I was concerned about. But after asking Tony Leukering and reading up on it in Pyle I don't have any concerns. Here is what they both have to say:

Tony told me that these birds would have already molted and would appear like any other adult male Bullock's.

Pyle says that complete molt of AHY birds occurs from Aug-Oct.

If for some reason this bird was a little delayed I think it would have shown a much "blacker" back and tail color should have been different along with all the white in the wing.

Baltimore - Immature birds show very similar backs but maybe finer streaking almost more like spots. No age shows the black throat patch and I feel the wing bars are much more prominent in this species than Streak-backed. As the bird I had really did not have wing bars. Like Bullock's this species has a shorter tail, see below.

Altimira - I only list this one because the immature birds are maybe the closest to the same coloring of Sterak-backed but this species does not show any streaks on the back and is noticeably larger.

Why I felt this bird had a long tail. When the bird flew the tail was almost like that of a Song Sparrow when it flies. It seemed to pump the tail up and down in a very exaggerated way. This to me would happen when you have a long tail versus a short tail. I believe a Streak-backed has a 8-10 cm tail to a full length of ~21cm, so almost half the body length. And of the similar species I see regularly (Bullock's and Baltimore) I never recall seeing either of these species look as exaggerated in their flight styles as this bird was.
