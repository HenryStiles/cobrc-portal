Description of the Bird

The overall size and shape of this bird is right for an oriole species appearing larger than a Bullock’s Oriole, yet slightly smaller and leaner than Red-winged Blackbird.

I believe this bird to be an adult female. All her feathers show even wear and there are no contrasting new flight feathers or greater and lesser coverts.

This bird has all the field marks of a Streak-backed Oriole; Dark orange, almost reddish malar, orange-olive face, olive head, finely black-streaked olive back, bright orange belly fades to orange yellow as it goes all the way down past the legs to the bright orange/yellow undertail coverts. Deep black mask with no line behind the eye, black throat patch. Lower mandible gray-blue tipped with black at least half way, beak large and straight. Primary projection barely comes to end of undertail coverts. Lesser coverts broadly edged in white, greater coverts edged in grayish-white.
Eyes dark, legs and feet gray-blue. The tail is uniformly worn, olive green at the base, fading to olive brown near the end.
Behaviors: This bird was first seen eating roasted peanut halves from a small tray feeder under the spruce tree. This bird has tried every type of seed and feeder in my yard. She began to eat suet pellets and grape jelly as soon as I offered them (her second day here) and now solidly prefers live meal worms and grape jelly with occasion snacks of roasted peanuts and suet pellets. She arrives in the spruce every morning from between 7:05 a.m. to her latest arrival so far on 12/29 of 7:54 a.m. She eats steadily for the first hour. Then spends her days alternately preening, sunning and eating. She is not a timid, shy or flighty bird in comparison to Red-winged Blackbirds or Starlings, and will sit on the feeder even when the snow blower is being used in the driveway. She is tolerant of smaller birds, but will defend her food source from Blackbirds and Starlings until they out number her. She shares the feeder peacefully with the Northern Flickers. She has become quite reliable to feeding and often comes down in less than one minute after meal worms are refreshed, if she happens to be in the tree.
Call: I have heard this bird vocalize twice. Once after a Sharp-shinned Hawk grabbed a house finch from “her” tree, and once as she approached and then landed on the tray feeder. Obviously an Oriole chatter, deeper and harsher sounding than Bullock’s.
Plumage: Adult Female

Similar Species Discussion

Bullock’s Oriole ruled out, because of bright orange malar, orange-yellow on chest and belly goes all the way past legs. Upper mandible all dark with contrasting blue-gray lower mandible. Bullock’s mandibles would be all pale gray.
