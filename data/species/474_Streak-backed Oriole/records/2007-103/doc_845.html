Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized passerine, significantly larger than nearby House Finches, but noticeably smaller and slimmer than nearby Red-winged Blackbirds. The bill was fairly long (for a passerine), broad based but thinning quickly and sharply pointed, and the upper mandible was dark gray color, and the lower mandible was mostly very pale blue-gray, with a darker gray tip about the same color as the upper mandible. The overall shape was typical of an oriole, but broader based than expected for a Bullock's Oriole. The eye was black; the legs were medium gray.

The crown was orange with a strong greenish tinge, as was the nape. The superciliary and fore most part of the forehead were a bright burnt orange; the malar was the same color if not a little brighter. The throat and lores were black; the lores narrowed towards the eye, and came to a sharp point just barely behind the eye; this was not noticeable except at very close range. The cheek varied, from bright burnt orange, to the orange with a greenish tinge, as in the crown.

The back was gray with a noticeable orangish tinge. There were obscure but noticeable black streaks on the back, made up of thin lines, not chevroned shaped dark centers to the back feathers. The wings were black, with bold white edges to the flight feathers and coverts. There were two bold white wing bars as well.

The underparts were bright yellowish orange, becoming slightly paler towards the belly, but still bright yellow-orange. The lower flanks were gray, with only a slightly yellowish orangish tinge. The tail was olive with a strong dusky tinge in the central tail feathers, but brighter on the outer tail feathers. The undertail coverts were the same orange-yellow color as most the underparts. The uppertail coverts and rump were yellow-orange with a more noticeable olive tinge.
Behaviors: the first day the bird was seen it spent a few minutes perched still in a grove of junipers. It then flew to some weeds on the ground, to a spruce tree, and finally to the platform feeder. It spent a few minutes there, and then flew back into the spruce tree briefly before flying high into a large cottonwood directly over my head. The last I saw it that day was when it flew quite a distance to a small bush along the main road.

During the second day it spent most of it's time in the spruce tray, making frequent forays onto the ground feeder, and occasionally onto the platform feeder. It appeared to be eating a combination of dried fruit, grape jelly, and maggots.
Call: I heard a chatter during the first observation; it sounded like a fairly typical oriole chatter, and since I was on the phone at the time I was not able to take note of what exactly it sounded like.
Plumage: first cycle

Similar Species Discussion

Most other species of oriole were ruled out by the orange color overall, and the black throat and lores. Baltimore Oriole was ruled out by the black throat and lores, as well as the thin streakes on the back, short primary extension, and broad based bill with a dark tip to the lower mandible.

Bullock's Oriole was ruled out by the contrastingly bright burnt-orange malar; the lack of black behind the eye, the thinner streaks than expected on the back, short primary extension, and the broad based bill with a dark tip to the lower mandible.

The hardest thing to rule out, in my opinion, was an intergrade Bullock's x Baltimore. Conceivably there could be a bird that matched many of the features that this bird showed. However, the broad based bill with a dark tip to the lower mandible, as well as the back with thin streaks and orange ting, lack of black stripe behind the eye, and the pattern of burnt-orange on the face, rule out that possibility.

Altamira Oriole was ruled out by the streaked back, broad white edges on the wing and no orange wing bar, smaller size, and different pattern of brunt orange and yellow that expected for that species.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Streak-backed Orioles in Mexico and Costa Rica, but have not knowingly seen this plumage before, nor have I spent nearly as much time as I should have studying them as I should have. I have seen many, many Bullock's Orioles, and know all plumages of that species quite well. I am also familiar with Baltimore Oriole, and moderately familiar with Altamira Oriole
