Description of the Bird

A smallish orange oriole, the bird was first identified as a Bullock's as it was similar in appearance to that species. The forehead was fairly bright orange with the color dulling and greening toward the rear of the crown. The lores were black with some extension of that black around the gape and connecting to a fairly extensive black throat patch. There was just a very small bit of black behind the eyes forming a very, very short eyeline and this was only visible given very close views (but is visible in some of my pictures). The rest of the face was bright orange, the brightest color in the plumage. The back of the head and neck were orangey-yellow with a slight greenish cast.

The back was grayish-brown washed a bit with orange. There were obvious and distinct dark streaks along the shafts of the back feathers forming obvious dark longitudinal streaks. I did not see the rump well enough to know what color it was.

The underparts were mostly orange, with the brightest of that color on the upper chest and the color getting more yellow toward the upper belly. The center of the belly was grayish with a wash of orange or intermixed orangish feathers. The vent and undertail coverts were pale orange.

The remiges and their associated covets were blackish with obvious and distinct whitish fringes, with some of those fringes having a brownish aspect, too. The scapulars were similar in appearance to that of the back feathers, but without quite so obvious dark shaft streaks.

The eyes were dark, as were the legs. The bill was fairly long - being about 2x the length of the distance between maxilla base and eye - and quite pointed. The maxilla was blackish and the mandible was black-tipped with a bluish-gray base. The culmen was virtually straight and the mandible was a bit concave.
Behaviors: Visiting feeder eating grape jelly and other treats

Similar Species Discussion

Bullock's Oriole is most similar and is easily eliminated by a number of features, particularly the back pattern of distinct dark streaks. Also important is the precise face pattern, with little in the way of black behind the eye (Bullock's should sport an obvious eyeline) and the very bright orange on the face that should not be present on any but adult male Bullock's Orioles. Additionally, the very restricted amount of gray on the underparts helps rule out Bullock's. Other orioles (e.g., Baltimore) are even more readily eliminated by the bird's back pattern and face pattern.
