Description of the Bird

I hiked west from the trailhead signs. Soon I reached a sharp northerly turn in the trail (to my right), which would parallel the stream and its valley up to and around the Lake Dorothey dam. It eventually crosses over the stream just below the spillway from Lake Dorothey. It was along this curve that I first heard the absolutely unmistakable "song" of a BLACK-BILLED CUCKOO. Not knowing its status and distribution in the immediate area, and not having a recording of any kind yet of this furtive species, I fumbled to get to my recorder to record the song. It was singing its standard, repetitive, almost rhythmic chant: "ker koh-koh-koh, ker koh-koh-koh, ker koh-koh-koh." I was able to capture two recordings with the bird as a background species. They are nonetheless diagnostic; nothing else sounds quite like it. There were many grassland-type and riparian birds singing amidst a wonderful dawn chorus that were between myself and the cuckoo (as heard in the recordings). The bird suddenly stopped singing as I tried to walk to a quieter spot, and I instictively looked up to see if it had flushed. Sure enough, the signature long, lanky, floppy-fast flight pattern of a cuckoo was heading up-canyon (towards Lake Dorothey). It was a bi-colored bird laterally speaking, with a uniform chocolate-brown look to the upperparts and off-white look to the undercarriage. Critically, there was clearly no rufous in the wings: though my looks were distant, the much more uniform milk chocolate brown color was spread equally across the bird's upperparts (vs. the striking bi-colored effect that YBCUs in flight show due to rufous flight feathers paired with brown mantles and rectrices). Mangrove Cuckoo can be eliminated by a lack of salmon to the underparts; it would also be the least expected by far of the three regularly-occuring US Coccyzus sp. for this area and time of year.

I encountered the bird a second time just after hiking over the culvert that takes the stream under the trail. It was singing from a perch that must have been much closer to me than the first time. However, the sound of water cascading down the spillway from Lake Dorothey made hearing the bird difficult, let alone attempting to find it and get a recording. The bird was in a spot that made sense based on its flight trajectory earlier. I did not encounter the bird again, either above the dam or when hiking back to the parking area later.

RE: Plumage, I selected "breeding" in the drop-down on the previous page, although this is just an assumption based on locality and time of year; I don't know how much this species' molt might affect its appearance at different seasons and localities.

RE: Sex, I similarly selected "male" only because the bird was singing persistently; I do not believe the sp. to be sexually dimorphic.

Previous Experience

I have very little experience with BBCU, which is why it's nice that they have such a straight-foward, unique song.
27 May 2010 -- Detected one singing bird (presumed male) during contracted breeding bird surveys for the Georgia Department of Natural Resources at Rabun Bald, Georgia. In this case, too, I immediately grabbed my cell phone and began recording before trying to get better looks at the bird, which I later did.
20 April 2013 -- Relocated a rare migrant that had been reported by others at a park in Atlanta, Georgia. Several other birders where present; photos taken, etc.

I have equally little experience with MACU (3 observations), but I feel that the likelihood of this sp. being at this location at any time of year is extremly unlikely.

As expected for a birder/field tech from the Southeast, I have by far the most experience with YBCU; a quick check of my eBird data alone shows 415 individual birds recorded through early July 2016.
