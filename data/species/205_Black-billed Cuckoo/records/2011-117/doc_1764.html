Description of the Bird

Around 3:30 on the afternoon of 11 September 2011, Tracy Clark and I were walking around a willow and cottonwood fringed pond adjacent to Prewitt Reservoir in Washington County. A bird flushed in front of us, landing at about eye level, about 20 feet away (perhaps as close as 15 feet). Bright shade was the lighting conditions, and I was using Swarovski EL 10x42 bins. The bird remained stationary for a couple minutes, then slowly slinked through the tree, eventually giving us excellent views of undertail before taking off and disappearing. Total time of view 5+ minutes. My camera had broken during a fall earlier in the day, alas.

The bird was long and slim, typical for a cuckoo (Yellow-billed/Black-billed) in shape and size, perhaps appearing a bit smaller/less bulky than I'd expect from a YB Cuckoo. The head was the first part of the bird observed in detail. The bill was blackish, slightly decurved and a bit blunt shaped, giving the appearance (with some creativity) of a dark gray, somewhat straight, banana. There was no yellow in the bill. The eye was dark and was surrounded by a pale amber-yellow orbital ring. The forehead and crown were brown washed with gray fading into a more pure brown somewhere between mid-crown and nape. There was a faint hint of a duskier/darker facial mask. The throat and upper chest were pale dingy gray, forming a bibbed appearance. There was a fairly distinct demarcation between this and the remainder of the underparts which were dingy white washed in a fawn-buff. The tail was quite worn, and from beneath, appeared dull silvery-gray without any markings. The back and wings were dull brown, lacking any rust tones at all, while perched or after taking flight.

Similar Species Discussion

The only similar bird would be a juv YB Cuckoo.

The eyering color might suggest YBCU as it does not fit what is described in some guides for juv BB Cuckoo, but fits some on-line pics and the photo in Crossley's guide quite nicely.

Unbeknownst to me at time of observation, the throat/upper chest color was typical of juv BBCUs but not seen on YBCU. The non-white underparts washed in fawn color is also a juv BBCU trait, as is the lack of rust in wings. The undertail pattern fits juv BBCU nicely, especially given state of wear
