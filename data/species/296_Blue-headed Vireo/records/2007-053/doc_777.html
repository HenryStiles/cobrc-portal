Description of the Bird

This bird was with a group of other migrant passerines including Yellow Warblers and a Cassin's Vireo. Kevin Groeneweg and I observed the bird a couple of times for about 10 seconds each in bright sunlight with a favorable light angle. The bird was obviously of the Solitary Vireo complex, with prominent white "spectacles" and two distinct wingbars. When we first viewed it from the front, the clearly delineated edges of the white throat had us hoping for a Plumbeous, which is the rarest of this group in Kansas and always much hoped for there. However, as it turned to the side we were able to see that the blue-gray head plumage was cleanly separated from a bright green back. The green tertials and primaries were edged with yellow-gold, quite apparent given the good light conditions. Also the wingbars were yellow instead of white. The flanks were broadly smudged with bright yellow. So it was apparent to us that this was a typical Blue-headed such as we are quite familiar with in east-central Kansas. The presence of a Cassin's Vireo in the same tree offered a good comparison of these two birds.
Behaviors: The vireo was part of a mixed-species flock of warblers and vireos that was moving through the middle canopy of the riparian corridor below Two Buttes dam. It was fairly active during the brief presence of this small flock of birds. We were unable to relocate either vireo when a couple of other members of our party caught up with us.
Call: silent
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

See description above. Plumbeous is out because of the bright green back. Cassin's would be much more dull overall and lacks the clearly delineated white throat, and also the cleanly separated plumage color between the blue-gray head the bright green/yellow tertials and primaries. The yellow wingbars are also lacking in both of the above.

Resources Used

We consulted Sibley briefly when we returned to the car about an hour later.

Previous Experience

Blue-headed is a common migrant in our home area. I've observed Cassin's several times during fall trips to the western Kansas migrant traps. IN fact we found two more in western Kansas the following day. I have observed Plumbeous in Arizona and SW Colorado during the breeding season, and once in Kansas in September about 10 years ago. Plumbeous in particular is a highly-sought species in western Kansas in fall and consequently most of us who visit the area are acutely aware of the critical identification points between these three vireo species.
