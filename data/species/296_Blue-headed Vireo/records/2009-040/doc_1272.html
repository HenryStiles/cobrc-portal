Habitat

In a grove of Siberian elms right around the headquarters building of the Fox Ranch. This site is great for vagrants. For example, there was a Tennessee Warbler, a dead Ovenbird, and a live Overbird there at the same time.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Nikon 10x EDG binoculars
Distance:  As close as 50 feet, but with a significant vertical vector.
Light:  It was sunny, but the bird was in and out of the shadows in the vegetation. Given the extreme importance of being able to accurately assess contrast and brightness in Cassin's-v.-Blue-headed field IDs, I'd call the conditions fair.

Description of the Bird

It was definitely a "Solitary Vireo." As it was one of the more brightly colored species (Cassin's or Blue-headed), it was of immediate interest, as either of the brightly colored species is unusual in far eastern Colorado in spring.

The bird was brightly colored and brightly patterned overall: blue face with white spectacles: greenish-yellow above, yellowish on the flanks, and white below. In my field notebook, which I had with me at the time, I drew a very crude rendering of the face, coarsely indicating the good contrast between the bird's dark face and pale throat, i.e., sharp malar contrast.
Behaviors: Vireo-like, the bird moved slowly in the emerging vegetation in the tall Siberian Elms at the headquarters of the Fox Ranch. We saw it in and out of the leaves for about 2 minutes. Then, despite constant searching off and on for 30 minutes, we could not relocate it. Nobody saw it fly off, but it may have. Or maybe it simply stayed out of view somehow. The bird never vocalized. We saw it snag or at least attempt to snag several caterpillars (probably family Geometridae).
Call: not heard
Plumage: probably definitive basic

Similar Species Discussion

Cassin's Vireo, obviously.

I've already touched on points of similarity, above.

Here, I'd like to make 2 points of context.

First, I am totally aware of the Cassin's-v.-Blue-headed conundrum in Colorado. If anything, I have a reputation for being somewhat hard-headed about Blue-headed records in Coloado in September and especially August. Conversely, I believe that Cassin's is OVER-reported in the spring. Yes, I suspect a few wander to Colorado in the spring. But I also wonder if some of those "Cassin's" are actually Blue-headeds (not to mention bright Plumbeous Vireos). I provide brief thoughts at: http://tiny.cc/KwyJV In any event, I would say that a non-Plumbeous "Solitary Vireo" in far-eastern Colorado in spring is perhaps as likely to be a Blue-headed as a Cassin's. So I don't think Cassin's is the default species there, not at all.

Second, I accept that the Cassin's-v.-Blue-headed problem is, at some level, insoluble in the field. A Blue-headed with a bad diet or bad genes might look exactly like a Cassin's. Conversely, a Cassin's with really good genes could like a Blue-headed. I don't know how to deal with that philosophical conundrum, except to say that the two aren't field-identifiable in Colorado. But I don't think we should say that. Alls I can say is that this bird matched Blue-headed, plumage-wise, better than Cassin's, PLUS I think Blue-headed is as likely as, given location and season, if not more likely than, Cassin's.

Previous Experience

Extensive. I frequently ponder the "Solitary Vireo" problem. I have seen and heard hundreds, perhaps thousands, of Blue-headeds back East. I have had several encounters with "intriguing" "Solitary Vireos" in Colorado that I concluded were either Cassin's or in the gray area between Cassin's and Blue-headed. This was the best case for a Blue-headed I'd ever seen in Colorado.

Comments: The bird matched Blue-headed in plumage. If that's good enough, then I'd vote for it. If you want to get into the philosophical conundrum of "What, really, is a Blue-headed Vireo?", then I don't see how to evaluate this or any Blue-headed Vireo record for Colorado.
