Description of the Bird

The bird was small, a bit bigger than a chickadee but with a short tail. The bill was thicker than a warbler with a small hook at the tip of the upper mandible. The bill was grayish-blue at the base and had a dark tip. The flanks were washed in a bright lemon yellow. The crown, nape, hind-neck, and cheeks were a slate-blue color. The dark eye was surrounded by thick white 'spectacles' that included the lores. The throat, vent, and belly where white. There was strong contrast between the slate-blue cheeks and the white throat. The back and wings were predominantly greenish-yellow. The inner secondaries and primaries were dark but distinctly edged with brighter yellowish-green. The bird had two bold, white wing-bars (median and greater coverts). The tertials had bold white edges. The legs were a powdery blue color.
Behaviors: When first spotted, it was about half-way up a ~70ft tall Plains Cottonwood tree. It was slowly moving from branch to branch gleaning insects. There were several Wilson's Warblers in the tree nearby, and the vireo's movements were much more furtive. The bird flew from the large cottonwood on the south side of the Boulder Creek Path to the riparian vegetation on the north side of the path. From there I pished it in to about 6 ft at eye level. When first observed high in the cottonwood, there was no doubt that it was a very bright "Solitary" Vireo, but it became conclusive that it was a Blue-headed Vireo when it came so close and at such a level height.
Call: none
Plumage: fall, if hatch-year or adult is unknown

Similar Species Discussion

Identified as a vireo by the thick bill with a hook at the top. The most similar species is the formerly conspecific Cassin's Vireo. This bird was distinguishable from that species by the overall very bright plumage, especially on the wings and flanks, and the strong contrast between the slate-blue cheeks and the white throat. Although some birds fall into an "intermediate" category between Blue-headed and Cassin's, this was not one of them.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

Having lived and birded in RI for many years, I have extensive experience with Blue-headed Vireos on the breeding grounds and particularly during migration. I have less experience with the similar Cassin's Vireo, with a few scattered sightings of this species in AZ, CA and CO.
