Description of the Bird

Somewhat delicate looking shorebirds, they had fairly short bills for their size that were dark and slightly decurved. The eyes were dark and seemed large for the size of the head, primarily because the heads were fairly small for their body size. The foreheads were pale buffy-brown and their crowns were finely streaked with pale buff and blackish. Except for whitish partial eye rings, the faces were quite plain and buff-colored. Their chins were white. The short, but thin, necks were generally brownish-buff and paler on the underside, darker on the upperside. The back feathers were black fringed in white, as were most of the scapulars, with the outer and lower scapulars having brown centers.

The wing coverts were medium brown with obvious blackish shaft streaks and very pale buff to white fringes. The tertials were similar in pattern to the wing coverts and barely extended beyond the base of the tail. The wingtips extended precisely to the end of the tail and were tipped minutely with white.

The underparts were plain, being mostly whitish, except for a slight buff wash on the upper sides and some on the upper chest which was also dotted with small blackish spots; the flanks were ever so slightly more buff than the whiter bits of the underparts. The legs were yellow and of medium length -- shorter than those of Lesser Yellowlegs, but longer than those of Baird's Sandpipers, both of which species were in direct comparison to at least one of the Buff-breasteds.
Behaviors: Birds foraged for surface animals primarily in higher and drier parts of the beach.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

Baird's Sandpiper is smaller, shorter-legged, longer-tertialed, longer-winged (relative to tail length), and with more of a pattern on the face and chest. Lesser Yellowlegs is taller with longer legs, paler, with a different pattern above. Stilt Sandpiper, particularly juvs, are quite differently-patterned above and with longer, more decurved bills.
