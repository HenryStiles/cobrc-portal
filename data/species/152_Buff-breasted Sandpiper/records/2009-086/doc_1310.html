Description of the Bird

Medium-small shorebird with distinctive pigeon-like posture and overall warm buffy tones. Although distant, direct size comparisons with nearby Killdeer (smaller than), Baird's Sandpipers (similar in size but more upright), and unidentified peeps (bigger than) showed it to be a medium-small shorebird. Plumage detail in the back hard to discern at distance, but back looked contrastingly darker than the warm buffy underparts, neck, and face. Bill short and straight, eye looked relatively large on the roundish head, and the bird had a slender, long neck. Legs yellow and relatively long (longer, proportionately, than the peeps or Baird's, for example.)
Behaviors: Stayed near water's edge but didn't enter the water like other shorebirds except Killdeer. Walked with an unusual (for a shorebird) pigeon-like gait, alternating between picking at the mud and picking at sticks, twigs, and branches draped with dried algae (and presumably insects.) All other shorebirds were probing underwater or picking from the muddy surface- none were picking from above ground level like this bird. Walked by many other shorebirds but didn't associate with them or move in tandem with any others.
Call: not heard
Plumage: likely juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

Probably the most similar in terms of overall color and long yellow legs would be juvenile Ruff, but in direct comparison to other nearby birds it was clearly smaller than a Ruff. Pectoral Sandpiper is similar in size and has long yellow legs but lacks the bright buffy wash, shows a well-developed bib, and doesn't look as slender or delicate. Other yellow-legged shorebirds lack the combination of size, bright buffy wash, short straight bill, and odd pigeon-like posture & gait of a Buff-breasted Sandpiper. I'm also not aware of other shorebirds frequenting the shorelines of Colorado lakes that routinely glean food from emergent vegetation or dead twigs and branches like Buff-breasted Sandpipers do.
