Description of the Bird

I first noticed this bird with binoculars and noted the slightly larger size than a nearby Baird's Sandpiper, long legs, and overall tawny appearance, and immediately suspected Buff-breasted Sandpiper. With a scope I was better able to confirm its size as being slightly larger than a nearby Baird's Sandpiper, and also smaller with decidedly less bulk than a Killdeer that was also in the area. The dark bill was short, straight, and tapered to a sharp point. The bird's plain, unmarked "face" from just above the eye to the hindneck was a rich tawny color that extended unbroken onto the chest and upper abdomen, becoming lighter on the bird's lower abdomen. The crown was mottled with dark streaks that extended onto the hindcrown to the upperback. The scapulars and wing coverts were solidly black centered with broad buffy edges giving a very scalloped pattern to the upperparts. The primaries were also darkish centered with buffy edges. Wings extended, or nearly so, to the tip of the bird's tail. The long legs were dull yellow. Its eyes were solidly black and appeared rather large for the bird's head.
Behaviors: The bird foraged in the mud and grassy upland portions near a small pool of water apart from a much larger nearby pond. Although it was near a Baird's Sandpiper and Killdeer, it did not interact or associate directly with either of those birds. I observed it running short distances and reaching down into the grass or mud surface, apparently foraging on small terrestrial prey.
Call: None heard.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The combination of buffy unmarked "face" and underparts and short bill ruled out all other species. A juvenile Ruff is perhaps the most similar, but it has a relatively longer bill, a postocular line, and would have been much closer in size to the Killdeer, whereas this bird was closer in size and proportion to a Baird's Sandpiper. A juvenile Red Phalarope, although buffy, would have short dark legs and a large dark postocular patch.
