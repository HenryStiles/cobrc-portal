Description of the Bird

The bird was working in a patch of green algae away from the water line about 10-12 feet. It would walk and pick up bits in a typical shorebird type motion. The bird was light tannish buff overall and slightly larger than the Bairds Sandpipers that foraged right at the waters edge. The pictures showed the bird to have yellowish legs covered with the green algae and mud. The pictures also showed a shorebird with a longish neck and a somewhat small head. The shortish bill was black and quite straight. The pictures also showed a bird with a scaly pattern on the wing coverts and scapulars with dark feather centers and whitish-buff edging. The greater coverts had a buffy center to the feathers rather than the dark centers as on the other coverts. There were a series of small darker spots to the sides of the neck that came down to the upper breast and faded away at the center of the breast. The face and neck were tannish-buff, slightly darker than the underparts. The underparts were very light buff with no appreciable markings. the lower belly and vent areas were lighter than the breast area. The tertials were darker and extended past the tail tip. The detailed description of the feathers and bill and legs came from the pictures that Jack Bushong took.

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was larger than the Bairds Sandpipers and Western Sandpipers nearby and smaller than the associated Killdeer in the area and was significantly smaller than the Dowitchers that have been seen at the lake. The bird also appeared larger than the Semipalmated Sandpipers and Least Sandpipers that had been reported earlier in the Summer at the lake. The bird tended to forage by itself away from the waters edge. Pectoral Sandpiper was ruled out by no darker markings on the upper breast and by the smaller straight bill. Stilt Sandpiper was ruled out by the somewhat plain tannish face and shorter straight bill and by the plain buffy underparts with no markings on the sides of the breast or flanks. Sharp-tailed Sandpiper was ruled out by the lack of warm brownish edging to the coverts and scapulars, by the lack of a brown crown, and by the lack of a whitish supercilium.

Previous Experience

I have seen several Buff-breasted Sandpipers in Logan County at Jumbo Reservoir in years past. I have seen all of the Calidris Sandpipers normally seen in Larimer County dozens of times. I have seen Ruff one time in Alaska and both of the Dowitchers numerous times both here in Colorado as well as in California and Alaska.
