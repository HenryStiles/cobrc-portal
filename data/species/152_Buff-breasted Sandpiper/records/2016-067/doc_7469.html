Description of the Bird

We picked it out from the numerous Baird's Sandpipers by it's larger, more delicate look from afar. We then viewed it through the scope and camera for about 15 minutes from close range (approximately 50ft) before going back to the parking lot to inform others about it. After coming back across the dam we saw a Peregrine Falcon swoop down and flush near all of the birds along the south shoreline. After several failed attempts it nabbed a shorebird in midair(possibly the BBSA). We could not relocate it. This was a beautiful, medium sized, dove-headed shorebird that appeared slightly larger and more slender than nearby Baird's Sandpipers. It had a buff-colored wash on it’s entire body, except the vent(which was white). Fine dark streaking on crown, short, thin bill. Unmarked, buff underparts instead of the streaked/spotted underparts of other similar shorebirds. Pale, buff face and neck. Distinctive yellow legs, sometimes partially covered with mud or in a shadow. Buffy all over. Not calling. Many photos

Similar Species Discussion

Baird's Sandpiper easily eliminated by yellow/olive-colored legs, different bill shape, and buff extending only to breast. Upland Sandpiper much larger with bicolored bill (orange/yellow with black tip), large eye, long, thin neck, and such. No other species were considered in the identification of this bird. 

Resources Used

I used prior knowledge of this birds appearance from looking through field guides, such as the Sibley Guide to Birds. Immediately after the bird was seen we looked through another birders guide to confirm the identification. Field marks were used in the field to identify the sandpiper, and after the observation we emailed Tony Leukering who confirmed the identification. This write-up was made by reviewing photos and by memory. 

Previous Experience

I have little experience with Buff-breasted Sandpipers, but have a fair amount of experience with Baird's Sandpiper, Least Sandpiper, and the like
