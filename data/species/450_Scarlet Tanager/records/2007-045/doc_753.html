Habitat

Open ponderosa pine forest with some Douglas-fir, without much understory cover; a number of dead snags and open meadows in the area, mostly on steep slopes. In the gully bottoms, deciduous riparian forest dominated by cottonwoods. Interestingly, there is riparian forest a half mile downstream that is much more diverse, much thicker, and much more extensive than the area that the bird frequents.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  8x42 Eagle Optics binoculars
Distance:  25 feet at closest approach
Light:  Full sun, mostly at my back.

Description of the Bird

It was a tanager, same size and shape as Western Tanager, but bright fluorescent orange-red on all body feathers, with solid black wings and tail. Bill was large and pale, maybe slightly larger than the bills I am used to seeing on Western Tanager.
Behaviors: Bird was foraging and singing from the tops of trees--it seemed to prefer the cottonwoods in the draw beside the trail, but also sang regularly from the dead top of a live ponderosa pine near the trail. It seemed territorial but did not appear to have a mate. There were female Western Tanagers in the area, including one in the same tree as the SCTA at nearly the same time; I never saw them interact and I never saw any indication that they were paired. The fact that the male was still singing frequently, almost continuously, at 10:00 AM on July 7th strongly suggests to me that he was unpaired.
Call: Bird was singing frequently, through at least 10:00 AM when most other birds were silent. Song was almost indistinguishable from those of nearby Western Tanager males: strict series of 3-5 well-separated 2- or 3-syllabled semimusical burry phrases, delivered within each series at ca. 2/sec, the series starting a few seconds apart. I believe the song may have included a few phrases that were clearer than most Western Tanager phrases, but I was never certain I could distinguish the species by ear, even with direct audio comparison.
