Description of the Bird

In shape the bird was clearly an oriole -- a little smaller than a robin and more slender, with a long tail and a medium-length sharp bill. The bird was yellowish-green with some black on the face and upper breast, and on the wings and tail. I think the bird was likely a young male, as its plumage didn't closely match anything in the Sibley guide -- it had more black on it than the adult female pictured, but nowhere near as much as the adult male. Overall it was a scruffy-looking individual. When it flew away I coudl see that the rump was yellow, and I could see a vague yellow-and-black pattern in the tail. The wings were mostly black and almost lacked the white wingbar, as I recall.
Behaviors: The bird was very active and vocal. It arrived on the fencepost right behind the group and sang once loudly, at which point Dave Leatherman looked at it and said, "everybody needs to look at this oriole!" By the time he was in the middle of his sentence the bird had begun flying over the group, singing again, and I called out "Scott's Oriole!" because Dave's identification to genus had allowed me to recognize the song. The bird landed in a small bush and sang 1-2 times, then flew much farther up the hillside, sang another time or two, and disappeared over the ridge. We never saw it feed.
Call: The bird sang several times. Its song was a series of loosely connected clear musical whistles on various pitches, with various inflections, with a sing-songy cadence reminiscent of the song of Sage Sparrow, in that the last part of the song phrase tends to recapitulate the first part. The song of Scott's Oriole is distinctive, and I was able to identify the bird first by its vocalizations, before I had even gotten a clear look at it.
Plumage: probably an immature male

Similar Species Discussion

Other orioles (e.g., young Orchard) were ruled out by the yellowish rump contrasting with a mostly-dark tail, and (especially) by the song, which was the classic Scott's Oriole song.
