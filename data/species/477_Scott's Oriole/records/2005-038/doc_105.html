Habitat

The bird was in the tree closest to the feeder he had been reported to be using. This is an older residential neighborhood with established plantings.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  8.5 x 42 Swarovski binoculars
Distance:  about 60 feet
Light:  The bird was in good late afternoon light. The oriole was to the east of me and the sun was to the west so there was no back-lighting. There was slight haze but no clouds.

Description of the Bird

This bird was slightly smaller and more slender than American robin with a proportionally longer tail. It's head, breast, back, end of tail and primaries were black. The belly, flanks, undertail coverts, and lesser coverts were bright lemon yellow. I could not see the rump or base of tail. The bird had two bright white wing bars, the primaries appeared to be edged in white. The bill was moderately long, slender and pointed. I didn't note the leg color.
Behaviors: I observed the bird just briefly in the top of a large willow tree that had just begun to leaf out. He flew off shortly after landing.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult basic

Similar Species Discussion

Of the orioles with black heads only Scott's and Audubon's share yellow plumage. Audubon's was eliminated by lack of black back and breast. Adult male Orchard and Baltimore share the black head and back but lack the yellow coloration.

Resources Used

Sibley's Guide to Birds--consulted later

The bird was initially reported on CObirds as a Scott's Oriole, so that is what I was expecting.

Previous Experience

I first saw Scott's Oriole in May 2000; I most recently saw one in Kern Co. California in April 2005. I've seen Audubon's in Texas in 2000 and 2003. I'm also familiar with Orchard Oriole, Baltimore Oriole and others less similar to the Scott's.
