Description of the Bird

I was walking in the Estes Park golf course, through the snow, at about 7:45am, under high overcast skies. Most of the conifers on the golf course had a ring of open grass, free from snow, beneath them. These areas accumulated birds, mostly thrushes and sparrows. As I walked up to one such tree, a thrush flushed from one tree and flew to another tree, about 50 feet away. During its flight, its rusty back was evident, quite different from the multiple Olive-backed Thrushes (and one Hermit) that I'd already flushed that morning.

I worked my way towards the thrush, and it was nice enough to drop down near the tree trunk. I paused to watch at a distance of about 30-40 feet. Shortly thereafter, I noticed that there was an Olive-backed Thrush within 3 feet of it (perhaps it had hopped out from behind the tree trunk.) I worked my way slowly towards the two birds, stopping at a distance of 20-25 feet, and then closely compared them. I was able to watch the two together for about 20-30 seconds, and then the Olive-backed Thrush flew. I watched the Russet-backed on the ground for a little while longer. Then it flew up into the tree, far enough out on a limb, that I had brighter lighting on it, which made its rusty colored back (relatively speaking; not as bright as a Brown Thrasher, for instance) more obvious. It then flew to the next tree, and the chase was on. In the end, I watched the bird for about 2 minutes, including 4 observation periods in a variety of lighting conditions, mostly at a distance of 20-40 feet.

Description
The bird was similar in shape and size to the nearby Olive-backed Swainson's Thrushes (though I did not carefully compare sizes, so subtle differences may have been present). The back/wings were a warm-brown verging on Veery-like rust, consistent with the RB Thrushes that I saw many of while living in w. Washington. This color was distinctly different from the cold brown color of the Olive-backed Thrush it was compared with and other OB Thrushes that I've seen in CO (which has been many). The proximal tail was similar in color to the back/wings/rump, but the color was duller more distally (distal half duller?), something that I have noted on Russet-backed Thrushes in w. Washington, but not on Olive-backed Thrushes (In other words, in my experience, OB Thrushes have uniform colored tails, tails that are the same color as the back. RB Thrushes have tails that are duller colored distally, making the tail uneven in color.) The flight feathers, when briefly exposed, seemed rustier than the remainder of the wing as well.

The face pattern was less pronounced than that of the OB Thrush, lacking the bold posterior border to the auriculars commonly seen on OB Thrushes. The auriculars were warm brown. The narrow eyering was complete and was washed in pale buff. I did not notice presence or absence of loral stripe. There were narrow dark "lateral throat stripes." The throat and chest were suffused with buff. The chest had diffuse brown spots that were not as dark, well-demarcated, or extensive as those on OB Thrushes. The belly was off-white (precise hue/tone not really noted), except the sides, which were pale brownish-grayish, becoming dull buff posteriorly (on flanks). Leg and vent/undertail color/pattern not noted. No vocalizations. Bill color, pattern, size not noted other than it was not dramatically different (eg, long and curved, bright red, etc).

Identification
The separation of Olive-backed and Russet-backed Thrushes was a quandary that I'd become keenly interested in during my last couple years in WA. Beyond the back color and flank color differences, the differences in spotting (as noted in description above) seemed quite reliable. Since moving here, I've been struck by the bolder face pattern of OB Thrushes, and in comparing photos, this difference seems at least somewhat reliable. This bird was immediately evident as different from the many Olive-backed Thrushes, with the first view (mostly a back view) being startling enough to cause me to consider Veery. The direct comparison in back colors verified that the rust color of the RB Thrush was not some odd misperception. I have seen thousands of RB Thrushes and hundreds of OB Thrushes in the last decade, and feel confident in separating most of them with good views. This bird provided quite nice views, and I personally feel confident of its identification as a Russet-backed Swainson's Thrush.
