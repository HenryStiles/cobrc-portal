Optics:  Leica 77mm APO scope with 32x WW lens.

Description of the Bird

These were large white aquatic birds with longish arched necks, i.e. swans. The bills were black with yellowish lore extending onto bill. Body gray white with humped back. Legs blackish, feet not seen. Bill with rounded upper edge between eyes. Eye dark. Head rounded and neck noticeably shorter than that found on Trumpter Swan. Necks often bent in "S" curve while swimming. I looked for the suggested curved sides to the bill when in profile which was present but not noticeably different than the Pueblo TRSW. The head appeared quite rounded. The bill shape was not like that portrayed by Canvasback and therefore TRSW. The center of the bill was slightly concave and the bill seems shorter than what I remembered from the Pueblo TRSW.
Behaviors: Searched for food in shallow water but I did not see them find any food. The never tipped completely butt up to feed, but instead kept their whole head and only a small amount of neck under water. Three swans stayed quite close all of the time and near the fourth all of the time. When the group moved towards or away from shore all members of group did the same. Often seen near a roosting flock of Ring-billed Gulls and searched for food with three species of dabling duck. Did not seem too nervous when a jogger went by, but again the distance was significant, albeit totally open.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult basic

Similar Species Discussion

Trumpeter Swan looks quite similar but have noticeably longer necks. Head profile is slightly different with flatter top of head, seeminly longer, straighter bill, Canvasback like, and a "V" where the top of the bill joins the forehead and not a slight curve as in Tundra. Also TRSW usually without yellow lore. Back is more humped usually. The angle of the bill along the side of the head is straighter but I don't believe that this is a reliable character after studying TRSW 2 weeks ago, both the TRSW and these four birds had rounded edge to their bills as seen in profile.

Whooper Swan very much larger than TUSW with noticable larger amount of yellow on bill.

Additional Information

Comments: From far away with scope and closer with binoculars, the yellow lores could not be seen. Only when fairly close and with scope was the yellow apparent on all four birds. With time, the shorter necks and more goose-like appearance became more and more evident. They all kept their necks bent much more than the Pueblo TRSW did.
