Description of the Bird

the birds were very large all white birds, far larger than anything else on the reservoir (including greater Canada Geese nearby). The overall shape was typical of a swan, with a long, thin neck, goose-like body, and large, black bills. The bills on the adults were black; some of the birds had a small yellow spot at the base in front of the eye. The young birds had mostly pink bills, with a little bit of black on the base and the tip. The overall shape included a noticeably concaved culmen, and an angled "cheek contour", rather than the smooth line from the eye to the gape of Trumpeter Swan. The bill was connected to the eye by only a very thin strip of black skin. The feathering on the top of the bill where it met the bill was rounded.

The plumage of the adult birds was entirely white; that of the young birds was dusky brown, darkest on the head and neck, and mottled with whiter feathers on the back.
Behaviors: The birds were first found when they were landing on the reservoir. They swan around for about 10 minutes before they took off again and flew in various directions for a while. They eventually followed the foothills north. Later we saw then fly into the reservoir again, almost land, and repeat the same thing with flying in various directions. Eventually, they were last seen flying to the east.
Call: The birds were heard to utter a goose-like honk, but the birds were far away and it was hard to hear it well.
Plumage: adult and juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than swans were ruled out by the distinctive shape, color, and size. Trumpeter Swan was ruled out by the relatively smaller bill, yellow spot at the base in a few birds, angled cheek contour, rounded feathering at the base of the bill, and smaller size. The plumage of the young birds, with pinker bills and mottled with white also helped rule out Trumpeter Swan.
