Description of the Bird

These were very large birds (much larger than the Canada Geese on the lake), with long necks and all white plumage; both were adults. Since features of the head are most critical in separating our two native swans, I deal here mostly with those. Both birds had small amounts of yellow at the base of the maxilla in front of the eyes, one having about half again as much as the other; otherwise they were identical, and I hereafter treat them as such.

Except for the yellow patches, I could not discern any color on the bill other than black, though I do not believe that I could have seen the usual pale cutting edge. The meeting of bill and feathering at the gape was vertical and then slanted back to the eye. The eyes were dark and seemed isolated from the bill, which met at the forehead in a step, not in the long, continuous angle of Trumpeter Swan. The neck was held with a slight kink at the base.
Behaviors: Swimming

Similar Species Discussion

Mute Swan easily eliminated by black bills, adult plumage, yellow bill patches. Trumpeter Swan a bit less easily ruled out, but yellow bill patches, bill structure, bill-feathering interface, and neck posture all conspire to do the job.
