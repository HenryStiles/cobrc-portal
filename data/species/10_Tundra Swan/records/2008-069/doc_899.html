Description of the Bird

The bird was obviously a swan because it was entirely white, very large (larger than a goose), had a long neck and black bill. The swan had yellow lores. The neck was slightly heavier and shorter than the neck of a Trumpeter Swan. The black facial skin formed a high, slightly curved arc on the forehead. The bill was black. The slope of the bill was steepest at the base of the culmen. The slope of the remainder of the culmen was more shallow.
Behaviors: The swam was swimming on the pond and used feet to stir up food source.
Call: No vocalizations heard.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

The neck of a Trumpeter Swan appears relatively thinner and longer (providing a more graceful appearance). The yellow lores on the Tundra Swan were diagnostic. The bill characteristics were observed. The slope of bill of the bird was steeper at the base of the culmen (~1/4 of bill). The slope of the remainder of the Tundra Swan's bill was much more shallow a curve. The slope of the bill of a Trumpeter Swan would be much straighter. The black facial skin on the forehead didn't form the deep V-shaped notch that a Trumpeter Swan would possess. Instead this boundary was slightly curved.

Resources Used

We referred to "The Sibley Field Guide to Birds of Western North America" while out in the field. Later, I consulted "Birding in the American West" by Kevin Zimmer for other details of the differences between the 2 swans.

Previous Experience

I've had sufficient experience with Tundra Swans in recent years. My recent experience with Trumpeter Swan is also sufficient. Several Trumpeter Swans which are not wild have been in Archuleta County for several years. Incidently, 3 of those Trumpeter Swans moved over to Zink's Pond for a couple of weeks in December 2007. (To the best of our knowledge, the Trumpeter Swans were the swans from Pinon Lakes in Pagosa Springs.
