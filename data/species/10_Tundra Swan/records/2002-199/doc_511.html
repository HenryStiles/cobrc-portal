Description of the Bird

the birds were obvious swans, very large in size, significantly larger than nearby Canada Geese, with very long necks, small heads, and completely white plumage. The neck length, when laid out on the back, was a bit shorter than length of the body. The bills were black, and fairly small (for a swan), with a noticeable concavity to the culmen. The cheek contour was angled about halfway along its legth, and not a smooth line. There was a small yellow spot at the base of the bills on both birds, though this is not visible on the photos. The skin between the bill and eye was black, but very thin, barely connecting the bill to the eye. The head was rounded, with the peak on the middle of the crown.
Behaviors: the birds were seen swimming near the edge of the lake, and loafing along the shore.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than swans were ruled out by the large size, long neck, and white plumage. Mute Swan was ruled out by the black bill, larger bill with less concavity, and larger size with less arched back shape.

Trumpeter Swan was ruled out by the smallish bill with noticeable concavity to the culmen, angled cheek contour, yellow spot on the base of the bill, rounded head with peak in the center of the bill, limited connection between the bill and eye, and the length of the neck.

Additional Information

Comments: report made from notes taken the night of the observation, and from photos taken of the bird.

In case I haven't mentioned it yet, I strongly believe that Tundra Swan should not be a write-up species. It is a regular and not even very rare migrant, from what I can tell of historic records it has not declined much from it's historical status (contra the popular belief among many Colorado birders), and to me does not seem to meet the criteria needed to be a write-up species.
