Description of the Bird

the birds were obvious swans, very large, entirely white except for the bills, with very long and thin necks.

The neck length was 3/4 the length of the body. The back of the birds was not evenly curved, with a noticeable peak in the center of the back. The bill was fairly slender and (for a swan), somewhat smaller than the size of the head, with a noticeable concavity to the culmen. The eye was fairly isolated from the bill, with only a very thin piece of black skin connecting the eye and the bill. The cheek contour was noticeably and sharply angled about halfway along the length. I was unable to discern any yellow at the base of the bills on any of the birds, but distance was great.
Behaviors: the birds were in two groups, a group of two and a group of four. We later learned that the group of two was originally on Spinney Reservoir before flying to Eleven Mile.

All of the birds were swimming near the edge or in the center of the lake, sometimes sleeping, but usually alert. We were unable to find the birds an hour after the original observation, and we believe they flew to the south.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

All other species other than swans were ruled out by huge size, long and thin neck, and all white plumage. Mute Swan was ruled out by the black bill, and straight neck.

Trumpeter Swan was ruled out by the smaller bill with a concave shape, isolated eye barely connected to the bill, and by the sharp angle in the cheek contour, giving the bill a more "stuck-on" appearance with the head. Shape of the back, with a noticeably uneven curve, and relative shortness of the neck when compared to the body length were used as supporting evidence.
