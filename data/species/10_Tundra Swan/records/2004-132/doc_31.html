Description of the Bird

Large white, long-necked waterfowl. Bills of the adults mostly black, long, with slightly concave culmen, with thin reddish-orange line visible along the basal half of the tomia. The juvenile bill had a similar shape, but had more reddish-pink coloration throughout the maxilla. On the adults, the junction of the bill & forehead produced a rather flat curve, while the juvenile showed a mope pointed junction here. The adult birds showed yellow loral patches. One had a tiny, tear-drop shaped yellow spot, while the other had extensive yellow lores (closely matching the "maximum yellow" illustrated by Sibley.) The bird with the extensive yellow lores was a bit smaller than the other adult. The juvenile had a pale grayish patch in its lores, but no yellow. Other than the black eye, the rest of the adults were completely white. The juvenile had a grayish cast over its body, with the most gray in the head.
Behaviors: Mostly the birds were feeding by tipping down. Vegetation plucked from the lake's substrate was sometimes visible hanging out of their bills as they tipped back up. A few other waterfowl came and went, such as American Wigeons and Mallards.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: 2 adults and 1 juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

The birds' long necks, large size, and white coloration rule out all waterfowl except swans. Mute Swans have orange bills. Trumpeter Swans lack the yellow lores. The juvenile was about the same size as the adults, ruling out the possibility that is was a 'foster child' Mute or Trumpeter Swan, both of which would be significantly larger.
