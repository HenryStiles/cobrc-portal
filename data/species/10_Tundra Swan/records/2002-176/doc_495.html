Description of the Bird

The birds were very large (a lot larger than nearby Canada Geese, but significantly smaller than the nearby adult Trumpeter Swan), with the distinctive swan shape with a long neck and small head with a large bill.

The bodies of the birds were white overall, with a few dusky feathers mixed in on the back and wings, but not very many. The overall impression was of a white back. The necks where brown, darkest on the head and palest towards the body, with imm. number 1 having more brown on the neck below the head than imm. number 2.

The feet where black, and the bill on one bird (imm. number 1) was mostly black with a tiny bit of pink in the middle, and the bill of the other (imm. number 2) was mostly pink, with only a little bit of black at the ends. The culmen of imm. number 1 was slightly concaved. The culmen of imm. number 2 was more concave than that of the other immature, though not very much so. The length of the bill was about equal to that of the head. The eyes were distinct from the bill on both birds, more so on imm. number 2.

The heads were rounded, with the peak of the head in the middle of the head, and the cheek contour with the bill was not a smooth curve, but rather had notches in it, and it went straight down near the gape. The lengths of the necks were shorter than the body. The swans were significantly smaller than an adult Trumpeter Swan they were with.
Behaviors: The two swans were loosely associated with an adult Trumpeter Swan on the lake, though the immatures tended stay together, and were not always with the adult. I never saw one immature far from the other.
Call: none hears
Plumage: first basic molting into first alternate

Similar Species Discussion

All birds other than swans were ruled out by the distinctive shape, very large size with long, thin neck.

Immature Trumpeter Swan was ruled out by a number of characters. The first, and most obvious, was the amount of white plumage on the birds, since Tundra Swans molt earlier, they appear whiter this time of year. A Trumpeter Swan at this time of year would still have more dusky plumage (as was evident when I viewed the Trumpeter Swan immatures a few days later at Tom Frost Reservoir). The length of the bills when compared to the heads was less than it should be for Trumpeters, and the length of the neck compared to the body was also significantly less. The peaks on the crowns on the swans were at the middle of the crown rather than near the back of the head as in Trumpeter. The eye was very distinct from the bill, more so than Trumpeter's (Alan Contreras, pers. comm).

The clincher was the size when compared to the adult Trumpeter they were hanging out with, especially the much thinner legs. These birds were significantly smaller than the adult even when seen on the same plane as the adult bird, and outside the range of female Trumpeter (Ruth Shea and Alan Contreras, pers. comm.) Swans reach nearly adult size by November (Ruth Shea, pers. comm.), so any swan significantly smaller than another, as these birds where, is a Tundra and the larger bird a Trumpeter (seen in photos, above). The exception to this is a runt Trumpeter Swan, but these are very rare, especially one surviving so long. Another clue that rules out a runt is the amount of pink in the bill of imm. number 2, which, while not conclusive, makes me lean towards Tundra. A Trumpeter Swan looses the pink on the bill earlier than Tundra.

Resources Used

The Sibley Guide to Birds, National Geographic 3rd edition, Identifying Trumpeter and Tundra Swans, by Michael A. Patten and Matthew T. Heindel, published in the October 1994 issue of Birding. All these sources, especially the Birding Article, influenced my ID towards Tundra.

Additional Information

Comments: this write-up was written shortly after the observation in 2002, and submitted at the time, but to my knowledge has not been voted on, and I am resubmitting it with the assumption that the original write-up was lost.

I have removed a number of photos of other swans I've seen in Colorado used for comparison that I used in the original write-up, along with the associated text.

These birds were very controversial, with many excellent Colorado birders disagreeing on the ID. However, I sent the photos to Steve Heinl and Alan Contreras, two well-known birders in the pacific NW who see many swans. And I also sent the photos to Ruth Shea, who may know more on swan ID than anyone else alive, and they all said the bird was a Tundra Swan.
