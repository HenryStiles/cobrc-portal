Description of the Bird

The bird was first identified by binoculars as a loon by its large size and low profile on the water, bill carried in a slightly upturned fashion. When we scoped it, in side profile the bird showed a dark back, gray head and neck with some fine stripes up the back of the neck, none were seen on the side of the neck. Red eye. As the bird turned to face us in direct sunlight, red throat obvious. The bird lacked side neck striping and lacked purple coloration that we have seen in breeding Pacific Loons in Alaska. Also didn't appear as "big headed" as Pac loon would, head appeared more delicate. When bird came up from diving, small white patch visible on flank.
Behaviors: Bird sat low on the water, larger than nearby Western Grebes. Frequently engaged in behavior of looking below the water, whcih we have seen in diving birds, but much more in loons. Did actively engage in diving/feeding, and submerging itself under water.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Alternate

Similar Species Discussion

We initially recognized the bird as a loon in prolife. When we realized that the back was dark, we considered loons out of breeding plumage, but distinct gray on neck without jagged appearance ruled out common loon. With bill smaller and dark, and based on overall size of bird, we ruled out yellow billed loon. Obvious red on throat when bird turned toward us, and clear striping limited to back of neck ruled out pacific loon and/or arctic loon.

Resources Used

Had our field copies of two books, used at the time of sighting. The Sibley Field guide to Birds of Western North America (2003) and Peterson Field Guide to Western Birds, third edition (1990).

Previous Experience

We have seen this species in breeding plumage at close distance in Alaska in 2005. Were able to "play it forward" when we traveled to Nome this year and again see large numbers of Red throated loons in breeding plumage, comparing our bird favorably to those seen later in the year.
