Habitat

A man-made freshwater reservoir. Baseline is at about half capacity due to the water level being lowered in order to repair the damn. It still, apparently, attracts diving ducks, grebes, and loons.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Leica 77mm Televid scope at 20-60 magnification.
Distance:  About 2/5 mile at first, then at about 150 yards
Light:  I first spotted the bird from the extreme SW corner of Baseline Reservoir off Cherryvale Rd. I positioned myself there because although the distance to the open water was great than other vantage points, the sun was behind me so the lighting was better. After observing the bird for about 20 minutes, Bill Kaempfer and I went to the north shore, adjacent to Sterns Dr. on Baseline Rd, to observer the bird at closer range. From there we were only ~150-200 yards from the bird, but the sun was behind it so the bird was a little backlit, although at that distance the relevant details were still apparent.

Description of the Bird

A small loon with overall gray plumage with a paler throat and flanks. The bill was small and gray and was always held up at a ~20 degree angle, never held horizontally. There were small white marks on the back-not pale fringes to the feathers but small white spots. The flanks were evenly white. When first seen, the bird was sleeping. The long body was particularly loon-like, and the pale flanks and pale gray color overall indicated that it was a Red-throated Loon. As soon as it lifted its head, the distinct bill size and position were apparent. There were no dark wedges on the side of the neck, only diffuse gray feathering.
Behaviors: For the first couple of minutes the bird was sleeping. It eventually woke up and moved around a bit. It spent some time preening and flapped its wings on two occasions. When it was actively feeding, it sat low in the water, at times having its upper back submerged so just its rear and head/neck were above water. It caught a large (~4"), thin fish at one point which it swallowed whole. When it was relaxing/preening, it would roll over on its side exposing a bright white belly.
Call: none heard
Plumage: 1st cycle

Similar Species Discussion

Since Red-throated is the smallest loon, it can not only be confused with other loons, but also with large grebes and even immature Double-crested Cormorants. The bird's bill was gray and the body plumage lacked pure black tones, which eliminates similar species like Double-crested Cormorant and Western and Clark's Grebes. The bill was also too long for smaller grebes like Horned and Eared. The bill was, however, too small for other loon species. The posture of the head, with the bill held up at a constant angle, is also distinct. The much larger Yellow-billed Loon, and to some extent other loons, will hold their bills up at an angle. Other species like Yellow-billed and Common have a distinct gonydeal angle on the lower mandible. The bill color was off for a Yellow-billed. Common Loon shows dark wedges on the sides of the neck. Pacific/Arctic Loons are darker on the back and hindneck, and they have a more bulbous head shape. Pacific often shows a dark 'chin-strap' which this bird lacked. Also, the extensive white flanks are almost unique among Loons. Other loons can show extensive white flanks when positioned in a relaxed or rolled-over position, but they don't show it while actively foraging.

Previous Experience

Extensive. Having lived and birded in coastal Rhode Island, I have observed thousands of Red-throated Loons in all of their various plumages. I have also seen them off the coast of California on several occasions. I have only observed one other in Colorado, a bird at Union Reservoir, Weld County.
