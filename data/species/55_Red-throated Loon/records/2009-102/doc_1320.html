Description of the Bird

The bird was clearly a loon; it was a long, low diving bird, thicker-necked than any of the grebes on the reservoir. When I originally saw it through binoculars, I thought it would be a Pacific Loon, but when I got it in the scope, it was immediately obvious that it was a juvenile Red-throated. The bill was very thin for a loon, with a basically straight upper mandible profile and a lower mandible profile distinctly upturned at the gonys; the bill was held consistently above the horizontal. The back of the bird's head and neck were an even gray, paler than expected on a Common Loon but darker than the silvery color expected on a Pacific Loon. The bird's throat and face were whitish. The boundary between the white and gray along the bird's neck was fairly even, but kind of blended, not distinct like you'd expect on an adult Red-throated or on a Pacific/Arctic Loon. The whitish color extended into the grayish around the eye, so that the eye was distinct against the white. The bird's back was dark; I couldn't see the white spotting I'd expect on an adult Red-throated Loon.
Behaviors: The bird was floating on the reservoir, active and alert but not diving while I watched.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

The bird's thick neck, upturned bill, and distinctive face pattern ruled out all grebes (hundreds of Western Grebes were on the reservoir at the time, including many juveniles for comparison). The bill shape and its upturned posture ruled out all other loons except for Yellow-billed, but this bird's bill was much smaller, the bird was darker overall, and the bill was darker (grayish instead of straw-colored). See "description" for other similar species considered.
