Description of the Bird

Bird was about as long in the water as adjacent Aechmophorus grebes, but its neck was noticeably shorter and much thicker, and its bill was different, gunmetal gray and rather thin, with a straight culmen but with a lower profile that bent slightly but noticeably upward at the gonys. The bird's back was grayish and the back of its neck and top of its head were medium gray also, sharply and smoothly demarcated from the clean white of the rest of the face and neck. When the bird was riding high in the water, white was visible all down its side along the water line. The white on the bird's face extended a short but noticeable distance above the dark eye.
Behaviors: Bird was diving frequently, sometimes for periods of a minute or so. It sometimes rode fairly high in the water but also spent some time riding very low in the water, with its upper back almost submerged. It consistently held its bill slightly above the horizontal. When diving, it lowered its head forward into the water and submerged smoothly, the way loons do.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Aechmophorus grebes were abundant for direct comparison. The loon was much shorter- and thicker-necked, and its bill was a distinctly different shape and color--the greenish bills of the Western Grebes were quite noticeable in the same scope view, while the Red-throated Loon's bill kind of faded into the background of the water. The loon's facial pattern was also much different, with an even gray instead of blackish crown and hindneck; its eye was outlined by white feathers on the face, rather like a Clark's Grebe, but unlike Western. Diving style also differed from the grebes. A Pacific Loon was nearby for near-simultaneous comparison; the Pacific was larger than the Red-throated, larger-headed, with a thicker and more symmetrical bill held perfectly horizontal, and with a very smoothly rounded head instead of the flattish head of the Red-throated. The Pacific had the silvery nape typical of the species, while the gray on the head of the Red-throated was darker and even in color. All other loons ruled out by small size, bill shape, bill position, and head/neck coloration.
