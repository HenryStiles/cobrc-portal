Description of the Bird

Bird was swimming low in the water among Western Grebes and diving frequently. It was about the same length in the water as the Western Grebes but had a very different head and neck shape: a much thicker, shorter neck and a larger head, with a longish, thin, slightly upturned bill frequently held above the horizontal. The bird.s back was plain dusky grayish, at least as far as I could tell from the distance I was viewing from; its head and neck were slightly paler, smudgy all over, palest on the throat area; there was no clear demarcation at all between the darker crown and hindneck and the paler foreneck, and this is why I think it was a juvenile. Once while the bird was facing me in the water it reared back and flapped its wings a few times; I could see that the wings were mostly pale underneath with an ill-defined dusky border all around the pale area; the bird definitely did not have the white secondaries of a grebe.
Behaviors: Bird was hanging out with Western Grebes and diving frequently. It also preened occasionally and flapped once while we watched it.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: first basic

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was smaller than other loons. Its distinctive shape, with thick neck and thin upturned bill, distinguished it from the Western Grebes, with which numerous comparative views could be had. Other loons would have more obviously demarcated face patterns and larger bills, and the only other loon that holds its bill consistently above the horizontal, Yellow-billed, has a much larger bill and much different shape overall, and is a much larger bird.

Previous Experience

I found and studied the adult Red-throated Loon at Union two weeks ago. I have seen the species one other time in Colorado and many times off the coasts of Oregon and Washington, though rarely in basic plumage.
