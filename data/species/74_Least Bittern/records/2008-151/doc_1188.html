Description of the Bird

The bird was a very small wader, roughly killdeer sized, although the body appeared fuller. The overall color was medium dark rusty brown with the distal half of the wings being blackish, as was the back and top of the head. It had a long bill, sharp and thin at the tip, much thicker at the base (i.e., like a heron, not a typical shorebird). The flanks were whitish. The underside of the wings did not have the pattern of the upper wing surface and appeared to be dark gray thoughout. The feet extended just beyond the tail tip. I could not discern (or do not remember) their color.
Behaviors: The bird was flushed from a narrow area of dense cattails bordering open water on one side and a wide peninsula of land on the other. It flushed about 10 feet away from me, flew about 30 yards with cattail leaf tops between me and the bird, and landed again in the cattails (my glimpse was perhaps 4-5 seconds and I never saw the bird again). As reported above, the bird gave an aggitated flight call a couple times during its brief flight.
Call: in flight, the bird gave a rather loud "ruck" note 2-3 times, spaced a few seconds apart during a 30 yard flight
Plumage: adult?

Similar Species Discussion

About the only other bird with which this bird could be confused that occurs with any regularity in this area would be Green Heron and subadult Black-crowned Night-Heron. Green Herons are only slightly bigger but in no plumage would show the strong rust and black pattern to the upper wings, combined with the strong white flanks, medium rusty brown (strong hint of yellow in my bird) neck and the Green Heron flight call is typically much louder and with a strong "-ow!!" intonation at the end. As for young Black-crowned Night-Heron, the bird in question was simply much smaller, not striped, nowhere showed gray feathering except for the underwing but I would have to say the flight calls of the two are surprisingly similar with a strong "k" sound at the end of the utterance.

Previous Experience

I have seen dozens of Least Bitterns, mostly on the east Texas coast and in Ohio. This is my second in Colorado, having photographed one at close range about a mile north of this same location back on 3June83 (see cover of Western Birds, Vol22(2)1991). I have seen hundreds of Green Herons and thousands of Black-crowned Night-Herons.
