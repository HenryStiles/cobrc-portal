Description of the Bird

The bird was seen among a flock of mostly smaller white-cheeked geese that included 3 or so Greater White-fronted Geese, two apparent GWFG x Cackling Goose hybrids, a funky white headed Canada/Cackling hybrid, an apparent Snow/Ross's x Cackling hybrid, and a dead coyote presumably shot by one of the nearby land owners. It had been seen that morning on Milavec Res. where most people had been seeing it, but a Bald Eagle had flushed most of the birds. The bird was walking around grazing acting just like the other geese the entire time I was there. 


The bird stood out (eventually) due to it's warm brown head and neck, and then the distinctive mostly dark (black or nearly black) bill with a pink saddle on the top of the bill (dark at the base and the tip). This, combined with the upper parts on the body showing light grayish tips to the otherwise brownish grey feathers suggest this is an adult bird, not a 1st winter bird (which would be more uniform brown on top, from what I can tell, and would have more extensive pink on the bill, especially towards the base). 


So where'd this bird come from? 

I'm not sure whether the multiple Pink-footed Geese seen so far this winter in the northeast are higher than usual or not, but the north atlantic population is and has been increasing steadily over the past few decades, and so this species should be expected to show up more and more frequently in North America. I can think of at least two ways such birds could land in CO. First, returning overshoots could summer in Canada and end up going south their second summer into the eastern and central parts of the U.S. including the western Great Plains. Second, birds that migrate the wrong way down around the atlantic or otherwise wind up in North America and overwinter there might then migrate into northern Canada with northerly breeding Canada, Cackling and/or Snow Geese. From those northern parts of Canada, a bird that survives the summer might then return the following fall into the central and eastern part of the U.S. This scenario seems very plausible as an explaination of how a bird from the north atlantic population could wind up in CO. This highly migratory species can easily cover the distances needed to get here on it's own, and the fact that this particular individual has been hanging out so closely with small (high latitude breeding) white-cheeked geese further supports "wild origins" as a highly plausible scenario. Add to that the timing of it's arrival, and especially how very uncommon they are in captivity in North America, and wild origins seems even more likely. While there is the remote possibility this was an escaped captive, I find that a very unlikely scenario given the bird's behavior, timing of arrival, it's overall appearance. While I didn't see this in person, others have photographed the bird's feet and both hind toes are intact (Van Truan posted some excellent shots of these toes to facebook -- but presumably others have submitted such documentation).

Furthermore, for this to be a wild bird would not be entirely unprecendented, given the January 2006 Nebraska bird and information that appears to not have been known at the time of the NOURC's decision to not accept that individual over concerns of it's origin.  As summarized at https://birds.outdoornebraska.gov/pink-footed-goose/ "Recovery data from neck collars on Snow Geese from the same flock in which the Pink-footed Goose was found showed individual Snow Geese were captured at Queen Maud Gulf Migratory Bird Sanctuary in Nunavut, Canada (Paul Dunbar, personal communication)." In otherwords, we know there's an established connection between birds that breed in the far northeast of N. America and winter in Colorado, and it's entirely plausible a Pink-footed Goose could wind up in that region during the summer and follow those birds south for the winter. Additionally, records from Washington State further suggest a route into North America beyond just the northeastern U.S., further adding to the plausibility of a wild bird showing up in CO.

ADDENDUM: I saw the bird again on January 2nd, 2019 on the golf course adjacent to Fredrick Lake (Milavec Reservoir) as documented in this eBird checklist: https://ebird.org/view/checklist/S51222754

Similar Species Discussion

There are eight or so species in the genus Anser, and the black bill with a small pink saddle combined with the pink legs and feet rules out all of them but Pink-footed Goose. Nothing about this bird looks atypical for that species that would suggest a hybrid or some other alternative.
