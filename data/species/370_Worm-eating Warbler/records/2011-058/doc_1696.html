Habitat

Riparian cottonwood grove. Ground was covered with leaf litter. Many shrubs, dead branches, and limbs on the ground as well making it hard to see the bird.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Nikon Monarch 10X42 Binoculars
Distance:  50-60 feet estimated
Light:  Bird was in shady undergrowth so the angle of the sun had no effect on the observation. Light condition was uniform shady where the bird was.

Description of the Bird

The short view I had of this bird was downhill in thick undergrowth. I was looking down on the back of the bird. I saw a uniform olive-brownish back, a slightly lighter brown head, and three jet black stripes running from the forehead area to the back of the head. The bill was rather long and needle-like.
Behaviors: The bird was constantly on the move between the leaf-covered ground and low branches. Difficult to observe much behavior as the bird was in thick undergrowth and in sight only for brief moments.
Call: A rapid, metallic buzz almost reminding me of a cicada.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Swainson's Warbler has a similar shape and color pattern but the three black strips across the head of this bird rule out Swainson's Warbler which has a rufous crown.

Resources Used

We saw thst Dave Leatherman had seen this bird earlier in the day and put it on the CFO sightings board. We listened to the song on I-Bird Pro Explorer before arriving at the location and used Sibley Field Guides to study the bird before and after we saw it.

Additional Information

Comments: When trying to refind a skulking warbler, it is beneficial to listen for the bird for a few minutes instead of plowing into the woods and undergrowth like a herd of buffaloes. You might pass this on to most of the birders on the Sunday CFO Convention Chase Trip who, of course, did not refind the bird.
