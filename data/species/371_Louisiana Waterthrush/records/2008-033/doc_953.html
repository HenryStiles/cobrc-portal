Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized warbler, much smaller than nearby American Robins and slightly larger than a nearby Orange-crowned Warbler. It had the typical Waterthrush shape, with a short tail, horizontal posture more reminiscent of a Pipit or an Ovenbird than most other warblers, and overall stocky proportions. The bill was black, fairly long, and thin, with a slightly thicker base tapering to a sharp point. The overall length appeared to be on the long side for a Northern Waterthrush and more normal for Louisiana, but this was hard to quantify without direct comparison. The legs were bright bubblegum pink. The irides were dark brown or black.

The bird was evenly dark brown on the crown, auriculars, upperparts, and tail. The lower part of the auriculars was slightly paler, creating the appearance of a darker brown eye-line. There was a thin white crescent under the eye. The bird had a bold and long white supercilium that was very subtly buffy in front of the eye and bright white behind it; towards the posterior end of the eye line it broadened out slightly and flared up towards the nape.

The bird had a white throat with a very few tiny brown spots. The chest and belly were white with bold dark brown streaks about the same color as the upperparts; these streaks were uniform in size except at the lower edge of the throat and a couple of areas on the belly, and were more sparsely distributed on the underparts than I would expect for Northern Waterthrush. The flanks and vent area of the bird had a faint salmon-tinged buffy wash that contrasted with the white belly, chest and throat; the undertail coverts and vent appeared whiter than the flanks in some light, and the same color in others, usually when facing away. The undertail was the same brown color as the uppertail.
Behaviors: The bird spent most of its time foraging along the stream, and most of that time on one island under the bridge of Pike Road. It would walk and hop along while constantly bobbing its tail in sweeping semi-circles; this tail-bobbing pattern had more horizontal distance than vertical distance, and the bird would move the whole rear half of the body while doing it. A couple of times the bird flew up into some nearby vegetation, when it became shy and hard to see, but it spent very little time up in those areas and soon returned to the stream bed.
Call: I only heard the bird call once; this call to me sounded "weaker" than the call of Northern Waterthrush, with a less emphatic sound.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than Waterthrushes were ruled out by the combination of the habits of the bird, the small size, and the brown upperparts with dense brown streaking on the underparts.

Northern Waterthrush was ruled out by a suite of characteristics, the main ones being the supercilium that got wider and flared upwards towards its posterior end, was subtly bicolored, the nearly unmarked white throat, without a continuation of small streaks from the chest extending upwards onto it, the white underparts with not hint of yellow, the contrasting pale salmon-buffy flanks, the very bright bubble-gum pink legs, the proportionally larger bill, and the very different tail-bobbing pattern with more horizontal than vertical motion. The call note also sounded different to me, though this was hard to judge from hearing it once.

Resources Used

Sibley and the Peterson Warbler guide were consulted in the field to rule out Northern Waterthrush

Previous Experience

I have seen many Louisiana Waterthrushes in the eastern US, including many hundreds during a summer in Illinois. I have also seen many Northern Waterthrushes, and have a lot of comparative experience in separating the two species in the field.
