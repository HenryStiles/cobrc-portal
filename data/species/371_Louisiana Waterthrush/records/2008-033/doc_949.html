Immediate vicinity of Left Hand creek, a small (6-8 inches deep, 4-6 feet wide) watercourse with some trees and underbrush, though few of either downstream (north) of the bridge.

Optics:  Eagle Optics 8x42 binoculars, other people's scopes

Description of the Bird

The bird was clearly a waterthrush, all dull greenish-brown above, streaked with brown below, with a white supercilium; it was about the size of a Hermit Thrush (direct comparison) but chunkier and shorter-tailed, with a long straight bill. The bird's supercilium was completely white and got wider behind the eye, continuing widely all the way to the back of the head, ending where the back begins. The throat was almost completely white, except for a couple of very tiny dark specks that were only really visible through close scope views. The underparts streaking was about the same color as the upperparts coloration, not blackish like on a Northern Waterthrush. The underparts background color was mostly clean white, with a slight tinge of color on the flanks and around the vent. This is the typical Louisiana color: people called it "salmon" or "rusty" but it's actually more like a tan version of yellow; "the color of bathtub scum" was my colorful metaphor. The legs were bright pink.
Behaviors: The bird foraged actively on exposed rocks in the middle of a small stream, and also along the banks, frequently underneath overhanging vegetation. It was mostly silent. When pressed, a couple of times it flushed away from the creek and up into dense shrubs in people's yards. The bird was seen to eat a number of good-sized worms. It bobbed its tail frequently. Many people commented on the "u"-shaped bobbing motion--as Andrew Spencer put it, "with more horizontal than vertical motion." I wouldn't have described it this way; the tail never moved separately from the body; whenever the bird tipped its back end down, it would frequently turn its whole body at the same time, and I think this is what people were talking about when they were saying that its tail traced a "U" in the air.
Call: The bird gave two chip notes that I heard and recorded. Sound file and sonograms attached.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Northern Waterthrush was ruled out by the combination of tan wash on the flanks, white throat, broad white supercilium broader behind eye, bright pink legs, and tail-wagging behavior. The sonogram of the chip note is also diagnostic: LOWA's "chink" call generally takes the form of a very, very narrow “V” with bottom point around 4 kHz (occasionally much higher, but apparently only during excited series that also contain the “normal” calls). NOWA's call begins much lower, around 2 kHz, sweeping rapidly up above 8 kHz or so, then has a smaller downslurred or "V-" shaped component in addition. Because of the starting component, the call sounds slightly upslurred to my ear, like it begins with a flicking “whit” sound, whereas LOWA is more of a “pik” sound.

Resources Used

I did not consult any field guides, but looked at other people's photos of the bird again before writing this report. I made extensive use of the Macaulay Library's online bird recordings in comparing sonograms.

Previous Experience

I have seen Louisiana Waterthrush probably 6-8 times in the eastern United States. I am quite familiar with Northern Waterthrush, having grown up where they are common migrants and continued to see them at least annually in migration in Colorado.
