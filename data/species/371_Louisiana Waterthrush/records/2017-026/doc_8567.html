Description of the Bird

I was walking along a small stream at BGR where a group had encountered 4 waterthrush, but most not seen/heard well (found that out later), and an Ovenbird. I found 2 Ovenbirds, but was waterthrushless until near the end, when I had a waterthrush pop up in a tangle in front of me. Distance was less than 30 feet. The bird was facing me, with head turned about 45 degrees to side or directly at me while I watched it. When I first looked at it, the head was turned about 45 degrees to one side, and I was startled by the size of the bill. So, I started thinking of L Waterthrush. I then looked for other marks.

The supercilium was broad, white, and extended well behind eye. The lores looked buff to me, but there may be some expectation bias here. The legs were bright pink. The tail was not being wagged/bobbed straight up and down, but rather moved with significant side-to-side action, causing more of a U as the tail went up and down. The throat was white and without streaks/spots. The bird had prominent dark malar steaks and moderately prominent blackish streaking against white chest and upper belly. Back looked dull olive, though exact hue hard to determine in light. I never saw the underparts much past level of uppermost belly. The bird called 3x. I can not improve on Sibley's description "A loud, strong spich, not as hard as hard as Northern" Kind of like a Northern Waterthrush's call with a lisp. I am only a couple weeks back from Aruba, where I encountered 50 or so NOWA per day, almost all detected via call notes, and the difference was substantial
