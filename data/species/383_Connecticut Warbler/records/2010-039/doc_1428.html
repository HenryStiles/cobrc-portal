Description of the Bird

The bird was sitting about 1 foot off the ground, around 6 to 8 feet from me. He was sitting in full sunlight with his back to me. I knew from its behavior (sulky, staying low to the ground, and being rather quite) that it was an Oporornis Warbler of some kind. I noted that the back of the head did not have defined contrast between the hood and saddle color, at that line was high on the head. I made a small pshing noise to try and get the birds attention. He then turned his head to look in my direction. I was able to then note the very strong, very crisp complete eye ring. The bird was in direct sunlight and there is no doubt in my mind that the eye ring was indeed complete and not broken like a MGWA. The saddle of the bird and its back and rump seemed to be the same general olivey gray/green color. The bird then dropped off the branch he was sitting on and I never saw it again. I never did see the breast or front of the bird so I was unable to see the color of the throat or undersides. The bill looked a little longer and skinnier than the bill of a MGWA that I am familiar with, and the body seemed stockier. It did not seem puffed up because of the wind. The tail was also very short n comparison with the rest of the body.
Behaviors: The bird initially caught my eye because it was low to the ground and being very secretive. Not like the Yellow and Orange-crowned Warblers that were in the area. It stayed low to the ground, never more than 2 feet off the ground and with it's back to me. Only after I pshed did it look at me, and within moments it was gone. It seemed much more secretive than any other Oporornis warbler I have ever encountered. I did not see the bird walk or hop so I can not comment on that behavior that is know in the CONW.
Call: The bird made no sound while I observed it
Plumage: Spring Breeding

Similar Species Discussion

I was able to see a complete eye-ring on the CONW, not a broken one like on the MGWA. I also never saw the dark pseudo eye line that a MGWA has, even though the bird was in direct sunlight that should have been easy to see. This bird was even very different from a female MGWA based on where the hood color changed to saddle color and how stocky the bird looked.

Additional Information

Comments: I also found a male and female MGWA around an hour later out near Bass Pond, they looked little like the CONW I had just seen. On both the male and the female I was able to see a darker colored hood and a broken eye ring. They also stayed a little higher in the bushes and not as close to the ground as the CONW. I was also able to see the dark eye line on the male MGWA that I did not see on the CONW.
