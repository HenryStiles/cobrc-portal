Description of the Bird

The bird was on the ground but unseen as I left the creek bank and pushed through the brush.   I flushed it and it flew about 15ft and landed in a small bush about 12 inches up from the ground.   It was facing me when I was able to view it with my bins.   It had a totally gray head and throat and a complete white, circular eye-ring.   The lower body was dull yellow.   I lifted my camera and got a two photo burst of its profile before it dropped to the ground and out of sight.   I pished to bring it back without success.   I examined my photo and realized it might be a Conneticutt, so I used Merlin App on my phone to play the song.   No response.   I stayed in the immediate area about an additional 20min to try and re find the bird without success.

Similar Species Discussion

Nashville Warbler--However, no yellow on throat.   I was able to get a good view when it was looking straight at me.
MacGillivray's Warbler--Complete circular eye-ring, not broken
Mourning Warbler (female)--The eye-ring was very bold, unlike a female Mourning

Resources Used

Used Merlin app in the field for initial id.   Walked over to see Steve Brown (3 miles away at the Clear Springs Ranch banding area) and discussed it with him and showed him my picture.   He had a warbler field guide (unknown source to me) which had the comparisons between the various warblers.   He thought the bold eye-ring probably ruled out a Mourning.   When I got back home I consulted my Sibley's Second Edition along with the Peterson Warblers, authers Jon Dunn and Kimball Garrett.   The photos and description of the bird in all the guides led me to conclude it was a Conneticutt Warbler

Previous Experience

This was a life bird for me.    I have seen a number of Nashville Warblers both in Colorado and Texas.   Quite a few MacGillivrays, but only one Mourning.
