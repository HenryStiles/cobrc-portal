Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized dabbler, about the same size and the same shape as the adjacent American Wigeons. The bill was pale blue gray. I did not see the legs.

The head of the bird was brownish rufous, fairly even in color except for a tiny spot of iridescent green behind the eye and a pale, buffy colored crown. The color of the head was a bit less intensely rufous colored than that of a fully alternate bird; I believe this is because the bird was not entirely out of eclipse plumage.

The chest of the bird was pinkish buffy colored, cleanly separated from light gray flanks. The back was also light gray. There upper part of the flanks, just below the wings, has a little bit of rufous feathering, similar in color to the head. I believe this was the remnant of eclipse plumage. Rear flanks and lower belly was white; most visible in flight. The undertail coverts were black.

The flight feathers of the bird were very dark gray. The secondary coverts were white, creating a bold white patch visible in flight. The secondaries were iridescent green. The underwings were clean gray, with a slight whitening in the axillaries, but without any strong contrast between tones of gray.
Behaviors: The bird was first found walking around on the mud of a small reservoir. It was in the company of a number of American Wigeon. Upon approach, the whole flock flew and remained in the air until out departure. Later in the day we refound the bird swimming on the reservoir.
Call: none heard
Plumage: molting from eclipse into alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Most other duck species were ruled out by the
combination of medium size, gray flanks, rufous head, and overall shape identical to that of American Wigeon.

American Wigeon was ruled out by the rufous head, gray flanks, gray underwings, buffy crown, and lack of a broad green stripe in the face. Hybrid wigeon was ruled out by the lack of any white in the underwing, lack of green in the face, and overall lack of American Wigeon features. I believe that the rufous in the flanks of this bird was a remnant of eclipse plumage. American Wigeon doesn't have this color rufous in the
flanks, except in eclipse, and it is just as likely
that the rufous could be the remnant of Eurasian Wigeon eclipse plumage as that of American.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted in the field to research field marks on hybrid Wigeons. Birds of Europe was used later to research various Eurasian Wigeon plumages. I used this source to determine that the bird was a bird molting out of eclipse plumage.

Previous Experience

I have seen a number of Eurasian Wigeons previous to this one, but never one in quite this plumage. I am familiar with male alternate Eurasian Wigeons, but less so with other plumages. I have seen many American Wigeons in all plumages.
