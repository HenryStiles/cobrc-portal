Description of the Bird

Swimming in flock consisting largely of Aythya ducks (Canvasback, L Scaup, Redhead, RN Ducks) but with a goodly sprinkling of Am Wigeon, shoveler, GW Teal. Oddly, the many Gadwall present eschewed joining this group (about 400-500 birds total)

Dabbling duck similar in size and shape to AMWI present (swimming in proximity to several AMWI). Head evenly, and entirely bright chestnut-cinnamon, excepting a band of golden buff from bill towards crown. Head color quite what I've come to expect from male EUWI, but forehead stripe not as bold/contrasting as full breeding adult. Chest similar in color to head, but paler and not as richly colored. Body, sides and back a patchy admixture of gray and brown forming an overall a gray, tinged with brown, appearance. Towards rear of sides, the beginnings of the black undertail coverts and white "vent strap" of adult male wigeon could be seen. Bill bright pale blue with well demarcated black tip, similar to that of other wigeon present but no evidene of black at base of bill -- and view, at times, seemed good enough to have distinguished such. 

Similar Species Discussion

AMWI and AMWI x EUWI
Even in this transitional plumage, both AMWI and hybrid should show some uneveness to color of head, either a bicolored appearance in the reddish hues or darker marking around the eye (though some pure EUWI show the latter as well). The patchy forhead stripe was of appropriate color and like that seen in transitional birds. The color of this stripe was typical, in my experience, of EUWI without any evidence for AMWI influence. 

The patchy brown and gray appearance of the body was somewhat similar to https://macaulaylibrary.org/asset/39521151 as was the head color and the head/chest contrast. Other images show a partly formed forehead stripe. I must admit to assuming the bird to be an adult male based on timing of molt transition (this is when we'd seem them like this in WA), but I could be wrong and this could be an immature male, perhaps. In any case, the substantial gray of the body was typical of male EUWI, and the bits of brown were as that commonly seen in transitional males to breeding plumage
