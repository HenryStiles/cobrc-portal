Description of the Bird

On Jan 12 I was scanning the lake from the public trail, studying the many species of waterfowl present especially the American Wigeons, hoping that maybe today of all days I might stumble upon a Eurasian Wigeon, although I had no real expectation of doing so given how exceedingly rare they are. But within 30 seconds of scanning, to my genuine shock I spotted an actual Eurasian Wigeon. It was swimming about in a manner similar to the many dozens of American Wigeons nearby, dabbling, changing directions, seemingly undecided about where to go and what to do next. It presented multiple angles of views, and each time I tried to rule out Eurasian Wigeon, but all the field marks were right for an adult male. It had a red head with a creamy-colored crown stripe, and no greenish tinge whatsoever to the head. The flanks were gray and not brown, and the breast had a pinkish wash to it. This particular bird also had white wing linings more pronounced than the similar linings on the sides of all the American Wigeons.

I showed the bird in my scope to another experienced local birder who just happened to be walking by at the moment (Carl Starace) and he agreed with my assessment. I then notified the local birding community through BOCOBirds and COBirds, and within an hour or two dozens of birders arrived and were photographing and viewing the bird. It remained on the lake most of the day, and was viewable at Pella almost every day until January 24, the last known sighting of the bird. I personally visited it at this exact same lake on 8 total occasions between Jan 12 and Jan 23, though I only photographed it myself on the first day. Other birders should have equally convincing if not better photos than mine, despite the distance to the bird.

Similar Species Discussion

Male American Wigeon is closely related and has a similar plumage template – a colored head with a white or creamy crown stripe, and with a white wing line seen on the flanks. The colors of this bird however did not match the AMWI template.

Redhead has a similar head color, but lacks the creamy crown stripe. It also has an all-black breast that EUWI does not have.

Resources Used

Sibley Guide to the Birds of North America was used as a reference after the viewing.

Previous Experience

I had seen EUWI on a few occasions prior to this: 3x in California where the species is more regular than in CO, and once before in CO, in Larimer County in 2009. All occasions had been males, like this bird. I've seen AMWI hundreds if not thousands of times over the years, and Redheads hundreds of times.
