Description of the Bird

The Western Gull was originally located by Floyd and Sibley at Smith Reservoir in Costilla Co. in early June; the Rawinski's relocated the WEGU at the Blanca Wetlands, and once Blanca opened to the public I tried for it. I observed the bird on two seperate occasions, once on July 20th and again on July 23rd. On the 20th the bird was on Pond 115, resting on a sandy island in the middle of the pond with coots and Am. Avocets. I observed the bird through a 15-45x60 scope, as the bird preened and rested. The mantle of the bird was dark slaty gray, noticeably darker than any of the CAGUs on the pond. The legs were pink, the eye pale and orbital ring yellow. The bill was thick and stout with a red gonydeal spot. On the 23rd, the bird had moved to the large body of water to the east of the N. Mallard parking area. The bird was more distant on this observation, but was feeding next to a few California and Ring-billed Gulls, allowing for a nice size comparison. The WEGU was slightly larger than the CAGUs. I photographed the WEGU on both days, although others have posted much better photos than mine.

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was previously reported as WEGU, and photos of the bird support that, so it was the only dark-mantled gull that I would expect to see at Blanca. The mantle color and size of the WEGU are on par with an adult Lesser Black-backed Gull, but the pink legs on this bird eliminate LBBG which have yellow legs. Great Black-backed Gulls have pinkish legs, but GBBG would have dwarfed the nearby gulls, and the birds legs were more pink than the faded pastel-pink that I'm used to seeing in GBBG. Slaty-backed Gull has similarly colored legs and mantle, but on top of being virtually unheard of in the lower 48 during the summer, SBGU have a reddish orbital ring, unlike the yellow orbital ring on this bird. SBGU also have a very wide, prominent tertial crescent, which the Blanca WEGU lacked. More expected large white-headed gulls, like Herring and California, can be eliminated by mantle color alone. 

Resources Used

Since this was a previously reported bird, I did not use a field guide to ID the bird. I did however see Ted Floyd's pictures from June and David Dowell's excellent pictures of the bird from July 16th prior to going to Blanca.

Previous Experience

I have seen Western Gulls along the California coast where they are very common. I have spent time birding in the Puget Sound/Olympic Peninsula area of Washington State, where WEGUs hybridize pretty extensively with Glaucous-winged Gulls. We spent some time scanning and scrutinizing the "Olympic" Gulls, looking for potentially pure Western and pure Glaucous-winged, so I had previously studied the species field marks.
