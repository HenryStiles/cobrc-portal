Description of the Bird

The bird was a large gull. It dwarfed the Ring-billed Gulls it was standing next to, and was both bulkier and longer-bodied than a Mallard in direct comparison. It had an immaculate white head, tail, nape, and underparts, indicating that it was an adult. Its bill was huge and bulbous -- the culmen was straight, and so was the bottom edge of the mandible partway out the bill, but the distal third of the mandible was greatly swollen, so that the width of the bill 75% of the way to the tip was about 133% of the width of the bill 25% of the way out. The bill was school bus yellow with a red gonydeal spot and no black. The bird's gape and orbital ring appeared yellow. The bird's iris was dark, although not as dark as the pupil. The legs were pink. The mantle was evenly charcoal gray, and the wingtips were black with worn white tips. The mantle was several shades darker than those of the Ring-billed Gulls, about as dark as those of the Lesser Black-backed Gulls I see in Colorado, but not as dark as the back of a Kelp Gull or Great Black-backed Gull. The contrast between mantle and wingtips was still pretty distinct. The tertial crescent was large and bright white, and there was a very broad, bright white fringe on the secondaries that was very visible on the folded wing. The bird seemed chunky overall, not long-winged and slender like a Lesser Black-backed Gull. When the bird spread its wings, the trailing edge of the underwing was obviously and contrastingly darker than the underwing coverts, which were clean white.
Behaviors: The bird was loafing on the shore, performing a lot of preening behaviors and sometimes sleeping. It spread its wings a few times so that I could see the underside. It seemed to be favoring its left leg, which was always held slanted, never bearing weight, although the bird did use the left leg to preen its head several times.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Pink legs and shape ruled out Lesser Black-backed Gull. Paler mantle and yellow orbital ring ruled out Kelp and Great Black-backed Gulls.
