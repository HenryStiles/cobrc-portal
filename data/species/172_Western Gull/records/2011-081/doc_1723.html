Description of the Bird

A large gull, Herring Gull sized but bulkier, as seen next to several Ring-billed Gulls and a California Gull. The bird had a dark mantle (about the shade of Lesser Black-backed Gull), white head, neck, breast and underparts, a large yellow bill with a bulbous tip and small red spot at the gonys. The primaries were extensively black with a small, square shaped mirror on P10 and small white tips on P10, P9, P8, P7, P6 and P5. P7, P6 and P5 also showed much less black at the tip with small, white sub-terminal spots. P5 had a large white tip with a small black mark in the center. The rest of the primaries and all of the secondaries showed a large white tip on an otherwise dark grey (mantled-colored) feather. This gave the appearance of a thick white line across the trailing edge of the wing, seen in flight. The tail was white, the eye was darkish, about the color of a Mew Gull's iris. A bright yellow orbital ring circled the eye. The legs were pink, as was the gape as seen in the open mouth. In the sitting bird, there was a large, white tertial crescent, and a broad tertial skirt.
Behaviors: The bird was seen preening, flying, feeding, resting and swimming. It appeared to have an injured left leg. It did not stand or put pressure on the left leg, but was seen using it's left leg to scratch it's face. The bird was seen flying around the lake, roosting at 1 of about 4 different locations.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The size, dark mantle and pink legs of this adult bird left only Western Gull, Great Black-backed Gull and Slaty-backed Gull to consider. The combination of yellow orbital ring and pink gape, which were studied and photographed at close range, eliminated both Great Black-backed Gull and Slaty-backed Gull. However more in depth identification was performed by several observers to further eliminate these 2 gulls, as well as the possibility of a hybrid gull.

Even though Great Black-backed Gull can vary in size, the bird was just not large enough or dark enough to be this species. Also, P9 did not have a white mirror.

Slaty-backed Gull shows sub-terminal white spots on P8, which Western Gull and this bird did not. Also, Slaty-backed gull shows black in outer webs of primaries only, giving it a less, blackened look to the entire wing tip.

Primary color was closely examined to see if anything other than black was visible, (grey or some shade of grey would suggest a possible hybrid), but the primaries appeared black.
