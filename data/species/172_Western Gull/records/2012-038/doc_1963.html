Description of the Bird

On 29 March 2012, Nick Moore and I decided to pursue an interesting large gull that Mark Peterson had seen, at some distance, a few days earlier on Prewitt Reservoir. Before reaching the reservoir, we stopped at the pond along CO-6, where Washington CR R ends. To our delight, there was a nice group of gulls present. Nearly the first bird I put my scope on was a big dark-mantled gull with pink appearing legs. “Western Gull,” I called out. Nick was quickly on the bird, but then we somehow lost the it (the bird, that is) in the swarm. After a 3-4 minute period that, perceptually, was a couple hours, we re-found the gull in question.

It was a stocky large gull that was somewhat bigger than the nearby Herring Gulls. It was in high breeding plumage, with its soft parts nearly fluorescent in their color. The bill was massive and colored brilliant yellow, bordering on orange. The gonydeal spot was red. The orbital ring was brilliant yellow. The eyes were muddy colored, not unlike those of a Thayer’s Gull, and not as dark as those of a California Gull. The toes, webbing, and “knees” were all bright pink, but the remainder of the leg was an admixture of bright yellow and pink hues (see photos).

The head, tail, uppertail coverts, and underparts were all bright white. The mantle was darkish gray, darker than the gray I was used to seeing in Western Gulls in Washington and Oregon and nearly as dark as the birds I have seen in Baja California Sur. The folded wingtips were black and contrasted somewhat, but not starkly, with the mantle. Some white spotting could be seen on folded primaries. In flight, the upperwings and mantle were concolorous, excepting that the outermost 3 or 4 primaries were blackish, with a white mirror only on P10. The underwings were white, except dusky secondaries and inner primaries shading into blackish outermost primaries (3 or 4), again with a white mirror on P10.
Behaviors: See above. Mostly standing about, occasionally preening, on muddy edge of marshy pond

Similar Species Discussion

The only gull that really resembles this bird is Yellow-footed Gull, especially given its impressively sized beak. However, Yellow-footed Gulls nearly always have yellow or white eyes and are even darker mantled than this bird was. Additionally, per Howell and Dunn’s “Gull” book, high breeding plumaged Western Gulls often have some yellow on their legs. The dark mantle eliminates GW x Western hybrid gull as a possibility. Kelp Gulls and Greater Black-backed Gulls are darker backed with a different leg color (typically).
