Description of the Bird

The bird was soaring with Turkey Vultures and was roughly the same size, but quite different in shape. It had very broad, yet fairly pointed wings. It had a much shorter tail than the turkey vultures, one that didn't extend far past the ends of its wings, and which was fan-shaped with very sharp corners. The short tail combined with the prominent (though small) head combined to give the bird a very front-heavy look, especially compared to the Turkey Vultures. The plumage of the bird was entirely black, except that the outer primaries had very pale highlights that were visible on the wings from both above and below.
Behaviors: The bird alternately flapped and soared in circles as it gained altitude along with the Turkey Vulture flock. When soaring, the bases of its wings (near the body) were held very close to the horizontal, but the wingtips tended to sweep upwards somewhat. The flapping of the Black Vulture was very distinctive, completely different from that of the Turkey Vultures, much faster and snappier (3 to 4 wingbeats per second) and more frequent. At one point during the observation, the Black Vulture dropped its feet below its body for a few seconds, so that they hung straight down. I have never seen a Turkey Vulture do this (although I've seen many species of hawks do it, including Zone-tailed Hawk). BNA does not mention anything about this behavior in either vulture species.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Turkey Vulture and Zone-tailed Hawk were ruled out by shape, wingbeat speed, and the white highlights in the primaries. Both species of Black-Hawk were ruled out by the white highlights in the primaries and the complete lack of white in the tail, plus the flapping behavior that is so distinctive to Black Vulture.

I have seen many hundreds or thousands of Black Vultures in the southern United States and in Latin America. I have learned to identify them at a distance by their distinctive shape and flight style.
