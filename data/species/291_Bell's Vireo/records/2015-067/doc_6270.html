Habitat

Willow carr: extensive willow shrubs and wild plum shrubs along a running stream; punctuated by a handful of taller willow trees and one Rocky Mtn. Juniper and one Gambel Oak; understory of snowberry, Oregon Grape, Mullein, Wild Rose, and others.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Binoculars: Brunton 8.5 x 43 (Urling), Svarovski 8.5 x 42
Distance:  5-30 feet
Light:  Bright gray cloudy day

Description of the Bird

Behavior: First observation: heard only; we could not find it even though it moved through the brush between bursts of song.
We heard five bursts of the diagnostic Bell's Vireo song. A rapid fire, 1-2 second song: "Tweedl tweedly tweedly deep!"
It sang the song  10-20 times, then stopped. A few minutes later, it repeated the process, usually in a different place.
We looked & looked & could not locate the bird. It hid in the willow shrubs somewhere. It moved a couple of times, between bursts.
We came back 45 minutes later and it sang from the original site (more or less).

We returned 2 hours later and, admittedly, used a recording to see if the bird would show itself, which it did. Field marks:
        Urling's description:
Small (but larger than warbler), gray green
Two thin wing bars on one side; one thin wing bar on the other side.
Breast: Yellowish but whiter down center.
  Yellowish lower sides and rump.
Face: Pale yellowish-gray eyering & lores, contrasting with grayish face. Reminded me of a Warblng Vieo face since the eye ring was so pale & didn't contrast strongly with rest of face.
First ID by song & habitat: thick plum patch at edge of small willow-filled stream bottom.
Very agitated and responsive to Bell's Vireo recording.
Voice: wheezy & musical.
ON May 25, two trips, heard one, only, burst of song.

        Hugh's description:
Size: small -- smaller than other vireos (e.g. Plumbeous)
General color: Olive brown/yellow, depending partly on the light
Head: Brownish-gray; eye ring with whitish lores - not as prominent or conspicuous as a Plumbeous Vireo
Breast: pale yellow, slightly yellower next to wings. Belly brighter yellow.
Voice: song matched recording that we listened to later. Scolding call also matched the recording.

Similar Species Discussion

We know of no other bird with a similar song.
When we saw it, we could compare field marks with
        Plumbeous Vireo (larger, more crisply marked, eye ring & lores more prominent, different song);
        Warbling Vireo (a little larger, marked more drably, different song).

Resources Used

Our knowledge of the song from observations at Bonny and elsewhere.
 
After the first observation we played a recording -- from Righter & Keller, Birds of the Rocky Mountains, which confirmed our ID.
After seeing the bird, we wrote our descriptions, then consulted a Sibley field guide.
I went back a third time (and the next day) to try to record the song -- no luck.

Previous Experience

We have seen and heard Bell's Vireos many times over many years at Bonny State Park (ex) and occasionally at other sites.
