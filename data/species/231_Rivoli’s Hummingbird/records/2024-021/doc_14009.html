Habitat

Feeder is located in an urban neighborhood that is south of Garden of the Gods Park. There are siberian elm, cottonwood, and ornamental crab apple trees nearby. There is a bluff with pinon pine, three leaf sumac, mountain mahogany, etc. immediately to the west of the yard.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  None. Feeder was ~12 feet from observer.
Distance:  ~12 feet.
Light:  Sunny and bright. In the early morning hours, the feeder is in the shade of the house. The sun comes over the house and illuminates the feeder by 10 am.

Description of the Bird

I observed the hummer at 3 different times (within the same 5 minute span). During the first two observations, the bird flew towards the feeder and then it flew away (so maybe 1-2 seconds each). The bird was very skittish and did not land. Right away, I noticed it was large with darker green coloration (when compared to the black-chinned and broad-tailed females that are also currently visiting the feeder). A few minutes later, the bird visited a third time. This time, I was waiting with my camera held to my face (ready and hoping it would appear). When it showed up, I took as many photos as possible. Based on the time stamps of the photos, I observed it for one minute. It has not shown up again. It appears to be a female Rivoli’s. The bird was significantly larger than the usual hummingbird species and I noticed the large size right away. The bird was (1) darkish green overall, (3) had a small post-ocular spot, (2) greenish spots on the throat and gray underparts, (3) long bill that had a very slight downward curve, and (4) small white tips on the tail (that is demonstrated in the photos). The female blue-throated mountain-gem would have more white in the tail, darker underparts, and a stronger post-ocular white stripe.

Similar Species Discussion

The female black-chinned and broad-tailed hummingbirds were eliminated by their small sizes. This bird was noticeably larger and had dark green coloration on its sides and appeared darker overall (versus the lighter underparts of the female black-chinned and lighter underparts and buffy sides of the female broad-taileds). The blue-throated mountain-gem is another similar species as it is the other large hummingbird species found in the U.S. The female blue-throated mountain-gem was eliminated as she would have more white in the tail, darker underparts, and a stronger post-ocular white stripe. Although, I observed the female Rivoli's for a short amount of time, I did not observe the tail being flashed around or fanned (which is a behavior of the blue-throated mountain-gem). 

Previous Experience

I have visited southeast Arizona twice in my lifetime, so I have limited experience with this species. However, as a birder who has lived in Colorado for over 20 years, I am familiar with the reguarly occuring hummingbird species (that are much smaller in size, of course). When one is familiar with the common species, it can be easier to notice the uncommon species.
