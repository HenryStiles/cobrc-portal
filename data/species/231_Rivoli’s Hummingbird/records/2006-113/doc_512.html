Description of the Bird

Big hummer, at least 1.5 times size of nearby Rufous; direct comparison. In bins, with bird perched on feeder facing away, all-green iridescent back and rump were obvious. No bronzy coloration. In flight, pale tail corners were not obvious, nor were they obvious on perched bird. White postocular spot trailed by faint whitish line, but definitely not a strong white postocular stripe like you'd expect on Blue-throated. No white below the malar; throat faintly streaked with rows of darkish spots. All grayish-white below; no rufous coloration there or in tail. Bill seemed kinda longish but no guarantees on that; didn't quite get the looks at it that I'd have liked.
Behaviors: Came in to feeders at 7:26 AM. Stayed about 35-40 seconds, sitting on several feeders during that time, in several different positions. Returned about five minutes later for a very brief visit.
Call: bird was silent
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The large size eliminates all North American hummingbirds except Blue-throated, which is eliminated by the all-green back and rump, the throat faintly streaked with darkish spots, the postocular spot instead of a strong postocular line, the lack of a darkish auricular mask, the lack of a whitish malar stripe, and the lack of obvious big white tail corners.

Resources Used

I had been studying Howell's photographic guide to hummers intensively for three minutes when the bird showed up, so I literally looked up from comparative photos of Mag and Blue-throat, and put my bins on the bird. Saw back coloration very well in bins; saw tail pattern, throat pattern, eyespot, face pattern very well in scope. Size obvious with naked eye.

Previous Experience

I have seen Magnificent and Blue-throated hummingbirds many times in Arizona and Mexico, but I had never really set about studying the females before this day.
