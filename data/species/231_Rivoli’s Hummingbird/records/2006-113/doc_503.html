Description of the Bird

the bird was a large hummingbird, appearing at least two-thirds again as large as the nearby Rufous Hummingbirds. The bill was black, long, about twice the length of the head, and slightly curved.

The cap and nape were brilliant irredescent green. There was a bold white spot behind the eye, and an indistinct white line behind this spot. The throat was light gray with small and indistinct dusky-greenish spots. The belly below was very pale gray. The back and rump of the bird were brilliant irridescent green, with no brownish or bronzy tones. The tail was dark, with the underside being black, and with fairly small white tail corners. The upperisde appeared dark when the bird was in flight, but when perched appeared the same brilliant green as the back and rump, due to the green coloration of the central tail feathers. The tail was also fairly short, not sticking out beyond the wingtips when perched, and with a slight fork.

The bird flew with notably slower wingbeats than the nearby Selasphorus hummingbirds, but were still too fast to easily discern individual wingbeats.
Behaviors: The bird was only seen visiting feeders, which it did on two occasions during the morning. The first time it was seen for about 30 seconds, alternating between four different feeders. It spent most of its time perched on the far side of the feeders, though once it perched on the near side. It did not seen to be bothered by the numerous Rufous Hummingbirds visiting the feeders. The second time it flew in for only a second or two and quickly left.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Almost all species of hummingbirds were ruled out by the very large size, much larger than nearby Rufous, Broad-tailed, and Calliope Hummingbirds. Furthermore, all these species were ruled out by the complete lack of rufous anywhere on the plumage, and by the relatively slow wingbeats. Black-chinned Hummingbird was ruled out by the much large size, slower wingbeats, greener plumage above, etc. Anna's Hummingbird was ruled out by the much larger size, more slender shape, shorter tail, relatively longer bill, slower wingbeats, etc. Plain-capped Starthroat was ruled out by the much greener plumage above, with no hints of rufous, by the lack of a white striped on the rump, by the much less complex facial pattern, by the lack of a red throat, etc.

Blue-throated Hummingbird was ruled out by the greener plumage on the upperparts, with no hint of bronze coloration, by the smaller tail with a slight fork, green central tail feathers, smaller white tail corners, and by the lack of continual fanning of the tail associated with this species. Other marks that ruled out Blue-throated include a bold post-ocular spot and not a uniformly bold line, lack of a malar stripe, lighter color of the plumage below, spotting on the throat, and relative longer bill. The speed of the wingbeats was also faster than I would expect for Blue-throated.
