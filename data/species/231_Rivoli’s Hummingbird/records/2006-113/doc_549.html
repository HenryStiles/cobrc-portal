Optics:  Ziess 8x42 on the first two sightings and Kowa 823 telescope with 20-60x eyepiece on the third sighting.

Description of the Bird

This was a large female adult hummingbird. It was about 25% longer than a Broad-tailed. The bill was long, straight and dark. Overall the bird was green on the back and rump and grayish on the underparts. There was a prominent post ocular spot that extended back from the eye in a fine white line. There was also a faint whitish area from below the bill to just past the eye, but this mark was difficult to see and not sharply defined. The throat was very finely spotted and could only be discerned with a scope at about 75 feet. The flanks were lightly washed with greenish-gray. The tail was fairly large and dark underneath. The outer rectices were tipped with white and the white seemed more extensive underneath than on top. The rump was green and this extended well into the tail.
Behaviors: The first time I saw this bird (of three sightings) it was confronted by a Rufous and it fanned it tail broadly and rapidly several times. I did not see this tail fanning behavior in subsequent sightings. The wing beats were slow and visible, rather than the blur of most hummingbirds. The bird was an infrequent visitor to one set of nectar feeders and despite many observers, we could not locate where this bird went in its long periods between visits. Even then, visits at the feeders were short.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

I incorrectly identified this bird as a Blue-throated based on marginal looks, original tail fanning, post ocular stripe and seemingly large tail spots on the undersides of the outer rectrices, and information from the condo owner. However, on my third sighting (and after Spencer et al had IDed it as Magnificent), I had a much better look through my telescope and saw that the rump and tail were green and not bronze. I was also able to see the tail spots better and they were not as large as I originally thought. Finally, I was also able to see the fine throat spotting and the greenish washed flanks. Photographs in Williamson and Howell for both Blue-throated and Magnificent closely approached this bird, but the longer bill length, green rump and upper tail, throat spotting and greenish-washed more mottled flanks all eliminated Blue-throated.
