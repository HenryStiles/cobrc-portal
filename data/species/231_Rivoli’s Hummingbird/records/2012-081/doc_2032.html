Description of the Bird

Size was the most obvious characteristic. This bird was much larger than the Broad-tailed Hummingbirds that we normally have here. We also noted the long thin bill, the white spot behind the eye, the green color coming in on the throat, the dark forked tail, green on the wing coverts, back and rump, and speckled belly. We believe this bird is a young male coming into adult plumage. We rejected the possibility that this was a Blue-throated Hummingbird because it had no visible white in the tail and had a very prominent white post-ocular spot rather than an eyebrow like the Blue-throated.
Behaviors: The bird appeared at 6:40 am July 9 while we were sitting on our balcony enjoying coffee. It hovered over our hummingbird feeder hanging above us. Because of our presence, it apparently was afraid to sample the feeder and flew 20 feet away to perch in the top of an aspen tree, where Nancy was able to photograph it with a 400mm lens and I was able to view it with Leica 10X42 binoculars. Its flight pattern appeared much slower than the resident Broad-tailed Hummers and it made no sound. It flew off after a few minutes and had not reappeared as of this writing 5 hours later.
Call: None
Plumage: juvenile coming into adult plumage
