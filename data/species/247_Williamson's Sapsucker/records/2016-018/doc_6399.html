Description of the Bird

Williamson’s Sapsucker (Sphyrapicus thyroideus) in Jefferson County does not require documentation.  However, I provide this documentation because an individual was seen out-of-season. The typical Spring arrival of this species is early April; the earliest date I have or know of for Genesee Mountain is March 30.

A male Williamson’s Sapsucker was observed by me daily from January 27, 2016 10:17 through January 30, 2016 16:47 drilling wells in a ponderosa pine at 7500 feet at my residence on the south side of Genesee Mountain at US-Colorado-Golden-1298 Northridge Ct - 39.689x-105.295, Jefferson County, Colorado, US.  

The bird never called. I observed it for 5 hours over a period of 4 days.  I saw the bird on only one ponderosa pine, and it drilled 100-plus wells as its primary behavior while observed.  Wells were in ponderosa 35 feet +/- 5 feet above ground. About 1.5 hours of observation were done outside within 200 feet of the bird and close enough to hear sounds it made.  He was more present in the morning hours, and could be absent for 20-30 minutes in the afternoon hours.

This individual was reported by me to eBird and to COBIRDS’s RBA. The eBird regional volunteer Tony Leukering questioned whether this was an adult or immature male. I could not ascertain this based on coverts, so I report it as “Age Unknown”.

Male Plumage observations: red throat, yellow belly, two white malar stripes, white wing patch, black back, white rump.

Attached are photographs by me and one by Mark Chavez of Green Mountain.

** I originally reported a MALE Williamson's Sapsucker on January 27, 2016 at my home in Genesee at 7500'.  This is out-of season, as males start arriving here March 30 and early April. This male remained from Jan 27-30. It was always in one Ponderosa Pine at 35', where it drilled 100+ wells.  

** UPDATE#1: The male appeared twice (that I saw) in February, on Feb 14 and Feb 22.

** UPDATE#2: A FEMALE WISA appeared on February 24, 2016.  It was tapping on a different Ponderosa Pine thirty feet away at 25' height. It was gone when I returned to take its photo.

Similar Species Discussion

- Red-naped Sapsucker adult was eliminated because there was no red color on crown or nape, only on throat. Also back was primarily black and not barred.  Also different malar white stripes.
- Yellow-bellied Sapsucker adult male was eliminated because there was no red color on crown, only on throat. Also back was primarily black and not barred.  Also different malar white stripes.

Previous Experience

- I listen daily for their distinctive knock when the males arrive in early April.  
- I am a local Audubon and Denver Field Ornithologists field trip leader to the Genesee Mountain sites (8000'-8400' elevation) where they nest and fledge in late June and early July.
