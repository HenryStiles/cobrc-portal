Description of the Bird

     On 3/22/14, a sapsucker was in several trees in my yard.  The medium-sized woodpecker had a black and white patterned face with a fairly extensive red crown.  The chin and throat of the bird were red.  The bird had a black chest patch and yellow underparts.  The sapsucker's extensive white patch on the wing coverts and barring on the back were observed.  The bill was fairly long and pointed.  The wings were black with white barring on the primaries and secondaries.    
     Since a Red-naped Sapsucker had been visiting my yard for the previous 9 days, I initially assumed that the Red-naped Sapsucker had returned.  Luckily, I gave this sapsucker more scrutiny.  The sapsucker seen on 3/22/14 had a completely red throat and chin.  Lateral to the throat and chin was an uninterrupted black border.  This black border was continous (without red color intruding anywhere along its length).  The lower section of the black border merged with the black chest patch without any interruption.  In addition, this sapsucker did not have a red nape.  The back had a black vertical patch down the center that was narrow.  On the back, lateral to the black patch, were horizontal bars which were light brown alternating with black.  This barring was very even and organized rather than showing the uneven patchiness of the back of a Red-naped Sapsucker.  
          

Similar Species Discussion

I used a combination of 3 field marks to distinguish this Yellow-bellied Sapsucker from a Red-naped Sapsucker.  The black border lateral to the chin and throat was solid black; the red color of the chin and throat didn't bleed into or interrupt the solid black line.  On an adult Red-naped Sapsucker, the black border is interrupted with an expansion of the red color of the throat.  The Yellow-bellied Sapsucker had a white nape rather than the red nape seen on a Red-naped Sapsucker.  The distinctly barred back (see description above) did not have the random mottled appearance of a Red-naped Sapsuckers back.  The Yellow-bellied Sapsucker's back was light brown and black rather than black and white as in the Red-naped Sapsucker.   
  

Resources Used

    I knew the field marks that separated Yellow-bellied Sapsucker from Red-naped Sapsucker.  I saw those field marks well.  Consequently, I was able to identify the bird without referring to the field guides until afterwards.  At that time, I referred to The Sibley Guide to Birds and the National Geographic Field Guide to the Birds of North America. 

Previous Experience

    My experience is mostly with Red-naped Sapsuckers, but I have had experience with Yellow-bellied Sapsucker over the years.  Even though it was my first Yellow-bellied Sapsucker in the state of Colorado, I've seen the species in a number of other states.  I was completely aware of the necessary field marks for separating the two species.  In addition, I was aware of the fact that hybridization can complicate the identification of these 2 species.
