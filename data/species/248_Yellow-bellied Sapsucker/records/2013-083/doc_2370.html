Description of the Bird

NOTES TAKEN 06 FEB - Referring to pg 311 of Big Sibley, note the juvie plumages shown for RNSA (through October) vs. YB (through March). Our bird was overall brownish with a small bit of red on the crown/forehead, striped facial pattern wasn’t strong like an adult RN/YB, was more like the juvies, except for a fairly strong black border above the throat (which was not completely white but smudgy-buffy), no red visible on the throat, and a few flecks of black were visible below the light throat area where adult RN/YB show strongly black...
NOTES TAKEN 08 FEB – we got longer, closer looks today in the same row of pines. Overall brownish-grayish bird with weak facial stripe pattern and the throat has darkish margins (not as black as I previously described). The red patch on the fore-crown seems more pronounced compared to the views we had two days ago, and is about the only adult-appearing bit of plumage. Whitish coverts apparent, and the back was overall splotchy/barred.
Behaviors: this bird is often sitting still, locked in place on the trunk of a single pine tree, but at times actively forages and moves vertically on a given tree, sometimes between trees, seldom departs this row of pines. Prior encounters with YBSA in eastern US (and in TX in winter) surprised me how accommodating YBSA is at close range (like this bird is) vs. the shyness of RNSA. No vocals.
Call: none, heard foraging on occasion, but not drumming
Plumage: mostly juvenile, transitioning into adul

Similar Species Discussion

juvie WISA has no obvious facial stripes, no white secondaries, no obvious white barring on the back, and molt schedule is wrong. Per Sibley’s molt schedules, RBSA and RNSA should both be in adult plumages by Feb, whereas YBSA should appear as a juvenile to/thru Mar. Overall, this bird is brownish/grayish with otherwise subdued contrast on the face, almost no adult-like field marks on the chest and throat, but with a red crown (refer to photos in the FLICKR link below).

Additional Information

Comments: Many sites reference an article published in the November/December 2006 issue of Birding, Variation in Red-naped and Yellow-bellied Sapsuckers, Mlodinow, Barry and Cox that claims that all juvenile Red-naped Sapsuckers studied had at least some red in the nape by October, and they found Yellow-bellied Sapsuckers collected north of the usual wintering grounds in the fall that already had red in the crown. Bird Nerd

Many juvs, freshly plumaged and otherwise, of both RN and YB Saps have red crowns, but consisting of juv feathers vs adult feathers. This bird is a classic YB Sap, methinks, besides, by Feb YB Saps should be showing a reasonable amount of non-juv feathering on head. Jessie Barry, Cameron Cox and I published an article on this general topic in Birding a few years back. If anyone wants a copy of the PDF, just ask. Steve Mlodinow

Reads like YBSA, looks like YBSA. Tony Leukering

I dug out the 2006 Birding article by Mlodinow, Barry, and Cox. I can see the collection of characteristics that led Steve to declare your bird a yellow-belly. Take the overall buffy look, prevalence of back bars and general back paleness, lack of any red on nape, AND wideness of the white stripe below the eye all add up to YBSA. Also the black around the throat patch developing to be a complete encirclement seems a valid diagnostic. Ron Lambeth
