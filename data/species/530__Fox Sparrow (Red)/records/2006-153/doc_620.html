Habitat

The black locust grove on the west side of Neenoshe is well known as a migrant trap. It is bordered on the west side by a gravel road. To the east of the road there is a variable width strip of grasses and short weeds. Next is an intermittent series of low- to medium-sized deciduous shrubs interrupted by black locust trees in spots. There are also a few Russian olives in the area. To the east of the locusts is a band of smaller deciduous trees and then large cottonwoods are closest to the reservoir when it is full.

Description of the Bird

Taiga subspecies

I first saw this bird flying away from me and the bright rufous on the tail and wings were obvious. By overall size and medium length tail, it appeared to be a large sparrow and not a thrasher. It soon appeared at the edge of a low shrub where I had a good look at the perched bird in the shade of the shrub. The top of crown appeared rufous, although most of the face around the eye was grayish. The eye was black. The auriculars immediately behind the eye were grayish, but immediately faded to rufous and the lower check was also rufous. The sides of the nape were gray. The upper mandible was mostly dark, particularly the culmen, and the lower bill was straw-colored. The bill was medium-sized for a sparrow. Below the bill there were strong rufous moustaschial streaks that extended into a 'necklace' below the throat which was whitish. The rufous streaking on the upper breast was very dense on the sides and upper breast and continued down the flanks as a series of closely connected splotches. The center of the belly was mostly whitish. The wings were rufous with two indistinct white wing bars and the primary tips were dark. The back seemed rufous and gray, but not seen well. The legs were shaded, but appeared light-colored. The tail was rufous as were the upper tail coverts.
Behaviors: I first saw this bird flying low from one short deciduous shrub to other, where it disappeared. It then reappeared at the side of another deciduous shrub, where I had very good looks at the bird, even though it was shaded. Most of the leaves were off the shrub. It then flew out of sight and I did not follow it any further.
Call: Not Heard
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Brown Thrasher is similar in coloring, but was immediately eliminated because of overall size, tail length, and later by bill size and shape. Song Sparrow was eliminated by size - it is smaller and slimmer, grayer head and face and darker bill. Some southwestern subspecies approach the coloring of this bird, but breast streaking is much finer then the dense streaking of this bird. Swamp Sparrow has a gray breast that is faintly streaked, if at all. The other Fox Sparrow subspecies do not have extensive and bright rufous coloring on the face, head, back, or breast of this bird.

Previous Experience

The iliaca subspecies of Fox Sparrow was the common subspecies in Ohio where I birded for twenty plus years before moving to Colorado. I have seen all of the species and subspecies described above in many locations throughout the US many times.
