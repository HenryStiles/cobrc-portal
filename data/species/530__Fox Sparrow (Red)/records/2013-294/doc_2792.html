Description of the Bird

The iliaca fox sparrow was first noticed when I was looking at the mixed group of dark-eyed juncos I always have under my niger feeder. It was clear to me there was a bigger and plumper bird in there with the juncos. So I looked at it through my binoculars and saw it was a kind of sparrow, scratching in the ground and not just picking at the surface with its beak. From white colored breast to the flanks it was heavily streaked with thick rusty blotches with a central blob on the upper breast where the streaks came together. The gray head was also streaked rusty and on down to the back. The wings and especially the tail were rusty red. I then double checked the bird against the National Geographic Field Guide to the Birds of North America and confirmed that the painting was identical to the bird on my property. I then got it into my spotting scope at the lowest setting of 15x because it was so close. The fox sparrow was there intermittently all day and again the next morning. I saw it several times in good lighting. 

Similar Species Discussion

The closest similar species was the song sparrow, but my bird was noticabley larger, rustier colored overall, and its streaking was heavier than song sparrows with which I am familiar. Its foraging behavior was more like a towhee than a song sparrow.

Resources Used

National Geographic Field Guide to the Birds of North America

Previous Experience

I am very familiar with song sparrows all through the west, visually in the field and have banded them. I have seen many of the gray and chocolate colored fox sparrows in California, even banded some of those. I have seen the gray colored Rocky Mountain form of fox sparrow as well. This is the first iliaca or "red" fox sparrow I have seen.
