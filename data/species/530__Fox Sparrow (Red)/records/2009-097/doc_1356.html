Habitat

In open, scrubby floodplain. The bird flew from one shrub to another.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  All of us had binoculars. I used 10X Nikon EDG binoculars.
Distance:  Initially, quite close; within 25 feet. Then ~100 feet away. Also seen in flight.
Light:  It was a clear morning, but there was not yet any direct sunlight, as I recall. I had recently looked at my watch, and it was 6:38, when I did so. At 6:38, there was enough light to identify juncos to subspecies, e.g., Pink-sided vs. Slate-colored. By the time of the Fox Sparrow observation--around 6:45 a.m.--the light would have been better.

Description of the Bird

The bird was a "Red" Fox Sparrow of the nominate -iliaca- group. It was notably larger than nearby juncos, and it had large, "blocky" red streaks and splotches on the breast and flanks. Its face was hard to make out in the early light (see below), but it was not as uniformly red as the red coloration on the underparts. The tail was red, notably so in flight. On the perched bird, the wings were reddish. Flying away, the birds red tail contrasted well its less-colorful upperparts.
Behaviors: We heard the bird first, and I was fairly certain it was a Fox Sparrow, based on the call-note. When we first saw it, it was in a nearby shrub, seen in profile view. It was calling repeatedly. We spooked the bird, and it flew away to our right, a distance of about 100 feet away. We did not hear it give a flight call, and I do not recall if it gave a call-note after landing.
Call: Inititially and provisionally identified by call, a repeated "tsook" note, louder and richer than the "tik" notes of nearby Dark-eyed Juncos.
Plumage: unknown, but something postjuvenal

Similar Species Discussion

I first struck by the junco-like call-note, but I was nearly certain it was not a junco. The call-note of Lincoln's Sparrow is lighter and more lisping; one of the call-notes of the Hermit Thrush is more liquid. I was pretty sure it was a Fox Sparrow, but, of course, I wanted to see. Given the bird's large size (for a sparrow), its distinctively reddish and blotchy underparts, its contrasting red tail, and its red-and-dull face and upperparts, it had to be a Fox Sparrow, and, indeed, a Red Fox Sparrow at that.

Previous Experience

I have seen hundreds of Red Fox Sparrow in winter and on migration in eastern North America. I have seen a few dozen on the breeding grounds in Colorado, and scores elsewhere in the West, especially Nevada and Colorado. I would say that I have extensive familiarity with both of the expected subspecies-groups (iliaca and schistacea) in Colorado, along with the megarhyncha and unalaschcensis groups farther west (not expected in Colorado).
