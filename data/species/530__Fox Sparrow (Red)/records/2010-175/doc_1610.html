Description of the Bird

The bird was smaller than a Wood Thrush, bigger than a Lincoln's Sparrow. The upper parts at a distance appeared dark rusty brown, the underparts white and rusty-brown striped. This was a large sparrow, heavily striped, suggesting a large Song Sparrow with the dark parts being very reddish brown. The bill was mostly yellow, with the upper mandible being grayish on top grading to corn yellow along its cutting edge, the lower mandible being entirely corn yellow, the tip of both mandibles being grayish black. The eye was black with a moderately wide, white eyering, very narrowly broken both in front of and behind the eye. The aucular was solid rusty-brown, and pointed at the rear. The supercillium was broadly gray, extending back to the nape and extending around the pointed rear of the auricular to the top edge of the wing. The crown was essentially rusty brown, marked with very fine darker streaks, only visible at close range (or when the photos are highly magnified). At the base of the upper mandible (i.e., just above the rusty-brown lores at the front of the supercillium, is a pale, whitish gray area (not unlike Song Sparrow and other Melospiza sparrows). The throat was mostly white, with a very wide, rusty brown lateral throat stripe or "patch". The background color of the entire underparts was white with a slight gray cast. The lower throat and upper breast were marked with mostly rusty, wide, short stripes. The lower breast and upper belly were marked with short, wide, somewhat chevron-shaped stripes that were mostly black. The wings were mostly rusty-brown. The wings were marked with two inconpicuous white-buff wingbars, with the lower being more evident in most views. Rather than being a complete wing slash, these wingbars were more like a series of closely-spaced pale dots (the tips of the scapulars). The mantle was rusty-brown and light gray striped. The rump was gray, contrasting with the mantle and upper tail. The upper tail, and rest of the upper side of the tail was bright rusty-brown. The legs were pinkish-gray. The undertail was white. The flight feathers were blackish, broadly edged with rusty-brown, more so on the leading edge than the rear.
Behaviors: Mostly stayed on the ground or in the lower portions of Russian-olives. Often heard and seen scratching in the leaf litter with a double-footed, backwards jump, presumably looking for Russian-olives and associated arthropods. Fairly slow-moving and inconspicuous, but when approached slowly not overly wary. Loosely associated with White-crowned Sparrows.
Call: not heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Too large for Lincoln's Sparrow. Song Sparrow is the biggest issue, particularly given the existence of rusty subspecies and larger ones like the Aleutian birds. The yellow bill color and definite pale wing bars seem like the best distinguishing features. Also helpful were the rusty red and black chevron-shaped short stripes on the underparts. The overall large size, and lack of a central crown stripe is additional evidence of this being a Fox Sparrow.

Resources Used

Sibley,Rising/Beadle and Beadle/Rising. In addition the photos were sent to Dr. Jim Rising for his opinion on the subspecies. He said it appeared to be P. i. iliaca, although did not rule out the possibility of it being one of the other subspecies within the "Red" group (altivagans or zaboria), a hybrid. Hopefully, the photos submitted may be of help in this regard.

Previous Experience

This is the 6th "red" fox sparrow I've seen in CO/WY. I have a fair amount of experience with the "slate-colored" forms which breed in CO, a fair amount with the Pacific NW forms ("sooty") and have seen two "thick-billed" forms in CA. I have seen several Fox Sparrows in the East but admit to not studying which form or "species" they were, presumably they were predominately "Red" of one type of another. I have seen thousands of Song and Lincoln's Sparrows.
