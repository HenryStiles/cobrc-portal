Habitat

The bird was captured at the banding station located in the riparian forest, consisting of mainly cottonwood and willow tree species, surrounding Barr Lake.

Description of the Bird

The bird had a reddish olive crown, gray supercilium, and rufous auriculars. The malar stripes were dark rufous and widest at the posterior end. Its upperparts were grayish olive with heavy rufous streaking, and the rump and uppertail coverts were rufous brown. The throat and underparts were white with distinct, triangular-shaped, reddish brown breast spots more clustered at the center of the breast and becoming darker in color but less numerous toward the belly. The whitish wing bars were indistinct and narrow. The bill was large with an exposed culmen of 12.0mm and width at nares of 8.0mm. The mandible was yellow, and the maxilla and tip of the bill were both dark. Based on the overall reddish plumage, streaked back, and wing chord (85 mm) that measured larger than the tail (73 mm), this Fox Sparrow belongs to the Northern Red Group (P. i. iliaca). This FOSP was aged as Hatching Year because its skull was incompletely pneumaticized.
Behaviors: Normal behaviors of the Fox Sparrow were not observed during its capture and banding.
Call: not applicable
Plumage: reddish brown upperpts spotted breast

Similar Species Discussion

This bird was separated from the majority of sparrows based on its overall large size. Its wing chord measured 85 mm, which is outside the upper range of all Song Sparrow subspecies groups, with the exception of the Alaskan Island Group. The Fox Sparrow's bill was larger than a Song Sparrow's bill; its width at nares was 8.0 mm, whereas a SOSP bill usually between 4.1 to 6.1 mm wide. The coloration of the bill was mostly yellow, whereas a SOSP's bill lacks yellow and is darker in color.
