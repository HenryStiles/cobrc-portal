Description of the Bird

I noticed the bird while doing Project Feeder Watch on November 21, 2013.  It was feeding on the ground, with a group of Slate-colored Juncos, under a row of chokecherries about 30 - 40 feet from our house.  The bird was back-scratching, towhee-like, feeding, I presume, on millet I had scattered on the ground.  Over two days, November 21st and 22nd, it made intermittent trips to the yard, feeding on the ground and coming to a platform feeder stocked with millet and sunflower chips.  Sadly, on the 22nd, the bird was a window-strike victim (our kitchen window) while evading a Sarp-shinned Hawk which frequents the yard.  I heard it hit the window, and recovered it immediately.  After taking several photographs, I took the dead bird to DMNS, and it is in their collection.  


Live Bird - A large sparrow (appeared larger than Dark-eyed Juncos with which it was feeding), with extensive brownish streaking on the breast and flanks, with a somewhat smeared central breast spot, rusty red tail and rump contrasting with a brownish, slightly streaked back, faint wing bars, relatively plain grayish face with some rusty red in the malar area, two-tone bill with yellow lower mandible.


Based on my observation of the live bird, I thought it was a Slate-colored Fox Sparrow of the Canadian Rocky Mountain population as shown in Sibley p. 496, i.e., somewhat between Slate-colored and Red in appearance.  


Dead Bird - In hand, the bird had a much more rusty red overall appearance, with the streaking, especially on the breast, having a more of a rusty appearance than I detected on the live bird;  as well, the streaking on the back was much more pronounced than had appeared on the live bird,  More importantly, there was much more rusty red color on the face (ear coverts) and crown than I could see on the live bird.  Adding the wing bars, the markings better fit a Red Fox Sparrow.  

Similar Species Discussion

Distinguished from other steaked sparrows by size, and the rusty red tail and rump contrasting with the brownish back.  More specifically, distinguished from the Song Sparrow by the lack of a bold brown lateral throat throat-stripe and presence of a yellow lower mandible and slightly notched tail.  

Resources Used

The Sibley Guide to Birds (David A. Sibley, 2000) and Sparrows of the United Staes and Canada (Beadle and Rising, 2002)

Previous Experience

I have been birding for a time and have experience with a number of the streaked sparrow species.  However, my experience with Fox Sparrows is limited, having seen this species on only a handful of occasions.
