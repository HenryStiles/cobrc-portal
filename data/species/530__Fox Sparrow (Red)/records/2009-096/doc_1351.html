Habitat

Riparian strip on a perennial stream. Bird feeder on side of tree and ground feeding station on ground in shadow of trees and building. Mixed vegetation, western juniper, blue spruce, box elder, shrubs. Within town adjacent to city park.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Brunton 12X42 binoculars, Panasonic FZ-35 digital camera.
Distance:  18 feet (tape measure)
Light:  Twilight before sunup at start of observations. Direct sunlight for approximately 2 hours during the day, then shade. Day was clear and bright, snow on ground surrounding viewing area. Sun was to righ of and then behind observer (bird was north of viewing location)

Description of the Bird

The bird was slightly larger than house sparrows feeding at the same location. Plumage was typical of the "red" fox sparrow, including a rufous tail, rufous triangular spotting on a white to light gray breast, and an irregular rufous chest patch. Eyes were dark, bill was short and sharply pointed, yellow at the base and bottom and dark on the top surface. Head was gray and rufous, with faint rufous striping on the top of the head. Legs were dark dusty pink. Wings were gray/rusty with tips extending just past base of tail when folded.
Behaviors: Bird was present at a ground feeding station at 0745 on 9 December 2009, accompanied by several darkeyed juncos and a spotted towhee. Bird was feeding on commercial birdseed mix. Bird fought with the towhee at one point early in the morning and was not run off by the towhee. Bird continued to feed on the ground throughout the day (unitl 1430 when this report was started), feeding for two to ten minutes then retreating to dense cover for ten to twenty minutes, then repeating process. I photographed the bird throughout the day.
Call: None heard or recorded
Plumage: typical red

Similar Species Discussion

Other sparrows seen at this location in the past include house sparrows (no streaking or spots on breast), Lincoln's sparrow (substantially more gray and with different pattern of spots on breast), and song sparrow (different plumage). House finches smaller, with gray plumage with gray streaking. Wrong shape and plumage for thrushes.

Resources Used

First sighting impression was a red fox sparrow due primarily to plumage color and shape of spots. Took numerous photographs, sent email with attached photos immediately to Jason Beason, RMBO. Confirmed ID with National Geographic and Sibley field guides, Cornell website. Jason replied to email with confirmation of ID and observed bird later in the day at this location, again confirming ID.

Previous Experience

I have seen fox sparrows in the Pacific Northwest on numerous occasions. I have seen fox sparrows on two other occasions in this area, once with several other birders on a trip, and one capture in a mistnet while doing bat surveys.
