Cemetery with no underbrush but many mature shade trees both deciduous and coniferous. Bird preferred the deciduous trees.

Light:  Mostly sunny. Bird was seen in a variety of different light conditions including full sun and well-lit shade.

Description of the Bird

The bird was a classic parula: small for a warbler, chunky, with a very short tail. When it would fly from tree to tree it was immediately distinguishable from all other birds in the cemetery by its truly tiny size. Its underparts were mostly bright yellow, extending up the throat to the base of the bill, and including the malar area adjacent to the lower mandible. Belly and undertail coverts were white. There was an orangey wash across the upper breast without any hint of black or blue highlights. Above the malar was a sudden transition to the pale bluish upperparts color of the bird. This included the eye, which had no white eye crescents. There were two white wingbars. The bill was bicolored: upper mandible dark, lower one pale.
Behaviors: The bird sang nearly continuously all morning, often while foraging, but sometimes simply while sitting in one place. It preferred the highest portions of the deciduous trees, but also entered the spruces occasionally.
Call: The bird sang the same song type over and over. In 2 or more hours of observation, during which the birds sang nearly continuously, I never heard it vary the song phrase except to double it or, occasionally, to truncate it. The song type was a very high-pitched, nearly toneless, strongly upslurred buzzy trill, culminating in an extremely brief high buzzy toneless monotone or downslurred note, total about 1.25 seconds.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Northern Parula is the species to rule out. Lack of eye crescents, lack of black/blue in the breast, and yellow malar all eliminate it. Sonagrams should also eliminate it, though I do not yet know enough to say so conclusively.
