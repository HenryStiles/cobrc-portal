Description of the Bird

A very small, aboreal warbler with a stunning yellow mandible blending into its yellow throat and a slaty blue head lacking any bright features. The area around the eye was the darkest, appearing almost black and giving the bird a masked appearance. The yellow throat deepened to a bold orange upper breast. The breast completely lacked a band of darker black or rufous tones. The orange patch faded back to yellow on the lower breast and transitioned into a white belly and white undertail coverts. From underneath the tail feathers appeared to be white centered with broad dark edges and corners. The bird was generally above me, so I did not see the back or upper wings well. I could see that the bird had grayish-blue wings with two white wing bars. The bird had grayish legs with bright yellow feet.
Behaviors: The bird was always noted high in trees, generally deciduous but also entering pine trees at times. When not singing the bird was nearly impossible to find. Presumably it was feeding and resting at these times. The bird could be located when it was singing. Fortunately it frequently sang, and often sang for extended periods, allowing observers to track it down.
Call: Frequently singing, very high pitch, shrilly rising bzzz bzzz bzzz bzzz with the end note generally trailing up to the limits of my hearing. Generally sang 3, 4, or 5 syllable songs. Bouts of singing often lasted 10 or more minutes, with phrases separated by a few seconds. The bird also had variable periods of time, some fairly long, when it was silent, perhaps while it was feeding.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

The only other warbler with a yellow mandible blending into its yellow throat would be a Northern Parula. Northern Parulas show white eye arcs and a dark breastband, both of which this bird lacks.
