Habitat

Deciduous trees in a cemetary with mixed coniferous and deciduous trees.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10x binoculars and once 30x telescope.
Distance:  30 to 40' at closest approach.
Light:  Bright sunny day, and the bird was mostly in very good light.

Description of the Bird

Smallish warbler, but with a quite long, flesh colored bill. The head, neck, wing coverts and upper tail were a dark blue. Two white wingbars were very conspicuous and easy to see. There was a small olive patch in the center of the back that was much more difficult to see. The area of the cheeks was blackish, below the dark eye and behind the bill. There was absolutely no sign of eye arcs that I could see. The chin, breast and belly were quite bright yellow, with the upper breast being slightly darker and a little more orangish. The under tail coverts were white, and the legs were dark.
Behaviors: The bird was singing and foraging mostly near the tops of several deciduous trees in a surburban cemetary.
Call: The bird was singing almost continuously, but I couldn't distinguish it from that of Northern Parula.
Plumage: Alternate or Breeding

Similar Species Discussion

By far the most similar species is Northern Parula.
However, the male of this species has bold, white
eye arcs, and red and black bands across the chest.
No other warbler has the described combination of colors, wing bars etc.

Resources Used

National Geographic Field Guide, and the Dunn and Garrett book on Warblers.

Previous Experience

Have seen this species once in the US, and several
times in central America. Am quite familiar with the
more regularly occuring Northern Parula.
