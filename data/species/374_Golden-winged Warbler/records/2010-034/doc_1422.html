Habitat

The bird was foraging in cottonwood and box elder trees in riparian habitat along the Animas River.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski 8.5 x 42 binoculars were used.
Distance:  Distances ranged from 135 feet to 35 feet.
Light:  Light conditions varied significantly. At times the viewing was excellent, particularly when the bird was lower in the canopy.

Description of the Bird

The bird was identified as a warbler due to it’s small size, the bill shape (pointed), and the foraging behavior.

Similar in size to Orange-crowned Warblers in the vicinity.

Details:
The crown was yellow.
The face was very distinctly patterned. A dark gray auricular patch contrasted with a thin, white supercilium and broad, white malar region.
The chin and throat were a slightly lighter gray than the auricular patch.
The bill was very pointed.
The upperparts were light gray – similar in color to the chin and throat.
The wings showed an upper and lower wing bar. Both wing bars were yellow. (The lower wing bar was very broad while the upper wing bar was conspicuous but narrower. Essentially, the 2 wing bars on each wing appeared as a patch with a slight interruption in it.)
Tail was about average in length for a warbler. The color of the tail was gray above and mostly white from below. When the bird fanned her tail, the outer tail feathers showed extensive white with a thin gray outer edge.

The underparts were mostly light gray, becoming whiter at the belly.
Behaviors: The bird was extremely active. She would often hang from the foliage of the cottonwoods and box elder trees while gleaning insects. At times, she would drop a few feet before rising straight up a similar distance. She probed for small caterpillar-like prey from dead leaves.
Call: A very high- pitched chip (given in flight) would sometimes help with locating her.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Ruling out a Blue-winged/Golden-winged hybrid was considered. The auricular patch was gray and extensive, not a thick eyeline as in Brewster's Warbler. The bird didn't have a yellow wash on the underparts which would be evident on a Lawrence's Warbler. In addition, the combination of a gray auricular patch, a white supercilium, and a white malar created a patterned face with more contrast than shown for female Lawrence's Warblers in the field guide.

Resources Used

"A Field Guide to Warblers of North America" by Jon Dunn and Kimball Garrett was referred to both at the time of the sighting and after the sighting.

Previous Experience

I have had prior experience with adult males in New Mexico and in Illinois. I have no experience with the hybrids.
