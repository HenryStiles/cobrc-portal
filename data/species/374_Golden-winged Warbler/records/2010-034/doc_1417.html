Description of the Bird

The olive yellow crown was the first feature to catch my eye, followed by the yellow "slash" along the wing. The bird had a bluish/gray color on the upperparts and very light gray underparts. The bill seemed thin and almost needle-like. The head of the bird had a darker gray cheek patch with a white stripe above and a white malar stripe below. When I later consulted field guides, this bird seemed a bit more dull in overall color than the illustrations.
Behaviors: This bird was constantly feeding and always flitting from tree to tree. Often the bird was upside down while feeding, hanging from a clump of leaves or buds. It often seemed to favor clumps of dead leaves in its feeding efforts. At times it was probably 60 feet off the ground and at other times it was within five feet of the ground. I also observed two strange interactions in which hummingbirds seemed to approach this bird and then be driven away.
Call: thin, short, high chip given mostly in flight
Plumage: breeding

Similar Species Discussion

By size and behavior (constant movement and foraging), the bird seemed immediately to be a warbler or maybe a kinglet. It wasn't green and didn't have an eye-ring so away goes kinglet. The standout yellow slash on the wing ruled out most other warblers immediately. The lack of yellow underparts helped rule out a hybrid with Blue-winged Warbler. The bold cheek patch and facial stripes helped rule out female American Redstart which has a bit of yellow on the wing.

Resources Used

I had no field guide on my person but had seen a male Golden-winged Warbler in breeding plumage in Wisconsin. This bird immediately struck me as a somewhat faded image of that bird. I later used Susan Allerton's amazing knowledge and later still, National Geographic's Birds of North America, to confirm my sighting.

Previous Experience

I have twice before seen this bird in breeding plumage in Wisconsin and have seen the Blue-winged Warbler in the Lower Rio Grande Valley of Texas.
