Description of the Bird

Background:

Around 7:15 A.M. on Saturday morning, August 19th, I decided to walk the Yampa Core trail from the Hampton Inn, west toward downtown Steamboat Springs. I was in Steamboat Springs to drop off my son at school. The birding along the first part of the trail was quite busy, with many Yellow Warblers. I heard and saw a Northern Waterthrush fly downstream, so I decided to investigate its location. As I pished, a medium sized warbler sprang up in front of me across the river. I immediately recognized the bird as an adult male Golden-winged Warbler. For the next 8-10 minutes, I observed the bird in excellent light at distances as close as 2 meters. Three days later on Tuesday morning, August 22nd, around 7:30, I refound the bird further west on Skull/Hitchins Island. Again, in excellent light and at close distances. 


Description: 

Medium sized warbler. Gray upperparts, extending from the top of the head, through the back, and onto the uppertail coverts and upper tail. I did note a slight yellowish wash across the back, but only in good light and when I was looking down at the bird.  The crown was bright yellow, starting at the base of the forehead and extending through the crown and abrubtly ending at the top of the crown. The supercilium was white and extended from the lores to just above the auriculars. The auriculars were black and extend from the lores through the eye and ended near the nape. The malar was white. The throat was black and extended from the base of the lower mandible forming a "bib" like feature extending to the chest. The greater wing coverts were bright yellow and there was some slight yellowish coloration to the scapulars. The underparts were grayish white. In flight, I noted white outer tail feathers. The call note was was a rather low "tzup" or "tzip". I am very familiar with this bird from Michigan, Ohio, Tennessee, Mississippi, Georgia, New Jersey and a few vagrants in California

Similar Species Discussion

There are not too many warblers this bird could be confused with. The combination of facial features and coloration made this a rather straightforward observation and identification. 

Resources Used

None. I am very familiar with this species. 

Previous Experience

I have seen multiple birds in various states. I have seen at least three (3) vagrant birds in my home state of California (Kern County/Orange County/Los Angeles County). I have seen birds as recent as May in Tennessee (Millington). I have seen migrant birds in Georgia (Kennesaw Mountain) New Jersey (Cape May), Mississippi (Tunica), Ohio (McGee Marsh), and Michigan (Tawas Point).
