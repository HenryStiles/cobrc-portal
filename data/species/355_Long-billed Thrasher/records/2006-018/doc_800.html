Description of the Bird

Having heard of a Long-billed Thrasher being seen in Denver, I arrived at the site during early morning and almost immediately found the bird along the southern fence line of the property. The bird was roughly the size of a Brown Thrasher and was shaped similar in shape. The bill was a bit longer and a bit more decurved, and was dark gray across both maxilla and mandible, differing from Brown Thrasher as that species usually has a pink base to the maxilla. The head was mostly gray, except for a thin black moustache stripe separating the gray auriculars and white malar. The throat was white, with a thin black stripe separating it from the malar. The upperparts were a rich brown, although gray from the nape bled into that brown. The wings were also a rich brown, not rusty as with Brown Thrasher and had two white wingbars along the posterior edge of the greater and secondary coverts. The breast, belly, flanks, and undertail coverts were basally whitish, differing from the yellowish-white underparts of Brown Thrasher. The entire underparts, save for the undertail coverts, were streaked with black. The tail was long, roughly two-thirds the length of the body and was rich brown. The legs were gray and the irides were yellow.
Behaviors: Perched in planted shrubbery along property perimeter fence. Also observed it fly to bird bath along the house.
