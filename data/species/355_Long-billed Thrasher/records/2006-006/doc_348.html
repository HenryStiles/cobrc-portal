Description of the Bird

the bird was a thrasher about the same size as the Brown Thrasher we saw at the same location (but never near each other). The bill was slightly but noticeably longer, about the length of the head, and with a bit more of a decurve (which was also slight but noticeable). The bill color overeall was black, with a pale gray base to the lower mandible.

The bird's upperparts were a rufous-brown color, much browner than the nearby Brown Thrasher, but still with a noticeable rufous tinge. The cap was this rufous-brown color, with the cheek being a medium gray, with a bit of the rufous-brown bleeding in around the top and back edges. The iris was bright orange-yellow. The bird had a black and fairly bold malar stripe, and a very faint stripe of white between the malar and the gray cheek patch. The remainder of the upperparts were the same rufous-brown, uniform except for two thin white wingbars and very slightly more rufous in the uppertail coverts.

The bird's underparts were clean white, with very bold black streaks down the length of the underparts, except for the unmarked throat and lower belly. The undertail coverts were also white with some very faint streaks that were only visible in the scope when the bird was close.
Behaviors: The bird was mostly perched low in thick brush, hopping around some but most staying still. It would occasionally fly from bush to bush, and also occasionally would scratch on the ground.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

All species other species of thrasher other than Brown were ruled out by the heavy dark streaking below, and the rufous-brown upperparts. Wood Thrush was ruled out by larger size, longer tail, longer, thicker, and decurved bill, etc.

Brown Thrasher was ruled out by the much browner upperparts color, without the strong rufous color typical of Brown Thrashers. The streaking below was bolder, and darker, especially on the flanks and at the lower end of the streaking just above the undertail coverts. The gray cheek patch was bolder than that on a Brown Thrasher. The eye color was more orange. The bill was slightly longer, and more decurved, with a pale gray base to the lower mandbile rather than flesh colored. There were also some very faint markings on the undertail coverts.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Brown Thrashers throughout the US, including one just after seeing the Long-billed. I have seen a good number of Long-billed Thrashers in the Rio Grande Valley, and also saw some a couple of weeks before this sighting in northern Mexico that I got very good looks and photos of.
