Habitat

The bird foraged almost exclusively in the Russian olive forest at the banding station

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Leica 10x42 Ultravid binoculars
Leica APO 77 mm spotting scope with 20x-60x zoom placed used mostly between 20 and 30x. Photographs were taken using 20x on the scope and with a Leica D-lux 2 digital (8.2 megapixles) camera through the scope.
Distance:  As close as 35 feet
Light:  I observed this bird in all types of light. When photograping I always try to get the sun behind me. When I first spotted the bird the sun was at an angle to me and low in the sky.

Description of the Bird

This was a large brown-backed thrasher with long brown tail and cap. The cheeks were noticeable medium gray and the gray extended down onto the sides of its neck. The bill was blackish and slightly decurved. Breast, belly and undertail coverts were streaked with light black on a cream background color. Legs were dark. Eye was orange to yellow depending upon the light. The tail was very worn from feeding on the ground throughout the time it was at the Chico Basin Ranch.
Behaviors: Some days this bird was a skulker prefering the densest matted Russian thistle piles but on other days it would forage on the open, always eating Russian olive fruits, sometimes feeding from fruits on the ground while at other times feeding from fruits still on the trees. On some days the bird would perch partial in the open and feed in various parts of the olive trees. It would often fly from the ground to put a tree brach between itself and me.
Call: on one of my many visits I heard it sing its soft song for one phrase. It frequently gave a shark chakk call and a few times when I got too close it gave an alarm call, the rattle call described in Sibley
Plumage: basic

Similar Species Discussion

Curve-billed Thrasjer was present, often foraging in and adjacent tree or adjacent patch of dead tumbleweed. Curve-billed has a gray back and a big honking down-curved bill and is a big dude compared to the LBTH.

There was also a Brown Thrasher (actually two) the wintered in the olives at Chico Basin Ranch. Only one of them foraged with or near the LBTH. The BRTH always had a reddish orange back and only in poor light did it ever appear to have a gray face. The rich rufous of Brown Thrasher was a nice contrast to the earthy brown of LBTH.

Resources Used

When I got home I was convinced that the bird that had been called Brown Thrasher was indeed a Long-billed Thrasher. I consulted first my Sibley guide and then I looked for photos of LBTH on the web. After seeing the photos, I called Mark Peterson and e-mailed Brandon Percival and told them they had better make a quick trip to CBR to look for this great bird. Luckily it stayed at the Ranch until moments after it was banded in late April or early May, 2006.

Previous Experience

I have seen quite a few LBTHs in the Rio Grande Valley of Texas, but never with a Brown Thrasher and never before in CO, so I was extremely careful in making the I.D. call, but had I been in TX seeing this bird there would not have been any doubt.
