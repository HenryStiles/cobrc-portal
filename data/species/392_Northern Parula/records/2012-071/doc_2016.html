Habitat

Lush riparian cottonwood habitat exists on this stretch of the Animas River.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swaroski EL 8.5 X 42
Distance:  Best views were within approximately 50 feet; most views were within that distance.
Light:  Early on there were some storm clouds above Durango that allowed sufficient light without blaring light conditions. Later in the morning, the bird was difficult to locate at times because of a milky sky. However, once the bird was refound, he was mostly seen well because the foliage of the cottonwoods served as a good backdrop.

Description of the Bird

I was searching for a Northern Parula because I was hearing him sing. I located the very small warbler by pinpointing the direction of the song. The bird was a dark, bluish-gray color on the head, upperparts and upperside of tail. The chin, throat, and chest were yellow. The eye was surrounded by an upper and lower, white eye arc. The lores were black. The yellow chest was marked with a broad chest band which was deep red in color. The red band was bordered by a narrower black band above. The belly and undertail coverts were white. I noticed the green patch on the back several times. The bird had two white wing bars; the upper wing bar was more prominent than the lower one.
Behaviors: This warbler was actively feeding in the foliage of cottonwood trees. The activity of the warbler suggested that he was gleaning insects from the leaves.
Call: He sang a buzzy song that was somewhat similar to a Black-throated Gray Warbler, except it was faster and ascending. In addition he sang an ascending trill, followed by a note or two.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

I had no need to consider other species. I recognized the song before seeing the bird. When I first located the bird, I confirmed that I was right about the singer. I suppose, the most similar species to consider would have been Tropical Parula. (I wasn't that lucky.) The Tropical Parula would lack an eye ring and would lack the bicolored chest band.

Resources Used

I didn't refer to any resources at the time of the ID. Six days earlier, I found an adult male Northern Parula at NM Tech in Socorro, NM. At that time, I looked at The Sibley Guide to Birds to refresh my memory on the species. Today, the NM bird served as the refresher course for the song and field marks.

Previous Experience

I have had plenty of experience with seeing the species in the eastern U.S. In addition, I had recent experience in NM (as mentioned above).
