Habitat

The location was a rocky canyon with steep north facing slopes of Pinyon/Juniper. Most years the canyon floor would have water, but it was dry in 2012. The canyon floor was covered with rock and some good sized boulders, and in places the floor was covered with Goldenweed. Although there was some willow in the dry creek bed, the BCSP seemed to prefer the dense stands of Goldenweed to feed and provide cover followed in preference by the trees in the PJ habitat on the north facing slopes. Not sure how the habitat would be classified - a dry creek bed that normally has some running water this time of year. Places with large boulders and not much vegetation as well as areas with thick stands of Goldenweed waist to shoulder height and some willow about the same height. No other plants or shrubs in the creek bed. The PJ forest on the north facing slope comes right down to the creek bed. The south facing slope of the ravine is much rockier with sparse vegetation.

Description of the Bird

The adults were slim,dark gray sparrows with long tails, and a striped brown back. The male's head was mostly gray with a sooty black lores and black chin/throat with a pink bill. The lower belly and undertail coverts were a dull white. The female was similar to the male but with less black on the chin and throat and maybe a little duller in color. The fledglings were closer to the female, but without any black on the chin & throat and with some streaking on the
chest and underparts. They reminded me of Gray-headed junco fledglings with their streaked look(not quite as extensive streaking as on the junco).
Behaviors: I visited No Thoroughfare Canyon and found a male Black-chinned Sparrow (BCSP) feeding a fledgling perched low in a juniper tree in the exact same area described by Cheryl Teuton in her observation the previous day. Over the course of the morning (roughly 8:30 to 10:30 A.M.), I
observed adult BCSP's feeding a fledging numerous times. After observing the young fledgling being fed, I stood in one spot and took a GPS measurement. I then noticed one of the adult birds fly a short distance from the juniper on the hillside to a thick clump of Goldenweed (Oonopsis foliosa) on the canyon floor, where the adult proceeded to feed another fledgling. The birds seemed to like the thick clumps of Goldenweed both as a place to feed as well as for cover. I followed the small flock of BCSP as they made their way slowly up the canyon. The fledglings were able to fly well enough to follow the adults, and I observed the young being fed in the low branches of junipers, on the ground in the Goldenweed cover, as well as perched on large boulders on the canyon floor and hillside. The birds traveled slowly upstream for about 120 yards. At this point, the small flock of BCSP seemed to move up the hillside a short way into cover and became less active - don't know if it was the heat later in the morning, a subtle change in habitat or what. I believe there were as many as 6 birds total. At one point I saw 3 birds fly up the hillside into cover and about 20 seconds later I saw two more birds fly into the same general area. As I started walking up the canyon, I flushed another youngster out of the thick cover of the Goldenweed that proceeded to fly up the canyon in the same general direction. So, although I never saw all 6 birds at the same time, I'm pretty sure there were at least 6 birds in the small flock. I took another GPS measurement at this spot. Also talked with a lady hiking with a group of young girls (pre-teen and teen-agers), who indicated this year was the first time there was not some water in the creek bed. Not sure how long she had been hiking in the area, but she acted like she had some historical perspective. I then proceeded to follow the trail to Pool 1, which is at the bottom of a small waterfall. There was only a small trickle of water at the site, and the pool was much smaller than normal judging from the water marks on the rocks and pictures of the site at the Devil's Kitchen Trailhead. I took another GPS measurement before starting back down the trail. Pool 1 was probably 150 yards from where I last saw the small flock of BCSP. When I returned to the spot where I last saw the BCSP, I observed an adult feeding a youngster perched low in a juniper on the hillside. My feeling is that the family of BCSP are presently using the canyon floor and hillsides between the two points to feed and find cover. Although there was some willow in the dry creek bed, the BCSP seemed to prefer the dense stands of Goldenweed to feed and provide cover followed in preference by the trees in the PJ habitat on the north facing slopes. Not sure how the habitat would be classified - a dry creek bed that normally has some running water this time of year. Places with large boulders and not much vegetation as well as areas with thick stands of Goldenweed waist to shoulder height and some willow about the same height. No other plants or shrubs in the creek bed. The PJ forest on the north facing slope comes right down to the creek bed. The south facing slope of the ravine is much rockier.
Call: did not note calls or song; perhaps as a result of my hearing loss.
Plumage: breeding plumage with fledglings

Similar Species Discussion

The only bird they resemble is a Gray-headed Junco, and the BCSP appeared smaller and slimmer than Gray-headed Juncos and did not show white on the outside edges of the tail when they flew.
