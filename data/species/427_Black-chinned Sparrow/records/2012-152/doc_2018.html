Description of the Bird

First encounter was "buzzing" insect like call as above. Then, while we were trying to figure out what made the noise emanating from nearby Juniper, three Black-Chinned Sparrows (BCSP), 2 adult and a slightly stripey chested fledgling popped up. In ensuing hour and a half, watched, photographed and audiotaped 2 adult BCSP feeding a juvenile. Adult BCSP showed gray bodies with brown, streaky backs, black on faces, and pink bills, with dark orangish legs. On review of photographs, one bird that appeared to us to be female had black confined to chin, while apparent male's black appeared to extend above the bill. Tails long and dark. Juvenile gray bodied with vague stripes on chest. No black on face, left bird with "blank" look. Very orange around periphery of open mouth on this bird. Numerous photos taken, selected sent, but more available.
Behaviors: Occasionally (three times observed), one bird (presumed male), came to top of Juniper or Pinyon and gave softer version of typical song, longer introduction, with quickening, "bouncing ball" sound at end. We had heard territorial singing by this species last year near this location, this song less than half volume, and much less frequent. Adult birds observed gathering food, then frequently feeding the young. Begging sound was very buzzy, insect-like, and was heard given by all three birds, based on number of calls and volume. Recorded (low volume) by cell phone app (tape-a-talk). We only observed and photographed one fledgling that we are certain about. If there were more, we did not detect them.
Call: Heard one adult give softer version of typical "bouncing ball" call 3 times, watched it call once. Buzzing calls between all three birds were our first alert to their presence.
Plumage: Breeding (2) and Juvenile (1)

Similar Species Discussion

Not Black throated sparrows, which were also seen, based on slighter build of the reported birds, while nearby Black-throateds were stockier. These birds lacked any white in "face mask" as expected in Black throated, and brown streaked backs on reported birds as opposed to gray backs of Black Throateds. Not Lark sparrows, as reported birds show black around bill and lack "harlequin" face pattern, also did not show white fringe on tail. Not Blue-gray gnatcatchers, as reported birds were larger, and did not show eye ring or outer white tail. Call of vizualized bird was also distinctive.

Resources Used

Initially used our stored brains, as we saw same species last year near this location, and these birds were hoped for, but slightly unexpected. After observation and digital images reviewed, we consulted The Sibley Field Guide to Birds of Western North America (First Edition, 6th Printing October 2007, Knopf, publisher), later reviewed photos of juvenile birds through Google seach online. Also reviewed National Geographic Field Guide to the Birds of North America (6th edition, 2011, National Geographic Society, publisher).
