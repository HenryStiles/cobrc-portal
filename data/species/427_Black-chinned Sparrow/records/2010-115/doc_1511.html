Habitat

Habitat consisted of extensive sagebrush habitat with isolated clumps of Gambel’s oak and serviceberry. A pinyon/juniper woodland was at the periphery of the territory.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski 8.5 X 42 binoculars were used.
Distance:  Usually 100 feet or more, but within 40 ft. several times.
Light:  Since the initial sightings were looking to the north, the sun would have been fairly high, but slightly behind me (to the south). I recall excellent lighting and observation conditions, even though I didn't specifically note the position of the sun in my records.

Description of the Bird

Description of the males:
The birds were dark, long and slender sparrows. The head was gray with extensive black on the anterior of the face. The black lores connected with the black chin to create a patch of black on the otherwise gray head. The bill was pink with a slight reddish cast. The bill was petite for a sparrow. The back was rufous with distinct dark streaking. The rufous back was often visible on the birds in flight. The wings were brown. The tail was long and gray. The underparts were mostly gray. Some white was visible on the belly.

Description of the females: The females were very similar. The main differences were that the black lores were inconspicuous and the black chin was less extensive.
Behaviors: The males were singing from exposed song perches on taller shrubs. (A favorite perch was on dead branches that extended above the green vegetation of a serviceberry bush.) At one time on 6/28/10, a male that initially perched to sing was followed (not chased) by a second bird of unknown gender. The tops of junipers were song perches on 7/5/10. The last 2 weeks that the birds were seen (7/10/10 and 7/20/10), the males were still singing, but not as much and from lower perches (lower shrubs, i.e., oak and sagebrush).
One female was seen in the afternoon of 6/28/10 by a second observer. The appearance of that female occurred after pishing. A likely conclusion could be that a nest was approached. On 7/20/10, I saw 2 females (about 120 meters apart from each other). These females were staying low and difficult to observe.
Call: In the morning, the bird sang one introductory note, followed by a descending note and then a trill. The trill resembled the sound of an accelerating, bouncing ball. Between 2:00 PM and 2:25 PM, the song started with a descending whistle. The whistle was followed by the "bouncing ball" sequence. A third song was sung in that same time period. This song had 3 introductory notes which were short and similar in pitch. The songs always ended with the accelerating thrill.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

When I saw the initial bird for 1 or 2 seconds, Black-chinned Sparrow immediately came to mind. In that brief time, I noticed a gray sparrow with a patch of black on the anterior of the face, surrounding the bill. I was seeing the bird in profile and the reddish back stood out. I immediately considered the extent of the black on the face of a Black-chinned Sparrow vs. a Gray-headed Junco. Because the Black-chinned Sparrow is a gray bird with black on the anterior of the face, and reddish on the back, a Gray-headed Junco is the closest species in appearance. The differences were readily apparent. Unlike the junco, the black on the face of this bird isn’t restricted to the face, but includes the chin. In subsequent observations, the rufous-colored back had distinct brown streaking through it. (The reddish back of the junco is a solid color without any streaking.) The bird was more slender than a junco and didn’t have white outer tail feathers. The habitat was too low in elevation for juncos during this time of year. Black-chinned Sparrow was the only species that made sense to me, even from that first brief look.
