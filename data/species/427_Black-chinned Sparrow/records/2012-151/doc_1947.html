Description of the Bird

These are my notes from the time of the sighting:

On June 11th, just around noon, I was walking the Devil's Kitchen Trail
looking for Gray Vireo. In the area below where the "steps" rise up to the
kitchen proper, I heard a Gray Vireo singing. The bird flew from my right to
my left and landed up on a shelf and continued singing. I photographed it,
then began pishing to try to draw it in closer. Immediately, a Black-chinned
Sparrow landed on this same shelf about 30 feet away from me (maybe 5 feet
above eye level) in good light and it SANG. It was on a little snag very
close to the ground. It was facing right with the tail to the left and
looked up towards 2pm when it sang. I was looking with Swarovski 8x32s; The
song started with very clear dropping notes and then ended with acclerated
notes -- it was almost a two part song -- the liquid clear dropping notes
evenly spaced then an accelerated finish. The bird had a black face that
extended below the chin; it was generally bluish steel -- bluer than a
Gray-headed Junco (and more mask) -- It had brown tones on its back, but not
the rich mantle color of a GHJunco. It had a long tail, and acted like an
agitated Field Sparrow. I did NOT note bill color. When the bird flew-up, it
was a very basic and straightforward sighting; I was like "Cool -- a
Black-chinned Sparrow". I then -- SHOOT ME IN THE HEAD -- took two steps
closer to try to get a better photo (not thinking how rare this bird might
be). It flew off. I then sat there and started thinking to myself that I
don't think that these guys are supposed to be here. Still unsure, I called
my birding friend back in Illinois and asked him to do a quick check on
BChSparrow in Colorado. I checked an iPhone range map, and realized I better
get this damn thing photographed. I did see it again -- it popped up right
next to a female House Finch (it was longer and more slender than the House
Finch), in much worse light, and then quickly flew from the east to the west
-- left to right - over to the rocky slopes to the right, where I heard it
sing again.

I do not know with what one can confuse a good look at an adult male
Black-chinned Sparrow other than maybe a Gray-headed Junco. I know you do
not know me, but I started birdwatching when I was three or four (I'm 43
now). I've been coming out west -- usually Wyoming -- almost annually, since
1970 (with a gap in the '90s). I consider myself very familiar with all of
the western Juncos. I have birded numerous times in the southwest -- Az,NM,
So.Calif. I have seen Black-chinned Sparrows several times in those areas.
My ABA list is around 660, and my Colorado list is probably around 180. I
only mention these points so you can hopefully have some confidence in the
observer. When this bird popped up, there wasn't any question -- light was
good, the bird SANG while I was looking at it -- I could have photographed
it right then and there -- that was a terrible screw-up on my part.

I returned after an overnight in Ouray and spent two hours up on this shelf
and in this immediate area midday without any sign -- visual, song,call.

The habitat here is pretty damn good for this species -- rocky slopes with
scrubby shrubs interspersed. I am curious how much coverage this place gets
-- this bird acted as if it was on territory -- I think that's why it didn't
register at first as a rarity -- he looked like he was exactly where he
thought he should be.

Please let me know if you need any other details, if you think I'm missing
something, or for any general feedback.

Thanks, everyone.
