Habitat

The water table is rising in the Country Club Hills area, causing problems for those with basements, even though it is in the sand hills. New ponds have arisen in the last two or three years and cattails have moved in as well as frogs, turtles, blackbirds and ducks.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Nikon with a 400mm lens at full extension.
Distance:  60 feet at first shooting, 40 at second.
Light:  Good to too bright. The sun was westering but not enough to help photography.

Description of the Bird

The purple was not as deep as the painting in Sibley's, my only other knowledge of this bird.
As noted above, its plumage was not as pristine as I might have expected. Beaten a little
but not badly. The flecks of gray feathers in the two blues and a bit on the back make me
wonder if it could be molting or still just getting its seasonal, adult plumage. It's back was
also not as green as in Sibley's nor did it reach as high up on the neck.
Behaviors: Since I have never seen one I instantly knew it was a rarity and after quickly discounting that it was some coot in the rushes, I knew what it was from pictures--although my mind said "galliute."

Huge feet, it walked on floating vegetation, at times clinging clumsily to dead branches on a bush that has died in the recently-formed pond.
Call: I heard none.
Plumage: Slightly ragged

Similar Species Discussion

There is no bird in our area that is similar. I already said I thought of a coot at first because of where it was and how it was walking, but all similarities ended there.

Resources Used

I used Sibley's to confirm what I was sure of and to get the correct name and spelling.

Previous Experience

Minimal. This spring the most common water birds where I have gone are the white-faced ibis and the Wilson's Phalarope.
