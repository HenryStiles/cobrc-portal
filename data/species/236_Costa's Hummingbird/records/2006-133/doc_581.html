Habitat

Lawn with Locust trees, Pinion Pines, some plum trees, and blooming Russian Sage, Red Slavia, and Honeysuckle. Two hummingbird feeders were hung near window. A Black-chinned visited the same day.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10x42 birding binoculars 10x auto zoom camera lens
Distance:  varied from 8' to 20'
Light:  Good

Description of the Bird

-Overall greenish bird with whitish underparts
-Overall length about 3.5
-Appeared smaller and rounder with a thick neck
-Relatively short, curved bill
-Green Crown, whitish supercillium
-Short tail
-One photo shows wing length about the same as tail length (but may be slightly tucked under tail)
-Another photo appears to show wing length longer than tail
Behaviors: The bird fed longer at one time at feeder than other hummingbirds I've observed; it also buzzed over the blooming plants, but drank from the feeder.
Call: Not noted
Plumage: Female or Immature maybe molting

Similar Species Discussion

I have observed Black-chinned, Ruby-throated, and Calliope Hummingbirds in my yard. The immature and females have very similiar coloring. The Archilochus are a little longer and the Calliope is smaller. Mathematically measuring images in the photo, my engineering son helped me determine the length of the bird to be about 3.5".

Also in an email Tony Leukering observed from the photo the following: "Actually, there is a much easier way to determine if the bird is a BCHU or not, and that is the relative widths of the inner primaries compared to that of the outer primaries. The two species of Archilochus differ from all other North American hummers in this regard by having very narrow inner primaries with distinct differences between P5 and P7 (see Howell's hummer book), as illustrated in my comparison pic (attached).

Thus, your mystery bird cannot be a BCHU and....... I suppose that it has to be a COHU!
