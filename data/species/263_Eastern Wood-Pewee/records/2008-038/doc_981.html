Description of the Bird

the bird was a small flycatcher, much smaller than a kingbird, but somewhat larger than a Least Flycatcher I saw just before. It was identified as a Wood-Pewee by a combination of a very indistinct eye-ring, very long (compared to a an Empid) primary projection, by the dusky colored chest, the noticeably crested look, and dusky, indistinct wingbars. I did not see the bird well enough to notice whether or not the physical features favored one wood-pewee species or the other, only well enough to tell that it was a wood-pewee.
Behaviors: The bird was singing high in a tree, where it was hard to see. After it stopped singing, it moved off to where I could not see it. I heard the bird sing about a half-dozen times or so.
Call: The song, which was heard well a half-dozen times or so, was a a falling then strongly rising "pee-a-wee!", without the burry quality associated with Western Wood-Pewee song.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Empid species were ruled out by the much less distinct wingbars and eyering, the longer primary projection, by the darker overall color, especially on the chest, the larger size, and crested look. Olive-sided Flycatcher was ruled out by the evenly colored chest, without the darker vest, by the smaller size, and more compact shape. Greater Pewee was ruled out by the more compact shape and smaller size, and especially by the very different song. All species of Empid, and Olive-sided Flycatcher were ruled out by the song.

Western Wood-Pewee was ruled out by the different song, especially the strongly rising end and clearer quality, without the descending, burry component of Western's song.
