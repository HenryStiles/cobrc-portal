Description of the Bird

The most distinctive feature of this bird was its song, with rising inflections on the "Pee" and "wee." This is what first caught my attention as I was hiking along the nearby trail.

It had a similar size and shape to a Western Wood-Pewee (which was common in the area), with an erect posture, slender build, no eye ring, and buffy wingbars. This bird, however, had a complete, bright orange lower mandible, and pale whitish throat and breast. No dark markings were visible on the breast, as viewed in bright light.
Behaviors: The bird was very active, flying around a circle of 200'-300' diameter, perching 10'-15' up in ponderosa pines, singing once or twice, and then flying on.
Call: "Pee-wee" or "Pee-a-wee" song, repeated numerous times
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The obvious similar species was Western Wood-Pewee. I ruled out this species by voice, bill color, and throat color (in that order).
