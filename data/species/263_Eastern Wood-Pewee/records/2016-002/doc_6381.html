Description of the Bird

I observed a group of five Wood-Pewees for about half an hour.  They appeared to be a tight group.  When one would fly to another tree, the other four would follow.  They often perched close to each other.  They were quite active catching bugs.  As they became more comfortable with my presence, they allowed me to observe them at very close range.

The plain grayish appearance, weak eyering, long wings, and thin wingbars were typical for Wood-Pewees.  At least one of the birds -- shown in the attached photos -- had only faint wingbars and was presumably an adult with worn plumage.  At least two appeared to be juveniles with fresher plumage -- more contrast on both the underparts and upperparts with crisper wingbars.  Lighting in thick overcast conditions was poor, so I would not have been able to observe any buffy color on the wingbars if it was present.

The birds were identified as Eastern Wood-Pewees by voice.  The photographed adult was calling repeatedly with clear, high, upslurred whistles (recordings attached).  Briefly, two of the birds, including the photographed adult, called in the same way.  Later, three birds called simultaneously, one with a long clear whistle and the other two with short clear whistles.  It seems likely that all birds were Eastern Wood-Pewees since all calls heard during the long period of observation were typical of Eastern rather than Western Wood-Pewees.
