Description of the Bird

A wood-pewee by long wings (longer than any Empid) and bill shape and posing on an exposed limb in the open. Identified by song, but looking at my photographs the bird shows the lower bill mandible is mostly all orange with just a tiny dark tip. The bird was less vested (see photos) than the common Western Wood-Pewee that was absent from this site. The two whitish wingbars were prominent and contrasted with a mostly light gray body that had some hints of green.
Behaviors: Perched high in a cottonwood snag, up about 80 feet and then flew lower in a giant plains cottonwood that is living. Sang periodically which is how I saw the bird after being alerted by song. Stayed for up to a minute at a time perched in the same spot, then fly-caught and returned to same perch or one nearby. Disappeared for a few minutes when it moved to a living tree.
Call: singing - slow peee-a-weee with the weee drawn out. After a long pause it also then gave a down-slurred per-yer, also draw out. Repeated but long time before songs given, as much as 2-3 minutes.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Western Wood-Pewee can look almost identical. This bird was identified by its song, but the photographs show the differences with the two equally strong white wingbars, compared to one strong and one weak in Western W-P. The vest is weak unlike the very strong, darker vest in Western W-P. The light gray seems to be a character of EAWP like seen here and also was a character noticeable on last year's bird at Fountain Creek Regional, park, and compared to the darker brown tones in Western W-P.

Resources Used

None, but I read the article in Birding on separating the two wood-pewees and I learned about the vest contrast differences, plumage coloration differences and wingbar differences in that article.

Previous Experience

I lived for 30 years in Ohio where Easter Wood-Pewee was a breeder and I heard it through the summer months. Here, it is impossible not to at least hear Western Wood-Pewees all summer and into early fall.
