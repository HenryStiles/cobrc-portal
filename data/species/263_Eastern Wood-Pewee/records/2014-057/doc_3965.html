Description of the Bird

When I first observed this bird it was facing toward me and I noted based on it's long wings, slightly crested head without an eyering and behavior that it was a Pewee, however instantly felt that it was atypical for the expected Western Wood-pewee, as it showed almost no vesting to the front and was very pale on the entire chest. Upon closer examination I noted the bird also had an entirely orange lower mandible without the typical duskiness of WWPE. At that time the bird started flycatching and returning to the same general area so I was able to see the back and wings. The bird showed two clear and bright wing bars, nearly white in color, which was much brighter than I had seen in WWPE. The upper wing bar was nearly as bright as the lower one. I watched and photographed the bird for a bit, hoping it would vocalize on it's own, however to that point it did not so I played a recording of Eastern Wood Pewee. As a response the bird vocalized twice a nice clear PEEEE-A-WEEE that matched the recording for Eastern Wood-Pewee I had played.

Similar Species Discussion

Of course the similar Western Wood-Pewee was the first species and most difficult to eliminate since the species can overlap in traits. I found this ABA article discussing the two species quite useful:
http://www.aba.org/birding/v40n5p34.pdf and feel that my bird ticked all of the boxes for Eastern Wood-pewee.

Firstly the call was consistent with Eastern, with the timing and pitch being different than Western. Although my bird did not call very much it was a more drawn out call, without nasal-ness or buziness to it like the call of Western. The call was loud and clear.

The wing bars were very bright and contrasted strongly with the wing. The upper wing bar was nearly equally as bright as the lower wing bar, a feature supporting Eastern over Western.

The lower mandible of this bird was orange all of the way to the tip, without any duskiness.

The overall coloration of the underparts were very pale without a dusky cast or strong vested look.

And a trait discussed in the ABA article, but that I was not aware of while observing the bird is a slightly cocked tail giving Eastern birds a less streamline look than Western. Although not specifically noted in the field, a number of my photos do show this bird to have its tail cocked slightly downwards.


Willow Flycatcher and other empidonax were eliminated based on the long primary projections, call, bill size/shape, and lack of eyering.

Previous Experience

I have moderate experience with Western Wood-Pewee, Willow Flycatcher and the various Colorado empids. I have seen a few Eastern Wood Pewee in other states, but do not consider myself to be very experienced with them.
