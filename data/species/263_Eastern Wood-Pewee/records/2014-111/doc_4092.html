Description of the Bird

The bird flew from tree branch to tree branch, stopping each time to perch and sing. The song was a textbook version of this species' song, which I would characterize as Peeeeeeee-a-weeeeeeee, without the urgency on the first note that is typical of Western Wood-Pewee.

Similar Species Discussion

I considered Western Wood-Pewee and empidonax flycatchers. The bird I saw was a small gray(ish) and white flycatcher, with a peaked head, and two faint whitish wing bars. I ruled out empidonax flycatchers because this bird was fairly dark and had indistinct wing bars. Also, it was singing peeee-a-weeee, repeatedly, while perched. The song allowed me to eliminate Western Wood-Pewee and more to the point, I knew it was the song of EAWP. Before moving to Colorado 3 years ago I lived in Wisconsin for 20 years where EAWP is a very common summer breeder. The song is very familiar to me and distinct from Western Wood-Pewee.

Resources Used

I used my binoculars and the Sibley field guide. I also used iBird Plus recorded songs of Eastern and Western Wood-Pewee 20 minutes before I arrived at the site to make sure I could distinguish the songs.

Previous Experience

I've seen and more importantly listened to the songs of Eastern Wood-Pewees many, many times while living in Wisconsin from 1992-2011. It is a very common bird there in summertime. Since moving to Colorado three years ago I have observed many Western Wood-Pewees. I am still more familiar with the Eastern's song but both are familiar and distinctive.
