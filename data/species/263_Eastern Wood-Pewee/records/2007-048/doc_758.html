Description of the Bird

A medium sized flycatcher the size of Western Wood-Pewee which was present at the same location. The bird was almost entirely identified by the song. I did get a good scope view of the bill however from underneath, so the comments are about the mandible and the underneath parts of the mandible. The mandible was almost entirely bright orange, with a distinct darker tip, the was about 1/6 or 1/7 the lenght of the bill, i.e. only a tiny bit of the tip was dusky in appearance. The bird had two whitish wingbars, no eyering, a crest, a long tail and was seen many times in an upright posture. When visible, it was always perched on a dead stick, usually inside the canopy of a live tree, but at times near the top of a dead snag that still had most of its finer branches on it.

I did study the vest on the bird's breast and to me it was noticeably lighter than the typical CO Western Wood-Pewees. I saw its back briefly and it appeared lighter overall than the nearby Western Wood-Pewees.
Behaviors: Very active, but didn't see it actively foraging. Mostly changed perches every 4 or 5 seconds, returning after some time to the same tree, but never seemingly to the same perch. It sang on landing on new perches and the perches were as low as 3 feet off the ground (once) to over 60 feet high. Mostly perched from mid-storey to upper levels of the tree.

On four occasions the bird interacted with Western Wood-Pewees, whereas the Western would chase the Eastern off of the selected perch. The Eastern interacted on three occasions with the same Western, but it also interacted on separate occasions with as many as three different Western Wood-Pewees.
Call: Yes,

This bird sang, almost continuously for a little over an hour. It sang the classic Eastern Wood-Pewee song, i.e. a plaintive drawn out, "pee-a-wee" and about 10 percent of the time it also sang, always after the "pee-a-wee" song and then about 3 seconds later a downslurred, two-not "pee-you".
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

In the U.S., the other wood-pewee is a look alike but it has a totally different song and call. The Western Wood-pewees that were present were not singing or calling, except for one made a scold call as it chased the Eastern Wood-Pewee away from a perch.

Generally speaking the Eastern Wood-Pewee was lighter gray, especially on the light vest on the ventral surface, but also dorsally, the few times that I saw the back, it appeared to be lighter gray.

Additional Information

Comments: I was surprised to hear this bird at Chico Basin Ranch in August. I at first thought that maybe it was a European Starling doing an immitation, but then I watched the bird singing in my scope. I did look for overall coloration, but the song was totally distinct, and the bird never gave any other call, just the characteristic song. I tried the entire time to get a photograph but the bird never stayed on the same perch long enough.
