Description of the Bird

This was a medium-sized flycatcher with long primary extension, two wingbars, gray-brown above, pale below. The head was rounded but not peaked. The bird showed a very thin eyering, perhaps a bit thicker toward rear half. The upper mandible was blackish-gray and the lower manible entirely yellow-orange. The throat was white or very light gray, depending on the lighting. The breast was light gray-brown, forming a fairly narrow, not all that conspicuous "vest". The flanks were the same gray-brown as the "vest". The lower breast/upper belly was very pale, almost whitish, gray. The lower belly was very pale yellow. The upper wingbar was slightly less prominent than the lower. The three innermost secondaries ("tertials") were broadly, sharply edged with white. The secondary edges were very thinly marked with whitish-gray. The primary extension was much greater than would be found on any Empidonax, perhaps extending beyond the longest secondary by an inch. The upper parts of the bird (head, nape, back, and upper tail) were an even brownish-gray. The legs and feet were blackish.
Behaviors: This appeared to be an adult male "desperado" in search of a mate. It slowly, but fairly constantly, moved about Grandview Cemetery and adjacent City Park Nine Golf Course trees singing, seeking a mate, ranging widely over the course of over 30 days. If it had a preference, it appeared to be the golf course/cemetery interface about mid-way along the south boundary fence of the cemetery. It tended to stay in the mid-crown of mature, and to a lesser extent sapling, deciduous trees. When not singing, it was flycatching for millers and other flying insects, including wasps.
Call: Mostly gave the typical "PEEaweee!" song a few to several times a minute at all times of the day for a period of over a month. Occasional would give a downward "Peeuhh" note and other short, single-syllable notes difficult to describe.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The primary extension and large size ruled out Empidonax. Lack of rusty coloration rules out any of the smaller Myiarchus of similar size. Primary issue with this bird is separation from Western Wood-Pewee. All-pale lower mandible is a helpful character, which this bird displayed. Paler, crisp coloration of the underparts is helpful, which this bird displayed. Perhaps the constant, consistent giving of the typical "Eastern" pewee song was the best factor, and the calls also match those of typical "Eastern" pewees.
