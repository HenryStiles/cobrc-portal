Description of the Bird

flycatcher, larger than Empids, without an eyering. Long wings. Strongly crested. Gray appearing on dorsal view and face. Two EQUAL whitish wingbars. orange bill with lower mandible with only a faint dusky tip. Gray tail. Long primary projection. Underneath light contrasting with dorsal coloration. NOT VESTED. Back with a hint of greenish coloration. Bill wide and stout.
Behaviors: Perched in three favorite spots over edge of small pond. Fly-caught frequently often returning to the same choice of three perches. Low, many times below head height and as high as 10 feet above the water. At one time in a chase with another wood-pewee, I think the other was a Western Wood-Pewee, but it never stuck around for comparison.
Call: gave both its full song, PEE-a-WEEe, pause... peeooo and on occassion an upslurred pawee
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The only other flycatcher that is very similar is Western Wood-Pewee. Western's have two wingbars too, but their upper wingbar is noticeably fainter than the lower (Birding, 40: No.5 pp- 34-40).
Bill of Western is almost always dark tipped on lower mandible, sometimes more than half, never like this bird's bill. Western is vested on breast not like this bird. Western sings a very distinctive, burry breer, nothing at all like this bird's song.

Resources Used

Birding magazine article on how to separate the two wood-pewees - Birding40: (5) pp. 34-40.

Previous Experience

I spent my first 30 years in northeast Ohio where Eastern Wood-Pewee was common and where I learned its distinctive song. Western Wood-Pewee sings all summer in all riparian areas and is easy to learn.
