Description of the Bird

We never saw the bird. While conducting a Big Day with Chris Wood, Larry Semo, and Tom Schultz, we were listening to the marsh for nocturnal marsh birds. There were numerous Virginia Rails, Soras, and Black Rails calling and we were waiting for American Bittern, when Tom said something like, "Hey, isn't that King Rail." We all immediately agreed, as we'd all heard the bird, which, over the next few minutes, gave the call two more times.

Note: The website will not let me include a question mark in this field, so the Tom quote, which should have an ending question mark, has a period instead.
Behaviors: 'Singing' from marsh
Call: The bird gave the "male advertising call" at least three times -- a longish series of relatively low-pitched, hollow 'kek' notes (>9, <15 individual notes) with very similar inter-note intervals throughout the 'song.'
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

Clapper Rail has very similar vocalization, but typically varies the inter-note intervals in length (speeds up or slows down) and has generally shorter inter-note intervals. Virginia Rail has not call that is so 'monotonous' in pitch and tempo. Neither Sora nor Black Rail have a call similar to what we heard.

Previous Experience

I had heard King Rails about 6 times previously and have heard Clapper, Virginia, Sora, an Black dozens to hundreds of times, and have heard Yellow about 6 times, too.
