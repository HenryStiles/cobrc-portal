Description of the Bird

When I first observed this bird I was working my way up a ditch (approx 15 feet deep) which is filled with weeds. The ditch had been only moderately “birdy”, mostly with House Finch, American Goldfinch and a few common warblers and House Wren. At one point heard a regular interval chipping note coming from the ditch (unfortunately I am not good at birding by ear, but the chip note that I heard is not consistent with the species observed and most likely was being emitted by one of the other birds in the vicinity). With hearing the chipping I knew several birds were foraging deep in the willows, so I pished and almost immediately a small brown bird popped up on top of the weeds, where it sat in the sun for about 2-3 seconds, before dropping back down in the weeds. Not a long look, but the light was excellent and I was able to get a clear impression of the bird (discussed below) The bird continued to move down the ditch away from me over the course of several minutes, and I pished several more times, getting two additional views of 1-2 seconds in length. From that point the bird flew out of the ditch and into a Juniper Bush across the ditch and on a private residence. The bird appeared to land in the Juniper about 15-20 feet up. At this point I lost sight of the bird. I took one photo of the general part of the Juniper that I thought that the bird had landed, which is where the one photo submitted was pulled from (I was not even aware that the bird was actually captured in the frame until the next day).

 When the bird first popped up, my initial thought was that it would be a wren, as I had observed several House Wrens in that ditch, however very quickly I dismissed that as this birds build and color pattern (will be discussed following), posture and general behavior did not fit that of a wren. The bird seemed to fit better as a warbler. 

The bird was pale buff on its lower parts, however a moderately cool toned gray-tan, not very warm toned yellow-tan. The lower parts were unstreaked and fairly uniform, though slightly paler on the throat and lower abdomen. The upper parts of the back were a uniform and unmarked dark brown, absent of any wing bars or spotting. It had a very clear break between the dark brown on the wing and the buff on the flanks and chest. The brown extended up to the top of the head, which was also dark, but had a somewhat warmer red-tone to it. The bird had a distinct, but fairly thin eyeline extending from the lores toward the back of the head. The eyeline appeared to be dark brown, becoming somewhat more diffuse toward the back of the head (Artifacts of an extreme crop does not show this trait in the photo). There was a distinct pale supercilium, which was either off white or pale buff in color. The cheek was also pale, leading into the chest without a strong color shift. I was unable to see the legs and feet in the quick views that I got, however the one photo I captured shows them to be pink or flesh colored. At the distance I observed the bird the bill appeared grayish in color (not black is it appears in the photo). As I was looking at many things at once in a very short time period, I did not make special note of the bill size and length in my observations and it did not make an impression on my memory. The general outline was that of a somewhat stockier and heavier bird, certainly larger in size and heavier in build than the very familiar Wilson’s Warbler.

Throughout my brief observational time the bird appeared to be foraging in the lower portions of the weeds until it popped up with pishing. The tail did not ever project upwards like a wren. The bird did not vocalize at any time during my observation.

Similar Species Discussion

Various wrens were the first species to be considered in the field, however both my observations and what can be seen in the photo rule out all wren species. The bird appears to have pinkish legs, which no wren has. The bill shape appears to be straight, rather than curving downward. The bird did not project its tail upwards at any time during my observation (it seemed to mostly hold it still and straight out behind its body). The dark eyeline does not fit any wren particularly well. While the bird was somewhat heavy chested it was longer bodied and did not appear to have the short egg-like body shape of a wren.

Upon the suggestion of a more experienced birder that saw the photo, Dusky Warbler was considered, however experts familiar with both species said that this bird was a better fit for Swainson’s Warbler. Paul Lehman's comments regarding the bird when compared to Dusky Warbler were "Swainson's Warbler, not Dusky."  When asked why not Dusky he elaborated "Supercilium too straight and bold and pure whitish (no buff). Bill too long. Overall size of bird looks a bit too large/long. Upperparts with too much of a rusty/rufous/reddish tone (Dusky is truly brown). Dusky's sides, flanks, and undertail are gray-buff."

I posted the bird to the list ID Frontier, where a variety of people who were experienced with Swainson's Warbler voiced in that there was nothing about the photo acquired that would point away from that species and no additional species were a better fit.  Those familiar with Dusky said that it did not fit that species.  While the bill might appear slightly short for Swainson's Warbler in this one photo, it most likely is simply an artifact of the angle of the head in the photo and a severe crop from a large photo.

Resources Used

In the field I consulted iBird Pro.  Once home I consulted a variety of online sources including Cornell's website.  I consulted both my Sibleys and Stoke's Field Guides.  I sent the image on to Steve Mlodinow, who replied that he thought it looked like a Swainson's, but that he did not have much experience with the species.  The bird was posted on COBIRDS list, and later IDFRONTIER list.  People with extensive experience seeing Swainson's Warblers on the breeding ground replied back with confirmation that this bird looked good for that species and not for others.

Previous Experience

I am inexperienced with this species.  I had only seen photos and read about it.
