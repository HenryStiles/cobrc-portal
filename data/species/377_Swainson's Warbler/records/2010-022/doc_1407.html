Description of the Bird

The bird was an oddly-shaped warbler, very chunky and short-tailed, almost neckless, with a long very sharp dark bill. The overall jizz was that of an obese House Wren. The upperparts were a soft brown similar in color to the back of a Swainson's Thrush. There were no wingbars or markings of any kind on the wing feathers, coverts, back, rump, or tail. The underparts were paler overall, ranging from a yellowish cream in the center of the breast to a decidedly grayish color on the flanks. There was a dark thin line from the bill through the dark eye and a pale supercilium above that. The crown was rusty brownish, contrasting with the rest of the bird. There was a hint of a pale crown stripe at the base of the bill but it did not extend far back onto the crown. The legs were pink.
Behaviors: The bird foraged constantly on the ground, picking through wet leaf litter, sticking its bill under a leaf and then prying the leaf up by opening its bill wide. While it foraged, it often performed an odd "shivering" behavior that involved a very slight, very rapid shaking of the wings and tail. It didn't seem perturbed when doing this; it just seemed like a stereotyped foraging behavior, somewhat like the bobbing of a waterthrush. The Swainson's was remarkably tame and approached me within 5 feet; it was not disturbed by my movements and did not respond to pishing or Eastern Screech-Owl imitations.
Call: The bird was silent.
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

The Swainson's was really distinctive once seen well. No other warbler is similarly shaped. Worm-eating has a vaguely similar coloration but lacks the shape and the chestnut crown, and has a complete central crown stripe. Wrens would have barring in the tail and wings, and they would be shaped differently and act differently.
