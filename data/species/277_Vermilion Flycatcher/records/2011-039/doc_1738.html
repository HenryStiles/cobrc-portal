Habitat

Pasture with a few scattered saplings.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10 by 42 Swarovski and a Swarovski Scope
Distance:  16 feet, by near focus
Light:  Cloudy morning. It had rained earlier that day and it was quite cool. The bird was to my northwest and viewing conditions were very good.

Description of the Bird

Average size flycatcher, grayish back, off white throat and chest with light gray streaking, belly and undertailed coverts salmon colored. The head was slightly peeked. The eye was dark with a gray patch behind the eye which matched the color of the back. The tail was darker than the back and in flight showed some white in the outer tail feathers. The bill was small in diameter but about the lenth of other medium size flycatchers. Wing bars were only slightly noticable.
Behaviors: Flycatcher was sitting on a fence attempting to find food. After watching the bird for a minute or two at very close range I back up the car to increase my distance from the bird. The bird flew a good distance to the top of a elm sapling. I watched to bird in the scope for a few more minutes but the flycatcher was still when I drove back home. I did not see the bird catch any food.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult female

Similar Species Discussion

When I first noticed the bird I thought it may be a very early Western Wood-Pewee. When she truned around and I could see the pink belly I knew immediately it was not a WEWP. Say's Phoebe are common at this time of year but are larger, do not have streaks on a gray chest, dark gray throat and lack the white outer tail feathers I saw on this flycatcher.

Resources Used

I did look at a couple of field guides when I got home to try to determine the subspecies, flammeus or mexicanus. Sibley's and National Geopraphic gave me some clues but I remain uncertain as to the subspecies.

Previous Experience

I have seen Vermilion Flycatchers many times in most of the countries in the Western Hemisphere. I have had a lot of experience with most of the flycatchers in North America.
