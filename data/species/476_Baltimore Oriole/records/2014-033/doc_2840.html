Description of the Bird

The bird was a bit smaller and more slender than a Robin.  Both male and female Bullock’s Orioles were present at the same time, size and shape were the same.  The bird had a dark pointed bill, dark eyes, and black cowl over the entire head.  Orange feathers extended from the breast to the belly and undertail.  Legs were dark.  The upper tail feathers were orange toward the tips and black toward the rump.  When the bird flew one could see that the central retices were black all the way to the tips and the rump was orange.  Wings were black overall with orange extending into the median coverts.  Black greater coverts were tipped in white and some white streaking ran lengthwise through the flight feathers.

Similar Species Discussion

Among possible black-cowled orioles are Scott’s, Audubon’s, and Orchard Oriole.  Scott’s and Audubon’s Orioles and similar in size but are black and yellow, rather than black and orange.  Scott’s Orioles breed on the Utah border of Colorado and Audubon’s orioles would be extreme long distance vagrants from Mexico and The RGV of Texas.  Orchard Orioles are smaller and with black and rusty brown breast and belly.  They breed in eastern Colorado and there are a handful of Orchard Oriole records in western Colorado.  All three of these species can be eliminated by plumage.  The most likely similar bird would be a hybridized Bullock’s x Baltimore Oriole.  These are found frequently in eastern Colorado where both specie’s ranges overlap.  In fact, a first spring male BUOR x BAOR was seen and photographed at these same feeders on 5/18/00.  Hybrids should show less black on the head, much more white in the median coverts and flight feathers.  Hybrids would not have the clean orange tips to the outer tail feathers as well.  It too is eliminated by plumage.
