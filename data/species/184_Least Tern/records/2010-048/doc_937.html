Reporter:  Eric Defonso  2950 Golden Eagle Cir,   Lafayette, CO  80026
E-mail:  bay.wren@gmail.com
Other Observers:  none; I was alone when I observed the bird.

Species, Date, Time and Location Information

Species:  Least Tern
First Date/Time:  6/7/2007
Last Date/Time: 
Duration (total time in view):  3-5 minutes
County:  Jackson
Specific Location:  along the levee embankment at Meadow Creek Reservoir, along County Road 30 east of CO 14 near North Park.
Number:  1
Age:  Unknown
Sex:  Unknown
Plumage:  Other/Unknown

Habitat

Large, open reservoir, on a levee embankment. The watershed below the dam has fairly dense willow thickets, and above the dam on the hillsides is a mix of evergreen forest and sagebrush scrub.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Binoculars - Swift 8x40s, and my Kowa TSN-824 spotting scope, for initially observing at a distance.
Distance:  I estimate ~ 75-100 feet at closest approach.
Light:  Fairly low overcast - I had encountered wintry conditions when passing over Cameron Pass 10-15 minutes earlier. At the time of the sighting, the ceiling was probably several hundred feet. However, the bird is distinctive in size and plumage, so light conditions weren't much of a factor.

Description of the Bird

Small (killdeer-sized), with gray wings, white underparts, a noticeably yellow bill, and a black cap with a white forehead mark that at a distance can look like a partial eyebrow stripe.
Behaviors: I came across the bird as I began to drive onto the reservoir. At a distance of a few hundred feet, I got my scope out and IDed it. It was sitting on the road, facing into the wind which was coming from the NW or WNW. I drove a little closer, and photographed it. It did not move. I drove even closer, and carefully approached it, but eventually it flushed. It flew out over the water, and then angled northward, flying with some gulls away from the reservoir. It did not return in the short time that I remained in the area.
Call: none
Plumage: definitive alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Other terns, like Forster's, Black, Common, or Bonaparte's. Most similar to Forster's in overall coloration, but much smaller, and bill was bright yellow as opposed to orange. Also, distinctive white forehead mark present that Forster's should never show. Similar in size to Black Tern, but Black Tern should never show coloration as light as this bird, at any age or stage of molt.

Very small size for a bird of that body shape, as well as the white forehead mark and bright yellow bill distinguish it from any of the aforementioned terns.

Resources Used

Sibley Guide to Birds in North America. Used after the sighting.

Previous Experience

I had been to the Gulf Coast of Florida 2 months earlier, and had observed and photographed Least Terns repeatedly at close range (mostly at Fort DeSoto County Park). I also repeatedly viewed other tern species on that trip, like Royal, Sandwich, Forster's, Caspian, and even Gull-billed, providing many opportunities for comparison.
