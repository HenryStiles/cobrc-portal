Habitat

Ornamental grasses (including a very tall pampas grass) around the feeder area. Old apple tree, various landscaping shrubs and lots of lilac bushes around the backyard, too. Other tall deciduous trees in the neighboring yards plus numerous blue spruce in our front yard and in other nearby yards.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  REI 10x32 binocular

Fuji FinePix S9100 camera
Distance:  Approximately 30 feet
Light:  Sun was about due south with the bird on the west-southwest side of the feeder. Sky was mostly sunny.

Description of the Bird

Between pine siskin to house finch sized. Had a distinct red crown patch. Beak was short, conical, yellow. Dark, grayish patch on face around the beak. Eyes appeared black. Wings and tail feathers very similar to pine siskin or gold finch: dark and light streaked. Tail feathers appeared to spread out further than a pine siskin's. There was no indication of any eye patch or bar.

First impression was that I may have spotted some sort of sparrow (e.g., chipping or American Tree sparrow) or even a Cassin's finch. After comparing descriptions and pictures of such birds in my Stokes Western Region field guide and American Bird Conservancy field guide with my photographs, I noticed the redpoll (most likely the common variety) was an exact match for my bird despite being out of its normal range.

Several local birding experts (e.g., Ken Pals, John Drummo) came by and confirmed the identification of the bird in my photograph.
Behaviors: Bird fed at my one of my nyjer thistle seed feeders and appeared with many pine siskins and a fewer number of house finch. Stokes field guide stated redpolls feed at thistle feeders.
Call: Not heard.
Plumage: Brown streaked, red crown patch

Similar Species Discussion

Pine siskins (relative size and wing and back feathers although it looks like the redpoll's tail feathers are broader). No yellow streaks in feathering like a siskin. Beak was yellow and short, conical in shape; not sharply pointed and dark like a siskin. No eye bars like chipping or American tree sparrow; nor did it have any rufous coloring like the sparrows. The red patch on the crown was very distinct and bright. Beak color and size not comparable to a house finch.
