Description of the Bird

(My response to Tony on 1/04/13 after the CORE report was flagged on E-bird. Tony forwarded this to Rachel and she requested that I submit it to the records committee as well.)
Hi Tony-
As you already know, this is a big year for Common Redpolls in Colorado &
elsewhere south of their normal winter range. In the Roaring Fork Valley
the first was reported by Jim Conway in West Glenwood on Dec 1st. My bird
was here on the 10th and on the morning of the 11th. Redpolls were reported
at the Aspen village trailer park (Pitkin) on 12/20 (1), 12/24 (1), 12/27
(15), & 12/29 (Aspen CBC 6). I had another redpoll at No Name on Christmas
Eve and Robin Henry in Glenwood had three birds on 12/27. Linda Vidal had
four birds eating niger on New Years Day, seen by myself, Dick Filby, Robin
and Kendall Henry, and Linda of course. Quite an invasion so far!
On all occasions the Common Redpolls at No Name were associating with House
Finches and eating black-oiled sunflower. Smaller than the House Finches and
similar in size and shape to the Pine Siskins and American Goldfinches that
are regular around here in winter. Distinctive makings were the yellow
bill, black "soul patch" under the bill and the red crown. The birds on
12/10 and 12/24 had a pink wash on the breast and strong streaking on the
flanks and whitish wing bars. The redpolls could not be mistaken for Pine
Siskin, Lesser or American Goldfinch because of bill color, red crown and
the black "soul patch" They could be confused with Hoary Redpoll (highly
unlikely vagrant and never seen in Colorado) but all of the redpolls I have
seen this season have had strong streaking on the flanks, those at No Name
included. That would definitely eliminate Hoary Redpoll.
Sorry I didn't get a photo of the No Name birds, but I know that Filby
photographed the birds at Linda's house. Have a great New Year!
Tom

Since December I have had no other redpolls visit feeders here at No Name. I did go twice with Filby and Levanton to Kremmling in January 2013 (-17’) where we observed 100 – 200 Common Redpolls on each visit, and two pale individuals, one highly suggestive of Hoary Redpoll. After studying the photos and asking for outside help (Leukering, Mlodinow) we decided the bird was another Common Redpoll. I also visited the Discovery Museum in Fort Collins on 2/09/13 where there were about 200 Common Redpolls. One individual among the pale birds was extremely pale, clean flanks and UT coverts, with a tiny bill, most likely one of the probable Hoaries reported there. Common Redpolls are still being seen in our area although I have had none. The past weekend twenty were reported in Eagle and they are coming daily to a feeder near El Jebel.
Behaviors: Associating with other feeder birds
Call: Did hear vocalizations
Plumage: Adult Basic
