Habitat

Feeder near spruce tree, with other cover available nearby. This feeder is somewhat awkwardly positioned to provide shelter from hawks that patrol our yard daily; that also makes photography there very difficult.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  None necessary; birds were just on the other side of the window.
Distance:  4 feet
Light:  Partly cloudy but very good light.

Description of the Bird

Small finches

Short bill

Red cap, much less extensive than the read on a housefinch

Pinkish wash on breast

Notably white below, with streaking only on sides of breast

Dark chin patch
Behaviors: On two different occasions, redpolls were seen eating nyjer seed at a finch feeder outside our kitchen window.

(The feeder is positioned behind a broad frame between the two halves of the window, which prevents kitchen users from startling birds but, when combined with the wavy glass of a century-old house, makes photography almost impossible.)

We only saw two at one time and one at another time, but we are rarely home in the daylight hours at that time of year. That feeder is very popular and birds cycle through it rapidly, so I have no idea how many there actually were. They seemed to be staging in a large, dense spruce tree with house finches and a few goldfinches. There is no really logical redpoll habitat nearby.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Winter

Similar Species Discussion

House and Cassin's finches have streaked breasts and larger bills, as well as differently shaded breasts and much larger red caps.

Red crossbills are darker, with dark beaks.

Kinglets are smaller, with dark breasts and red not reaching to the bill.

Resources Used

Kaufman, Sibley, Nat Geo, at the time of sighting. We also had been paying attention to Internet reports of redpoll sightings in Colorado.

Previous Experience

We have numerous house finches and Cassin's finches at our feeders, as well as occasional crossbills. Redpolls were occasional visitors to our feeders when we lived in Montana.
