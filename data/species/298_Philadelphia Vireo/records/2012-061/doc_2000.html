Description of the Bird

At about 4:15 pm at Burchfield SWA, the rain had ceased. I was at the edge of small wooded pond (similar to that at Last Chance), pishing, and a vireo came in. I was immediately struck by yellow on the throat and chest as well as some down the sides. As I studied the bird, a second vireo came in, a Warbling Vireo. It had a bit of yellowish wash on the upper (anterior) sides, but otherwise was gray. It was larger billed, flatter headed, and a bit longer tailed than the Philly, giving it a rather different overall structural appearance that could be seen without bins (this was noticed separately, and commented upon, by Nick Moore).

I watched the bird for about 2-3 minutes before calling Nick over. He arrived about a minute later, and we watched the bird together for another 2-3 minutes. The birds were mostly overhead at a distance of 15-20 feet. The sky was overcast, with the light somewhat filtered through the canopy of cottonwood leaves.

This was a vireo along the lines of Warbling/Red-eyed. It had a whitish supercilium bordered above by a dusky line (as in REVI, but not nearly as bold), a dusky transocular stripe (from bill to just behind the eye), and a diffuse pale arc just below eye (as opposed to a crisp partial eyering). The bill was relatively stout compared to that of most warblers (ie, not thin and sharp like a Tennessee or OC Warbler), but the beak was distinctly (but not dramatically) shorter and a bit thinner than that of the nearby WAVI. The throat, chest, and upper sides were yellow. Depending on the bird's position (depth of shade, angle of incoming light, etc) this yellow varied in appearance from a faint wash to appearing moderately prominent. The flanks/lower sides never appeared yellow. The undertail coverts were similar in color to the chest. The tail was a bit shorter than the nearby WAVI. The forehead was more rounded, not as flat. The upperparts and wings were plain, without streaking or wingbars. The precise color of the upperparts was difficult to ascertain due to lighting and angle. The back looked vaguely olive, the crown grayish. At no point did the Warbling Vireo look even vaguely as yellow beneath as the Philly.

Similar Species Discussion

Identification: Structure eliminates all similar warblers. Of course, the most worrisome confusion species is Warbling Vireo. The yellow beneath (and more importantly, distribution thereof) was typical for a spring Philadelphia Vireo (fall birds tend to be brighter). Warblings that look yellow beneath due to reflected light do not consistently show the pattern described in my experience; such birds tend to look yellow on flanks/vent or simply diffusely yellow. In addition, the structural differences, though subtle, were consistently distinctive in this direct comparison. Finally, the dusky lores and dusky upper border to supercilium should preclude the ID of Warbling Vireo.
