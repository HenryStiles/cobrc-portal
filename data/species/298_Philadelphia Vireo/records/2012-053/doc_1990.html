Habitat

Mixed habitat of deciduous trees and shrubs, with the bird having used both a taller deciduous tree and then primarily a shorter, shrubbier tree perhaps 12-15' tall.

Description of the Bird

Somewhat stubby (shorter/rounder) appearance as compared to a warbling vireo, but roughly the same size. Thick, rounded, slightly hooked bill (easily noted in comparison to the sharp bill of a probable female Nashville warbler seen in association with the vireo). Medium-gray cap, well defined white supercilium extending back and forward of the eye, with dark eyeline (approximately same hue as the cap) extending to and including the lores. Undersides mostly a very bright yellow with some mixed white, with the throat and undertail coverts being particularly bright yellow, the belly showing a more mixed yellow/white, but without any sharp distinction between those areas. Mantle and wing coverts a grayish olive, wings slightly darker, and without visible wing bars. Somewhat short-looking tail, possibly due to more prominent/longer undertail coverts.
Behaviors: The bird was foraging in close association with a warbler, 5-10 feet above the ground in a shrubby tree, having dropped down from a somewhat taller adjacent broadleaf tree. Active, but not as much so as the warbler, and with more pauses. I did not observe any tail wagging/dipping or other body-specific movements. Movement pattern while foraging was what I considered to be "classic vireo," with no particularly unique movements that I observed.
Call: Not singing or calling.
Plumage: Adult breeding

Similar Species Discussion

Warbling vireo and Bell's vireo were the two leading alternate identifications, with red-eyed/yellow-green and Cassin's/blue-headed vireos being more distant considerations. In the end I did not find this to be a particularly difficult identification, notwithstanding the rarity of this bird in Colorado, but did study the bird carefully with attention to multiple field marks.

At first naked-eye sighting I had expected the bird to be a warbler, like the bird it was near. However, upon first getting my binoculars onto the bird (just a couple of seconds after taking my first look at the warbler) I realized that it was a vireo -- this determination apparently being a rather subconscious one based on overall appearance and my familiarity with warblers and vireos, though I did look specifically to the bill, which was fairly short but thicker, more rounded, and slightly hooked than a warbler bill. I also very quickly thought the bird was a Philadelphia vireo, based I think primarily on head shape and pattern and the very bright yellow undersides, but more as an overall impression than based on the field marks per se. Given that I was neither expecting nor thinking about vireos generally, let alone Philadelphia vireos in particular, the fact that my initial impression upon getting my binoculars focused on the bird was that it was a Philadelphia vireo I think is worth considering, though not definitive.

However, given the lengthy, close-up, unobstructed observation I had of the bird, I also had time to carefully look at specific field marks of the bird that distinguished it from other vireos. I eliminated a warbling vireo identification based primarily on the dark lores and bright yellow throat, though the impression of a somewhat shorter, chunkier bird, with more rounded head, also played a role -- as did the overall very bright yellow undersides, which significantly exceeded the brightness of any warbling vireo I have ever seen, including the many warbling vireos I have seen in the eastern U.S. (Looking later in the Sibley field guide, I noted that even the "bright adult eastern" plumage warbling vireo is not nearly so bright as the bird I observed, and has the brightest yellow on the sides, as opposed to this bird, which was bright throughout the undersides, but particularly on the throat and undertail coverts.)

Bell's vireo was eliminated as an ID based on the overall rounder/stockier shape of the observed bird, bright yellow throat, darker cap set off by the more extensive white supercilium, more defined dark eyeline, and the absence of any visible wingbars during an observation in which the bird was seen clearly in several different postures and from many different angles. (I also don't recall ever seeing a Bell's vireo that was not vocalizing, but I did not consider this to be of any significant import.) The absence of noted tail-flicking behavior in the observed bird was also of interest relative to Bell's vireo, though again not something upon which I placed much weight.

As for other less likely vireo IDs, the overall shape of the observed bird was very different (shorter, more rounded) from that of red-eyed and yellow-green warbler, the bill on the observed bird was much shorter, the head much rounder, and the eyes black, not red (I have never seen red-eyed vireo at this short range that did not show a red eye). Though the yellow-green vireo does show substantial yellow on the undersides, the level of brightness and the degree of yellow on the throat of that bird also falls short of that seen on the observed bird, and it lacks a well-defined dark eyeline, not to mention that it would be considerably more rare in Colorado than even a Philadelphia vireo.

The absence of wing bars, "spectacles,"and an extensively gray head/face, and the presence of the bright yellow undersides and specifically the throat, on the observed bird distinguished the observed bird from Cassin's or blue-headed vireo.

I did not immediately look to compare the observed bird with any warbler species. However, if compared to warblers of even roughly similar plumage (albeit very different bill length/shape, and overall shape), the observed bird would be distinguished from orange-crowned warbler by more clearly-defined dark cap and white supercilium, lack of white eye-arcs or ring, and the presence of very bright yellow throat and undersides. The bird would be distinguished from female Tennessee warbler by very bright yellow undertail coverts and throat, and lack of greenish upperparts and wingbars. The bird would be distinguished from Nashville warbler (the probable identity of the bird seen in association with this bird) by the presence of a white supercilium and dark eyeline and the absence of an eye ring. I am quite familiar with all of these warblers and am confident the observed bird was not any of these species (or any other warbler species).

Resources Used

I felt fairly confident identifying the bird without reference to other sources, but nonetheless did consult The Sibley Guide to Birds after getting back to my vehicle, within 20 minutes or so of the observation. The "bright adult" plumage Philadelphia vireo depicted there is consistent in every way with the observed bird.

Previous Experience

This was only the third Philadephia vireo I have seen, both of the others having been seen outside of Colorado. But over the last 20 years of birding in Colorado and all around the country (particularly in the last two years, much of which was spent birding in the eastern U.S.) I have seen and personally identified all the ABA vireos and close to all of the ABA warblers, including many, many dozens of warbling and "solitary" vireos, numerous red-eyed vireos, and several bell's, white-eyed, gray, yellow-throated, and Hutton's vireos. Though this was the only vireo I came across in the LCC woods on this trip, elsewhere on this same trip I did see and identify warbling vireos, plumbeous vireos, and a Bell's vireo, none of which looked much like the bird I observed at the LCC woods.
