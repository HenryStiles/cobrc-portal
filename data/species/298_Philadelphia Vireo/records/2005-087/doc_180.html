Description of the Bird

While birding at Bonny Reservoir, I stopped at a hedge of Red Cedar, Siberian Elm, and Eastern Cottonwoods along the north shore of the reservoir. I intended just to stay in the car and pish for a minute to see if anything was present. While pishing, an Orange-crowned and Wilsons Warbler popped out, along with a Brown Thrasher. I also noticed a bird that was seemingly larger than a warbler foraging within a Siberian Elm so I got out of the car. The bird was indeed slightly larger than most warblers, had a short, thicker bill, was stockier than a warbler, and foraged very slowly through the leaves. The crown was gray, but slightly tinged with olive. The upperparts were a grayish, olive-green. There was a dull grayish white supercilium and a darkish streak through the lores, eyes and slightly beyond the eye. There was a lightening of gray beneath the eye as well. The wings and tail were a dark grayish brown with olive edging and there was no discernible presence of wingbars. The breast and flanks were pale yellow. The throat was a whitish, pale yellow. The eyes were dark and the legs seemed bluish gray.
Behaviors: Foraged slowly through treetop
Call: None heard
Plumage: Basic

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was obviously a vireo. Those vireos that do not possess diagnostic wingbars include Gray, Yellow-green, Red-eyed, Black-whiskered, and Warbling. Gray can be eliminated by overall coloration and lack of facial pattern. Yellow-green and Black-whiskered, which would both be extremely unusual in Colorado, have more distinctive head marking and are larger with larger bills. Red-eyed Vireo is larger with more distinctive head markings. The pale yellow instead of white breast of the subject bird eliminated Warbling for the most part, except Warblings in fall can show extensive yellow from beneath. The main separation between the two is that Warbling is longer, lacks the distinct dark streak through the eye, and generally has more extensive supercilium. The Philadelphia Vireos thicker, blunter bill and slightly larger size eliminated Tennessee and Orange-crowned Warblers, which have slender, more pointed bills besides other differing field marks.
