Description of the Bird

Dan and Mark had pished in a nice little flock of birds that weren't sparrows, our first such flock of the day, at about 8 am. I came up behind them as they were pretty much finished with the flock. As they wandered off in search of new prey, I started pishing. I had a steady trickle of new individuals arrivimg, so kept at the pish, squeak, owl-imitation bit.

After about 5 minutes (truly), an Orange-crowned Warbler came into view followed shortly by a vireo. The vireo was entirely clean yellow beneath, brightest on chest and throat (I'd rate as moderate intensity there). There was no streaking or other marks below. The head showed a gray crown, pale yellowish supercilium, dark line through eye (so lores dark). There was a weak pale crescent beneath the eye, not nearly as marked as that of a Warbling Vireo. The bird was relatively small billed for a vireo, but the bill was still hefty compared to that of the OCWA. The bird lacked wingbars. Back color not seen well.
Behaviors: Feeding ever so slowly 10-25 feet up in cottonwood and other trees, loosely associated with RCKIs, OCWAs.
Call: not heard
Plumage: not determined

Similar Species Discussion

The stubby bill and relatively stocky build (compared with vast majority of warblers, not other vireos), eliminates warblers for the most part. In particular, OCWA and Tennessee Warbler have a thinner bill, lack vague arc under eye, and for OCWA, have some smudging on chest. OCWA does not so well defined gray crown and at this time of year, neither should Tennessee.

Now, down to business. REVI would be much larger billed, less yellow, bolder face pattern. Warbliing Vireo would not be totally yellow underneath and does not show throat/chest as brightest area. Additionally, the dark lores would eliminate WAVI. The bill looked to diminutive for WAVI as well, and the arc under eye too subdued
