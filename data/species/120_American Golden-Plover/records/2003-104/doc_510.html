Description of the Bird

the bird was a large Plover, about the same size as a Killdeer (one of the few shorebirds nearby). The bill was black, fairly short (for a shorebird), and thin but blunt-tipped. The legs were fairly long, and black.

The bird had a bold white eye-line, a darker cap with some gold-spangling, and a fairly dark (not white) face. The back was dark gray/brown, with bright gold spangles visible at close range. The underparts were mottled gray. In flight, the underwing was plain grayish-brown, with no black. The tail and rump were the same grayish-brown as the back, with some gold spangles. The flight feathers were blackish.

The wingtips were fairly long, with four primaries visible sticking out beyond the tertials.
Behaviors: the bird was foraging on the shore of the lake, and was fairly tame. When pressed, it would fly to the opposite side of the lake.
Call: When flushed the bird was heard to give a sorrowful sounding "chweedle" call, fairly high pitched and fast. When I listened to the call of American Golden-Plover immediately after the observation it was a perfect match.
Plumage: juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than Golden-Plovers were ruled out by the plover shape and Killdeer size, gold-spangling on brownish-gray upperparts, mottled gray underparts, etc. Black-bellied Plover was ruled out by the bright gold spangling on the upperparts, the uniform brownish-gray underwings without black "wingpits", the uniform rump and tail color with no white patch, and by structural charateristics.

I spent a lot of time ruling out Pacific Golden-Plover. The main plumage characteristic I used was the four primary tips extending beyond the tertials, a mark I saw very well. The color on this bird was extremely bright for American Golden-Plover, and closer to that of Pacific Golden-Plover. The call was heard very well, and matched that of American rather than Pacific.

Resources Used

Sibley and National Geo were consulted in the field to rule out Pacific Golden-Plover. A song CD, either Stokes or Peterson, was consulted to confirm that the call was AGPL

Previous Experience

I have seen many American Golden-Plovers previously, and had studied some of them closely. I have only seen on Pacific Golden-Plover previously, and studied that one extensively.
