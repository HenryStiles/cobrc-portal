Description of the Bird

A large tern, significantly larger than the many Black Terns on the Reservoir, with much slower wingbeats, and really notably long, thin, pointed wings. Tail noticeably forked, though long outer rectrices, if present, could not be seen. Very dark on back, essentially black; very clean bright white below, all over on underparts and underwings, except underside of dark primaries & secondaries showed through on underwings, creating a two-toned pattern. Other than this, it was a completely 2-colored bird. Extreme darkness of upperparts could be judged well on many occasions when the birdd banked away against a dark background below the horizon. On these occasions no white was visible on the tail, rump, upperwings, back, nape, or crown. At one poitn the bird came halfway across the lake and scope views were much better. During this time, when it banked, i could see the spread tail well; no white was visible. I could also see the white forehead at this time only. Views of the uniform upper/underparts were good.
Behaviors: Bird was on the wing the entire time we watched it; it usually stayed high above the water, diving towards the surface only a couple times and never making contact that we could see. Its flight style was a little steadier than that of most of the other terns on the lake, with much slower wingbeats than the Black Terns.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was way too dark above to be a Bridled Tern. There was no contrast between the colors of the crown, back, and rump. No white was visible in the spread uppertail. Many Black Terns were present for direct comparison; the Sooty was totally uniform sooty-black above, and clean white below; it was also distinguished by its size and flight style.

Previous Experience

I have seen Sooty Terns in large numbers on the Dry Tortugas, but only once, in 2001.
