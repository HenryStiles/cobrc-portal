Description of the Bird

Ths is the bird first located and identified at Holbrook Reservoir earlier in the day.

It was a very large tern, identified as a tern by the the tern-like jizz that includes quicky jerky flight over water, small dagger-like bill, pointed wings.

The bird was dark gray on the dorsal surface. including the back, top of head (except the forehead) bill and tail. The undersides with white except for the dark contrasting primaries, giving the bird a Swainson's Hawk-like wing coloration underneath. The bill was all dark. The front of the head, between the cap and the bill was very contrasty white. The face was white with a noticeable dark eye"lore" extenting from the dark ey to the base of the bill, which was seen only at closest range (at least by me.)

The breast was imaculate white, turning slightly gray posteriorly. The feet were never visible. The tail was FORKED, and was dark both on the dorsal and ventral surface. Although I knew to look, I didn't look for the white edging to the tail. The bird did not have a light hindcollar as in Bridled Tern.
Behaviors: This bird was a very strong flier, flying mostly 50-80 feet above the water, but on at least two occasions it made dives to the water surface, but never plunge-dived, rather it seemed to pick something from the water surface. It flew non-stoop for about 30 minutes and then seemed to disappear.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The only Western Hemisphere tern that is close in appearance is Bridled Tern. The group watching the bird noted that the CO bird did not have a pale gray nape, as found in Bridled, rather it was blackish from top of head down to its back. It had a blackish back, significantly blacker than would be seen in Bridled, which is gray-brown backed.

Resources Used

Afterwards, I read about Sooty Tern in Handbook of the Birds of the World, Volume 3, and from the Online Birds of North America from Cornell U. I also looked at Terns of Europe and North American by Olsen and Larsson and compared it to Bridled Tern the only other look-alike.

Previous Experience

I have seen thousands at very close range at Bush Key, Dry Tortugas, but that was over 15 years ago. I have only seen a few Bridled Terns the same year.
