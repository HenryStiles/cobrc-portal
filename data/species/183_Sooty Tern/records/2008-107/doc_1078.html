Description of the Bird

A large tern (much larger than the many black terns flying around and looked even larger than the nearby Sterna terns) with pointed, long, narrow wings. This tern had a solid BLACK upper surface to the head, nape, wings, back rump, and tail. There was no contrast between any of the upper surface coloration (e.g. the cap was the same black color as the back.). A noticeable constriction or narrowing of the black at the nape could be seen when the bird banked steeply.
Undersides were: breast, belly, flanks, and undertail coverts were white
Underwings were: white flight coverts; entirely dark primaries; secondaries were white with black tips giving the bird a trailing dark edge to the secondaries.
Forehead was white and could be seen when the bird was flying directly towards us.
The white forehead was separated from the white chin and underside by a thick, black loral stripe between the eye and lower mandible.
Tail was definitely forked but not deeply so (the notch of the fork was withdrawn perhaps 1/3 of the tail length)

Relocation scenario:
The bird was first found at Lake Holbrook by Mark Peterson, Brandon Percival, Brad and Brian Steger. The bird was last seen leaving Lake Holbrook to the northwest. As several of us were independently driving down we'd periodically called each other with the latest status (bird hadn’t been seen for more than two hours and was not seen at Lake Cheraw by the original finding group). Since Glenn would be arriving first he checked Lake Henry. Then Steve, Glenn and I met at the north shore of Lake Meredith at the south end of Lane 21. After starting our scanning we noticed Black Terns and sterna terns (Common, Forster’s and Least). Shortly after arriving Steve Stachowiak located a large, dark winged tern and got Glenn and I on it. BINGO!
Behaviors: This tern was seen mostly flying along what looked to be the southern and eastern shoreline (I say “looked to be” because I could not tell how close to the opposite shore this birds was). Several times the bird flew to the middle of the lake and we marveled at the great views we were getting. The bird’s wings flapped with strong relatively shallow wingbeats. The bird was seen flying into the wind and at times made excellent headway dispite the wind. When flying with the wind this birds speed was INCREDIBLE! The bird was never seen to “plunge dive” like other Sterna terns but rather drop to the water’s surface and possibly pick something off the surface and then fly back up high over the water. From the distance we were observing from I could not tell if the bird actually picked up any food items from the surface.
Call: No vocalizations heard
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

All regularly occuring Sterna terns (Common, Forster's, Least, Royal (regular?) have light to medium gray upper wings not BLACK like this tern.
This bird was LARGER than surrounding Forster's and Common Terns.
Only Least Tern has a white forhead in adult alternate plumage, but Least Tern is smaller than Forster's and this bird was larger than Forster's.
Black Tern & White-winged Terns have black bellies in alternate plumage. Wings are gray not black. Black Terns are much smaller than nearby Common Terns and much, MUCH smaller that the Lake Meredith tern.
Brown and Black Noddys have brown or black undersides (throat, chest, breast, and belly).
Black Skimmer - Adults are indeed black backed and winged but have orange bills.
Bridled Tern - Bridled has white bases to the underwing primaries whereas this bird had ALL dark underwing primaries. Bridled Tern does have darkish wings but not "black" wings like the Lake Meredith bird. Lastly the black crown of the Bridled Tern contrasts with the back and wings whereas the bird I saw was uniformly blackish on the crown, nape, back, and wings.
