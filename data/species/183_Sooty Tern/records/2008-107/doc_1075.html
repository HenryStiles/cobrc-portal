Description of the Bird

the bird was a large tern, significantly larger than nearby Black Terns, but smaller than nearby Franklin's and Ring-billed Gulls. The overall shape was typically tern like, with long, thin, pointed wings and long thin body. The wings were proportionally longer and thinner than those of Black Tern, giving the bird a more slender look. The tail was long and deeply forked. The bill was also long and thin, and completely black.

The entire upperparts of the bird were black or very, very dark brown. The bird had a black nape and crown; the entire top of the head was this color except for a white forehead, which was separated from the white underparts from a dark eyeline extending from the dark cap to the bill.

Then entire underparts of the bird were clean, bright white. The wing linings were also white, contrasting quite strongly with the dark brown/black flight feathers. The tail was the same dark brown/black color, with no white visible at the distances at which we were viewing the bird.
Behaviors: For the entire observation the bird was flying over the water, usually quite high (almost always higher than Black Terns on the lake). The bird flew with deeper wingbeats than that of Black Tern, and with a more direct flight. Occasionally it would dive towards the surface, but was never observed plunging into the water.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Most other tern species were ruled out by the large size and very dark back. Black Tern was ruled out by the much larger size, much darker back, longer wings, different flight style, etc. Bridled Tern was ruled out by the darker upperparts that was the same color throughout, without any contrast between the nape and back or crown, by the darker tail without visible white, and by the darker flight feathers from below that contrasted very strongly with the wing linings.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted before the observation for clues on how to rule out Bridled Tern.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Sooty Terns on the Dry Tortugas, though it had been many years previous to this sighting. I have only seen one Bridled Tern, from shore and at a distance, and am not terribly familiar with that species.
