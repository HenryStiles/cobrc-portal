Description of the Bird

A large tern with strikingly dark upperparts and mostly white underparts. Entire dorsal surface (body feathers, crown, and upper wing and tail surfaces) appeared black with the exception of small white forecrown area. When banking the tail was seen to be deeply forked. If the tail had white edging it went unobserved at the distance and optical conditions I was operating under. The bill appeared long and all dark. A dark line extended from the bill through the eye, separating the white forecrown from the white cheeks and throat.

The underparts appeared to be essentially white including undertail, vent, belly, and throat, with white extending up to the cheek. The legs, tucked in flight, were unobserved. The wing lining was white, with a dark (almost black) trailing edge and broad black wingtips. The underside of the tail was not observed from my observing angle as I saw the bird from the side, from behind, and banking away, showing its dorsal aspects.

The wing was habitually held with a pronounced kink at the wrist, more distinctly exhibited than on other terns I've observed.
Behaviors: The bird was seen in flight twice, foraging on Lake Meredith. I saw it drop to the surface once, after which it flew to a gull/pelican/tern roost across the lake and landed, lost in the crowd and air distortion.
Call: not heard
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Incredibly wingy, strong, buoyant flight style eliminating gulls and other waterbirds. Large for a tern- wingspan approaching Ring-billed Gulls and noticeably slower, stronger flight cadence than other terns present, especially in comparison to Black Terns. Black (or nearly black) dorsal aspect eliminates most other terns. Black Tern much smaller, adult alternate plumage has black belly and all black head. Adult basic Black Tern not as black-backed, lacks black bar from eye to bill. Juvenal Black Terns not as dark backed, lack black bar from eye to bill. All Black Terns lack deeply forked tail, have much faster wingbeat, show much less distinctive kink in wing at wrist. Direct comparison was available with several Black Terns that were also present at the lake that day. Bridled Tern can show similar white forehead, but are not as dark backed. Also, underwing trailing edge of Bridled Tern not as contrasting and underside of Bridled Tern wing tip not as extensively black. White dorsal tail margins very wide on Bridled Tern, which was not observed on this bird. Brown & Black Noddy all dark, without white belly, lack deeply forked tail, etc. Black Skimmer has dark upperparts and white upperparts, but vastly different bill proportions, different flight style and wing proportions, no bar separating white forehead from throat and cheeks, and not as deeply-forked tail.

Additional Information

Comments: We scanned from dawn, so I had several hours of calibrating on gulls, Black Terns, and distant Sterna sp. terns, so when the Sooty Tern crossed my field of view the first time I was confident I had it even though it was distant and backlit. I couldn't get my companions on it at the time, though, before it landed amongst a large roost of pelicans, gulls, and terns, losing itself in the crowd. Fortunately, it flew by again about 45 minutes later, affording scope views for a couple of minutes as it travelled to the west and away from us, finally going out of view behind a point of land. As far as I know this was the last observation- I suspect the warm water-loving bird had finally warmed up in the sun after a cold, rainy night and foggy, misty morning and decided to leave for points unknown.
