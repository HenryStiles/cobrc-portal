Habitat

Backyard of planted and native trees and shrubs including Russian olive, Juniperus sp. and other unknown vegetation. Lots of already dry cheat grass, Bromus japonica, on hillsides all around. Water drip and water feature, hummingbird and seed feeders abundant.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Leica 77mm APO scope with 32x WW lens.
Distance:  about 100 feet
Light:  We were facing west and the bird came in from the east and seen by us looking south and then at the feeders viewed briefly looking at the tail illuminted by the low angled sun.

Description of the Bird

This was a stoinking male Larry's Goldfinch, identified by black crown and bib, contrasting strongly with a bright yellow breast and upper belly. The gray back in turn contrasted nicely with smart canary yellow edgings on lesser and greater wing coverts, as well as the yellow edging to the bird's primaires. Gray on flanks and nape as well as white undertail coverts. Very distinctive white pattern in the outer rectix as visible from my photo, alone distinguishes this as a Lawrence's Goldfinch, separable from both American and Lesser Goldfinch.
Behaviors: When seen, first perched on top of Juniperus sp. and then landing on thistle feeder. Unfortunately the bird moves behind the thistle feeder where only its tail and undertail coverts are visible. Feeds for 45 seconds.
Call: yes, bell-like tinking heard on two occassions.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

This is a comparison to the tail pattern only between male AMGO, LEGO, and LAGO which can be used to identify this bird as an adult male.


LAGO, see photo, has long tapered R6, worn tip with long subterminal white spot covering more than 2/3 of the undersides of R6. See Pyle, Identification of North American Birds, Part 1, page 685, Figure 348

AMGO - male adult AMGO has much smaller white R6 spot that becomes a terminal white spot that is significantly shorter, less than 50 percent the length of R6. See Pyle page 687, Figure 349

LEGO male has yellow, not WHITE undertail coverts (see photo that shows this bird has white undertail coverts - tail pattern somewhat similar to LAGO, but pattern of white somewhat shorter, and proximal edge of white interrupted by spike of black, see Plyle page 684, Figure 347
