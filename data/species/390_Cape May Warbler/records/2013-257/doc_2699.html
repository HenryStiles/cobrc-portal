Description of the Bird

See photos (one field mark not shown all that well in the photos was a yellowish rump, usually only visible when the bird was in flight).

As for behavior, the bird was almost exclusively foraging on large numbers of gall-making psyllids (Pachypsylla celtidismamma and P. celtidisvescula).  Adults of these two insects were emerging from nipplegalls and blistergalls, respectively, which occur on Northern Hackberry (Celtis occidentalis).  These adults typically emerge from galls in autumn and overwinter under bark of the host hackberry or nearby trees.  In this case, Colorado Blue Spruces flanking the hackberry were being selected for overwintering as much or more than the hackberry, and the Cape May spent the majority of the time I watched it inside a spruce crown (that is, mostly out of obvious view) calmly snapping up literally thousands of tiny adult psyllids.  The bird was seen in the hackberry (northeast corner of Grandview Cemetery's Section E) and was also seen on the day of discovery in the top of a spruce 100 meters to the sw foraging in and around cones.

I never heard the bird make a sound.

Similar Species Discussion

See photos

Most obvious field marks not shared as a set with look-alike species were: yellowish rump; light orange face patch behind the eye; very narrow, long stripes all across the breast and onto the flanks.

Previous Experience

I have seen dozens of Cape May Warblers in the East, four previously in CO (1982, 1992, 2010, and 2012).  Not a lot of experience with non-breeding plumage, however.  I have lots of experience with the look alike species (although more in spring plumage than non-breeding).
