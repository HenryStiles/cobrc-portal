Description of the Bird

     The gull had a white head with a black, heavy, vertical slash mark behind the eye. Another back, thinner (and smudgy) line was directly above the eye. The bill was black in color, except for a very small dull orange tip. (I thought that the base of the bill may have been slightly orangish also.)  The upperparts were almost entirely dark gray. (A white scapular crescent and white-tipped secondaries contrasted with the gray color.) The folded wing on the resting bird was black with several white apical spots visible. The underparts were white. The legs were dark red. In flight, I noticed the underwing coverts were all light in color. The underside of the primaries appeared darker with black wing tips.  The underside of the secondaries were darker than the underwing coverts.  I estimated that the gull was about 50% longer than a Killdeer that stood beside the gull briefly.

Similar Species Discussion

Since I was not expecting to see a Laughing Gull, I first was elimininating Franklin's Gull and Bonaparte's Gull.  Although Franklin's Gull's upperparts would have been a similar gray and it's also a small gull, I knew that an adult Franklin's in non-breeding plumage would have a partial hood.  To some extent, the head pattern on the gull sighted more resembled a Bonaparte's Gull's head pattern.  Bonaparte's Gull (adult in non breeding plumage) was quickly eliminated since it has a very petite bill, the back is a lighter gray, the folded wings are black without white apical spots, and the leg color is pink.  About this time the bird took a short flight.  The underwing ruled out Bonaparte's Gull - the underwing of the bird didn't have the characteristic wedge of white on the leading edge of the wings nor the black line on the trailing edge of the outer wing feathers.  The underwing pattern (which I described in the last section) in combination with the other field marks was diagnostic for an adult Laughing gull in nonbreeding plumage.

Previous Experience

Although I was not expecting to see Laughing Gull in SW Colorado, I have seen many on the Atlantic Coast.  Locally, I see Bonaparte's and Franklin's Gulls.  My notes of the sighting were written immediately after the sighting while I was still out in the field.
