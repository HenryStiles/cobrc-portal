Description of the Bird

On two consecutive days a small group of birders looked at the TNC's Fox Ranch for Sprague's Pipit(s).

The first afternoon, Bryan Patrick and I, walked in the former pipit habitat. The first birds we saw were three small sparrows that exploded out of the dense grasses, almost underfoot. To me the birds did not resemble anything I was familiar with. Both of us agreed they were short-tailed sparrows that showed considerable buff or orange/buff on their dorsal surfaces.

The next day our ranks increased to four birders and the number of these unknown birds increased to a minumum of 6 individuals (10 more likely). Ted Floyd immediately identified them as LeConte's Sparrows. After repeated observations, only seeing them in flight and only seeing the dorsal surface, I could see they all had short spiky tails, an orange rump and a buff back. I never saw their heads or ventral surface.
Behaviors: Skulkers that stuck tight to the grass clumps until almost being stepped on. Usually flushed from about 10 feet away, but two birds flushed from as close as 4 feet. Flew very low and directly away. Furtive describes them best. We looked for them where they landed and they were flushed a second and third time some distance 20-40 feet from where they seemed to land indicating they landed and walked. Flight were anywere from 60 to 150 feet before landing again.
Call: non given
Plumage: unkown

Similar Species Discussion

The three other Ammodramas sparrows we considered were Grasshopper, Henslow's, and Baird's Sparrows.

In flight, GRSP shows a buff gray back without the orangy rump
In flight, HESP shows more reddish in the wings and back and the rump would be darker orange
In flight, BAIS shows darkish rump with whitish contrasty braces on its back. These birds didn't have the white braces and the rump was orangy.
