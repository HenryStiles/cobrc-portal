Description of the Bird

Multiple birds were observed. At least 6 occupied one meadow, and there may have been 10 or more in one or more meadows.

All were seen briefly and poorly, but some were seen diagnostically. A photo was obtained.

All were seen in flight, some flushing from distances of less than 5 feet, probably less than 3 feet.

Key marks seen in flight: distinctive orange rump contrasting with darker and browner upperparts, including tail. Tail short and spiky. Brownish upperparts generally seen briefly and poorly, but on a few birds, back streaking ("braces") were evident.

Overall small size, short-distance flights, spiky tail, and furtiveness were significant points observed.
Behaviors: See above.

We saw many sparrow species on the morning of observation, and the Le Conte's Sparrows were notably furtive, compared even to congeneric Grasshopper Sparrows. They would flush up almost underfoot, then fly above the grass short distances (25-100) feet, then put down again.

They flew low and direct over the grass.

They did not call as they flushed, and that is a significant observation. The potentially similar Savannah Sparrow typically gives a flight call or "chip" note on flushing, but these birds did not. A few of the Grasshopper Sparrows that we saw also gave flight calls, but, again, the Le Conte's Sparrows were entirely silent.

All the birds we were certain of (6 total) were in the same field, consisting of tallgrass-prairie forbs and grasses (Bouteloua, Erigeron, etc.). I believe this microhabitat information is relevant to evaluation of this report.
Call: None heard. (Which is significant; see below.)
Plumage: multiple birds; mainly adults?

Similar Species Discussion

1. Savannah Sparrow. This species is small, short-tailed, and sometimes furtive. However, it usually flushes higher, it often calls when it flushes, and it can often (not always) be coaxed in for a view. Flight style is different, too. Savannah Sparrow has a more buoyant flight, often with some twisting and turning. These birds, instead, flushed very directly, staying tight above the grass, then dropping out of sight. In terms of plumage, Savannah Sparrow would not show a distinctive, contrasting orange rump, and the "braces" on the back would not be quite as distinctive.

2. Other Ammodramus sparrows. The only other Ammodramus likely to be seen in any number would be Grasshopper Sparrow, which is ruled out by its rump being concolor with the rest of the upperparts. We flushed at least 5 other Grasshopper Sparrows that morning, so we had good, fresh, comparative memory/experience. Even rarer Ammodramus sparrows would not likely number 6+ in Colorado, and they would not show a contrasting orange rump with otherwise fairly colorless upperparts (e.g., ochraceous on Baird's, rusty on Henslow's).
