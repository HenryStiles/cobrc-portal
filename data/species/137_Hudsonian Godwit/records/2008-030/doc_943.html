Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized shorebird, noticeably smaller than nearby American Avocets, but noticeably larger than nearby Lesser Yellowlegs. The bird had a very long bill, fairly thick at the base and tapering slightly to a blunt tip. The bill had a slight but obvious upturn. The basal half of the bill was a reddish-orange color, the distal half was black. The legs were black.

The overall color of the head was gray, contrasting with the darker back and underparts. The crown was darker gray, with black streaks, and the bird also had a darker gray (though paler than the crown) eye-line, which contrasted with the pale gray supercilium, face, and upper neck. The upper neck also had some faint darker gray streaks, darker towards the lower neck. The back of the bird was very dark gray, with small, pale buffy dots. The underparts were a dark brick red, with faint dark gray streaks towards the upper neck and faint black bars on the flanks. The undertail coverts were white, with strong black bars.

The primaries, when folded, came to a point just beyond the tip of the tail. In flight, the flight feathers were dark gray, and there was a faint white stripe at the base of the flight feathers. The wing linings were black, contrasting with the gray color of the flight feathers underneath (in the photo I have of the wing lifted the flight feathers appear white, which is an artifact of the bright sunlight on the feathers). The tail was white at the basal half and had a bold black patch at the distal half.
Behaviors: The bird was observed feeding and loafing at the edge of the water. It would feed by rapidly walking around looking down, and occasionally dipping its bill quickly into the water. The only times I saw it fly was when it was flushed by a raptor, and it would quickly return to the area it had just left.
Call: none heard
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Nearly every other species of shorebird was ruled out by the size and the brick red underparts, as well as the long, bicolored, slightly upturned bill. Dowitchers were ruled out by the larger size, very different bill, different feeding styles, and very different underwing and tail pattern. Black-tailed Godwit was ruled out by the gray neck and head, different color of red on the underparts, black and gray underwings, and black and white tail. Bar-tailed Godwit was ruled out by the proportionally longer legs, gray neck and head, black and gray underwings, black and white tail, and strongly bicolored bill.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted to rule out other godwit species

Previous Experience

I have only seen one breeding plumage Hudsonian Godwit before, but I have seen a few non-breeding plumage birds before. I have never seen either Black-tailed or Bar-tailed Godwits before.
