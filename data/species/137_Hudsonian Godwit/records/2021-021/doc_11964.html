Description of the Bird

First observed around 9:15am or so in the far southwest corner, in the rain and relatively dark conditions due to low clouds. At first I thought they were Long-billed Dowitchers because they were hunched over and probe-feeding dowitcher-style in a clustered group. But I noted they seemed a bit tall and lanky even when hunched over. They then flushed briefly and flew a short lap before resettling where they were, and while in flight they called, and I noted that it wasn't even close to LBDO calls - more like kek-keks instead of high-pitched chittering. I then figured they were Marbled Godwits, especially when I noted their slightly decurved bills, which still were not easy to discern in the suboptimal light at that distance. I did start to entertain thoughts of HUGO when I saw what seemed like a rich rufous belly coloration and relatively light, striped head on one of the birds, and then another, but I was still reluctant to call them that in these sketchy conditions. Finally, the birds flushed again and this time I followed their flight in the scope. They circled around and I could see the rufous bellies of some individuals more clearly, as well as wing striping. They landed on a somewhat nearer spit as a group, and in doing so all their tails flared out clearly showing the dark terminal band and white tail base, totally unlike Marbled Godwit. It wasn't a case of trying to single out one HUGO, they were ALL HUGOs. I was floored, and then had to arrive at a precise count. I then notified the Boulder birding community ASAP and prepared for the birder deluge. My tallies for other species then suffered as I remained totally fixated on these exquisite and hyper-rare visitors.

Similar Species Discussion

Long-billed Dowitcher - as mentioned in the description, these birds were taller and lankier while foraging in the water. Also, while they did probe-feed, the manner of feeding motion wasn't a perfect match to LBDO - less mechanical, more deliberate. And of course, when in flight, they lacked the lower back stripe typical of any dowitcher.

Marbled Godwit - No brownish or buffy tones seen in any of the individuals. Females appeared gray, not brown, and were similar sized to the apparent males. Males had rich rufous or ruddy bellies, dark upperparts, contrastingly light heads and necks, and all birds in flight had notable wing stripes above and beneath, and while foraging contrasting white undertail feathers and coverts. The dark terminal bands could also been seen on birds while tipped over foraging. MAGO never shows any of these characters.

Previous Experience

This is only my third sighting of Hudsonian, the prior two having been one at Duck Lake near Fossil Creek Reservoir in Larimer County in 2006, and the other at Union Reservoir in Weld County in 2008. I've had many experiences observing Marbled Godwits and Long-billed Dowitchers in the past decade, no fewer than 39 and 44 occasions reported to eBird respectively.
