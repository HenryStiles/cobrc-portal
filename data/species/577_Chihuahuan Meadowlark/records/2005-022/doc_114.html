Habitat

Fairly tall, lush grassland among sandy, yucca-filled hills. The Eastern Meadowlark territories seemed strongly tied to this taller grass, where yuccas were scarcer. Photos of the habitat submitted separately.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski 10x42 binocular and Swarovski STS80 scope with 20-60x zoom
Distance:  as close as 100 ft
Light:  Excellent, birds mostly to my south or west

Description of the Bird

One male seen was identical to Western Meadowlark with the following differences: 1. malar region was distinctly ENTIRELY yellow. 2. cheek was very sparsely streaked. 3. tail was extensively white, with outer two rectrices (r5, r6) entirely white, r4 being mostly white with razor thin line of brown along outer margin of outer web at tip about 1/4 in to 1/8 in in length, and with r3 being mostly white with fairly broad, squared off, rectangular patch of brown along outer web for about one-half in at tip of rectrix; 4. Back, covert, and tertial feathers apparently paler with narrower bars. Also, by analyzing photos, I have found that the rump feathers appear to have dark centers on Easterns, pale centers on the Westerns.
Behaviors: Males singing from perches (e.g., yucca stalks) in grassland. Females carrying food quietly, seen when perched on yucca stalks or shrub tops.
Call: Two males singing continuously, heard in different directions at the same time. Song was invariably a high, fluty, "Wheeee Seee-ooo seee". Did not hear call notes from either bird. Song very different from staccato, warbling, songs of Western Meadowlark.
Plumage: breeding plumage

Similar Species Discussion

Western Meadowlark eliminated above, with song as one of major indications along with the described plumage differences.

Resources Used

I am thoroughly familiar with the vocal differences between these two species.
