Similar Species Discussion

The sound recordings confirm this as a "Pacific" Winter Wren, which I believe is the rarer of the two types in Colorado, and which is a probable future split by the AOU Checklist Committee. The call notes of Pacific and Winter wrens are actually very different: Pacific's is toneless, consisting almost entirely of noise, and recalling the call of Wilson's Warbler, whereas Winter's call is much more tonal, with a nasal component, and strikingly resembles the call of Song Sparrow. The accompanying recording is terrible, because there was a very noisy Red Squirrel right next to the wren and a train moving through the area at the same time. However, the spectrograms confirm the identity of the caller as a Pacific Wren. I am attaching a spectrogram comparison file; the Gregory Canyon recording is at the top, squirrel and all. Immediately below it is a recording of a Pacific Wren from California. To the left is a Ruby-crowned Kinglet "chittit" call and to the right is a Wilson's Warbler call, which is actually the most similar-sounding species. Note that some of the Wilson's Warbler calls resemble Pacific Wren calls on the spectrogram, but Pacific Wren is still a better match for the Gregory Canyon bird -- note particularly the much more prominent chevron shape to the Wilson's Warbler call (with a more pronounced upslurred component at the beginning of the call) and the fact that Wilson's Warbler concentrates its loudest frequencies (the darkest parts of the spectrogram) much lower than the Pacific Wren: from 8 kHz all the way down to 3, rather than from 8 to about 5.5 as in the Pacific Wren.

Resources Used

A few weeks before this encounter, I wrote two articles on identifying Winter and Pacific Wrens by ear, which I posted on my blog. The one about call notes can be found at http://earbirding.com/blog/archives/774. In preparation for that blog post I had, in recent weeks, listened to every single online cut of Pacific and Winter Wrens at the Macaulay Library, as well as reviewed my own and Andrew Spencer's recordings of both species.

Previous Experience

I have encountered both Pacific and Winter Wrens countless times while living in Oregon and Massachusetts, respectively. I have recorded the call notes of both species.
