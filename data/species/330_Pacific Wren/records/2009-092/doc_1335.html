Description of the Bird

A small Wren with a small dark bill and a very short, persistently upwardly cocked tail. The entire bird had a rich rufous coloring. The supercilium was buff colored and prominent, yet low contrast with the rest of the head. The chest was also buff colored and nearly the same tone as the remainder of the bird.
Behaviors: The bird was scurrying on the ground in dense thicket, rarely flying up more than 6 inches from the ground cover. The bird responded to pishing by hopping up higher on small twigs where it remained briefly (< 10 seconds) before continuing to forage along the ground. The bird was located by following its calls, but the call frequency (calls per minute) was low, although not measured. At one point, the bird approached a group of nearly 10 bird watchers to a distance of nearly 2 feet and was close enough to me that I was unable to focus my binoculars.
Call: The bird was initially located by Nick Komar by its Warbler like call. We followed the calling bird until Nick was able to visually identify it. We quickly realized that the bird was not a Warbler but a Winter Wren. This Warbler-like call most resembled a chip note from an Oporornis or Wilsonia Warbler and was unlike the chip note from Song Sparrow.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

House Wren is excluded due to the very short tail and short bill. House Wren would have a more indistinct supercilium and a whiter or paler throat.

Sedge Wren is ruled out due to the lack of white streaking on the top of the head and back. The habitat and date of observation are also inconsistent with Sedge Wren.

Rock Wren is larger and considerably more gray.

Winter Wren (Troglodytes troglodytes hiemalis) is excluded by call note and general appearance. The call note of himalis is similar to Song Sparrow, while the call note of pacificus is similar to Wilson's Warbler. Additionally, the color of the supercilium and chest is grayish-white in hiemalis, while it is buffier and more rufuous in pacificus. This difference leads to the lower contrast, yet distinct supercilium and lower contrast chest/back color difference.

Resources Used

Phone conversation while the bird was present with Nathan Pieplow helped with the call identification. After observation, recordings of pacificus and hiemalis present on the website www.xeno-canto.com were of additional help. The Sibley Guide to Birds and the article Toews, D. P. and Irwin, D. E. "Cryptic speciation in a Holarctic passerine revealed by genetic and bioacoustic analyses". Mol. Ecol., 17:2691-2705, 2008. were used as a guide to subspecific differences.

Previous Experience
