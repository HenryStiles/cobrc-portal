Habitat

Bear Creek in set in spruce/fir forest incised in a canyon and the wren was there.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  binos and scope, the best money can buy.
Distance:  as close as five feet.
Light:  bright sunny day but at times the wren was in the shade along the creek.

Description of the Bird

Wren would sing from the top of a spruce tree and then come down to the creek and carry moss and disappear under a dirt bank only a few feet from me as I was sitting next to the creek. The creek was noisy so I could not be certain if the song was Winter or Pacific Wren. The finder of the wren does have a voice recording and other birders took photos.
The wren had a very short tail which was always cocked at a right angle to its back. The chest was rich brown and the belly was barred below as was the tail.  I am attaching two photos that will show these field marks and its rather dark back.

Similar Species Discussion

Due to the split, Winter Wren is the most problematic species. Winter Wren is not as rich in color on the chest and is lighter brown overall. House Wren has a longer tail and does not hold it in such a cocked position for long periods of time.
I can not eleminate a hybrid Winter/Pacific Wren. The wren is a real outlier as far as know range for either species. Upon a later visit the wren was still present but did not visit the possible nest site. No one detected a second wren so it appeared to be a male building dummy nests in hopes of attracting a female.

Resources Used

We used many sources from our library. We read literature pretaining to the split, Pacific/Winter, to find out if hybrids are possible in our area.

Previous Experience

Of course we are very familar with our local breeding House Wren. The song was not House Wren. Brenda and I have traveled many times to the west coast. We have been to Alaska twice as well as western Canada and Vancover Island and the Pribilof Islands and Aleutian Chain. In those travels we have seen hundreds of Pacific Wrens. The Winter Wren is also a species we have seen many times in eastern US and Canada. In Europe on several occasions we did see the "Wren". We are very familar with the song of the Winter and Pacific Wrens but I did not really pay that much attention to the differences between the two species because at that time there was only one species.
