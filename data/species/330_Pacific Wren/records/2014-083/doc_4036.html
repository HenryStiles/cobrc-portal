Description of the Bird

Jeannette and I were hiking up the Bear Creek Trail when a long, lovely song echoed from the nearby trees. We stopped dead in our tracks, and heard a "stub-tailed" wren. Not expecting to find one in the area, we took careful notes and attempted a voice recording using an iPhone. It was observed only briefly a top a spruce on the far side of the small ravine, but heard to sing consistently over a 10 minute period. Although "Winter Wren" immediately came to mind due to the complexity and duration of the song, the tone was not what we are used to (WIWR is a common breeder in our area). The very fast delivery, somewhat higher-pitched, buzzier, and arguably "less musical quality was noted. At first, the bird was singing relatively short bouts of several phrases, but the length of the overall performance was perhaps equal to or even a little shorter than WIWR.  However, shortly before we deaprted, the bird increased the length of the flourish, making each bout of singing noticeably longer than typical for WIWR. We came to a very tentative conclusion of "Pacific Wren" after comparing the bird's song to the samples of PAWR on the Sibley app on our phone. It was close to a "perfect match."  Although our experience with PAWR is limited - and most of mine is with the unique Pribilof subspecies - we are both well versed in the variation of eastern Winter Wrens.  That being said, we fully understood the variation in the songs of these two (or more) species. We made some general notes about the bird's location. Rushing water and light  winds made it challenging to hear the entire song, and make a recording using  the "Voice Memo" app for the iPhone 4S. 

Surprised by our sighting, we checked the range maps in our Sibley app, and noticed neither WIWR nor PAWR were expected anywhere near here. We promptly texted D. LaPuma, who was at Camp Colorado, to ask if he -  or any of his co-councelors - knew of the status of PAWR in Colorado in the  summer. His response, received as we were returning downhill, was simply "nonexistent in summer and rare in winter."

Meanwhile, during our hike, Jeannette and I discussed the bird, and the status and distribution of the two species.  We decided to be prudent and more thoroughly document the bird, and the bird's location.

We returned to the original area at about 12:50pm and promptly heard the bird singing once again, and remained here for about 20 minutes. During that time, the bird sang consistently from the tops of several tall spruces on the opposite bank. I attempted to obtain photos of the bird using my iPhone held up to my binoculars, and we made more extensive recordings and additional written observations:
- small wren with short, stubby, often up-pointed tail.
- although lighting was poor, and overall color tones and details were impossible to determine, we independently noted the lack of contrast between the throat of the bird and the rest of the body - the bird appearred almost concolorous. Even with our views in our light, we would have expected to have been able to see the often-considerably-paler and more contrasting through of WIWR.
- no other visual details were noted.

Voice: as noted above. After reading description in the Sibley app, we agreed with the "rapid, mechanical, and buzzy" description which was more accurate and better-worded than our description (see above). We did make an attempt to entice the bird closer for study and recording by playing the app as loud as we could, but the bird showed no reaction to it - with fast-flowing water between us and the bird, and the realtively low volume of an iPhone speaker without an external speaker device, this was not surprising.

Upon returning to town and data service, we emailed T. Leukering and T. Floyd to discuss the observation and inquire about the best approach to disseminating the information and documenting the bird.

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wren was the only similiar species, vocally or visually.  Comparison included in description of Step 4.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

Extensive experience with WIWR throughout much of its breeding range, especially in New England. PAWR limited to one mid-summer visit to Oregon 2-3 years ago and Jeannette had experience with the species from point count surveys in Northern California nearly 15 years ago. Derek was a guide on St. Paul Island in the Pribilofs of Alaska for three seasons, most recently in 2003. In other words, we both have limited pertinent experience with PAWR.
