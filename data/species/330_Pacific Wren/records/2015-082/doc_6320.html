Description of the Bird

Presumably same bird found by Andy Bankert on 3 July; same location and meets same description. It was foraging and singing along the opposite bank of Cony Creek as you approach the Calypso Cascades, both at 7:30-7:45 and again 9:45-10:15. By pacing, it seemed to be about 130 yards downtrail from the main Cascade bridge, along a stretch where the creek is narrow and especially where there is a large downed spruce sticking out from the other side. I had some good (though brief) looks at it from as close as 15-20 yards as it emerged onto perches to sing, and also heard it pretty well (though the creek noise was a problem). I also tried to take video with my iPhone, but it turned out to be no good.

I felt that everything I observed substantiated Andy's observation of a Pacific Wren. The bird was overall pretty dark, lacking in contrasts, and the face and breast in particular were a uniform rich cinnamon-brown color; if the throat was lighter, it was only slightly so. A thin buff supercilium was the only obvious lighter marking. There was little or no patterning (mottling or barring) that I could see anywhere on the foreparts including throat and breast. The back and wings were dark brown but did not contrast much with the brown underparts. With the overcast conditions, the dark background of the forest, and the bird occasionally out in the open, I felt I was getting a pretty true read on the colors I am describing here (i.e., no intensification by sunlight).

The song, very complex, was a mix of trills (often three) preceded and separated by a few high single or double notes, and concluding with a cascade or jumble of notes. The effect was pretty, but the trills were very rapid and "rattly" or even buzzy -- a term my wife agreed with as we listened together. It was hard to be sure of hearing the full song pattern, but it did seem different from one song to the next. It was neither as musical nor as constant in form as that of the bird I heard there on 2 July 2014, which I felt sure was a Winter Wren and documented as such; that bird had three young in the vicinity. (I continue to believe that bird was a Winter.) This time I also think I heard the "chit-chit" call (as opposed to the harder "kep-kep" of a Winter), but I couldn't be sure given the river noise.

NOTE: This bird was originally reported from 20 June by Jeff Connor and Mike Britten; Mike got a video of it singing and sent it to Dave Toews, the researcher (or one of them) who studied the wrens in the contact zone and proposed the split, and also authored the BNA account for Pacific. He replied to MIke on 7 July, endorsing the bird as Pacific on the basis of both appearance and song.

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wren:  See account. Rich cinnamon-brown of face and breast, with no pale throat apparent and no apparent barring or mottling on foreparts, are all good for Pacific Wren, as is the song that I described, being more rattly/buzzy and less melodic than a Winter's song, but also more varied.
