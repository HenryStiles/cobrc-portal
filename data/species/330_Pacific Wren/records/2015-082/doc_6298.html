Description of the Bird

The bird was a very small stub-tailed Wren which always held its tail nearly upright.  It was somewhat larger than a House Wren and its song was clearly not a House Wren.  I first saw it on the morning of July 4th by following its song, but I only got a very poor and brief look among dark roots of trees.  I returned on July 10th with Maggie and Alec, and the bird was singing when we arrived.  We saw it very briefly on the trail and then followed it first upstream and then downstream.  I obtained very good looks for about 30 seconds twice when it was not too low in trees, and poorer looks at other times.  The bird was almost uniformly a dark brown with the most obvious marks being a lighter (but absolutely not white) eyeline, and very small whitish spots on the upper wing coverts and along the flanks.  The upper breast and throat were slightly lighter than the rest of the bird, but nowhere near white in color: this was seen well the two times I saw it in good light for about 30 seconds.  The bill was dark and the legs were brown.  The attached photo by Alec Hopping is a very good image of what I saw on the bird.  I heard the bird singing on both visits, and the song sounded less rich and sweet than I remember for Winter Wren and European Wren.  Sound recordings were obtained by both Kathy Minh-Dunning and Alec Hopping.

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wren is not nearly so uniformly brown as this bird.  It has a whiter eyeline and a much lighter breast and especially throat than this bird.  Also the song did not sound like Winter Wren.  Kathy's recording was sent to Nathan Pieplow who wrote back that by sound and frequency he thought it to be a Pacific Wren.

Resources Used

Looked at field guides and pictures on the web, plus listened to Pacifc and Winter Wren songs and calls on Nathan Pieplow audio web pages.

Previous Experience

Have seen and heard Pacific Wren a few times in California and Washington.  Good experience with Winter Wren both in Colorado and the east USA.
