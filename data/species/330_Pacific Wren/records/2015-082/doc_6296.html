Description of the Bird

On my way to Pear Lake in Wild Basin on July 9th, 2015, I swung by Calypso Cascades to see about the probable Pacific Wren (PAWR) initially reported as a “stub-tailed wren” by Jeff Connor on the RMNP Solstice Bird Count on 6/20 and then eBirded and reported as PAWR by Andy Bankert 7/3 and Bill Rowe on 7/7.

Knowing that photographs are often not enough to separate PAWR from WIWR reliably, my goal was to obtain audio recordings in addition to pictures.

I first heard the song of a stub-tailed wren about 150 yards downstream of the Calypso Cascades bridge.

Determining where he was singing from was greatly complicated by the roar of Cony Creek which was probably in some sort of flood stage due to the rains the prior day/night.
 
I eventually located the bird singing about 20' up in a snag.  Most of my views were of the bird sitting high in one snag or another, but I did have several short views where it was sitting in good light near the ground.

The bird was a rich and uniform cinnamon brown color.  The throat was the same brown tones as the belly - a bit paler than the back which was very dark.  The back was un-spotted - an even brown.  I only noticed a few spots on the wings when he gave me a brief side view on the ground.  The lower flanks had barring which is visible in one of my pictures.  The bird had a thin buffy supercilium stripe also visible in a picture.

Eventually, the enthusiastic little songster moved to a location a short distance from Cony Creek where I managed to capture several audio clips.  I sent these off to Nathan Pieplow for his opinion.  I felt the song sounded like PAWR versus WIWR.

Nathan responded, “By spectrogram and by ear, this is a Pacific Wren.”

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was clearly a very small wren.  The tail was too short and the song all wrong for HOWR - far too pretty and unchattery.

WIWR was the more likely candidate in my opinion (having documented one from about five miles north as the wren flies on July 2nd 2012). 

PAWR is the warm dark brown bird.  WIWR is also a dark bird in my experience, but has significant grayer tones in the face/throat as well as more extensive spotting on the back and wings.  In addition the flanks of a WIWR aren't barred - they're more spotty.

For me though, Nathan's assertion that the sonogram of the song matched PAWR is the most conclusive piece of data.

Previous Experience

I'm quite capable of recognizing a stub-tailed wren by sight and sound.  Separating their songs is a challenge for me (hence my contact with Nathan).  Separating them by sight can be a bit of a challenge unless the bird is extremely cooperative and the lighting conditions friendly.  I have seen a number of each species in the field and have looked at lots of pictures over the last few years.
