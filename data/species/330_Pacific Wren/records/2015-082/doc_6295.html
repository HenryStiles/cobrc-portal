Habitat

Along a steep N facing slope near a mountain stream; lots of large old-growth trees, down woody debris and boulders in the area

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski 8X42 binoculars and Canon SX40 HS camera w/35X optical zoom lens
Distance:  ~10 feet at the closest
Light:  Sunlight filtered through coniferous forest canopy - excellent light conditions most of the time

Description of the Bird

I observed this bird first w/Jeff Connor on 6/20/15 during a bird count sponsored by Rocky Mountain National Park.

We first noted the long (5 - 8 or so seconds) song which complex with many different elements. It was high pitched and "buzzy." The bird sang nearly the whole time we observed it on 6/20/15. We did not get good looks at the bird but did see a small dark bird moving, rodent-like, close to the ground. I was difficult to spot the singing bird because the song had a ventriloquil quality and, even though we must have been quite close, it was hard to tell if it was above our heads or singing from within the tangle of down trees and boulders. Later we were listening to it sing when it stopped and flew downslope followed immediately by another small brown bird which we assume was a second wren.

I revisited the location on 6/23/15. The bird sang nearly constantly from 5:22AM when I arrived at the site until 7:00AM. On that day, I heard it once more at 7:15AM; I stayed another 15 min. but did not hear it again and left the area at 7:30AM. I saw the bird several times, once briefly but well, as it walked along a downed log. It was obviously a small all dark wren. I  made several recordings of the song using the video capability of my camera.

I revisited the location on 6/28/15, The bird sang steadily from 5:45AM through about 7:15AM when I left. I got very good looks and took several photos and made additional video recordings.

Overall, the bird was dark rufous brown w/darker (almost black) barring, especially underneath and toward the tail. The wings were barred dark brown (almost black) and rufous buff color with the buffy barring lightest on the outer primaries. The tail was short and barred dark brown (almost black) and buffy. It had a single row of small white flecks just above the wings. The throat was not distinctly paler than the rest of the underparts. The bird had a distinct buffy supercillium.

The bill was relatively straight and short and brownish (darker above than below). The eye was dark. The legs appeared long for the size of the bird and were flesh colored.

The bird was furtive and moved about quickly. When it was on the down trees, boulders and moss on the ground, it moved like a wild mouse or other rodent.

Similar Species Discussion

Winter wren. The bird was more rufous overall than winter wren which is browner. It was uniformly darkish rufous-brown below (not gray-brown as in winter wren), and not noticeably lighter on the throat. It has a row of a few white flecks on the upper wing coverts but not lots of white flecks here and on the back. The song was long, high frequency and buzzy and not musical as in winter wren. See attached sonogram.

House wren. There were several house wrens singing along the trail to Calyspo Cascades; but they were in brushy and dry aspen (mostly) habitat. This bird, which was in moist and mossy old growth conifer habitat with lots of downed logs and other debris and boulders, was much darker than a house wren w/dark barring below, esp. on the belly. It seemed much smaller than a house wren. The song was longer (5-8 seconds long for most songs), more buzzy and complex and higher pitched, and less musical than a house wren song.

American dipper. This bird, while it was near the stream, was much smaller and did not enter the water as dippers do. It was also rufous and barred and speckled, unlike dippers. There were dippers (and a dipper nest) downstream about 1/2 mile below Calypso Cascades.

Sparrows (e.g. Lincoln's sparrow) and junco. The bird has a thin, mostly straight bill, unlike the thick conical bill of sparrows and juncos. It had a stubby tail which it often held upright, unlike sparrows.

Resources Used

Birds of North America accounts for both winter wren and Pacific wren.

Sibley Bird App. version 1.9 and Sibley Guide to Western Birds.

Leukering and Pieplow. 2010. Pacific and winter wrens. Colorado Birds. Vol. 44, No. 4.

Toews and Irwin. 2008. Cryptic speciation in a Holarctic passerine revealed by genetic and bioacoustic analyses. Molecular Ecology.

Personal communication w/Dr. David Toews (author of "Cryptic speciation in a Holarctic passerine revealed by genetic and bioacoustic analyses). I sent Dr. Toews both the video recordings and photographs and he responded by e-mail:

        "The videos are great. It definitely sounds and looks like a Pacific wren to me. Thanks for taking time on this - mapping out the eastern edge of the PAWR's range is         important."

Personal communication w/Drs. Sallie Hejl and Jennifer Holmes (co-authors of the 2002 BNA account for the winter wren) who both thought it was a Pacific wren (but recommended I contact Dr. Toews since they felt he had more expertise on distinguishing the two species).

Previous Experience

I know winter wrens from birding in Virginia as a high school and college student and from working and living in Shenandoah National Park (~10 years total time in Virginia, seeing them several times/year, mostly in winter). I've seen a few "stub-tailed wrens in Colorado in the winter but not well enough to ID them to species.
