Habitat

riparian with dense understory of fallen trees logs and old tumbleweed species

Description of the Bird

stub-tailed wren - throat and chin with cinnamon tones. face dark with faint supercilium. Bill measuered and exposed culmen length was 9.8 mm and should be 10.0 in Winter Wren.  Silent in net, while being measured and banded, and when released.  See photos for more plumage detail. The number of white spots on the back were limited in number especially when compared to WIWR. Dark and cinnamon overall.

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wren is the only congener that looks similar.  As shown in photos, WIWR is over lighter with more white spots on back and wing coverts. Bill of WIWR, the exposed culmen, i.e. is longer.  Throat of WIWR is lighter and contrasts fairly strongly with all of the other areas of the bird. 

Resources Used

I looked at all my field guides but the best information is from Pyle who describes subspecies and shows the only measurement that can be used for separation of WIWR and PAWR is the exposed culmen length. I also read the original proposal by the two authors who proposed the split of this species into three species in 2012 and the reasons they gave for the split although they say both song and calls are best for separation but this bird was silent. 

Previous Experience

I saw a number of PAWRs when I worked for the research branch of the USFS Pacific Southwest Research Station. In addition to conducting point counts, and spot mapping to compare the best method for surveying birds, we also conducted nest searches one day a week. I found two Pacific Wren nests but at the time they were still called Winter Wrens - west side of Sierra National Forest in California. I have seen the large Pacific Wrens on Attu Island where they are common and big and not so reclusive.  I have seen and heard WIWRs in Ohio, Michigan, Minnesota in summer and seen about 15 in winter in CO.
