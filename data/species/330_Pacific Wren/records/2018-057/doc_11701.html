Description of the Bird

This bird was first detected by song along West Creek, about 80-100 yd beyond junction where West Creek trail diverges from North Boundary Trail, and thus in Roosevelt NF, not RMNP (I gave the location as RMNP as the nearest option; the park boundary was no more than 0.5 miile from the bird's location). I went down slope and observed a tiny dark wren in root tangles on the opposite creek bank; it then flew to my side of creek, came closer (as close as 15-20 ft) on bare tree branches at eye level and above, in plain view and good diffuse light from an overcast sky, and sang repeatedly. It was clearly a Troglodytes wren, with very short dark rufous tail with black crossbars and dark reddish-brown upper parts (wings were also barred black). The throat and breast were slightly lighter, of a deep cinnamon-buff or cinnamon-ocher color, while the belly was barred dark and appeared in our iPhone photos to be very dark in contrast to the breast. There were no pale gray or whitish areas on the underparts, and no pale mottling there. The only paler feature was a short dull buff supercilium, not prominent or very contrasty. Song was a series of trills on different pitches, to my ear fitting Pacific better than Winter (the trills were mostly faster, higher-pitched and drier than Winter's, less purely musical). I attached one iPhone photo to the eBird list, but it was poor and I have not bothered to attach it here, since others who found the bird on subsequent days have taken much better photos that you will no doubt see. I did, however, obtain good audio from two iPhone video clips, and those are attached here; one has a single song bout, the other two bouts. My eBird list, at https://ebird.org/view/checklist/S46723468, contains these clips as well as spectrograms produced from them by eBird. I feel confident of Pacific Wren, but of course it will be the records committee's call. 

Similar Species Discussion

All wrens other than Winter were eliminated by small size, overall dark color, and very short tail. Winter was eliminated (I believe) by the overall rich dark color, with nearly uniform cinnamon-ocher underparts including throat and upper breast; the color did not lighten up or become grayish on the throat. The supercilium was also slight, thin, and buff in color, less obvious than on Winter. Song was equally important, as described in the account. I did not hear the bird give any call notes, but it sang repeatedly and at very close range.

Resources Used

My own memory of the characters separating Pacific and Winter, and of past encounters with both (frequent with Winter, occasional with Pacific and only in Colorado over the past three or so years). Whitle preparing this doc, I checked a couple of points in Sibley.

Previous Experience

As just noted, long (55 years) and regular experience with Winter Wren, including song, mostly in the midwest; much less with Pacific, but have seen one or two others in the last few years near Calypso Cascades.
