Description of the Bird

The wren was small, had a short stubby tail, and was an overall dark rufous brown. The sides of the neck, chin, and upper breast were plain and unpatterned (unlike Winter Wren who has more patterning in these areas). The throat was dark and almost as dark as the breast. The throat wasn't light like found on Winter Wren, but was darker and warmer. The eyebrow was buffy/light brown (compared to paler/whiter eyebrow found on Winter Wren). The pale spots on the primaries were buffy (compared to paler/whiter spots as found on Winter Wren).


Bird was observed foraging and skulking around the creek bed in a mixed conifer forest. The creek lies in a canyon that has many shaded areas. Bird was hopping along logs and branches, and was also observed walking on top of leaf litter along the creek side foraging for food.

Similar Species Discussion

A similar species is be Winter Wren. Winter Wren was eliminated due to the following...
a) Winter Wren are paler and grayer all over. This bird was darker, warmer, and rufous all over.
b) Winter Wrens have patterning in the neck and breast, and have a paler throat. This bird lacked patterning on the neck, and had a throat that was nearly as dark as the breast.
c) Winter Wrens have paler/whiter eyebrows. This bird had buffy/light brown eyebrows.
d) Winter Wrens have paler/whiter spots on their primaries. This bird had buffy spots on their primaries.

Previous Experience

I only have past experience with Winter Wrens. I grew up in Massachusetts and observed my first Winter Wren around the age of 10 or 11 and I have observed Winter Wrens here in Colorado. I honestly have never seen a Pacific Wren, but I know that when birding in Colorado, all dark brown wrens should be observed carefully. With that said, I did not know that this bird was a Pacific Wren immediately. The bird seemed darker, warmer, and more rufous than Winter Wrens I have seen. This bird gave me that strange feeling that it was not what it seemed. When I got home, I immediately reviewed the photos and compared them to photos of Pacific and Winter Wrens online. The lack of patterning on the neck really stood out (along with the other characteristics previously described) and led me to believe it was a Pacific. However, I don't have any experience with this species, so the photos were sent to Brandon Percival for confirmation. He viewed the photo and said he also showed it to Mark Peterson and Doug Faulkner who all agreed that this bird looked good for Pacific Wren (despite that we did not hear the bird call).
