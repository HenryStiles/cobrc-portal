Description of the Bird

 As I approached the Calypso Cascades bridge, I heard this bird singing loudly just south (left) of the left end of the bridge. I soon recognized it as Pacific Wren and took a couple of iPhone videos to capture the song, even though I had not yet seen the bird. Eventually I did see it briefly as it perched on the tops of small conifers. It was tiny, with a very short tail, thin bill slightly decurved, some barring on the wings and mottling or spotting on the belly. The most obvious plumage feature was the rich uniform cinnamon color of the underparts from throat through breast and at least upper belly. The photo attached does not show this color well due to sunlight glare; my wife Margaret observed the bird too and commented on the lovely cinnamon tone of the underparts as the bird faced us at about 30 ft. I wasn't able to study the bird well enough to add any better details.

The bird kept singing in bouts of song that were c. 12 sec long, separated by relatively short intervals of perhaps 15-30 sec. In other words, it was a persistent singer the whole time I was there (around 15 min) and was still singing when I came through again on my return from Ouzel Falls an hour later.  The attached audio seems to me diagnostic of Pacific Wren.

From the CFO site, I see that this bird has been seen by m.ob., beginning at least 26 June. What can't be told from the rare-bird listings is how many others may have documented it. Hope this is not superfluous. 

Similar Species Discussion

The ony real confusion species is Winter Wren. Visually, the rich uniform cinnamon color of the underparts suggests Pacific raher than Winter; beyond that, I didn't see any distingishing features. The song, however, had the rapid chippery or rattling quality of Pacific, with several long, fast, high-pitched trills, and it lacked the somewhat more melodic and bell-like quality of Winter Wren song. I think the audio attached and the spectogram of it support the identification.

Previous Experience

Lots of experience with Winter Wren over 60 years, much less with Pacific except for my previous sightings of that species here in Colorado, which gave me a chance to observe the bird better than I previously had and to learn its song.
