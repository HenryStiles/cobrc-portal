Description of the Bird

On July 14, 2017, I took my nearly-annual hike to Thunder Lake in upper Wild Basin.  This hike takes me past Calypso Cascades, the home of at least one PAWR over the last two summers, so I knew to listen.  As I approached the cascades, I could clarly hear the song of a stub-tailed wren over the sound of rushing water.

It didn't take long to locate the songster who was moving about in an area which was almost 100 yards in length.

While I was taking pictures of songster, I heard the alarm call of another wren just to my right. This bird flew over near the first bird and both were visible together for about two seconds before first bird headed off to the left. The second bird's coloration was similar to the first (photos of both).

Once again, I was struck by the birds' overall rich brown coloration with little contrast.  The backs appeared very dark and plain, their throats matched their chests, both had a buffy-brown supercilium which matched their chest, and limited to no white spotting on the wings.  

The overall rich brown tones with no ashy tones is consistent with PAWR and not WIWR.  The buffy supercilium, lack of wing spotting and the throat which matches the undersides all eliminate WIWR.

The song again sounded more mechanical than that of WIWR.  I have audio, but at this time this website still will not take m4a's
 

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wren - Eliminated by the overall even dark tones of these birds, the lack of white spotting on the coverts, the lack of patterning on the back.
