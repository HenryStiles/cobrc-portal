Habitat

Olive and tamarisk/saltcedar along the sandy gravel shore of the Arkansas River, which was a little low at the time. Riparian habitat away from the river (tamarisk, cottonwood with many downed limbs).

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10x42 Nikon Monarch binoculars and a cannon dSLR with a 100-400mm lens.
Distance:  Multiple observations ranged from about 20 feet to 35 feet.
Light:  Sunny, but I often saw the bird in good light with the sun at my back, as well as backlit by the sun and in full shade.

Description of the Bird

See photos. Small, short-tailed wren, warm rusty brown color more or less uniformly colored background with a very faint but slightly paler rusty supercillium, and weakly barred flanks. Overall fairly unmarked. The color of the throat, face, neck and breast, together gave the bird a fairly unpatterned and uniformly colored look. Together, these characters suggest a pretty typical Pacific Wren.

I should add that, at the time, I was unaware of color differences (between the two species) in the barring on the flight feathers and this characteristic was not looked at or documented in the photos.
Behaviors: The bird was skulking in root tangles and bushes along the north shore of Arkansas River.
Call: The bird made some chatter calls while I was there, those unfortunate vocalizations consistent with both Winter Wren and Pacific Wren.
Plumage: Basic; I made no attempt to age the bird

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wren. This species was ruled out based on plumage details. I am not sure if others heard the bird, but the calls I heard, though higher pitched than the song-sparrow like calls of Winter Wren, were more likely aggitated scold notes, which are higher in both species (as I understand it). I never heard any Winter Wren like call notes from the bird.

Resources Used

At the time I relied on field guides that discussed the two subspecies, as well as word of mouth information from Brandon P., Bill M. and others in CO and at the Cornell Lab of Ornithology (I was attending grad school at Cornell at the time). Since then I've also gleaned additional details from online discussions of Pacific/Winter Wren ID including Nathan Pieplow's posts about vocalizations (http://earbirding.com/blog/archives/774 and http://earbirding.com/blog/archives/826), David Sibley's posts about plumage (http://www.sibleyguides.com/2010/08/distinguishing-pacific-and-winter-wrens/) and from Tony Leukering's "In The Scope" article on Pacific/Winter Wrens (http://cfobirds.org/downloads/In%20the%20Scope/016%20In%20the%20Scope%20Oct%202010.pdf)

Previous Experience

My only other experience with this species was after this observation (Oct 2012) in Seattle, WA. I've seen a handful of Winter Wrens in CO, and am very familiar with Winter Wrens from western New York.
