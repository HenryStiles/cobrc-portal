Description of the Bird

Ryan Votta found the bird on the morning of January 19th, at approximately 7:15 AM. He called me to say he had a very rufous short-tailed Troglodytes calling like a WIWA, and he believed he had heard, but not identified, the same individual the prior morning. Around 9:40 AM I finally arrived at the location, where I met with Ryan, who stated he hadn't seen or heard the bird in some time.  After perhaps 45 minutes of searching I left. At 1230 Ryan called me to say he had relocated and photographed the bird. When I arrived, the photos he showed me didn't convince me, as the photos made the bird look a bit more cold (albeit through the viewfinder of an older camera) but we were able to relocate the bird and the overall rufous or rusty color of the bird stood out at my first glimpse. I was able to watch the bird for only perhaps 5-10 seconds bouncing around in the woody debris - (quick looks at about 10 ft through 10x42s of this bird). Because Ryan had obtained photos of the bird, my priority was to obtain audio of the bird calling, which I did successfully at rather low quality and for only a handful of notes.  In this recording, the call notes of the bird appear to resemble a Wilson's Warbler rather than a Song Sparrow.

Previous Experience

Because I have mostly lived in areas where Winter Wren is fairly common or Pacific Wren is fairly common, I've spent very little time attempting to distinguish the two visually.  I have experienced both quite frequently, but because of relative geographic separation and the fairly recent split of these species, I can't say I've ever tried to pick an oddball out within the range of the other, and I've certainly never bumped into either of these in southwestern Colorado, where both are intriguing.
