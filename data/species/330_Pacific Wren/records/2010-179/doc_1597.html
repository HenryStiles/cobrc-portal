Description of the Bird

This bird was typically Wren shaped--chunky, a bit pot-bellied, with a short upturned tail held almost perpendicular to its body. The bird was noticeably smaller than a House Wren and its tail was also shorter.

Overall color was deep rufous above and below becoming slightly paler rufous in the throat and supercilium. Lower belly and undertail coverts had dark barring which contrasted with all rufous underparts. Short rufous tail had dark horizontal barring. Short rufous wings were also darkly barred. Bill was dark and slim. I did not see leg color well. There were no discernible white speckles anywhere on this richly rufous-colored bird.
Behaviors: Skulked in dense understory cover and leaf litter occasionally popping up to perch on a twig in clear view, where it gave its agitated chattering and call notes. This agitated behavior probably suggests a male, as does its very rich overall rufous coloring.
Call: A loud "chup, chup" or " chump, chump." If these notes are supposed to be similar to a Wilson's Warbler, I would say Pacific Wren's call notes are noticeably louder and sharper.
Bird also gave agitated chatter like rapping 2 marbles are rocks together, but there was a slight burry quality to this chatter. To my ears the difference between Pacific/Winter Wren call notes are subtle. The article in CFO Journal Vol.44, #4 was somewhat helpful in referring to website where both species' songs and call notes are recorded. But the article's sentence describing "chattering trills" was convoluted and unclear if not garbled.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wrens have much paler underparts than Pacifics making for greater contrast of pale underparts with browner upperparts. Upperparts of Winter Wrens tend not to be as rufous as Pacifics making for an overall duller colored bird than Pacific. There are usually more white speckles on back & wings of Winter Wrens than on Pacifics.
