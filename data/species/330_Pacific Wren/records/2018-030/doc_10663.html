Description of the Bird

January 11th 2018-  While walking to work at the Durango Fish Hatchery, I heard a familar call note coming from the banks of the Animas River.  I instantly recognized the call to be that of a Pacific Wren.  The call note is a sharp kit or kit kit repeated several times.  It sounds similar to the call note of a Wilsons Warbler, and is quite loud and explosive for such a tiny bird.  All of my sightings of this wren began by first hearing the call note and then being able to get views.  Overal visual impressions of this bird include;  tiny wren with very short stubby tail that is held in an upward position, round overal shape with thin beak and short wings, warm brownish color overal with lighter eyebrow stripe, wings and tail richer brown color with some blackish barring.  The wren acted much like a rodent hopping between and under rocks along the river.  During each siting the bird remained very near the ground, skulking in thick cover.  

I believe this wren to be a returing visitor because during January and February of 2017 there was a confirmed PAWR in this same area which I photoed and had many encounters with.  This year many birders were able to see this rarity and add it to there state lists.  The only other species that could be a possible ID would be Winter Wren.  However the call note is diagnostic in positively identifying it as Pacific Wren.  Also, compared to many Winter Wrens I have seen, this wren has much richer and warmer tones of brown.  Could it be possible that this bird is staying in Colorado year long and perhaps breeding here?

Similar Species Discussion

Winter Wren-  Eliminated by call note.  Winter Wren call not similar to Song Sparrow.  More of a squeaky chimp chimp.  This wren's call note was more like that of a Wilsons Warbler.  More of a sharp kit kit.


House Wren-  Extremely unlikely for House Wren to be present in the middle of winter.  Also this wren was smaller and had shorter tail than that of a House Wren.

Resources Used

Binoculars, field guides, support from other experienced birders.

Previous Experience

Many encounters with this individual bird over a two season period.  My house is adjacent to the location of where the wren is usually seen, so I am fortunate to hear and see this rarity many times.  Living in New Jersey for many years I am very familar with Winter Wren, which helped in ruling this species out.
