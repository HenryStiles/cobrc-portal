Description of the Bird

On June 24th, Steve Mlodinow reported hearing a Stub-tailed Wren singing near Calypso Cascades in Wild Basin. 

On June 28th, Bill Rowe eBirded a Pacific/Winter Wren singing near Calypso Cascades in Wild Basin - again "heard-only"

One July 4th, while hiking to Bluebird Lake above Calypso Cascades, Jeff Dunning and I heard a Stub-tailed Wren singing just downstream of Calypso Cascades.  The song of the WIWR/PAWR is fairly unique and easy to attribute to one of the two species.  I captured audio of the bird at that time.  The song sounded less musical and more mechanical than WIWR.  I had a brief view of the bird which was not terribly helpful for ID purposes.  At best, it struck me a "too dark" for WIWR.

On our return from Bluebird Lake as I turned to start down the trail after crossing the Calypso Cascades bridge, I again noted the bird singing just downstream.  This time the bird was far more cooperative and provided many opportunities for visual study and photographs as well as additional audio capture.

Visually, the bird was tiny with a short-upturned tail.  The overall color was a rich brown.  The crown, back and rump were solid rich brown.  The tail was banded brown and almost black.  The wings were dark brown with just a couple white spots near the upper part of the wing.  The undertail coverts and flanks appeared vaguely barred.  The throat and undersides of the bird were an even buffy color which matched the supercilium.

The overall rich tones of the bird with no grayish or ashy tones is consistent with PAWR and not consistent with WIWR.  The buffy supercilium, overall lack of wing spotting and the buffy throat which matches the rest of the bird's undersides also eliminates WIWR.  WIWR flanks while "barred", often seem are more spotted versus the very buffy toned barring on this bird.

To my ear, the song had a more mechanical sound than the song of a WIWR which is incredibly musical.  Examining sonograms, I noted that WIWR always seemed to have a portion of the song which looked really wild encompassing a significant amount of spectrum (a broad swath of kHz) - all at the same time.  This bird's sonograms do not have that wild stretch (nor did any PAWR sonograms I examined).
