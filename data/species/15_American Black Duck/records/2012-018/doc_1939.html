Description of the Bird

At about 2:30 PM on 19 February, I was walking along the north shore of Jackson Reservoir. The reservoir was mostly frozen, but the north edge was free of ice. Among the thousands of waterfowl in front of me, I noticed 3 rather dark largish ducks sitting on the ice within a couple hundred meter span. One was particularly black in appearance (one of the others was clearly an American Black Duck x Mallard hybrid; the other bird never was seen other than curled up sleeping). This blackest of the three was not sleeping, but was standing on the edge of the ice, 100 or so meters from me. The sky was high overcast, and I would?ve been somewhat looking into the sun were it out. Nonetheless the lighting was decent. I watched the bird for 15 minutes as it stood, then walked, then spread one wing out and gave one flap. I motioned for Tim to come from the car. He did, looked at the bird for 5 minutes or so, agreed with the ID, and returned to the car. Eventually the bird slid into the water with a group of Mallards and swam to parts farther away. In all, I watched it for about 30 minutes.

Description
Body appeared entirely black as it stood on the ice next to Mallards, excepting a vague silvery stripe on tertials that I frequently see in Am Black Ducks. The neck and head contrasted sharply with the body, and the line of demarcation between chest and neck was sharp. The neck was grayish-brown, and not as pale as that of Mexican Duck, nor as buffy. The crown and eyeline were blackish. The cheek just below the eyeline was a bit paler than rest of head/neck. The underwing showed bright white underwing coverts with some smudging anteriorly. This was, admittedly, seen but briefly. A very narrow white trailing edge to speculum was seen when wing drooped. There was no anterior white edge to speculum, which would have been visible, if present. Speculum color could not be discerned. Tail same color as body. No curled feather on tail/uppertail coverts. Feet/legs red. Bill bright yellowish, but not as lemon yellow as that of nearby Mallards. Nail black. Similar in size to nearby male Mallards. Did not appear to be paired with a female duck.

Similar Species Discussion

The color of this bird's body was darker than that of any Mottled or Mexican Duck, leaving Am Black Duck x Mallard as the major potential ID quandary. I have had extensive dealings with these hybrids, not only when living in Chicago, but in Everett, Washington, where a population of American Black Ducks numbering nearly 100 existed about the time I arrived there. Hybrids were frequent, and hybridization likely played a major role in this population?s demise. I've not seen a hybrid as black as this bird. Furthermore, there was no hint of green on the head. The bill color was perfect for Am Black Duck, there was no hint of Mallard in the tail color or structure, and the narrow white border to one edge of the speculum is considered within range of Am Black Duck (in publications, and in my experience from Chicago). The smudging under the wing is rather definitive for Am Black Duck as well, but such was seen only as an impression. In other words, I saw no characteristics of Mallard in this bird, and the bird?s characteristics fit my experience with Am Black Duck very well.
