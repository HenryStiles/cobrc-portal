Description of the Bird

I had just gotten out of the car at the second stop on the auto-tour. I was scanning down the canal looking at ducks and coots, and hearing rancorous, repeating songs from Marsh Wren, Common Yellowthroat, and Red-winged Blackbirds, as well as occasional Wilson's Snipe winnows and songs/calls. Underneath all these sounds was something I immediately recognized as the ticking sound of a Yellow Rail. However, I initially was quite skeptical and almost confused by what I was hearing. The steady and repeating ticking sound was coming from deep inside the bulrushes just southwest of the parking lot. The first sounds I heard were probably no more than 5-8 yards from where I was standing.

I started making recordings, and paying attention to whether or not the sound moved through the sedges. I noticed that the ticking sound moved slowly westward, shifting about 15 feet per minute in that direction. I felt that this movement ruled out a frog or insect. It also seemed unlikely to be a songbird like a Marsh Wren or Yellowthroat, since it stayed low and never strayed from the steady ticking sound. I figured a songbird engaging perhaps in some unfamiliar calling episode would stop doing that after a time, and erupt into something more familiar and move more quickly. Instead, it sounded like it was coming from a small bird ambling around invisibly through tall growth, like a rail would.

I realized that I were correct, I would very likely never see this bird, so I instead focused on just getting as clear and close a recording as possible. In the 45 minutes that I spent at the site, I made about 6-7 recordings ranging from 30 seconds to 5 minutes. The bird did move around the tall growth close to the parking lot, and would stop clicking for short periods before resuming a minute or two later. The cadence of the clicking never changed – it always a 5-click sequence, on endless repeat, but never speeding up or slowing down. The 5 clicks were 2 initial clicks, a higher and lower one, followed by 3 clicks at the same pitch as the higher note. The next phrase would start right after the previous one ended so that the clicks were almost all evenly spaced in time. This is easily seen in a spectrogram.

By 730, the bird had started move out of the taller growth and into some shorter grass further south, but still difficult to view. It continued to click even as I left. I did briefly see some shorter grasses swaying near where the sound was emanating about 30 yards away, but strained views through the bins for even a momentary glimpse yielded nothing. This was the extent of my experience of the bird – no visual, but a solid and nearly continuous vocal display unlike anything I'd ever heard at a marsh in Colorado. 

Similar Species Discussion

No regular Colorado bird species makes a 5-note clicking sequence like Yellow Rail. The only species I considered that make steady tapping noises like this are Green Kingfisher and the initial clicking notes of Yellow-billed Cuckoo song. Both these sounds however are obviously different from the Yellow Rail song once a long enough sample is heard, and neither matched this click sequence. (Of course the habitat was also completely wrong for either.)

As mentioned I was more concerned about this being perhaps an unfamiliar call sequence from a typical marsh bird, like perhaps a Marsh Wren, or a non-bird like a frog. Its slow but steady movement around the marsh ruled out any conceivable or likely frog species, and the cricket frog (northern or southern), the only frog I know that makes a sound anything like Yellow Rail, doesn't sustain the kind of regular clicks that are typical of Yellow Rail, nor is it present this far west into Colorado.

I investigated other tropical or world rails to see if other species have a clicking sequence like this. I focused initially on other Coturnicops rails, and did find a recording of Speckled Rail Coturnicops notatus from the southern cone of South America. Its song is also a steady clicking, but even more steady and unwavering than that of Yellow Rail. The recording at the Macaulay Library, however, is not described in Cornell's Birds of the World entry for Speckled Rail, so information on how typical or normal that clicking song is remains uncertain. Only 21 eBird records exist for Speckled Rail worldwide, with none further north than Paraguay or Sao Paolo, Brazil.

Swinhoe's Rail, the only other Coturnicops rail worldwide (Coturnicops exquisitus), resides in the Asian Far East, wintering in Japan, Korea, and south China, while summering in far southeastern Russia in disjunct pockets near the Chinese and Mongolian borders. Its song is notably different from either Yellow or Speckled Rail, not consisting of any ticks but rather purrs or rattles.

Resources Used

I briefly played a few commercial recordings of Yellow Rail I have on my iPhone from various sources, just for my own re-edification and comparison with what I was hearing. I mainly played the Stokes Guide recordings, which matched this bird perfectly.

Because I had no prior experience with the species, I did request confirmation from Nathan Pieplow for my recordings as well, who agreed with my assessment.

Previous Experience

No prior experience with Yellow Rail, except for many occasions listening to commercial recordings in my collection over the past 15 years.

I've heard and/or recorded a number of other rail species in the US and the Neotropics, including Virginia Rail, Sora, Black Rail, various wood-rails including Slaty-breasted and Gray-cowled Wood-Rails, and numerous crake species including Russet-crowned Crake and Ash-throated Crake.
