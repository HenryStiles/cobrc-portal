Habitat

Lake edge with shallow edge at pennisula the extends into center of reservoir. Muddy bottom with muddy shoreline with grassy edge and some willows and cattails in vicinity

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Zeiss 8x42 and Kowa 823 with 32x wide-angle eyepiece. Photographs with Canon EOS T1i with Canon 100-400 IS lens
Distance:  75 ft
Light:  Very good. Weather was partly cloudy with the sun over my left shoulder - i.e. southeast while I was looking mostly to the west while observing the bird. Wind was slight.

Description of the Bird

Large plegadis ibis with dark brown plumage with irridescant highlights of green. Brownish-gray bill was long and decurved. The facial skin was not red or reddish but rather grayish with bluish-white edges. These edges did not extend behind the eye. The upper edge reached the midpoint of the eye and the lower edge stopped just short of the midpoint of the eye. The surrounding area of the face lacked any white feathering. The eye was completely dark brown without any red or reddish highlight present. The legs were grayish-brown and the knee joint seemed very slightly reddish in tone.
Behaviors: The bird was associating with a loose flock of about 75 White-faced Ibises all of which had reddish facial skin and varing amounts of white feathering around the facial skin. It was feeding in shallow water near the water's edge. There was nothing striking about this bird's behavior except it remained near the edge of the flock while I observed. It moved with the flock.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Immature White Ibis was eliminated as there was no white anywhere on this bird. White-faced Ibis is very common migrant in this are at this time. However, this bird did not possess any redddish facial skin and totally lacked any white feathering near or adjacent to the facial skin. This bird not not have any white extending beyond the aft half of the eye. The eye of this bird not exhibit any red or reddish tone. The legs of this bird were almost completely gray-brown with the slightest hint of reddish at the knee joint. In contrast White-faced Ibises show red or reddish facial skin, various degrees of white feather edging around the facial skin, much more extensive red on the legs, and reddish eyes.

Resources Used

None. This species is establishing a pattern of occurring almost every spring in western Colorado in very small numbers. There are also occasional hybrids.

Previous Experience

I have seen this bird many times while I birded in the eastern US. I have seen White-faced Ibises annually in Western Colorado and probably 1000+ individuals pass through LaPlata County each year.

Additional Information

Comments: Susan Allerton and I routinely carefully scan through all flocks of plegadis ibises looking for this species and we are occasionally rewarded. Pastorius is a particularly good observation place because it is relatively small and the birds are frequently on the east shore in excellent light. I have recorded this species annually since 2005, but rarely more than one sighting per year.
