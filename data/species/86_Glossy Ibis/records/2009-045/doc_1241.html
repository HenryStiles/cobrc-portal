Description of the Bird

The bird was clearly an ibis, a dark-plumaged, medium-sized wading bird with a long, thick, downcurved bill. Its bill was a basic horn gray color, and the facial skin adjacent to the bill was dark, possibly with a bluish cast, but without any red tones. Its eye was similarly dark. A bright electric blue line ran along the upper edge of the facial skin from the bill to the top of the eye, and another similar blue line ran from the base of the bill along the bottom of the facial skin to the bottom of the eye. The bird's legs were grayish with an extensive red wash around the "knees" and adjacent parts of the legs.
Behaviors: The bird was foraging with 6-8 White-faced Ibis in a wet meadow dominated by thick lush grass that came up almost to the bird's belly. Luckily, this individual had a very helpful habit of lifting its legs high over the tops of the grass when it wanted to move around, which greatly helped our views of leg color.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: Adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

White-faced Ibis and White-faced x Glossy hybrids must be ruled out. Several White-faced Ibis were together with this bird, providing for direct comparison. Their red eyes, reddish facial skin, and completely red legs were obvious in the same lighting conditions at the same distance. The Glossy Ibis did not show any characteristics of hybridization--its face pattern and bare parts coloration were perfect and "classic" for the species, although the amount of red around the "knees" was probably at the upper limit of what you'd see on a pure Glossy.

Previous Experience

I have picked Glossy Ibis and possible hybrids out of White-faced Ibis flocks on more than a half-dozen occasions in Colorado, and I have spent extensive time studying the two birds in direct comparison over the past couple of years.
