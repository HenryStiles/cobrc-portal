Description of the Bird

While on a pre-convention trip to southeast Colorado, our group stopped at Box Springs in northern Crowley County. Most of the group had driven past the pond, though Bill Schmoker and I were driving the rear vehicles and pulled in to the springs. While awaiting the rest of the group to turn around, Bill and I began glassing the pond. Very quickly I spied a lone Plegadis ibis standing along the northwestern corner of the pond. The bird was of medium-size, colored in rich chestnut with greenish and purplish iridescence and had a long, decurved bill. What immediately caught my eye, however, was the blue edge around a brownish loral area. This thin blue border was present both dorsally and ventrally to the facial skin, but ended abruptly at the rear edge of the brownish eye. The bill was olive toned. The long legs were greenish-gray mostly, except for pinkish to red tones to the joints. Soon after finding the bird and obtaining a few poor pictures, Doug Faulkner and the rest of the convention tour arrived. Most, including Doug, also were able to see the bird for a short bit prior to it flying away.
Behaviors: It spent a bit of time foraging within the reeds, mainly by probing the soft earth. Once birders started looking at it, it simply stood there prior to taking flight.
Call: None heard
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

White-faced Ibis differs by having a lighter colored bill generally, white feathering that completely encircles a red loral area and the eye, red eyes, and all pinkish-red legs. Hybrid plegadis show variable traits between the two species, namely a slight bluish tint to the loral fringe area and a pinkish loral area. At no time did I find any suggestions that this bird was a hybrid.
