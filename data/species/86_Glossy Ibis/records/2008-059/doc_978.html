Description of the Bird

Bird was clearly an ibis, a long-legged dark-plumaged wader with a downcurved bill, associating with cows and about 300 other ibis in a wet field. Here is a verbatim excerpt from my field notes: "Legs with no trace of red; eye dark, also without trace of red; facial skin dark gray, faintly tinged blue (no red). Red in legs and eyes and facial skin of adjacent WFIBs was evident in direct comparison. Electric blue lines bordering facial skin: one from eye down to base of bill, the other along top of facial skin from eye to eye. Classic GLIB, except bird has a pale bluish orbital ring, and electric blue lines are narrower than I like on a “classic” obvious GLIB."
Behaviors: Bird was foraging very actively with many other ibis in a wet pasture, probing into the mud with its bill. The ibis in the pasture were feeding mostly on large earthworms, as far as I could tell, and occasionally smaller prey items.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

White-faced Ibis and White-faced x Glossy hybrids must be ruled out. White-faced were in the same field in huge numbers (ca. 300) and were ruled out in direct comparison by the complete lack of reddish tint to legs, facial skin, and eye, as well as the "classic" Glossy Ibis face pattern of electric blue lines discontinuous at the eye. Hybrids were a little more difficult to rule out. There was at least one, and possibly up to three, birds in the flock that I thought could be potential hybrids, but I wasn't able to spend enough time on them to produce sufficient notes for a writeup. The bird I am calling a Glossy was classic in most senses but bothered me on two counts: the blue facial skin bordering the eye was quite thin, about half as thick as I'd like on an obvious Glossy; and more troubling, the bird had a thin but distinct pale bluish orbital ring around the eye, about the same color as the electric blue facial skin, perhaps a little paler. I have never seen this on any ibis and can find no photos on the internet, of either species, that show it. I can find no mention of it in the Birds of North America accounts for either species. I'm not sure of the implications for the bird's age, sex, or blood quantum. I called the bird a Glossy based on the complete lack of red in the bare parts, which I was able to see well, and the near-classic face pattern.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen many thousands of White-faced Ibis in the past couple of years and have picked Glossies out of Colorado flocks on at least half a dozen occasions.
