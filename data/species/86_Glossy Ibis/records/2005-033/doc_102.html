Habitat

This was a flooded pasture with small pools of standing water visible in between the short grass.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Nikon fieldscope ED with 20-45 zoom eyepiece.
Distance:  30 feet at closest approach. Mostly 40 feet.
Light:  Sun was behind us and to the left (we were looking north and a little east). Birds were observed the entire time in direct sun.

Description of the Bird

There were ten ibis present: two GLIBs and eight WFIBs. The WFIBs were in various plumages--one with a good half-inch of white feathering all around the facial skin; another two without any trace of white feathering; the rest in between. All the WFIBs had red eyes and facial skin that was varying degrees of red. Most had entirely red legs, though there were some with red mostly around the "kneecaps." The GLIBs, in contrast, both had dark (brown) eyes, and neither had any trace of white feathering anywhere about the head (or elsewhere, for that matter). The edge of the facial skin was electric blue (we also used the words "sky blue" and "robin's-egg blue" to describe it) in a thin line of uniform thickness going from the bottom edge of the mandible up to the bottom of the eye, and then from the top of the eye across the forehead to the other eye. Thus, this pale blue border was discontinuous at the eye. The rest of the facial skin, inward of these lines and out to a vertical border with the base of the bill, was a much softer, darker bluish color. It probably would have looked dark had the light not been good or had we been farther away, but our excellent views allowed us to see that it had a bluish cast. The legs on the GLIBs were a pale grayish horn color, about the same color as the bill. One GLIB had a faint reddish wash to the anterior portions of the "knees." This would not have been visible at any greater distance or in poorer light. This same GLIB had ever-so-slightly thinner blue borders to the facial skin, but in all other respects (eye color, facial skin color and pattern) it was identical to the other GLIB.
Behaviors: All ten ibis were foraging actively in a wet field in the company of blackbirds and a few ducks. Sometimes they would stop to preen or to stretch their wings above their heads.
Call: birds in the flock were grunting, like hoarse Mallards, but it was impossible to tell whether these sounds were coming from the WFIBs, the GLIBs or both.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

The similar species is, of course, White-faced Ibis. See above for detailed comparisons. Here I will note that both species were in view for an extended period, and direct comparison was possible. With direct comparison, the differences between the species really stood out.

Resources Used

We did not have any field guides with us.

Previous Experience

I have learned here in Colorado how to pick Glossies out of flocks of White-faceds. I see hundreds of WFIBs each year and I now search them regularly for GLIBs. This is my third time seeing the birds together in the past few years.
