Description of the Bird

This bird was an oriole based on bill shape, size, and body coloration.

The bird was yellow, mostly yellow-orange, with a a black mask, chin, and throat. It had a black back with two whitish wingbars, or shoulder patch and wingbar, the upper one much wider than the lower one. The tail was long and black. The bill was thin and long, curved throught its length. The bird was lightest yellow-orange on the belly and vent. Upper tail coverts were also orangish.
Behaviors: The bird was observed mostly moving and perching in tall cottonwood trees. It was not seen feeding. It seemed to prefer the tallest trees in the area around the house in particular, but also flew across the G road and perched in cottonwoods there.
Call: none heard
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

This is most likely the nelsoni subspecies which is more yellow-orange than the birds found in TX. Looking at photos on the Net from CA and OR, the CO bird looked identical to these birds, making it the nelsoni subspecies. While it is still an orange bird, it has a yellow-orange look.

Altamira Oriole - has a straight culmen, this bird's was distinctly curved, also Altamira O. has and orange shoulder bar unlike the white one of this bird.

Streak-backed Oriole - all races of male Streak-backed Oriole have varying amounts of black streaks on its back. This bird had a pure black back. The orange color on Streak-backed is strongest on its face and on this bird the orange was yellow-orange, unlike the color of SBOR.

Orange Oriole - has a straight culmen and although it too has a black back it shows a white handkerchief at the base of the primaries.

Additional Information

Comments: This bird was totally tied in to a small area around a private residence. The Bigonia Trumpet Creeper vines have nectar of interest to HOORs. The HOORs rip open at the base of the flower with their curved thin bills. (Jaramillo and Burke) Someone who has permission to walk on the property should look for planted palms, especially Washingtonia fan palms of which this species is tied into in CA. Jaramillo points out that HOOR have spread into Oregon and northern Californina because of the planting of palms there.

I asked Rachel Hopper (pers. comm.), after her visit, if she saw any palm trees in the back yard and she thought that there might have been some. If so this species, HOOR, has the potential to colonize the SW corner of the state, if appropriate habitat (palms for breeding (also has used Pepper tree, Schinus mole, and a variety of oak species and even manmade structures). This bird also has a tendancy for vagrancy (Jaramillo and Burke) so it is more expected than unexpected as their breeding range extends into southwestern Utah, and northwestern Arizona and habitats there are not disimilar to the general habitats along McElmo Creek.
