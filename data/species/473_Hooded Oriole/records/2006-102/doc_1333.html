Habitat

Southwestern Colorado dry pinyon-juniper hillside habitat surrounded the riparian and ranchland bottom of McElmo Creek. The oriole was seen on an old ranch built in the 1920s, near which towered mature cottonwood trees. THere was a roughly 10 meter long, 2.5 meter high, ancient, floriferous Trumpet Vine (Campsis radicans) hedge along both sides of the driveway. Vegetatable gardens, fallow fields, and old orchard and shrubbery surrounded the shaded ranch house. There was a mature, fruiting Mulberry tree (Morus niger) near the house, but it was blocked from our view during times of observations. I thought that this tree might provide food for the oriole, making the site even more attractive to it. Seventy-five meters away McElmo Creek flowed lazily. Initially I saw the oriole perched on a barbed-wire fence, which separated the black-top road from a fallow field.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarvovski EL 8.5 x 42 binoculars on both days; also on 6-19 Leica Televid 77 mm APO angled scope, set at about 30x
Distance:  Between 20 and 50 meters
Light:  The sky was clear and the sun was behind me when I first saw the bird at 1931 on June 18th. The summer solstice was nearing, and the light was still bright at that hour. The next morning dawned clear, and again the oriole was observed in favorable light, in positions west and north of me. Less often the bird was in bright shade. I did not notice any difference in the apparent color of the bird when it was seen in either evening or morning light.

Description of the Bird

The field marks that I noted and called out within seconds of first seeing the bird that Norm Erthal had excitedly pointed our were these: orange crown, black throat and face, black wing with one white wing bar, all black tail! With further and calmer inspection the field marks were seen in more detail, clinching the identification and ruling out similar species and hybrids.

It was easy to see at first glance that the Hooded Oriole was quite similar in size to the four Bullock's Orioles with with it was quarreling - about 8.5 inches (22 cm). The overall shape of the bird was typical of the Oriole family, slimmer than an American Robin (Turdus migratorius) and with a longish, slender, pointed bill. With more thorough inspection the next morning, both mandibles of the bill were seen to be slightly down curved. The more slender shape of the putative Hooded Oriole became apparent, compared to the chunkier Bullock's Orioles, both perched and in flight. This gave it a different jizz, much as a Lesser Black-backed Gull (Larus fuscus) appears longer, more attenuated and less chunky than a Herring Gull (Larus argentatus).

The bird's head was relatively flat, more like that of a Bullock's Oriole or Common Grackle (Quiscalus quiscula) than the more rounded head of a Baltimore Oriole (Icterus galbula) or Orchard Oriole (Icterus spurious).

The "orange" color of the bird was really a yellow-orange compared to the rich orange of the nearby Bullock's Orioles. My notes described this color as half-way between the lemon fruit (Citrus limonia) color of Scott's Oriole and the orange fruit (Citrus sinensis) color of Bullock's. All of the yellow-orange parts of the bird appeared to be the same color, without and deeper or more faded hues. This color was more consistent with the western subspecies, I.c. nelsoni, than the Texas or eastern subspecies, I. c. sennetti.

The crown was also this yellow-orange color, as were the nape, sides, bely, rump (upper tail coverts) and vent. The tail was all black, above and below, with a slightly rounded tip. The combination of the yellow-orange vent and rump created a circumferential band of this color around the caudad part of the bird's body, separating the black upper back from the all black tail. The wings often obscured my view of the junction between the black upper back and rump, so it was not easy to say how far down the back extended caudad. I would say that the lower back was the same yellow-orange color as the rump.

Although the initial glance at the wings revealed what looked like an all black wing with the one strong, narrow, pure white wing bar, closer inspection showed that there were also subtle white edges of the primaries, secondaries and tertials. This created some subtle white "streaking" on that part of the wing, and in this respect the more distal part of the wing was not all black. I did not really see what looked like a second wing bar. I did not notice the underwing color.

The bird's throat and breast were adorned with a black shield, much wider than the beak and wide enough to be easily seen from the side, even if the bird's head was slightly turned away. This was continuous with a black mask that extended from the black lores down across the malar area and into the throat, but not posterior to the eye or above the eye. This pattern formed a right angle with the black area dropping straight down from behind the eye and straight forward toward the culmen. The cheekds and ear coverts behind the eye were yellow-orange, as was the nape. The lower edge of this breast-shield was rounded like the lower part of a police-man's badge or Greecian shield, not pointed like a Rose-breasted Grosbeak. Yellow-orange sides separated the black of the breast from the black wing.

Soft parts: The beak and eye were black. The feet were either black or dary gray.
Behaviors: For the initial, shorter sighting on June 18, the bird was perched on a fence, then was briefly harassed and chased to a nearby tree by two male and two female Bullock's Orioles (Icterus bullockii). Longer observations occurred on June 19th, and the Hooded Oriole, no longer bullied by the Bullocks, was perched high in large trees, 5-10 meters up. When perched it did not move around much. It was also seen flying directly from tree to tree, about every ten to twenty minutes and at times "disapeared" into the dense foliage. I once saw it perch on the large trumpet vine hedge, but never saw it feeding, preening or doing any other behavior.
Call: The Hooded Oriole was silent. If it did make any calls, they might have been drowned out by chattering Bullock's harrassing it.
Plumage: Alternate, male

Similar Species Discussion

a) The adult male Bullock's Oriole was the easiest and most important similar species to be considered, because that bird is the common and expected oriole in the McElmo Creek riparian habitat. Our bird's yellow-orange color, especially the crown, the all black tail, narrower wing bar and the wider area of black on the throat and breast ruled out Bullock's Oriole. It also lacked the thin black line through the eye of Bullock's Oriole. Hooded Orioles have black malar areas, whereas only the throat of Bullock's Oriole is black - the malar area is orange.

b) Baltimore Oriole. The adult male has a black hood, crown and upper back.

c) Orchard Oriole. The adult male is a chestnut color and has a black hood. First spring male Orchard Orioles are similar in patter, but not color or size to this adult male Hooded Oriole. They are about an inch smaller and have a more rounded head. The black areas on the face and throat are similar in size and shape to Hooded Orioles, and both species lack a black crown. However, first spring male Orchard Orioles are yellowish below, not yellow-orange and the upper parts of the bird are greenish. The wings and tail are dark greenish, not black, and that makes the wing bar appear less bold.

d) Altamira Oriole (I. gularis). This species is much larger and would have dwarfed the neary-by Bullock's Orioles. I saw the wing pattern well enough to notice that our Hooded Oriole lacked the orange intrusion into the bend of the wing that is a feature of adult Altamiras. Immature Altamiras are yellowish in color and lack black wings and tail. Hooded has more black in the malar region than Altamira.

e) Scott's Oriole (I. parisorum), present but rare in xeric parts of Montezuma County, looks a lot different from the bird in question. Adult and first-spring males are a lemon-yellow color with a complete black hood and nape. Adult Scott's Orioles' tails are only black distally, with the proximal part of the tail yellow both above and below. Immature Scott's Orioles tails are not black. Female Scott's Orioles are greenish-yellow, far different from the oriole at the Zwicker ranch.

f) What about Streak-backed Oriole (I. pustulatus)? Actually, its appearance is somewhat similar to the bird we observed, in that it also has an orange crown. However, there are many points of distinction, including the Streak-backed Oriole's richer orange color, more extensive white on the wings and the streaks on its back. I saw clearly that our Hooded Oriole had an all black upper back, not streaked across the back between the wings. The black area of the face, throat, and breast of the Streak-backed Oriole, while more extensive than on a Bullock's Oriole, is narrower than on the Hooded Oriole.

g) The Orange Oriole (I. auratus) of Mexico is somewhat similar, but is smaller, has an orange back and a different pattern of white in the black wing.

h) Other Central American Orioles: Bar-winged Oriole (I. maculialatus), Black-headed Oriole (I. graduacauda) and Black-cowled Oriole (I domenicensis) have completely black hoods. Our oriole lacked the breast spots fo the Spot-breasted Oriole (I. pectoralis).

i) For completeness, our bird had a bill that was pointed, narrower and blacker than the superficially similar Black-headed Grosbeak (Pheucticus melanocephalus).

Finally, compared to all other orioles that occur or could occur in Colorado, Hooded Oriole has a proportionately longer and slimmer bill, which is also more decurved, especially the lower mandible.

Previous Experience

I have seen Hooded Orioles in Arizona, but not for many years and never have had extensive personal encounters. I am very familiar with Orchard, Scott's and Bullock's Orioles, having birded actively in Colorado since 1974. I "grew up with" Baltimore Orioles, having birded in Missouri and other eastern states between 1957 and 1973. I have seen a few Altamira Orioles in Texas and Streak-backed Orioles in California and Costa Rica many years ago, as well as many Mexican and Central American oriole species.
