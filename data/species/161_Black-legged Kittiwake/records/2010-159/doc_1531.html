Habitat

Mainly in flight above the open water of Union Reservoir.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  I used a 10X Nikon EDG binocular. Connie had a binocular, plus she got the bird in a Zeiss scope
Distance:  See above. About as close as 250 feet.
Light:  Excellent. We had a bit of backlighting, but it was otherwise overcast. As Connie and I approached the vantage point and started looking at the gulls, we commented on the excellent viewing conditions.

Description of the Bird

Subadult, probably a juvenile. Seen in flight from above, the bird had a medium-gray mantle with a striking, thick, black "M"-pattern. The inner primaries and outer secondaries (under the "humps" of the "M") were extensively white. The bird had a dusky collar and a dusky patch behind the eye. Otherwise, it was white. In this regard, I note that the tail tip was either entirely white or almost entirely white; wasn't positive.

Seen in direct comparison with an adult Bonaparte's Gull (sitting side-by-side on the water) the bird was notably larger.

In flight, its wings seemed notably long and narrow. Its insubstantial black legs were briefly noted on several (relatively) close passes.
Behaviors: The bird was first seen in flight, passing quickly by from left to right at a distance of perhaps 250 feet. That is the closest we got to the bird. It was observed in flight--at all angles--for a full 2 minutes, at distances ranging from 250 feet to well over 1,000 feet. The bird flew about quickly and dartingly ("bat out of hell" comes to mind) most of the time it was under observation. It alighted on the water briefly (see above), then put into flight again.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Juvenile? (see below)

Similar Species Discussion

Well, the only expected similar species is juvenile Bonaparte's Gull, ruled out by plumage and direct size comparison. Sabine's Gull and juvenile Little Gull are ruled out by plumage and size differences. (See descriptions above.)

Resources Used

None, except for consideration of subsequent observations and descriptions of the bird posted online. Cole Wild considered the bird to be in its second cycle, whereas Nick Komar considered the bird to be in its first cycle and referable to the nominate subspecies tridactyla. Based on my observations, I would say the bird was in its first cycle, and I don't know the subspecies.

Previous Experience

I've seen hundreds of Black-legged Kittiwakes, including an inland bird in winter in New Mexico about 15 years ago; most others have been at seawatches or on pelagic trips (both coasts of the USA). I have extensive with all possible comparison species, except for Little Gull, which I've seen only once (but pondered on many occasions!).
