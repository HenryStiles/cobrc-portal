Description of the Bird

The bird was a medium-sized gull with a striking wing pattern. The head was mostly white with a slight gray smudge in front of the eye, a black spot in the auriculars the spread upward and paled to dark gray and formed a vertical bar that just reached the crown, a gray nape, and a wide, well-defined black nuchal collar. The nuchal collar was separated from the pale-medium gray back by a vague band of whitish. The lower scapulars had a brownish tinge and the rump and uppertail coverts were white. The tail was bright white with a wide, well-defined black terminal band. The underparts were entirely white.

The bases of the uppersides of the wings were the same gray as the back and contrasted strongly with the wide, black diagonal bar that extended from the innermost greater and median coverts throught the outer lesser coverts to the wrist and then angled out along the leading edge of the wing through the alula and primary coverts to encompass the outer four primaries. The rest of the remiges were white except that P5-6 had subterminal black straps. The inner greater coverts that were not black were gray and as that tract continued distally, the outer webs got more and more white. The innermost primary covets were white.

The undersides of the wings were almost entirely bright white, except that the very wingtip was black with that black continuing up the leading edge of the wing almost to the wrist.

The eyes, bill, and legs were all blackish.
Behaviors: The bird spent much of its time swimming on the water, but would occasionally fly around the northern part of the reservoir relatively low over the water looking for prey. At times it would plunge into the water trying for, and succeeding often, small fish. Almost every plunge I saw was in very shallow water in the NW corner of the small bay just east and north of the boat ramp on the north side of the reservoir.
Call: none heard
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

No other gulls particularly similar, though juveniles of Little, Black-headed, and Bonaparte's somewhat so. Those are readily eliminated by the upperparts pattern, head pattern, and leg and bill colors.

Previous Experience

I have seen 10s of 1000s of BLKIs, most recently in Alaska in July 2004. I have seen 1000s of first-cycle Bonaparte's Gulls, ~4 first-cycle Black-headed Gulls, and a few first-cycle Little Gulls. I have also seen ~10 first-cycle Red-legged Kittiwakes, which aren't really an ID contender.
