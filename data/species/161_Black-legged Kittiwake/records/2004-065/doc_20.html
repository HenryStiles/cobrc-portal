Description of the Bird

A medium-sized gull. Sitting on the water, a dark (nearly black) bar extending from the lesser coverts back through the tertials contrasted with the medium-gray upperparts. At times the forward part of this dark bar was partially obscured by white flank feathers, and at other times it showed more completely, giving the bird alternating lighter and darker overall appearances depending on the feather positioning. A nearly black nuchal collar separated the mantle from the upper nape. There was a dark bar running vertically behind the eye, about as wide as the white band separating it from the eye. It began slightly below eye level, black centered at the bottom and fading upward nearly to white by the time it reached the crown. The other facial feathers were white. The eye was black. The head had a rounded, almost domed shape. The bill was all black, not as pointy as a Bonaparte's Gull yet not as robust as a Ring-billed Gull's. The bill slightly droops in the distal third portion. The throat, breast, belly, and flanks, and undertail coverts appear clean white. The primaries appear mostly black (some occasional white inner webbing shows through depending on how the wing is held) and appear proportionately long on the swimming bird.

In flight the bird is seen to have black feet and legs. The uppertail coverts are white, and the tail is white with a solid black terminal tail band. A strong M pattern is formed on the upper wing surfaces by the black outer primary edges and ends of the lesser coverts. The back feathers are mostly medium gray, quickly transitioning to a thin white border just below the black nuchal collar. The rest of the upper wing surface (below each limb on the M is primarily white. The outer primaries are dark tipped, with the amount of white in the inner webbing increasing inward along the wing. P6 only has a small black tip, and the remaining inner primaries are white tipped. These dark primary tips are also visible from below and provide the most distinctive marking on the underwing. Otherwise there is a pale wedge in the flight feathers expanding outward on the trailing edge of the wing, with the coverts appearing more gray. There isn't a whole lot of contrast between the flight feathers and coverts on the underside of the wing. The bird has a double-banded appearance on the head and neck in flight, created by the post-eye bar and the nuchal collar.
Behaviors: When we arrived, My dad & I spotted the bird preening near the south shore. As we were moving to a better position we lost track of the bird. A while later Maggie Boswell spotted the bird standing on the north shoreline near the fishing dock. It made a looping flight and landed behind the dock out of view from the south side of the lake. We drove to the north side, where we met Tony and Nancy, who were watching the bird resting on the water near the fishing dock. It made a couple of other short flights, presumably foraging, before returning to the water about 30 meters from shore to rest. There were many other gulls present on the lake, most notably about 100 Bonaparte's Gulls.
Call: Not Heard
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

The combination of the nuchal collar, post-eye bar, dark wing bar, and black legs seems to rule out other N.A. gulls. For example, Similarly-aged Red-legged Kittiwake had reddish legs and lacks the bold "M" in the upper wings. Little, Bonaparte's, and Black-headed Gulls can all show a post-eye spot or bar but lack the nuchal collar and have colored (Red, pink, or orange) legs.
