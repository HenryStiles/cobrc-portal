Description of the Bird

the bird was a small gull, only slightly larger than nearby Bonaparte's Gull. The bill was quite short and thick (for a gull), and entirely yellow.

The head was white, with a bold very dark gray spot behind the eye, and a very pale gray wash over the nape and back of the head. The underparts were white. The mantle was a meduim slate-gray, a few shades darker than nearby Ring-billed Gulls, and about the same shade as the Mew Gull we saw later at the same location. In flight the paler gray primaries contrasting with the darker gray mantle was striking. The wings were tipped in black. The tail was white, and square tipped, with a very slight fork.

On the water, only the outer wingtips appeared black, with a white space between the black of the wingtips and gray of the mantle, distinctively different from the wingtip pattern of other nearby gulls.
Behaviors: We first saw the gull fly close by us below the overlook, and then saw it perched on the water fairly close to shore. It then took off and flew south, out of site. The weather was poor, with a snow-sleet mix for most of the morning, and I believe the bird was forced down onto the reservoir because of these conditions. We saw the bird during the first break in the weather that morning.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult basic

Similar Species Discussion

All other species of Gull except Mew were ruled out by the unmarked yellow bill, and all gulls, including Mew were ruled out by the distinctive wing pattern in flight, with the paler primaries contrasting with the darker mantle. The slightly-forked square tail and distinctive head pattern with gray nape and black spot behind the eye also ruled out all other species.
