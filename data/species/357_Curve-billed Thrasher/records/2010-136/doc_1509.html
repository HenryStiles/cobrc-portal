Description of the Bird

Gray bird approximately size and shape of nearby Western Scrub-Jays feeding on the ground near bird-feeders; tail without noticeable markings, approximately as long as body; wings with barely noticeable wingbars formed by pale gray tips to a few greater coverts. Apparently missing pale gray-tipped coverts may indicate active molt. Pale gray underparts heavily spotted with large dark-gray, roundish spots, heaviest on upper breast, fading on lower breast and flanks; belly becoming salmon-colored, heaviest on vent. Throat clear white with thin dark gray malar stripe, white malar with remainder of head cold gray as the upperparts (back, wings, tail). Iris orange. Bill long (2/3 or 3/4 as long as the width of the head from bill to hindcrown). Bill black with possibly paler base of both mandible and maxilla. Maxilla strongly curved; mandible weakly curved. Legs and feet black.
Behaviors: Hopping on ground feeding; territorial, chasing off other birds.
Call: none
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

LeConte's, California and Crissal Thrasher lack spots on breast; Sage, Brown and Long-billed Thrasher much straighter bill, and streaked rather than spotted on breast. Bendire's Thrasher less curved mandible; smaller less-rounded markings on breast; slightly smaller bird, although the size of this bird was not well observed or documented. Canyon Towhee, Abert's Towhee and California Towhee are similar in overall color but have shorter, conical bill; smaller size and unspotted.

Resources Used

Sibley Guide to Western Birds, consulted after the sighting; internet photos of Texas and Arizona Curve-billed Thrashers and Bendire's Thrashers.

Previous Experience

I have observed "oberholseri" Curve-billed Thrasher in Colorado about a dozen times in the last decade; several dozen recent observations of "palmeri" Curve-billed Thrasher around Pheonix, Arizona, including two adults in the hand. This bird does not exactly match the photographs or artist depictions of either type very well.

Additional Information

Comments: The lack of observed pale tips to outer rectrices may be due to the folded tail; tail was never observed in flight (spread). I suspect that this bird is a hatch-year that has completed its preformative molt, at least the remiges including the rectrices. Many passerines retain juvenal rectrices, which have much less white or pale gray in outer rectrices compared with adults. Several features of this Curve-billed Thrasher seemed unusual, including bright white throat and malar; very heavy spotting; shortish bill (perhaps due to young age); apparent pale base of bill, suggestive of Bendire's Thrasher - better photos needed. I do not know how T.c.curvirostre of southeast Arizona, or other subspecies of Curve-billed Thrasher (perhaps from further south in Mexico), differ from our "oberholseri". It may be that the Colorado-breeding birds differ somewhat from the Texas birds.
