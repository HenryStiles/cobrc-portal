Description of the Bird

DESCRIPTION – The decurved bill and brown/gray coloring immediately made this bird a Curve-billed Thrasher. Size was about the same as a Robin. The decurved bill was all dark and over an inch long and the iris was yellow. Coloring was uniform on the head and wings and lighter mottled brown/gray on the breast and belly Also the throat showed faint malar streaking. I did not notice wing-bars while observing the bird, but looking at the photos one can see very faint wing-bars. The lack of strongish wing-bars puts this into the Arizona ssp. (palmeri) rather than with the birds that are resident SE Colorado (oberholseri).

Similar Species Discussion

In size, general shape, and plumage this bird resembles several other species, most closely Bendire’s Thrasher. Also similar in silhouette and size are Western Scrub-Jay, American Robin, and the other thrashers. Lighting was adequate when this bird was seen so Scrub-Jay and Robin were eliminated at once by plumage. Several species have a decurved bill like the bird we observed, Brown Thrasher, Long-billed Thrasher, Ocellated Thrasher (Central Mexico), Curve-billed Thrasher (Adult), California Thrasher, Crissal Thrasher, and LeConte’s Thrasher. The bird seen was brown/gray overall, Long-billed Thrasher, Brown Thrasher, and Ocellated Thrasher are all rufous-backed and can be eliminated. Iris color is yellow on the bird unlike California Thrasher and LeConte’s Thrasher, both have dark irises. The bird seen had much weaker malar streaks and much less curvature of the bill than Crissal Thrasher. Sage Thrasher has a straight bill, not decurved like the bird that was observed on 12/05. Last Bendire’s Thrasher straight-billed, but is similar in plumage to the bird that we observed. Juvenile Curve-billed Thrashers have fairly straight bills, however the bill on the bird we observed was obviously decurved and could not possibly have been a juv. CBTH or a Bendire’s Thrasher. Our bird without question was an adult Curve-billed Thrasher.
