Habitat

Canyon wash in farm fields interspersed with Pinyon Juniper. Canyon was about 20 feet deep and maybe 300 feet wide. Within the canyon were a lot of cottonwoods and Russian Olive. Bird was frequenting a feeder where it was originally reported by the homeowner. Not a otn of low brush in the area by the feeders, but back down the washt ere was a good amount of low RO that the bird seemed to be sitting in when it wasn't at the feeders.

Description of the Bird

Finder: Gib Rokich

Identification: The bird was bright yellow on the chest and back, with dark gray wings and tail. There were white spots on the greater coverts, and the vent was white. The bill was massive and dark gray, with the lower mandible being lighter than the upper. I am not sure how to sex these birds, but there seems to be a general concensus that this bird is an immature male. Female birds seem to have more of an eye stripe, but I have no experience with these birds, and someone who knows them better than I would need to weigh in.

Issue of Providence: This species is one of many that is popular in the caged bird trade, and so eliminating the possiblity of this being a caged bird was at the forefront of my mind after I saw it. I am inclined to think that this is a wild bird for a few reasons, which I will list here. Firstly, the bird is unbanded. That doesn't mean it is wild, but that is one of the most obvious ways to tell if a bird is an escapee. Secondly, the bird was very well flighted, demonstarting this by chasing orioles around in the trees above the feeder to keep them away. It was very deft in its movements and really maneuvered its way through the trees, which is something I would not think them capable of doing if they were a caged bird. The bird also was relativly wary of humans, about the same as the other grosbeaks in the area. It would sit high in the trees and watch the people on the porch, and when one birder adjusted themselves for a better look, the bird spooked into the taller trees behind the feeders and then waited before coming back in. Once it was on the feeders, the bird was very confiding and didn't seem scared of people, but if someone moved too quickly or got to close, it would hop back into the trees before returning to feed. This was pretty much exactly in line with what the other grosbeaks were doing, as the black headeds and blue grosbeaks were coming in to the feeders that were about 10 feet away from people. It is possible that the birds have gotten used to people from having Gib on the porch all the time watching them. The issues of wear and timing are the last things that I want to mention, since I think that they are both important. In terms of timing, this record lines up perfectly with other records from AZ and TX. This bird shows up between the last week of May and the first week of July in the US, so this record makes sense in terms of its timing. In terms of wear, I do not think that this bird shows signs of any cage wear. The tail and some of the secondaries were a little bit worn, as were some of the wing coverts, but the primaries seemed in perfect condition. Comparing the tail of the YEGR to the tail of the BHGR, I saw that both were rather worn, so I would postulate that the bird had a worn tail and secondaries due to its extremely long migration overshoot, rather than from being in a cage. If it was in a cage, I would imagine that it would be a poor flier and that the primaries would be the most damaged, but the bird is both a strong flier and has pretty much perfect primaries.

 Behavior: Bird was timid at first, and was first seen in flight where it flew deep into a bush about 30 yards form Gib's back porch, along the river. It sat in the bushes for about 10 minutes before subsequently disapearing. It returned to the feeder about an hour later, and then sat on and around the feeder for about 20 minutes. It was defending the feeders from other grosbeaks and orioles, and it was much larger that the other birds it was chasing. 

Similar Species Discussion

Nothing was considered, since there aren't really any other possibilities. Southern Yellow Grosbeak is non-migratory, and the souther population of yellow grosbeak in guatamala is also non-migratory, which makes me think that this is from the northern, migratory population of yellow grosbeak.
