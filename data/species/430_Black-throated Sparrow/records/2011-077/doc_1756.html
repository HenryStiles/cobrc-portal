Habitat

The general habitat is open ponderosa pine with Gambel's Oak in the understory. In the immediate vicinity of the sighting, there was an open meadow with several clusters of Gambel's Oak, but no nearby trees.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski EL 8.5 X 42 binoculars were used.
Distance:  I estimated the distance to be 30 to 40 feet.
Light:  Lighting was great. We were looking west with the sun behind us at the time of the sighting.

Description of the Bird

The sparrow was perched on a branch and facing me. The bird had 2 long, bold white stripes on each side of the head: one a bold supercilium, the other a malar stripe. These white stripes contrasted with the gray auricular patch, and the black color of the chin, throat and chest. From this anterior view of the bird, the black on the chest widened and formed an oval bib which tapered to somewhat of a point at the bottom. Otherwise, the bird was mostly gray in color. The bill was conical. The tail was relatively long and rounded. The length of the bird was similar to a Chipping Sparrow's length, although the build of the bird seemed not quite as thin (proportionally) as a Chipping Sparrow's build. The determination that the bird was a Black-throated Sparrow was made without the need for an earlier step of acknowledging that the bird was a sparrow.
Behaviors: I first saw the bird perched in a Gambel's Oak. Soon after giving directions on the location of the bird to my friend, the bird flew into the grass. From that vantage point, the bird was concealed from me.
Call: No vocalizations were heard.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Sage Sparrow would be the only other likely candidate. The location is outside of the range for a Five-striped Sparrow. A Sage Sparrow doesn't have the long white facial stripes; the Sage Sparrow has a supraloral white spot. The Sage Sparrow lacks the black chin, throat and extensive black color on the chest. If we wanted to consider a Five-striped Sparrow, the center of the throat would be white.
