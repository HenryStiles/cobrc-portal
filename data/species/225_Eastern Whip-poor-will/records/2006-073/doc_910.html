Description of the Bird

On the morning of 20 May 2006, myself and a group of birders had been birding near the banding station at Chico Basin Ranch. As the sun started heating up the area and the birding became quiet, I wandered east through the Russian Olive grove in search of anything. Soon thereafter, I flushed a relatively large nightjar from the ground. The bird was dark brown overall, had long, somewhat rounded wings and a fairly long, rounded tail. What immediately caught my eye was that there were white corners to the rectrices. The bird wheeled and landed roughly 10 feet above the ground on a Crack Willow branch. When perched, I could see a large dark grayish head with a black median crown stripe and large dark eyes against a darkish rusty-brown overall face color. The bird was hunchbacked and deep chested. The throat was blackish and bordered posteriorly by a narrow white crescent. The upperparts were varied vermiculations of brown and gray. A bold gray slash was apparent across the scapulars, bordered by black. The underparts were light brown. When in flight, the outer wings were fairly rusty-brown. When perched, the tail, which extended well past the primary tips, were brown with black bars. I would say that overall, the bird was roughly 10 inches in size.

I tried to get a quick photograph of the bird, but the bird took off and flew into a dense thicket of Russian Olive. I then went back and retrieved the rest of the birders and we spent well over an hour trying to refind it, but could not. However, as I was pretty sure the bird would remain at that location, a few of us stayed until nightfall to attempt it to vocalize. Near dark, the bird began vocalizing its explosive whip-poor-will call for about a 10 minute period.
Behaviors: Observed in flight, a weaving, fluttery flight close to the ground. Also observed perched horizontal on a tree limb with diagnostic large round head, hunch-back, and deep breast.
Call: An emphatic and explosive Whip-poor-will, accented on the poor.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Common Nighthawk was eliminated by that species white wing patches, pointed wings, obvious barring to the undersides, wing tips that do not reach the tail tip, and a tail that is forked and lacks broad white corners. Common Poorwill was not considered as it is smaller, and has a short tail, with wing tips virtually reaching the tail tip. Pauraque was eliminated because of its much longer tail and presence of white wing bars. Chuck-will’s-widow was not considered due to its larger size, flattened crown, lack of scapular slash and black scap borders, and white in tail represented only along the inner webs of the entire rectrices (not white corners).
