Description of the Bird

After most of the birders had left, Brian Gibbons shone a powerful flashlight at the Russian-olives from which the bird had been calling, hoping to catch eyeshine. To our surprise, he did manage to catch an orange eyeshine from about 15 feet up in a large willow that was sticking up out of the Russian-olive grove. The bird was on the back side of the tree. Several times we saw the eyeshine move as the bird sallied out short distances from the perch, then returned to the same perch or to a nearby one. Sometimes the eyeshine would temporarily disappear during these forays. After a few minutes we decided to walk over to the other side of the shelterbelt to see if we could get a better look at the bird. When we arrived, we could not detect any eyeshine from the area where the bird had been perching, but then suddenly it flew through the flashlight beam, and Brian was able to track it for a good seven seconds or so as it flew straight down the shelterbelt to the west. It disappeared from view about 80 meters down. While it flew, I watched it naked-eye. Its size and shape were reminiscent of a mockingbird or, especially, a scrub-jay. It had rounded wings and a long rounded tail. The tail was probably a little shorter than a scrub-jay's tail. Overall the bird was mottled pale brown. There were no white flashes in the wings. I thought I might have seen a white throat, but I couldn't be sure. I definitely saw that the outer tail feathers were white from the tip to about 1/3 of the way towards the base.
Behaviors: See above for a description of its foraging behavior.
Call: The bird called several times spontaneously at around 8:40 PM (dusk/just after dusk). It repeated the classic "whip-poor-will" song a number of times, and also responded briefly to playback. I am submitting a sound recording of its song.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Nighthawks have white flashes in the wing and their wings are pointed. Their erratic flight style is totally different from the more controlled, somewhat owl-like, somewhat jay-like flight of this Caprimulgus nightjar. Chuck-wills-widow is far larger than Whip-poor-will and is eliminated by voice. Poorwill is smaller, with a much shorter tail, and is also eliminated by voice.
