Habitat

Plains riparian. Lots of cottonwoods and scrubby undergrowth. LOTS of leaf-litter everywhere along the south side of Sand Creek. Bird always flushed from dense cover of twiggy undergrowth.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Brunton 7.5x43
Distance:  Estimate 6-30 yards (flushed at 6-10 yards)
Light:  Sky was overcast with even lighting all around.

Description of the Bird

Medium sized brown bird a little larger than the Wilson's Snipe we flush about 7 minutes prior to seeing the woodcock. Bird was plump or "chunky" as it flew. Bill was long and straight. Wings relatively short and slightly rounded (not severely pointed like most other shorebirds). Head, back and tail from the top were medium to dark brown. The undersides were a light reddish-buff we all noted this as we had just seen a Wilson's Snipe and all immediately called it correctly based upon seeing the white belly as it flew past us.
Behaviors: We initially flushed the woodcock unknowingly and only knew it once we heard the twittering of its wings (I did not personally see the bird at that time, however Randy Lentz did as he was up high on the hillside). We regrouped and spread out to methodically go through the area the bird was see to land in. As we go close (approximately 6-10yards) the bird took off, flew up high over the hill side and nearly buzzed Randy as it flew east). We noted the birds likely position (could never see the actual spot the bird came down in due to the trees and underbrush blocking our view) and proceded to slowly search for it again. We flush it twice more (never getting to see it on the ground, typical!) and the last time I flushed it from a dense twiggy thicket and it flew out and over Glenn, Paul and Charlie who all got great looks at its undersides! The bird was looked for once more but could not be refound. Each time the bird was flushed it flew from some pretty dense scrubby cover (not from the leaf litter out in the open). And each time the bird flushed we could hear the wing "twitter" as it flushed. The "twitter" either stopped on its own or we could no longer hear it because we were out of range a short time after seeing the bird pass by. Basically, after the bird past us by, we stopped hearing the wing "twitter".
Call: No song or call heard. Wing "twitter" heard four times (the number of times the bird flushed). Wing "twitter" was of a short duration (either because it stopped as the bird flew or perhaps we became "out of range" and were not able to hear the sound).
Plumage: Basic

Similar Species Discussion

Wilson's Snipe has white belly (we saw a Wilson's Snipe fly by about 7 minutes prior to seeing the Woodcock) and all immediately called this bird correctly based upon the slimmer body and white belly.
Dowitchers do not prefer forest under story habitat, and do not have a wing "twitter" when flushed.

Resources Used

Members of the group had a Nat Geo Guide and a Sibley Guide with. Birders present did look up both snipe and woodcock prior to departing the car. That's how EVERYONE called the snipe correctly when it was first seen and gave us a great comparison for when the woodcock flushed.

Previous Experience

Have see one woodcock in Colorado (Rocky Ford bird) and many in Virginia in winter. Close up views were gotten in Virginia and at Crane Creek, Ohio.

Additional Information

Comments: Randy Lentz found this bird on the Denver Urban Christmas count and called me up asking how to write up and document a bird seen for only seconds and only the "ass-end" of the bird. I told him to do his best and just describe what he did see and hear. The next day several of us were going to bird the Pueblo / Canon City area. We all met and I asked Randy if he wouldn't mind relocating this bird. He agreed and we found it after a little effort. That's when the problem question came up. Do we (and how do we) report this bird. All day long we talked about this, Hugh Kingery even called me up that evening!

We decided that the birds welfare (and well-being) was at risk if we reported it. Since most birds you can drive up to and see the bird (waterfowl, warblers, etc.) without HAVING to disturb it. The ONLY way to see this bird is to flush it. Had we reported this bird MANY people would have come to see it and add it to either (or a combination of) their Live List, State List, Yearly List, 2005 List, Western half of the county List, etc. This would have stressed the bird out and it likely would have left the area and settled and area less conducive of survival through the winter. It was a tough decision as I know that many people would have chased it. It's a bummer knowing about a good bird but not being able to tell anyone. I even thought of leading a group or two to see it...Hugh Kingery said that the ABA convention trip to see Boreal Owl did this and lots of people got to see the owl with minimal disturbance to the owl...until he later found out that within two weeks of the trip there became a well-worn path from the road up to the actual nest box! Apparently, several of the ABA trip participants went back with friends and tapped on the nest box. At least the owl had and "option" of either flushing or not. The Woodcock really does not have that option since the footsteps would be getting closer, and closer....

I probably shouldn't even submit the record yet but wanted to check out the new website. If you or other records committee members go, please do NOT tell anyone else (or show anyone else). I appreciate your help in this matter. :-)
