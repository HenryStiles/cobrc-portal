Description of the Bird

Initial tentative identification of this bird by Joe Mammoser and me was based on "jizz" and habitat. Our confidence in that tentative identification was strengthened by other observations at the same location later in the same day.

At about 8:00am, Joe Mammoser was walking toward the SW corner of the property perhaps 10 yards in front of me. Joe flushed a bird that I saw flying past him and past me. The bird made no vocalizations as it flew. With my naked eyes, I saw the silhouette of a bird that seemed to have a shorebird profile and seemed to fly like a shorebird with its head higher than its wings. Relative to the size of its body, it had long wings and a very short tail. It seemed bigger than a peep but, on my first impression, a little smaller than a killdeer. In flight, the bird flew fast and stayed low to the ground, at roughly six feet. Due to the lighting conditions, I couldn’t observe anything at all about its coloration or its bill. As we approached the area where we thought the bird might have landed, I saw it fly across the Arboretum at 5-10 feet height and out of sight to the east. Again it made no vocalizations, and again my impression was of a shorebird in flight. 

Based on jizz, Joe and I agreed that the mystery bird seemed to be a shorebird. Given the habitat, especially the lack of water, we both speculated that the least unlikely of all the unlikely shorebird options for this habitat at this time of year was American Woodcock. I posted on COBIRDS that morning: “We flushed a bird from the ground that had the GISS [sic] of a shorebird...  Woodcock?” Joe called Dave Leatherman to relay what we saw. 

At about 11:00am, Andy Bankert and David Leatherman flushed a bird in the same exact location that they identified as an American Woodcock. Andy got a second look at the bird as it flew over a building and was able to see buffy underparts. Later in the day I returned to the property with Andy and four other birders. At about 3:45pm, Andy and Austin Hess and I were walking a tree/shrub line next to a parking lot in the apartment complex west of the Arboretum when we flushed the bird yet again from just a few yards away. It seemed to me to lift up from the ground before flying forward, and the flutter of its wings was audible. It flew out on the side of the tree line that Andy and Austin were on, and they identified the bird as American Woodcock. I was on the opposite side of the tree line from where it emerged, and was only able to see an obscured silhouette of the same shape and size that I’d seen in the morning, flying away at head height without vocalizing.

Similar Species Discussion

We considered other shorebirds, doves, and pigeons. The complete lack of water on or near the property argued against Wilson's Snipe and most shorebird species. Woodcock seemed to be more likely to be seen away from water than any other shorebirds except Killdeer. Woodcock seemed to be more likely seen in this forest setting, especially with some leaf cover, than any other shorebird, including Killdeer. The bird did not vocalize when flushed, which is uncharacteristic of Killdeer. It did not make the characteristic "scaipe" call that snipe make when flushed. None of the observers saw any of the diagnostic facial markings or red-orange rump of Killdeer, and the tail was too short for Killdeer. The bird was too large to be a peep sandpiper, and too small to be a yellowlegs or dowitcher species. It was too stocky and short-tailed for a dove or pigeon, and the sound its wings made in flight was not the whistle of a dove or pigeon.

Resources Used

Each time I saw this bird it was with naked eyes. There was no time to use my binoculars; later at home I used Sibley guide, iBird Plus phone app, and eBird as references.

Previous Experience

When I lived in Wisconsin from 1992 to 2011, I frequently visited the University of Wisconsin - Madison Arboretum in spring (maybe 8-10 times) to listen to and watch American Woodcock as they performed their mating flights. This happens in the very last light of the day. I have seen how these birds fly, and seen their silhouettes many times. I have seen them on the ground in dim light but have never seen one in broad daylight.
