Description of the Bird

Earlier this month (February 2014) an American Woodcock was observed by many observers on a Sunday and Monday in Fountain Creek Regional Park near trail mile marker 18.  Since I now live in Fort Collins, I was not able to search for the bird until Tuesday and I dipped on seeing it.  Before going down to Fountain Creek Regional Park, I thought I better check my field guides and look at field marks to refresh my memory.  I had never seen an American Woodcock before as far as I knew.

This past Saturday evening, February 22, I was perusing through my file of bird photos and came across some photos that I had taken in Fountain Creek Regional PArk on January 15, 2013.  Yes the year is correct, 2013.  I said, "Holy cow, you got to be kidding me!"  The photos looked like an American Woodcock to me.  I sent two of the photos to Bill Maynard, Mark Peterson and Brandon Percival and they all concurred that the bird was an AMWO.  Hence this rare bird report.  Might it be the first AMWO sighting for El Paso County?

What do I remember from that day over a year ago?  I remember being cold.  It was toward the end of the day and I was in a hurry to get back to the car to warm up. The particular spot where I saw the bird is along the small stream that flows out of the Widefield Homes sewage treatment and which flows parrallel to the regional trail.  It is the area that is a bit south of where the Winter Wren often shows up.  It is also the area where we see Wilson Snipe when they are present in the park.  I remember seeing the long bill and stripes on the bird's back and thinking, "Oh it is a Wilson's Snipe. Cool! Since it isn't moving, I'll take its picture".  According to the photos that I still have, the time was 4:29:01 p.m. - 4:31:06 p.m.

I am embarrassed that I did not pay closer attention.  I feel like I failed the test, especially when I have been teaching novist birders in the Birding for Beginners classes at the nature centers and encouraging them to look at all birds well.

My description of the bird comes from the photos that I took.  I did not write down any field notes for this bird.  The bird was roughly snipe-sized, rather plump, ten inches or so long. It was difficult for me to tell how long the tail was from the angle from which I was looking at the bird.  I also could not tell how long the legs were, the bird was resting on a small branch on the water. The branch being about an inch or an inch and one-half in diameter.  The two bold longtitudinal stripes on the back of the bird were light colored (gray/whitish).  The bill was 3-4 inches long. Looking at the photo is readily apparent that the sides and breast of the bird are buffy orange.  Much different than a Wilson's Snipe.  Also the stripes on the nape are horizontal on this bird, not longtitudinal as seen on a snipe.  The eye also appears quite beady or large compared to a snipe.  The bird also doesn't match a description for a dowitcher that would be way out of season here.  The bird did not make any calls or sounds from what I remember.

Similar Species Discussion

Wilson Snipe does not have the horizontal stripes on the nape as this bird does.  Also the longtitudinal strips on the back of this bird seem way to bold for a snipe.  The eye also seems larger and higher than a snipe.

Dowitchers also do not ahve the horizontal stripes on the nape as this bird does.

I am not aware of any other bird here in Colorado or North American that has a bill this long for the size of the bird.
