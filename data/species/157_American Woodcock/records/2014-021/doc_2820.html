Description of the Bird

Bird first noticed when it was seen "poking" at a very close female Gadwall with it's long bill.  First assumption was that the bird would be a Wilson's Snipe, but the bird was out in the open and easily visibile. Dan said "the head stripes are horizontal, not vertical" and I said "It's an American Woodcock!", or something to that effect.  Field markings were considered to be perfect for this species, plump orangish bodied bird with large dark eye with whitish eye ring, dark horizontal stripes confined to the head, with thin invervening yellowish stripes, and three dark horizontal body stripes on the bird's back, one midline, two other centered over the tops of the wings on the standing bird. The bird was always silent.  We were able to observe a lot of feeding activity.  The bird would push a foot into the marshy soil at the edge of the creek, with a kind of "pumping" motion.  It would immediately follow by probing, frequently deeply, with it's long bill, occasionally producing an apparent invertebrate, some of which were quite long, which it would consume.  It would take breaks, some longer than others, but never quit feeding, at least occasionally, in the observation period.

Similar Species Discussion

Wilson's Snipe, as noted in previous description.  Also, this bird had unmarked orangish body, rather than striped flanks as expected on a Wilson's Snipe. Humorously mentally ruled out other possible shorebirds with similar coloration (think dowitchers), as no other bird has exactly this body habitus (dumpy shorted legged bird with amazingly long bill).
