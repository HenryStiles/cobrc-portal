Description of the Bird

A group of a couple hundred cormorants were standing with some gulls pretty much due north of us. The gulls moved around a fair bit, preening, walking, etc. The cormorants moved less often. After banging our heads against a couple of ugly first cycle hybrid gulls, I scanned the cormorant flock again (I'd been doing so every couple minutes), and I saw a very small cormorant. I showed the bird to Dave. He noted the long tail. At that point, its head was tucked in. When it lifted its head, so that its position and posture was very similar to adjacent cormorants, we were certain of its petite size. 

We closed the distance by about 50%, so that we were about 100 meters from the bird. We watched it for 15 minutes pretty much continuously, both of us. My camera was back in the car. Here are my notes from the closer viewpoint: Much smaller than DCCO, even when bird fully erect. Bird appeared perhaps 2/3 length of DCCO and 1/2 bulk. Tail visible much of time and was distinctly proportionately longer than the tails of nearby DCCO's, which appeared not as sleek in comparison. Plumage appeared entirely glossy black. The gular pouch of the NECO was small and narrow and pointed posteriorly. The gular pouch color was yellow-orange. The bill and lores were black or blackish. The orange in the loral area in all nearby DCCO was easily visible. White border to gular pouch was not seen. Bill proportionately thinner with less notable hook than on DCCO. 

Comparing to images in Macauley Library: The size difference between NECO and DCCO in our bird was similar to that seen at ML95925241. A photo of an adult, or adult-like NECO, lacking white trim to gular pouch in spring can be found at ML95688831 (Note that the Upper Queens bird had a much smaller gular pouch than this bird). Other adult April/May adult NECO with minimal (that would not have been seen at 100 meters) border to gular pouch can be found at ML95412411 and ML95385401

Similar Species Discussion

DCCO and hybrid

The Upper Queens NECO was typical in size for that species. It had a small thin gular patch, pointed sharply to the rear. Its tail was proportionately and appropriately long. The lores lacked color. The bill was not as proportionately hefty as that of a DCCO. There was nothing to suggest anything but NECO except that we could not detect a white border to the gular pouch. However, similar birds can be found in Macauley. Another point is that most if not all of the adult DCCO raised their crests during interactions with neighboring birds while we watched. No crests were seen on the NECO

Resources Used

Macauley to more fully evaluate the significance of lack of visible white border to gular pouch. Otherwise, no resources
