Description of the Bird

I was driving south on Chipeta Road, south of Montrose, CO. As I approached Chipeta Lake, I slowed to scan the lake quickly for birds. I noticed an odd shaped, black bird perched atop a dead tree branch protruding above the surface of the lake by several feet.  I proceeded to the next pull-out and then turned around and went back to have a better look at the bird. I drove into a pull-out where I could park safely.  I got out my binoculars and studied the bird.  The bird was solid black. Its neck was bent in a downward position in such a way that I could not discern any bill color. Its beak was thin and narrow.  The bird had a long tail and long neck.  I have never seen any other bird perch on any of the old wooden stumps or the branch before. Typically Chipeta Lake draws numerous species of ducks, especially in the fall/winter. Grebes frequent the lake as well as many coots.

I have observed birds at Chipeta Lake on almost a daily basis since moving to Montrose in 2007. I live only a few miles away.  I moved to Montrose from San Juan Island, in Puget Sound where I had lived for 12 years.  There I saw Double-crested cormorants on an almost daily basis as well as Brandt's and Pelagics.  This bird was considerably smaller than a Double-crested cormorant.  I have seen anhingas in Florida and a Great Cormorant on Prince Edward Island, Canada.

In May of this year, a Neotropic Cormorant was sighted by Coen Dexter at Chipeta Lake. Numerous people saw the bird in May. It relocated to a good size pond in a Montrose city park named Ute Park. It remained there for a few days. I do not know the exact date it was last seen at Ute Park, but I believe it was in May.

If my sighting does not constitue as a Rare Bird Sighting, I would understand, since it was reported by Mr. Dexter in May.

This cormorant did not move at any point while I was watching it or display any behavioral activity.

Similar Species Discussion

Very few birds even resemble a cormorant. The typical birds seen at Chipeta Lake are smaller ducks, grebes and coots.  None of these birds ever perch on top of the dead tree branch in the lake.  In considering other species of cormorants, all reside on in coastal waters except the Double-crested.  This bird was too small to be considered a DC Cormorant.  I have been watching Chipeta Lake since Coen Dexter reported the first sighting in May, in hopes that it might "re-appear."
