Description of the Bird

One adult and two or three juvenile or immature Neotropic Cormorants were seen on a Denver Field Ornithologists field trip led by Buzz Schaumberg on Oct 22, 2010. (I believe Buzz submitted the record.) The birds were on Cherry Creek Reservoir among several dozen Double-crested Corms. The adult was perched on a stump in the lake about 50 meters from shore. The immatures were on logs in the lake at a similar distance. The light was good coming from diagonally behind us. Observation was with 10x Zeiss bins and 30-60x Zeiss scope. We had been alerted to the Neotropic Corms by a group of Greater Denver Audubon Master Birders who were leaving the area as we approached.

We spotted the immatures first, and I was a bit skeptical because some immature Double-crested Corms show a trace of the white edge at the base of the bill which is characteristic of the Neotropic. However, the adult was a good clean ID. It was smaller than the nearby Double-cresteds, had the somewhat longer tail, and most notably had a distinct white boarder to the orange gular pouch.

The immatures also had a white border to the edge of the gular pouch that was fainter and less extensive along the edge of the maxilla than the mandible. We had a difficult time getting a good look at the front of the immatures, but on several 3/4 profile looks the neck and upper breast revealed a browner coloration than is characteristic of juvenile Double-cresteds.

I am familiar with this species from New Mexico where I documented the first nesting of the species in the state and more recently (April) from the Gulf Coast of Texas. The adult was definitely a Neotropic and the juveniles almost certainly were. This raises the interesting question of where these birds came from.
