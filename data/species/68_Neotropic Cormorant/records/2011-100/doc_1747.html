Description of the Bird

At about 10:30 AM, Tracy Clark and I were birding along the edge of a moderate-sized pond surrounded by bushes, trees, and patches of cattails. Suddenly, I saw a cormorant swimming about 150 feet away where I'd seen naught before. I was struck by the its lengthy appearance, and it seemed not quite as thick-necked/large-headed as Double-crested Cormorants typically do. When I raised my binoculars (Swarovski EL 10x42), the Neotropic Cormorant gular patch was immediately evident. We observed the bird for 5-10 minutes thereafter.

Overall, this was a dark brown immature cormorant. It appeared sleeker and not as bulky as a DCCO, reminding me of the difference between a Pacific Loon and a Common Loon. The chest and anterior neck were a bit paler than the remainder of the underparts/body, but not as pallid as a typical DCCO. The gular patch was yellow with an orange tinge. It was pointed towards the rear, smaller than normal for DCCO and within range of NECO, and bordered by a white line (feathering, not skin). The lores were dusky. The one benefit of the lighting conditions was that the skin of the gular patch would light up very brightly when exposed to the sun. During the duration of our observation, the lores were exposed to the sun repeatedly, and not even a tiny bit of orange or yellow reflected; the lores remained dusky in color. The bill was mostly pale, but with a dark culmen and tip. The bird's tail looked long as the bird swam in the water.

As I tried to get closer for a photo, the bird flew. Tracy got the good flight views. She is rather inexperienced, but I asked her the open-ended question of "Did this bird look different from the DCCOs we've seen in flight?" She said, "Yeah, it seemed to have a longer tail." This was unprompted, and though she had seen many DCCOs prior, her experience level was, nonetheless, low.

Similar Species Discussion

DCCO was eliminated by dark lores, shape and size of gular patch, proportionately long tail and more delicate head and neck.
