Description of the Bird

At about 9am, I was walking along the edge of a pond adjacent to Prewitt Reservoir. This was the same pond that hosted a Neotropic Cormorant in early July. I walked up to the edge of the pond, and saw a sleek looking cormorant 250-300 feet in front of me, sun coming it at lowish angle from side. The proportions of the bird, overall dark brown color, yellow-orange gular patch pointed posteriorly, and dark lores caused me to pull out my camera and start taking photos.

However, as I pulled out my camera, a second cormorant came gliding in and landed closer to me. It looked very similar to the bird I'd been looking at, which remained on the pond. The new arrival was 150 feet or so feet from me. The main differences in appearance was that the gular patch was smaller, and the new arrival seemed a tad heftier.

I watched both birds together for about 10 minutes (first one already observed for a couple minutes before first arrived), and then the first bird ran on water, then taking flight. It circled the pond 4-5 times, frustrating my attempts at photography by passing close to upper tree limbs. I then watched the second bird for about 5 more minutes. Thus each bird was observed for about 15 minutes.

Description for both birds: Uniformly dark brown plumage. Dusky lores. Gular pouch yellow tinged with orange and bordered by distinct white line. The gular pouch's bare skin really lit up in the sun, when bird at proper angle, which occurred repeatedly with both individuals. No orange/yellow was present in lores of either bird; the lores consistently appeared dusky. Bill pale with dark culmen (actually, nearly entire maxilla dark) and with dark tip. Tail proportionately long, most easily seen on Bird 1 in flight, when the body/tail behind wing was noted to be similar in length to chest/neck/head in front of wing. Both birds had a more sleek appearance than that of a DCCO. This was partly due to tail length, but partly due to a more delicate head.

Bird 1 vs Bird 2. Bird 1, the first seen and more distant, had a larger appearing gular patch. Nonetheless, the gular patch in the field appeared pointed posteriorly. Bird 1 also appeared a bit slimmer/sleeker in body, head, and bill than Bird 2, and I do not believe that this was due to distance. Also, note that in photographs, the photo giving the largest/ most square-shaped appearance to gular pouch of Bird 1 was shot from an angle that is not a side view, as bird had its head tilted. Instead, the gular pouch is as if viewed partly from below.
Behaviors: See above. Not seen diving.
Call: not heard

Similar Species Discussion

Bird 2 is easily separated from DCCO by its small gular pouch, pointed towards rear, and dark lores. It also appeared longer tailed, and was darker than the great majority of immature DCCO.

Bird 1 is more challenging from photo views due to its gular pouch being larger and seeming squared off. However, I believe this is partly due to angle (on-line search seems to indicate that a view from below causes gular pouch to appear more square when viewed partly from below) , and I also believe there is some variability in NECO regarding this mark.

See the following URLs for pics of NECOs with larger gular pouches with less pointed appearance.

http://kiwifoto.com/galleries/birds/neotropic_cormorant/ and http://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/Neotropic_Cormorant/id

For the second URL, scroll down page a short ways to see Kevin Karlson's pic of an imm Neotrop.
