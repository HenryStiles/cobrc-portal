Description of the Bird

This bird was standing on a mud flat with a long line (about fifty) of Double-crested Cormorants and it was immediately obvious that this bird was about two-thirds the size of the DCCOs. It was both smaller and slighter in build than the DCCOs. The top of its head was at the level of the top of body of the DCCOs. It was all black in plumage. The head, bill, and neck also were proportionally smaller than the DCCOs. The bill was nearly as long as the DCCO, but definitely thinner and appeared lighter at the base. I was unable to distinguish any white at the base of the bill. The head was relatively smooth in profile and did not show any signs of a crest. When the bird moved, I was able to see the tail which was proportionally quite long and all black. The legs appeared black. Also, the posture of the head of this bird was different than the DCCOs. While the Double-crested typically held their heads with the head and bill tilted upward about 10 to 15 degrees, this bird usually had only a small upward tilt to the head and bill and at times it was horizontal.
Behaviors: Bird was standing with a large group of Double-crested Cormorants on a mud flat at the western end of Bonny Reservoir. While the bird moved several times while I was observing it, it did not fly, feed, or swim.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Alternate

Similar Species Discussion

The only likely similar species is Double-crested Cormorant. Direct comparison showed this bird to be much smaller in size with a proportionally smaller head and bill, and a long tail, as noted above. This bird had Double-crested Cormorants standing on each side of it, so that direct comparison was possible.
