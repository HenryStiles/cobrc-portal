Description of the Bird

When I first saw the bird, it seemed small overall and long-tailed; it seemed "different" from the other Double-crested Cormorants out there. In direct comparison with nearby Double-crested Cormorants (which came and went during the period of observation), the bird in question appeared smaller and longer-tailed.

I observed the bird's gular patch from the bird's right side, its left side, and from below (i.e., with the bird looking toward me, bill pointing up.) At all angles, its gular patch appeared orange-yellow, not orange or bright orange; viewed in both profiles (left and right), the gular patch appeared relatively small and thin. On the bird's right side, the gular patch was bordered by a white brace, most prominent below; on the bird's left side, this brace was less obvious. I take the relative thinness and variability of the white bracing to be normal for an adult in worn plumage by late August.
Behaviors: During the entire period of observation, the bird was in a short, bare tree along the shore of Timnath Reservoir. It was fairly active, looking around a lot; but during the 20+ minutes of observation, I never saw it fly away.

Initially, the bird was in the general presence of at least 50 Double-crested Cormorants. I had already provisionally ID'd the bird in question as a Neotropic when all the Double-cresteds flew off. I think it may be a (minor) point of note that the Neotropic stayed put; "birds of a feather...."

During the rest of the period of observation, small numbers of Double-crested Cormorants (also Snowy Egrets and Great Blue Herons) were constantly wandering in and out of view. A few of the Double-crested Cormorants perched in the same small tree, for a minute or so, as the Neotropic Cormorant, affording good comparisons of body structure, body size, and plumage.
Call: None heard.
Plumage: Adult, probably worn alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Separation from Double-crested Cormorant is the obvious challenge here.

See description above for main points of separation. To recap, the bird differed from Double-crested by plumage, body size, and tail size; also, the bird did not flush with a flock of Double-crested Cormorants, suggesting that it was different.

Previous Experience

This is the first for-sure Neotropic Cormorant I've seen in Colorado, although I've wondered about a few others I've seen in recent years!

In the early 1990s, I lived in the Rio Grande Valley of c. and s. New Mexico, where Double-crested vs. Neotropic is a routine ID conundrum. At the time, I became quite aware of the fine points of separation between the two species, as I had extensive comparative experience between the two.

I've seen many Neotropics elsewhere in the southern USA, as well as throughout the New World tropics.
