Description of the Bird

While photographing a LISP along a rocky stretch of the San Juan River downstream of the hot springs, a very small  brownish bird with a very short tail flew through my field of view and promptly disappeared into the rocks and bushes.

Pishing eventually popped the bird up. 

It was tiny and overall brownish.  The tail was exceedingly short and held at a cocked up angle. The crown and eyestripe were dull brown.  The supercilium was a paler tannish tone as was the cheek.  The eye was dark.  The unpatterned throat and upper chest matched the supercilium.  The uppersides were a brown similar to the crown, becoming a bit rustier closer to the tail.  The wing edges and coverts had white spotting.  The tail had strong rusty tones and was barred.  The belly was spotted/mottled with paler off-white spots edged with thin lines of darker brown set against the color of the upper chest.  Legs pinkish.  Bill thin, not terribly long and slightly downcurved.

It called frequently sounding vagly SOSP like, but faster and more agitated as one would expect of a wren.

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was clearly a wren.

The ridiculously short tail eliminate all wrens with the exception of WIWR and PAWR.

PAWR (which I almost would have expected to the "more" likely) was eliminated by the overall pale tones to the entire bird, but most notably th the throat and upper chest.  In addition, the extensive white spotting on the wings is not good for PAWR.  Finally, the call notes are strongly indicative of WIWR over PAWR.
