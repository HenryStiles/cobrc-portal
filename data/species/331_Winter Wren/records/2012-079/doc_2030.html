Similar Species Discussion

House Wren - Bird was overall too small, too dark and too rusty. Song was not correct. Tail was too stubby.

Pacific Wren - I could not personally determine whether this was Pacific or Winter. The attached audio clips were forwarded to Nathan Pieplow for a more expert opinion. The following is Nathan's response:

"The recordings of the wren below Black Lake bear the unmistakable hallmarks of a Winter Wren (Troglodytes hiemalis, the eastern species). The songs of Pacific and Winter Wrens are quite easy to distinguish on the spectrogram by the frequency ranges of the notes: Pacific's notes sweep up and down over half the spectrogram, Winter's are confined to a narrow range, especially in the trills. The Black Lake wren has a teeny tiny frequency range in its trills -- on the extreme end even for Winter Wren.

Furthermore, the two strophes Kathy sent me are identical. This is another mark in favor of Winter Wren; they usually repeat the same songtype over and over, whereas Pacific varies consecutive songtypes, particularly at the end of each song.

However, the song of the Black Lake bird is slightly atypical for either Troglodytes species. It ends with a series of very high whistled notes, much like the opening notes of a Ruby-crowned Kinglet song. I've never come across a Troglodytes wren that sang a similar motif (and I've been through all the Troglodytes cuts in the Macaulay Library's online collection). In general the song is shorter and less varied than is usual for a Troglodytes. The anomalies might be explained if this is a first-summer bird that never had a chance to listen to neighboring male Winter Wrens on territory (because it got lost and ended up in Colorado)."

Previous Experience

I've seen a good number of Pacific/Winter Wrens over the years and am quite comfortable with identifying that I've seen one of the two. Not comfortable with separating them at this point other than by location (Maine: Winter, Washington: Pacific). I generally assume that the birds seen on the eastern plains in winter are Easterns.
