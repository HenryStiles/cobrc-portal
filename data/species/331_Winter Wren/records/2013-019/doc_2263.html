Habitat

Dense brushy vine-tangled understory of riparian flood-plain. Don't recall or didn't note what most of the riparian trees are (maybe narrow-leaf cottonwood) but also rocky mtn. juniper. Bird stuck to dense brush and tangles of understory.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Bushnell Elite 10X43 binoculars
Distance:  As little as 5 meters, estimated
Light:  Light was fair to good. Bird was initially viewed from south-southeast and then from south southwest and west. Sun was high but shrouded by a high deck of clouds.

Description of the Bird

A very small passerine with a thin straight bill, a round body, and a very short stub tail which was usually held in an erect posture. The bird was a warm brown above with some pale barring on the scapulars, nearly-white spots along the leading edge of each wing, and darker barring on the tail. The bird was overall paler below from throat to belly with some indistinct darker barring. As the breast blended into the abdomen the underparts became darker, now with paler barring concentrated on the flanks. The face showed pale supercilliaries, extending mostly behind the eyes, and a bit of spotting or barring on the auricular region. The overall structure and plumage was distinctly wren-like and very small size, very short tail, nearly-straight bill, and distinct supercillia rule out all but the Winter/Pacific complex.
Behaviors: Very secretive and difficult to see. The bird was calling occasionally when I first detected it. After declaring that I thought it was a winter wren, based only on vocalizations, I caught a few brief glimpses and then the bird disappeared and went silent. Danny Paez softly played <10 seconds of Winter Wren song on an iPhone and the bird almost immediately responded by calling back. The vocalizations escalated to the "rachet" calls and the bird slowly worked in nearly a complete circle around us-the source of the playback. It would work through dense tangles of brush and vines, occasionally showing itself for a few seconds before flying in an arc to the next pile of brush. It would move through the brush in a mouse-like manner and would rarely maintain an exposed position for more than a couple of seconds. It kept it's stubby tail cocked at almost all times and often bobbed and pivoted it's entire body.
Call: Very distinctive calls, "chmp" and "chmp-chmp" with a quality VERY similar to song sparrow. I called out Song Sparrow after the first note, but then noted it was slightly off and delivered rapidly and often in pairs, though sometimes individual "chmps". Following playback the bird also delivered some very rapid machine-gun "rachet" series of stacatto call notes.
Plumage: Unknown

Similar Species Discussion

I have a fair amount of experience with Winter Wren and quite a lot of experience with Winter Wren. Since the split, most of my encounters with "stub-tailed" wrens have been of Pacific. Most recently I had encountered several in northern Idaho and British Columbia in August 2012, but I had also encountered Pacific Wren in southern Arizona twice in Fall 2011 and in early December 2012. I had been feeling confident identifying them by voice and was able to secure video (including sound) of the Dec 2012 bird which experts (including Dave Stejskal) confirmed were Pacific. Instead of the Wilson's Warbler like "jmp-jmp" call of a Pacific Wren, this was a very different sounding "chmp-chmp" very very similar to the common call of Song Sparrow. I feel a high degree of confidence that I recognize and correctly interpret the differences between these two call types. I as reassured, after encountering a number of Pacifics since the split, that this one sounded instantly different, instantly song sparrow like, and that I was able to identify it as a winter wren before I even made visual confirmation of a wren.
