Description of the Bird

I had been making a long series of stops along thie NF road in the extensive burned forest of the Hayman Burn. While I was in Jefferson County immediately north of the Park County line I saw this juvenile Red-headed Woodpecker fly in from east and land on a Ponderosa pine snag, then after 2 min. it flew off to the south into Park Co. I followed it along the road and refound it in Park County, figuring it was a good find for that county even more so than Jefferson. There I watched it again until it flew further southwest.

I'd never seen a juv. RHWO before, so it was jarring to see this distinctive bird fly in, but the ID was immediately obvious. Size and heft reminded me of Acorn Woodpecker. The head was all dusky gray contrasting with white underparts and marked by fine flecks of gray (flecks darker on breast and paler on belly). It had a striking blackish back, dramatic contrasting panel of white secondaries and tertials with at least one dusky bar, extensive white lower back and uppertail coverts, contrasting with black tail. It had a good size dusky bill. It made one sallying foraging attempt while I watched it, in the manner typical of the species. It did not call.

Similar Species Discussion

It was obviously a woodpecker. But the appearance was so distinctive there really were no other serious ID contenders. Gray head, blcack back, white rump and extensive white panel on wing was distinctive

Resources Used

I looked at Sibley, but the ID was apparent before using the book from my familiarity with the species and knowing what to expect for a juv

Previous Experience

I've seen adult Red-headds a number of times, but infrequently. I have extensive experience with all of the widespread western woodpeckers, and limited experience with some species of limited US distribution (such as SE Arizona), and limited experience with all the primarily eastern species.
