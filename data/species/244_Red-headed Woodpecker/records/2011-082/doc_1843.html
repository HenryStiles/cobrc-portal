Description of the Bird

RHWO is a unique bird. Size of this bird was about the same as a Robin and it perched on the side of a tree trunk many times as a woodpecker would do. Bill was unremarkable, with head, nape, and throat all red. The back, upperwing coverts and primaries were all black. Secondaries and rump were white in contrast with the black back and a black tail. The bird flew out frequently flycatching and showed white underwing coverts and white secondaries with white breast and belly, dark primaries contrasted below as well. The bird was clearly a woodpecker by it’s behavior and shape.
Behaviors: Flycatching from a large dead juniper
Call: Did not vocalize
Plumage: Adult Basic

Similar Species Discussion

By plumage no other woodpecker is similar to adult RHWO. The bird both perched and clung to the side of tree limbs while flycatching. Other species cling to tree trunks and branches while foraging ie: nuthatches, creepers, Black & White Warbler, but none are as big as the woodpeckers. Flycatching is common to Red-headed and Lewis’s Woodpeckers and rarely to other species of woodpeckers. This bird was separated from any other woodpecker by plumage.

Resources Used

ID’d without field guide, I consulted Birds of Western Colorado to see how many western CO sightings have been recorded (about a dozen records). Also checked Birds of North America Online to learn about flycatching behavior in woodpecker species other than LEWO and RHWO (rare in “picoides”).

Previous Experience

I have seen many in eastern CO and have seen this same flycatching behavior in RHWO before.
