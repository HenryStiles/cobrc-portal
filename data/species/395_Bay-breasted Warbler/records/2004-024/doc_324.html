Description of the Bird

While conducting the bird-banding operation at Chico Basin Ranch, I extracted this bird from net #25. It was a larger-than-average warbler with a typical Dendroica bill (not too thin, not too thick, not short, not long). The crown was greenish-gray with black streaking and the pale superciliary contrasted only weakly with the gray auriculars. The white eye arcs were prominent. The chin and throat were very pale with the sides of the throat having traces of the reddish-chestnut color that sprad from there down the sides. The rest of the underparts were pale, nearly white but not. The gray back was streaked with black and the rump was paler and unmarked. The uppertail coverts had thin black shaft streaks.

The upperwing showed a distinct molt limit (see Pyle 1997) in the greater coverts with the outer 3-4 having not been replaced in the pre-alternate molt. The primary coverts were edged broadly, but indistinctly, with gray. These two features (along with the non-particularly-pointed outer rectrices) suggest the bird was a full adult, but the lack of chestnut streaking in the crown and the worn aspect to the outer rectrices suggest an immature. On balance, I believe that the bird was an adult.

There were two wide and well-demarcated white wingbars. The tail was blackish with distinct white spots on the inner webs of the three outer pairs of rectrices, with the spots becoming larger from R4 to R6.

Similar Species Discussion

The only warblers with significant amounts of chestnut on the sides are Bay-breasted and Chestnut-sided. The latter is easily ruled out by the lack of bright green tones above, the lack of a distinct, complete, white eye ring, and the gray (not bright green) edges to various wing feathers.
