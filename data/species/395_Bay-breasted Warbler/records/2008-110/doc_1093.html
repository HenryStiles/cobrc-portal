Description of the Bird

the bird was about the same size as Yellow-rumped Warblers it was associating with. It was also basically the same shape, but with a relatively shorter tail and slightly less chunky look giving it a slightly slimmer appearance. The bird had fairly long primary projection. The bill was short, thin and pointed, and black. The legs were dark gray or black.

The crown and nape of the bird were dusky olive-greenish. The bird had a lightly contrasting yellower eyebrow, and a dusky eye-line. The throat, sides of the neck, and chest were buffy-yellowish in color, yellowest on the neck and buffier towards the lower chest and sides. The flanks were rufous, widest towards the lower part and tapering towards the bend of the wing. The belly was whitish with a slighly buffy-yellow wash. The undertail coverts were similar in color to the chest. The underside of the tail appeared to have large white patches, but I didn't get a good look at this mark.

The back of the bird was dusky olive in color, with black streaks. The wings were dark gray with two bold white wing bars. The tail was also dark gray.
Behaviors: The bird was associating with a large flock of Yellow-rumped Warblers, though it spent all of its time at mid levels in the trees and never came to the ground or water drip as the Yellow-rumped often did.
Call: none heard
Plumage: basic

Similar Species Discussion

Many species of warbler were ruled out by the bold white bars. Most other species with wing bars were ruled out by the unstreaked underparts and rufous sides, as well as the general underparts color. Pine Warbler was ruled out by the rufous sides, buffier coloration, different face pattern with slightly contrasting eyebrow and eyeline, and not an auricular patch, by the streaked back, and by the yellow undertail coverts. Blackpoll Warbler was ruled out by the lack of any streaking, even faint, on the underparts, by the rufous sides, by the black legs, and by the overall buffier coloration.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted to confirm some of the specifics on ruling out Pine Warbler.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Bay-breasted Warblers in all plumages in both the east and in South America. I have seen many of the similar species in all plumages as well.
