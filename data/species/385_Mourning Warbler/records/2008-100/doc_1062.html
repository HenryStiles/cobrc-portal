Description of the Bird

The bird was of typical warbler shape and size, that being a passerine of approximately five inches in length, the tail extending well past the wings, the wings reaching back to near the end of the undertail coverts, with a sharp narrow bill. The bird was a uniform olive green color from the base of the neck to the end of the tail. The wings were the same color with no wing bars. The head was a less olive green with a very slight grayish wash from the forehead at the base of the bill back to the base of the neck, extending down to the cheek area. There was a subdued white eye ring that was very weak at 3 o'clock even with the middle of the bill. The eye was dark. The bill was blackish on the upper mandible and flesh colored on the lower mandible. The throat was yellow down to near the top of the chest where there was a slight patch that was more of a whitish yellow and then it returned to a bright yellow for the rest of the chest all the way down to the undertail coverts and up on the flanks. There was no streaking on the bird. The leg color is what I would call a dingy peach/pink.
Behaviors: The bird was loosely associating with a small flock of birds that included Yellow Warblers, Black-capped Chickadees and a Western Wood-pewee. It was foraging low in some willows and small Russian Olives when it flushed up to about ten feet up in a Cottonwood tree. It then dove back down to another thicket away from me. The bird was foraging along the branches and chased and ate insects that it flushed.
Call: silent
Plumage: immature

Similar Species Discussion

The bird did not have the complete eye ring, a full "hood" extending down to the chest or dull underpartslike a Connecticut Warbler; The bird did not have thick arcs of white around the eye or grayish hood of a MacGillivray's Warbler; The yellow extended over all of the underparts unlike just the throat and undertail coverts of a female Common Yellowthroat; It was larger with a bigger bill than a Wilson's Warbler and the yellow did not extend up around the eye, supercilium and forehead; It was more olive green than the pale yellow of a Yellow Warbler and it did not have white edging on the wings or an all dark bill; There was much more contrast between the olive green back and the yellow underside as compared to a Orange-crowned Warbler and it did not have any indication of diffused streaking on the underside, and the eye ring was more complete and better defined.

Resources Used

I did look at some pictures of Mourning Warblers online about an hour after seeing the bird (I am writing this up late in the evening), and used a field guide to make sure I commented on all similar appearing species.

Previous Experience

This is the first immature female Mourning Warbler that I remember seeing. I have only observed adult males before. I have never seen a Connecticut Warbler, I have seen many Mac's, Yellow-throats, Wilsons and Yellows.
