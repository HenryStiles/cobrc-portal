Description of the Bird

While birding with Tom Schultz and Chris Wood at HQ Pond, Tom found a male Mourning Warbler. It was similar to a male MacGillivray's Warbler (MGWA), though lacked that species' white eye arcs.

The entire head was slate gray, slightly darker around the eyes, and this gray continued onto the upper chest. The lower chest was splotchy black and slate gray with no distinct line of demarcation between solid slate gray on the upper chest and the black and gray on the lower chest. At the chest-belly interface, there was a sharp line of demarcation between the black-and-gray chest and the bright yellow belly. The vent region and the undertail coverts were the same bright yellow.

The back, rump, tail, and wings were a dark olive with a brighter green cast. The undertail coverts extended at least halfway down the tail. The bill was bicolored dark above, pink below, and the legs were pink.
Behaviors: Foraging low in willows and Russian Olives
Call: None heard
Plumage: Definitive

Similar Species Discussion

MGWA is the most similar, but that species has white eye arcs, usually less obvious black on the chest, distinct black loral region, and much shorter undertail coverts. Connecticut Warbler is quite different in shape, being longer and lankier, with complete white eye rings and walks along limbs rather than hops, as our bird did. Nashville Warbler is quite different, being a more sprightly-foraging bird and having the yellow of the belly extending all the way up through the chin.
