Description of the Bird

This was was noted to be attracted to a pile of driftwood and fine debris in the stream course which had been caught in recent high flows by a barbed wire fence that crossed the creek. A number of other passerines were feeding at the same spot, including other warblers, sparrows and Dusky Flycatcher. There were also dense thickets of willow adjacent to the debris mat which the bird also visited.

This was a male seen at very close range a number of times over my stay here, including unobstructed views when it was working over the pile of driftwood. My very first impression when it came into view was of a MacGillivray's but turning the binoculars to it I noticed that it showed no eye arcs, even in excellent viewing conditions. The head was hooded in all dark slaty blackish, becoming black on the breast at the lower edge of the dark hood. The underparts were otherwise yellow. The upperparts were unmarked dull green. The legs were flesh pink and the bill had a dark upper mandible and a pale lower mandible with dark at the tip. This showed well beside the very dark face. Bill size and overall size of the bird appeared to me to be similar to MacGillivray's. I was thrilled as I realized what I was looking at, and the views were crippling as it foraged repeatedly in the open on the debris pile. Later I heard it call a number of times, and the call was different than the dry flat 'tick' of the MacGillivray's. It was more of a 'whit' than a 'tick', had a bit more attitude, and I made notes of the call as 'fwitch' or 'pwit'. 

Similar Species Discussion

MacGillivray's was the only contender but it was eliminated by the lack of eye arcs and the different call note. Given the great views, there was no question in my mind. I am intimately familiar with the calls of MacGillivray's from long experience with the species in CA (and now CO), and I also encountered one calling later this date and was able to compare its calls with the fresh memory of this bird's calls.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen a number of Mourning Warblers back east several years ago, and I have sen this species 3 times in CA. One was a singing male spring migrant.

I am intimately familiar with MacGillivrays from 33 years of highly active birding in CA (and now CO), including extensive work as a field ornithologist in the species' breeding habitat in the Sierra Nevada and the Santa Cruz Mountains of CA.
