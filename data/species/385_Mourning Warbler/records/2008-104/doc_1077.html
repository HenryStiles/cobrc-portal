Description of the Bird

The bird was roughly Common Yellowthroat sized warbler with a thin white eyering, possibly complete. The bird's throat was yellowish pale and the bird had a completely yellow belly with yellow undertail coverts. The yellow on the belly and undertail coverts were brighter than the yellow on the throat. The birds back was olive and the birds wings were unmarked. The bird was not distinctly hooded.
Behaviors: The bird was feeding in a willow thicket next to Boulder Creek. The bird never flew or fed higher than ~3 ft from the ground. The bird was calling with a chip note and responded to pishing.
Call: The bird was initially located because it was calling from the willows along Boulder Creek. The call was a chip note. My initial impression was that when the bird appeared from the willows, it would be a Common Yellowthroat. The call differed from Common Yellowthroat by having a more hollow sound more easily mimicked by "sucking ones tongue". I was able to "pish" the bird out into the open by mimicking the call note. The bird responded at least twice to the pishing before it retreated further into the willows.
Plumage: First Fall

Similar Species Discussion

MacGillivray's Warbler:

The absence of distinct, bold white eye-arcs suggests that the bird was not a MacGillivray's Warbler. Only a minority of immature MacGillivray's Warblers have dull yellow on the throat. Dunn and Garrett (1997) suggest separation from these individuals from Mourning Warbler by a combination of eye-arc shape, tail extension and call note. The call notes of MacGillivray's Warbler is crisper, sharper and shorter than that of the bird I heard suggesting that the bird was a Mourning Warbler.

Connecticut Warbler:

Structurally, the bird appeared more warbler-like and less thrush-like than Connecticut Warbler. Dunn and Garrett, (1997) note that throat of Connecticut Warbler is rarely tinged pale yellow also suggesting that the bird was not a Connecticut Warbler. The active calling and the amount of time the bird spent off of the ground in the Willows also runs counter to typical Connecticut Warbler behavior.

Common Yellowthroat:

The belly of female Common Yellowthroat would be interrupted by a white band just fore of the undertail coverts. Since the bird I observed had a continuously yellow belly, this rules out Common Yellowthroat.

Orange-crowned Warbler:

Some Orange-crowned Warblers may resemble immature Mourning Warbler. However, the throat of Orange-crowned Warbler is streaked and Orange-crowned Warbler shows a pale supercilium. Orange-crowned Warblers are often seen higher in the canopy and less often close to the ground in willows. Additionally, the overall structure of Orange-crowned Warbler suggests a smaller bird, unlike the bird I observed. The above characteristics combine to eliminate Orange-crowned Warbler.

Additional Information

Comments: I was unprepared to identify an immature Mourning Warbler when I encountered this bird and failed to notice important characteristics such as tail extension and length of the undertail coverts. Still, the characteristics I have outlined above are at least suggestive, and possible sufficient for the identification of first Fall Mourning Warbler.
