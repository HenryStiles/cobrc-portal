Description of the Bird

Bird hopped in and out of the understory along the forest edge.  Behavior was very skittish never stopping in any spot for a long time (10-15 seconds at most).  No calls were given.  General size was hard to compare without other birds nearby but was simiar to other warblers I have seen.  Plummage seemed relatively intact - no molting appeared to be occurring.  Head, nape, face, back, and wings were all a similar shade of olive brown.  Eye was dark with a thin white eye-ring that was complete.  Throat was a bright yellow.  The edges of the nape came down on the dividing line between the throat and breast giving the impression of an incomplete olive-brown hood.  The breast of bird was also yellow and extended into the belly before showing faded yellow/whitish coloring on the lower belly and vent.  The head coloring did not contrast at all with the coloring of the back.  No differences in color amongst the different wing feather groups.  Could not determine the leg or beak color given the light conditions - both appeared grayish.

Similar Species Discussion

The first feature that I was drawn to was the contrasting olive-brown vs yellow, but once I noticed the complete eye-ring and lack of any visible lore coloring I eliminated Yellow-breasted Chat.  The body shape and size also did not give the impression of a yellow breasted chat.  I noticed no wing bars or other wing color differentiations.  At that point I concentrated on the group of warblers that had similar features - the lack of a thick eye-ring along with its completeness led me to eliminate Macgillivray's.  I also did not associate the bright yellow throat with Macgillivray's.  At this point I reached the line of experience I had with this group.   I suspected based on the lack of contrasting head/back color, a thin eye-ring instead of thick eye-ring, and yellow throat vs throat color that matched the head color that this was a juvenile Mourning warbler instead of a juv Connecticut Warbler.   I compared my notes additionally to descriptions in my field guide (National Geographic Field Guide) and to photos on-line.
