Description of the Bird

My brother first observed the bird skulking in dense undergrowth along a thickly-vegetated ditch (mostly russian-olives). It came into view for a few seconds in response to his pishing before it disappeared. He immediately identified it as a male Mourning Warbler due to the complete lack of white eye arcs. We spent the next 15 minutes trying to locate it without success. Then, as I was heading out to the road, I located it foraging low to the ground in deciduous woods about 20 feet from where it was initially observed. I was able to get several good views in decent light, but I couldn't obtain any pictures. This was a medium-sized, chunky warbler with lemon-yellow underparts and a greenish back. It had a dark sooty/slate gray hood with a noticeably blacker patch at the bottom of the chest. Its head was uniform gray without any hint of an eyering.

Similar Species Discussion

Connecticut Warbler would be slightly larger and perhaps more sturdy with a paler grayish hood. The underparts would probably be more diluted pale yellow, versus the strong lemon yellow color of Mourning. Also, Connecticut would have a complete white eyering, which is absent in Mourning. MacGillivray's is the main contender species, and was primarily eliminated by the entire lack of white eye arcs (which would have been very conspicuous). Also, the bird had a slender black patch at the bottom of the chest, versus the more extensive black throat and chest of MacGillivray's. The head was uniform dark gray, whereas spring male MacGillivray's should have exhibited contrasting black lores (although I'm not sure how valuable of an identification feature this is). It also appeared more short-tailed than would be expected on a MacGillivray's, although this was difficult to judge. 


It may be important to note that my brother and I observed a total of three male Mourning Warblers, all at different locations, while birding in Yuma County on May 16. This suggests that there was a movement of Mourning Warblers through far eastern Colorado on that day.

Resources Used

My brother and I both immediately identified the bird in the field from past experience.

Previous Experience

I have extensive experience with MacGillivray's Warblers from Colorado, both as migrants and breeders. I have seen Mourning Warblers several times in Minnesota.
