Habitat

The north shore of this pond is lined with Russian olives and a few other fairly short but thickly grown trees, so that a view of the pond itself is very limited. The trees grow off the side of a dyke on which there is a rough trail, at that time thick with grasses. I never saw or heard the bird anywhere else but in the trees right along the pond.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Zeiss 7x42 TFL binoculars
Distance:  at closest, maybe 12 feet
Light:  Early morning light. The skies were clear, and the sun was generally low and to the left of where I was looking for the bird. Given that it was mid-May, the sun angle was actually not that bad - the trees in which the bird was most observable were not backlit.

Description of the Bird

The bird was a small passerine moving frequently through the tangle of branches of Russian Olives among Yellow-rumped Warblers, but I saw its distinctly yellow underparts, black bib, and gray head with the slight suggestion of a dark mask. There were no eye arcs or eye rings, just a dark shading around the eye and seemed to me to include the lores. I didn't observe much else about the bird after my initial identification, as I focused more on getting a photograph of it while I could.
Behaviors: The bird was very active, moving frequently from one branch to the next in a tree, and moving from tree to tree in a methodical manner seemingly with several other warblers, all Yellow-rumped and Orange-crowned as best as I could tell.
Call: sharp, loud chip notes given every second or two; no song heard. these call notes stood out from those of the other birds present in the area that morning, including other warblers (Yellow-rumped, Yellow, and Orange-crowned) and chickadees, etc.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

The other Oporornis species are the main ones I needed to eliminate. The one that is much more likely in this location and habitat is MacGillivrays Warbler. I mainly looked for eye arcs on this bird, which would indicate MacGillivrays, but found none. The presence of a bold eye ring, or any eye ring really would suggest a Connecticut Warbler or something other than a Mourning. Also, the bib feature was very notable on this bird, which the other Oporornis should not have in any plumage.

Resources Used

Sibley's Guide to Birds, and Stokes Field Guide to Bird Songs, Eastern edition. I listened to the recordings in the car before I headed out to search for it. I also looked in the Sibley guide before I left the car, and did not have it on my person in the field.

Previous Experience

I've seen a number of MacGillivray's Warblers in the past few years in Colorado, and photographed a few of them. I have never seen a Connecticut Warbler, but have studied it in field guides. This is my first ever sighting of a Mourning Warbler.
