Description of the Bird

Warbler size and profile. Mostly yellow on undersides, olive-brown back and wings that looked brown in areas where light was dim and olive only in better lit areas. Grayish hood was incomplete on underside - throat was dull yellow. White arcs around eyes were thin and incomplete. Bird may have chipped but can't be sure; other warblers including MacGillivray's warbler were present. At least one sharp warbler chip was heard that was more emphatic than nearby Wilson's Warblers, which were also chipping on occasion. 

Similar Species Discussion

We ruled out MacGillivray's Warbler by comparison when two 1st winter warblers were briefly side by side. The Mourning Warbler's throat was yellowish, not whitish/gray as in the adjacent MacGillivray's Warbler. The hood on the Mourning Warbler was less complete and the eye rings were significantly thinner and less complete than the adjacent MacGillivray's, or what I would expect for a Connecticut Warbler. We ruled out 1st year Common Yellowthroat because the underside of the subject bird was nearly all yellow.

Resources Used

I had Sibley's field guide, National Geographic field guide, and iBird phone app. I looked at all three in preparation for seeing the bird. When the subject bird was present, it was around long enough that we were comparing it to the drawing of a 1st winter female in Sibley's guide.

Previous Experience

Very little. I am not an expert birder.

I have seen many warbler species and hundreds if not thousands of individual warbler birds. This bird was a warbler, of that I am certain.

I have seen and identified MacGillivray's Warbler on just a few prior occasions, having moved to Colorado from Wisconsin just two years ago. I don't recall ever seeing (or at least identifying) a Mourning Warbler or a Connecticut Warbler. Common Yellowthroat is a species I have seen many, many times in different plumages and feel very confident about ruling out.
