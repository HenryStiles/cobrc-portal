Description of the Bird

A typical geothlypis warbler (size and shape) that was dull green on upperparts (appeared brownish in low light) blending to olive-gray on head except for thin white broken eye ring, and pale yellow throat and lores. Underparts bright yellow from vent to breast, with olive wash to upper breast. Gave a soft thick chip note, not as brash as the chip note given by a nearby MacGillivray's Warbler. The lack of any black feathers on chest and soft chip note suggests female sex. Usually very shy in thickets but occasionally flitted among small trees and other perches within a few feet of the ground. Often foraged in tall grasses that were matted down along edge of canal, sometimes out in the open. Observed on 5 different occasions, twice along the ditch in my backyard, once in garden vegetation (rose bush) in my front yard, once along the ditch in my neighbor's backyard, and the last time along the ditch about 100 yards to the north. 

Similar Species Discussion

MacGillivray's Warbler is the most similar species, but no plumage has yellow throat and lores. Connecticut Warbler has full eyering in all plumages. Common Yellowthroat female has less yellow on lower underparts and lacks white eye arcs.

Previous Experience

I have been birding avidly for 41 years, but I have infrequently encountered immature Mourning Warbler in fall. I probably saw a dozen or so  in the 1980s in eastern Massachusetts, and I have seen two others in Colorado in the last decade. I have seen about several dozen fall MacGillivray's Warblers in Colorado, a few fall Connecticult Warblers in Massacusetts and many dozen if not hundreds of fall Common Yellowthroats.
