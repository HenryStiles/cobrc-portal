Description of the Bird

Buteo with light underwing that had a prominent black band on the trailing edge of the wing. The underwing coverts were light brown.  The outer primary tips were black in color with a small "window" of light proximal to those dark wing tips. (The "window" extended behind the underwing coverts.) Patagial marks were absent. The tail had a narrow white tip, a wide black subterminal band with a wide white band above it. Two more slightly narrower black bands (separated by white) were visible toward the base of the tail, but partially obscured by the undertail coverts.

Note:  I had just seen a line of Turkey Vultures (about 7) passing through; the vultures were all heading south with a separation of distance between them.  The hawk turned up when I had the last Turkey Vulture in sight.  The hawk was initially fairly close to the vulture, but turned to enter a thermal.   

Similar Species Discussion

I very quickly recognized the first hawk as an adult Broad-winged Hawk.  I first noticed the distinctly banded tail.  I next confirmed that the underwing showed a distinct black band on the trailing edge of the wing.  The combination of the tail pattern and underwing pattern convinced me that my initial impression of a Broad-winged Hawk was accurate.  The ID to species didn’t really require elimination of any other species, although I recall thinking how the tail pattern of an adult Gray Hawk is very similar.  (I have seen adult Gray Hawks more often in recent years than Broad-winged Hawks.)  The adult Gray Hawk has distinct gray barring on the body.   The Broad-winged Hawks I observed (a second one became apparent within a short time) were both marked the same, without any gray barring on the body or underwing. 

Resources Used

I didn’t require any references to ID the hawks.  However, I always have the National Geographic Field Guide to the Birds of North America and the Sibley Guide to Birds in my car.  I certainly looked at the field guides soon after the observation.   

Previous Experience

I haven’t seen a Broad-winged Hawk in southwestern Colorado during the years I have lived here (over 30 years).  I have seen numerous Broad-winged Hawks in the eastern United States.
