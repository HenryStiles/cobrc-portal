Shortgrass prairie. Pond ephemeral with quickly drying muddly shoreline and grassy in the east and west sides.

Viewing Conditions

Description of the Bird

In a small evaporative pool the two species that stood out were American Avocets, and a tame, bright rufous/chestnut ventrally, phalarope with a stout yellow black-tipped bill. This bird had a blackish crown and nape. The gleaming white checks were set off by the contrasting colors of the bird's dark eye, dark area behind the bill, the blackish crown and the rich chestnut chin throat and neck.

The blackish scapulars and tertials were broadly edged with gold and white, adding even more color to this bird. There were about four noticeable still whitish breast feathers, and under the wings quite a few broadly tipped gray feathers remained from basic plumage (see photo). At times I could see the upper tibia and the leg color appeared dull greenish.

The bill was blunt tipped.
Behaviors: I watched this bird for an hour from the side of the rapidly drying small pond on Pawnee National Grasslands. The bird (similar to this species in Alaska) showed no fear of human intrusion. The bird feed 95% of the time, flew off (not people related) about 250 feet from the east side of the pond, and returned to feed, all within approxiametely five seconds, as if testing its musculature.

This bird was an active feeder, constantly moving about mostly the shallowest parts of the pond, moving around clockwise and covering the entire edge of the pond. It ignored three birders standing at different locations on the shoreline and even came directly at me to withing 30 feet, to pull a tadpole shrimp out of the shallows. I watched it also feed by swiping small insects out of the air, those being directly in the path of the birds paddling.

The bird did preen for about five minutes. It stood near a small rock but remained in water up to its belly (see photos).
Call: none heard
Plumage: mostly molted to alternate

Similar Species Discussion

The only other shorebird that has somewhat similar coloration iand about the same size in Spring is Curlew Sandpiper, and to some degree Sanderling.

CUSA lacks the yellow bill (its is blackish) and has a longer downcurved bill. It also is a wader, not a swimmer. The back coloration is entirely different in alternate plumage having many chestnut and black back feathers. The face of CUSA lacks the strikingly white check of the REPH. Sanderling has dark legs a noticeable nervous gait, spending most time on mud and out of the water. It too lacks the contrasty white cheek of REPH and has a white belly in alternate plumage unlike the CO bird here.

Resources Used

I looked at coloration of CUSA and Sanderling in alternate plumage in The Shorebird Guide (O'brien, Crossley, and Karlson).

Previous Experience

I have seen about 20 red REPHs in Alaska when I worked there for 5 summers, but most of them were males. They acted similar in their tameness. I have seen about 10 gray ones in CO, but only two of them this well.
