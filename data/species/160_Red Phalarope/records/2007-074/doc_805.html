Description of the Bird

No other shorebirds present for size comparison. Relative to Wilson's Phalarope which I see much more often, this bird appeared short-legged and shorter/thicker billed. In my experience with all three Phalaropes, bill structure is most distinctive for Red, with its blunt, shorter & thicker shape. This bird's neck also seemed shorter and thicker than Wilson's does during equivalent postures. Both bill and leg color looked black. If there was any lighter coloration to the bill base, I couldn't see it under the lighting conditions.
Clean white underparts, light gray upperparts, obvious, isolated black patch behind eye. White supercilium separating dark cap from eyepatch. Throat white, front of neck subtley shaded orange buff enough to contrast with white of throat. A few blackish scapular feather could be seen in the otherwise plain gray of the upperparts. These last two features made me think this was a young bird molting into first winter plumage.
Behaviors: First seen swimming in pool, subsequently foraged along shoreline on foot, swimming intermittently. A nearby young Sabine's Gull flew in and flushed the phalarope once, but the phalarope landed nearby and continued foraging within yards of the gull.
Call: no vocalization heard
Plumage: first basic or first winter

Similar Species Discussion

Red-necked Phalarope was the only real contender, (ie. with bold black eye patch) but that species' bill shape is thinner and upperparts should show streaking

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I'm quite familiar with Wilson's Phalarope in various plumages, and have limited experience with both other species both on and offshore. I once had the chance to scope a similarly-plumaged Red Phalarope at pretty close range in South Carolina.
