Description of the Bird

I here reproduce the relevant portions of my field notes verbatim: "About same size or slightly smaller than adjacent Pectoral Sandpipers, primarily white, gray and black but with orangely wash on sides of neck.sides of upper breast. Back, scaps and wing coverts maybe 80% basic--plain gray. Older feathers darker--flight feathers and a bar in the coverts. Blackish eye patch. Black stripe up back on neck joins dark crown starting to wear white in middle. Bill and legs all black. Bill thick (about as thick as the legs), straight-edged, slighly thicker at base than tip, blunt-ended. Underparts clean white except for distinct, rather fine gray streaks on flanks."
Behaviors: Relevant field notes verbatim: "bird was with other shorebirds, walking in short emergent vegetation. Actively picking stuff off water."
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: juvenile molting into basic

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was obviously a phalarope. Black eye patch, short bill, structure and other characteristics easily eliminated Wilson's Phalarope. Red-necked Phalarope was eliminated by bill structure and by lack of white "braces" on that part of the back that had molted into basic plumage. I'm not sure how good a mark the gray flank streaking is, but I've seen it on basic Red Phalaropes in the past and not on Red-neckeds.

Previous Experience

Over the past few years, I have seen about three Red Phalaropes in Colorado and one in Utah, all of which I have studied rather carefully, although none afforded such good looks as this one did. I see many Red-necked and Wilson's Phalaropes each year.
