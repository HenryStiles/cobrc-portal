Description of the Bird

The bird was a small shorebird, with a body about the size of a Pectoral Sandpiper, but much shorter legs giving it a smaller appearance. The legs were black, and quite short, that along with the short neck, fairly large head (for a shorebird), and overall stocky shape gave the bird a stocky appearance. The bill was black, short, a little more than the length of the head, and thick (at the base a little thicker than the eye, tapering a bit to about the thickness of the eye). The bill was also blunt-tipped.

The bird's face was white, except for a dark gray recangular mark through the eye, extending at the rear end almost to the nape, but only barely past the eye on the front end. This mark was paler on this bird than I am used to on phalaropes. The bird had a blackish crown extending a little past the eye from the rear, and a white forecrown. The blackish hind-crown was bifurcated by a stripe of white on the top of the head, connecting to the white forecrowne. The nape was white except for a thin stripe of gray connecting the crown to the gray back.

The back was a fairly pale gray, paler than the gray on the head and face, and unifrom. The scapulars were black, as were some of the wing coverts. For the most part, though, the wing coverts were the same color as the back. There was no white anywhere on the back or wing coverts. The wings were a little longer than the tail, and the flight feathers appeared very dark gray or black. What I could see of the tail appeared gray.

The underparts of the bird were white, except for a reddish-buffy colored wash on the sides of the neck, and a dark gray wash on the sides of the chest, connecting to the gray back.

I did not see the bird in flight.
Behaviors: the bird was foraging in very shallow water, with other shorebirds, and for the most part was walking in flooded grass, picking food off the surface of the water. It occasionally swam into deeper water, but always came back to the shallower water near the shore.
Call: none heard
Plumage: juvenal molting into basic

Similar Species Discussion

All shorebirds other than Red-necked Phalarope were ruled out by the combination of the small size, clean gray upperparts, short legs and dumpy appearance, and the black "phalarope mark" through the eye.

Red-necked Phalarope was ruled out by the thick bill with a blunt tip, clean gray back with no white stripes, buffyish wash on the sides of the neck, and dumpier shape.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen a number of Red Phalaropes off both coasts, and a few in Colorado. This was my first encounter with this particular plumage of Red Phalarope.

I have seen many Red-necked Phalaropes off both coasts, and many hundreds in all plumages in Colorado.
