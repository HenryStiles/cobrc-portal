Description of the Bird

the bird was identified as a phalarope by its small size (much smaller than anything else on the water nearby, including Bufflehead), by it's long, thin bill, long neck, overall shorebird-like shape, etc. The bill was black, about the length of the head, and fairly thin, but blunt-tipped.

The bird's crown was white, with a black nape that split and came over each eye as black eye-lines. The face was white, but with a bold black patch from the eye back towards the nape. The underparts were white. The back was an even medium gray color, with some of the color bleeding onto the sides of the chest.

The overall shape of the bird was heavy-set for a Phalarope, with a fairly thick neck, and overall heavy appearance. The bird was not seen in flight. This individual probably had the shortest bill of any individual of this species I've seen.
Behaviors: the bird was found when it was swimming far out in the middle of the reservoir. After watching it for a while we turned to other birds, and later were unable to relocate it.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult basic

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than Phalaropes were ruled out by a combination of small size for a waterbird, long neck and long and thin bill (compared to ducks and murrelets), and overall pattern of gray and white, with black on the face and crown.

Wilson's Phalarope was ruled out by the black mask on the face, relatively short, thick bill, relatively heavy-set shape with thick and short neck, etc.

Red-necked Phalarope was ruled out by the blunt-tipped bill that was thicker than I would expect for that species, by the evenly gray back with no white stripes, by the more extensive white on the crown, and by the heavier appearing shape.
