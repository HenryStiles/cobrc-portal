Description of the Bird

I saw the bird on open water on Baseline Reservoir. It was notably smaller than nearby Ring-billed Gulls. At times it spun around in circles, like a phalarope. The bird was pale overall, being white below and grayish above. Its back and wings ("mantle" on the swimming bird) were fairly uniformly colored, i.e., they did not show any obvious patterning. The bird's mainly white head showed a thick black post-ocular patch (the "phalarope patch") and gray-black smudging on the crown. The bill was dark and notably thick, with some paler (yellow or "flesh") coloration toward the base. I'm not sure of the bird's plumage. In essence, it was in "winter plumage," but I don't know if it was a bird coming out of juvenal plumage or an adult in mostly basic plumage. In any event, it was neither in (mainly) juvenal plumage nor in alternate/adult plumage.
Behaviors: As noted above, I observed the bird swimming out on the middle of Baseline Reservoir. On several occasions, the bird was spinning around like a phalarope. After the initial period of observation, I moved to another vantage point along the lakeshore, and I observed the bird swimming unidirectionally for at least 100 meters.
Call: None heard.
Plumage: see below

Similar Species Discussion

By its size, structure, and spinning behavior, it had to be a phalarope. Date alone (present from Oct. 30th into early November) is suggestive of Red Phalarope, as inland records of Red Phalarope are often from late October into early December. Also good for Red Phalarope was the generally "pelagic" proclivity of hanging out in deep water.

With its "phalarope patch" (postocular patch), the bird seemed an immediate candidate for either Red or Red-necked, but not Wilson's (which is more plain-faced in "winter" plumage). To separate it from Red-necked, I note the following: It seemed generally portly and robust (Red-necked is daintier), its "phalarope patch" was quite prominent (on Red-necked, the crown patch is often as prominent as, or more prominent than, the "phalarope patch"), its back was generally plain (the back of Red-necked is often darker and more boldly patterned), and, most important by far, its bill was notably thick and pale- or "flesh"-based.
