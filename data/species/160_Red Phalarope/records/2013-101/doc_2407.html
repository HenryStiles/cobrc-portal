Description of the Bird

Medium size shorebird, larger than the peeps it was feeding with. The bill was black and the legs dark. The bird was in heavy molt with primary feather a mix of black and light gray on upper side. The neck, chest and belly were already past molt and the bird was completely light gray on the underside. A dark eye patch, which extended well past the eye, stood out as the most noticeable field mark about the head. On the upper neck a hint of rufous could still be seen which suggested that this bird is an adult. In several photos a hind toe can be seen which eliminates Sanderling. One field mark that was missing was the yellowish base of the bill. The light was good enough if it would have been present we could see it in the photos.

In Sibley’s a Red Phalarope undergoes fall molt at about the time we found the bird or maybe just a little later. Red-necked Phalarope undergoes fall molt earlier and Red Phalarope. Sanderling have a similar fall molt schedule as does a Red Phalarope.
Behaviors: The bird was feeding on the mudflats with several Baird’s and Least Sandpipers. The mystery bird, at a good distance, looked to be a Sanderling in molt as it probed the mud. At this point the bird gave no indication that it may be a phalarope, a species that is usually found feeding on or in water. The bird was several meters from the edge of the water. Brenda then walked some distance along the edge of the mud to get close enough for the photos. The bird kept its distance but never flew as it kept probing for food.
Call: none heard
Plumage: Adult Heavy fall molt

Similar Species Discussion

Sanderlings do not have a hind toe and do not have a wide, dark eye stripe or patch that extends well back of the eye. A Red-necked Phalarope has a much finer bill. Dunlins have a down curved bill.
