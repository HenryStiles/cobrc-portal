Description of the Bird

the bird was a small shorebird, noticeably smaller than nearby Killdeer, but a larger than nearby Least Sandpipers. The shape was typical of a phalarope, with a chunky body, short neck, small head, long wings that extended to the tip of the tail or a bit beyond it, short tail, and long, thin bill. The bill was thick for a phalarope, and of fairly even thickness coming to a blunt tip. It was about as long as the head, and completely black. The legs were relatively short for a phalarope, and were gray in color.

The crown of the bird was white, with the black from the nape splitting and continuing on as two narrow black lines along the side of the crown until they ended just past (closer to the bill) the eye. The nape was black, and tapered to a thin line that continued down the neck and merged with the gray back. The back was clean medium gray, with no other colors. The wingtips were black.

The face of the bird was white, with a large black rectangular mark from just in from of the eye to the rear of the face. The underparts were white, except for an area around the bend of the wing which was the same gray color as the back.
Behaviors: The bird was observed swimming on the water near the far edge of the pond. It mostly swam with quick, jerky movements and occasionally spun around in a circle for a few times. At one point it walked out onto a small rocky spit and foraged in a manner more typical of a shorebird.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult basic

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than phalaropes were ruled out by the distinctive shape and behavior. Wilson's Phalarope was ruled out by the chunkier shape, black mark on the face and black on nape and neck, and thicker and shorter bill. Red-necked Phalarope was ruled out by the clean gray back, thicker bill, and chunkier shape.
