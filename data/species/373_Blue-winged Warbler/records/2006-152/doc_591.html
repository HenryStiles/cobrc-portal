Habitat

Woodland belt that borders the west side of Neenoshe Reservoir. This edge has a number of black locusts on the western side of the tree belt and there are a few Russian olives interspersed. Nearest the reservoir side there is a row of tall cottonwoods. In between there is a band of samller trees and I'm not sure of the tree species.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Ziess 8x42
Distance:  75 ft estimated
Light:  Excellent, the sun was nearly directly behind me. Since the trees were leafless, there was excellent light on the bird.

Description of the Bird

I immediately recognized this bird as a Blue-winged Warbler. The front of the head, throat, chest, and belly were all bright yellow. The eye was black and the lore was very dark gray and there was a short dark post ocular mark. The auriculars were a golden olive, as was the back of the crown and nape which extended to the upper back. The undertail coverts were white as was the majority of the undertail. The wings were a bluish-gray with two distinct white wing bars. The bill was blackish-brown and sharply pointed; the lower mandible appeared lighter colored. The legs were dark. The upper tail was dark. The rump was not seen.
Behaviors: The bird was feeding low - about four feet above the ground - in the leafless trees immediately adjacent to the locust trees at Neenoshe Reservoir. It was perched for about ten seconds before it flew. My view was almost a full side view with the bird turned slightly toward me.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Adult female probably

Similar Species Discussion

Yellow Warbler was eliminated by dark wings with wing bars and white undertail coverts and mostly white under tail. Prothonotary Warbler was eliminated by white wing bars and black lore mark; plus to me Prothonotary Warblers always have a beady-eyed look which this bird did not have. A very "bright" Orange-crowned Warbler can be eliminated by wing color and wing bars and white undertail coverts and mostly white undertail. A Blue-winged x Golden-winged hybrid would show at least some faint features of a Golden-winged Warbler which it did not. The yellow underparts were too extensive and uniformly bright yellow without any signs of a golden-winged black/gray face and chest markings, the wing bars were white and not yellow. Nothing suggested any Golden-winged genes in this bird's parentage.

Resources Used

None were used in the field. I did refer to Warblers by Dunn and Garrett to attempt to determine sex of this bird and to consider similar species including Blue-wingedxGolden-winged hybrids.

Previous Experience

I am very familiar with Blue-winged Warblers as they are one of the most common breeding warblers in SW Ohio where I birded for twenty years. I am very familiar with Prothonotary, Yellow, Goldened-winged, and Orange-crowned Warblers. I have limited experience with Blue-winged x Golden-winged hybrids.
