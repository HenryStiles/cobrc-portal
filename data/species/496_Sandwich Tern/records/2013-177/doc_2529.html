Description of the Bird

When I first saw the bird in binoculars, it was perched on a snag in the middle of the lake. The size, shape, and mostly white color immediately told me it was a Sterna tern.

Observed through a spotting scope, the long, thin, black bill with pale yellow tip stood out dramatically. The bird had a clean, white body with a solid black cap and black legs. The wings were nearly pure white when the bird was perched.

Observed in flight, the upper surface of the tail was pure white, and the tail was forked. The upper surface of the wings was white with a black patch near the ends. The underside of the wings was mostly or entirely white.
Behavior: Most of the time, it was perched on a snag. When it flew, it stayed within a small area, banking and spreading its wings/tail.
Call: no vocalizations heard
Plumage: breeding

Similar Species Discussion

When I first saw the bird with binocs, I expected it to be a Forster's Tern, with Common Tern as a lesser possibility. The most important marks used to rule out those species were:
1) very long black bill with pale yellow tip
2) black legs

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen approximately ten Sandwich Terns before, in North Carolina, Florida, and California. The California bird (May 11, 1997 at Bolsa Chica Lagoon in Orange County) was sufficiently rare that I spent a long time studying it when I chased it (i.e. I was not the finder).
