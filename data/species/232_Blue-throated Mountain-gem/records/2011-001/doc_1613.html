Description of the Bird

Here is my brief write-up on the BLUH that was posted on the western slope birding network list on July 1, 2010. The bird did not stick around and was only seen on 6/30 by myself and my wife Kay, too bad as the bird deserved to be shared with others. This was certainly a species I never expected to see at my home, there aren't many records in Colorado and this was a state bird for me.


> Late yesterday afternoon (6/30/11) and through the early evening we were visited
> multiple times by a large dark female hummingbird. The bird was about
> size of a Pine Siskin (in fact, I wondered at first glimpse what is a Pine Siskin doing around the hummingbird feeders), nothing remarkable about the bill, black
> through the eye, and white markings on the face (I didn't note these
> well enough to really describe them). Breast and belly were uniformly
> gray, not speckled. The underside of the tail is what I really
> focused on. It was dark, not green and not at all forked, with a huge
> amount of white at the tips of the outer feathers. In most poses the
> bird's tail feathers were not flared, and the tail would appear to
> have a 3/8" wide white tail-band . I studied the bird during it's first few visits and then attempted a photo with no success.
> Breast coloring, and tail shape and color are not consistent with
> Magnificent Hummingbird even though that would be the most likely big
> hummer around here. I have seen a couple of female Magnificent
> Hummers in Colorado and many in SE Arizona. My first impression, "This
> is not a Magnificent Hummingbird"!
>
> The good news, this bird was most certainly a Blue-throated Hummingbird.
> BOWC describes seven late summer records in western Colorado, none as
> early as this one. Bob & Bob mention 2 late June records in Grand
> County and El Paso County. The bad news, we have not seen the bird
> after around 8PM yesterday evening in spite of an intensive feeder
> watch by Larry Arnold this morning. Should the bird return, we will
> figure out a way that folks come and visit. I have my fingers crossed.
>
> Tom McConnell

Similar Species Discussion

By plumage the bird was unquestionably a Blue-throated Hummer. MAHU is discussed in the bird’s description above. There are a few other medium to large species of hummingbirds that show noticable white in the corners of the tail among the Mexican and Central American hummingbirds. These would all be extreme long-distance vagrants although there are a number North American records for Green-breasted Mango. The Green-breasted Mango and White-necked Jacobin have white bellies in both sexes while this bird had a dark breast. The sabrewings are larger and have decurved bills, while Blue-throated has the usual straight bill. White-tailed and Blue-capped Hummingbirds both have bright green breasts and bellies not at all like the bird I observed.
