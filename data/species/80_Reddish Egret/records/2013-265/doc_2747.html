Description of the Bird

I observed this white morph reddish egret in good light through a scope at range 300-500 feet.  This is almost certainly the same bird found by Duane Nelson at Adobe Creek Reservoir  (25 miles from Lake Meredith) on July 5, and observed at Lake Hasty (40 miles from Lake Meredith) by Duane Nelson, Jane Stulp, Jill Smith, and Janeal Thompson on August 21.

The egret was foraging actively, constantly moving and assuming various postures, providing opportunities to view the bird completely and near other birds.  The egret was smaller than the great blue herons and larger than the American avocets.  The body size was comparable to that of the white-faced ibises, but the egret had longer legs and a much longer neck.

During the hour that I observed the egret, I paid particular attention to the legs, feet, and bill, knowing these are key in identifying white herons.  The legs and feet were completely dark gray.  (I walked through shallow water as I approached the bird and noted at the time that the sandy mud was neither sticky nor dark.  Thus, I concluded that I was observing the true foot color of the foraging egret.)  The bill was long and thick, and it appeared from most angles to be completely dark.  However, I noted at the time that at certain angles, the eye appeared separate from the bill.  Now that I have seen up-close photos of the reddish egret at Lake Hasty on August 21 (http://www.flickr.com/photos/76674213@N08/9568993526/in/photostream/), this observation now makes sense.  The August 21 photos show some light color on the lores, which would have produced light-colored glare at the longer ranges from which I observed the egret.

My suggested aging of the bird as an adult is based on (1) the light color on the lores and (2) stringy plumes seen on the head, neck, and rear.

Similar Species Discussion

Other white herons -- white morph great blue heron, great egret, snowy egret, juvenile little blue heron, and cattle egret (particularly juvenile) -- were considered.  All of these could be eliminated based on bill color and characteristics, leg color, and foot color.  In addition, the bird's size was a better fit for reddish egret than for the others.
