Description of the Bird

Description:

Gray and “salmon” colored heron smaller than nearby Great Blue Herons, and slightly larger than Snowy Egrets. First found standing still on one leg face us. 

Marginal wing coverts gray-reddish/salmon. Primaries and secondaries gray. Head, neck and body dirty reddish-gray. Eyes yellow, bill and legs black.

 

Behavior:

When first found it was standing still in the water near the muddy shoreline. After about 40 minutes the bird began to become active and starting walking and periodically lifting its wings as it hunted small fish (shad sp.?). About 2.5 hours later it became VERY active walking and “sprinting” back and forth, chasing fish and actively (and often) raising it wings. Note: Later we noticed that the nearby Snowy Egrets were feeding in a similar fashion…which we thought odd as we thought only Reddish Egrets fed this way.

 

We were on the dam of Jackson Reservoir at the outlet canal looking north with the sun to our backs. I noticed this bird and thought, “Reddish Egret or Sandhill Crane?” Quickly realized that even standing on one leg it did not have the posture of a crane (more upright and less tail feather “fluff”).

Similar Species Discussion

Other herons eliminated due to color (no other heron is a reddish-gray color...except the larger Great Blue Heron...but, GBHE is not a uniform reddish-gray and has a thicker-based bill (that is not all black).

Sandhill Crane - color somewhat similar, but posture and tail feathers much different than a heron's.

Ruby-throated Hummingbird - Okay, NOT considered a contender. ;-)

Previous Experience

Seen several Reddish Egrets over the last 36 years in Texas, Florida and Colorado, but only two this age and plumage.

Last one in Colorado was an immature dark-morph bird very similar found at nearby Prewitt Reservoir on September 1, 2002.

VAST experience with Great Blue Heron, all the white egrets (yes, have seen white morph Reddish Egrets, too).  Some experice with Little Blue Heron and Tri-colored Herons in Colorado and several other states.

Have seen hundreds of thousands of Sandhill Cranes in Colorado and Nebraska.
