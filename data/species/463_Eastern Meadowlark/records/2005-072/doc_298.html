Description of the Bird

As my photo shows, this bird is clearly a meadowlark. The primary plumage feature indicating Eastern Meadowlark is the all white malar, with yellow only on the throat and on the lores. There is also a suggestion of streaking on the flanks vs. spotting. This does not show much in the photo, but was observed in person.
Behaviors: The bird was observed on a fence in a pasture. It was located by its singing. It would drop into the grasses unseen between bouts of singing. The bird seemed to respond to played Eastern Meadowlark songs by popping up and singing back, while it seemingly ignored Western Meadowlark songs played back.
Call: One of the primary factors identifying this bird as an Eastern Meadowlark was its vocal repertoire. Unlike the Western Meadowlark common to the area, this birds song contrasted as a little simpler, higher, and shorter. It is hard to describe, but comparisons in the field via iPod matched very well with the Eastern Meadowlark recordings on Stokes Field Guide to Bird Songs, Eastern Region. Other experienced observers such as Mark Peterson and Larry Semo report hearing the call as well, which distinctly matched Eastern Meadowlark.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Western Meadowlark ruled out by song, call, and plumage differences described above.
