Description of the Bird

The bird was clearly a meadowlark, with the classic "football" meadowlark shape, the mustard-yellow breast with the black V, the cryptic brown upperparts, etcetera. However, the bird was strikingly different in two respects from the Western Meadowlarks I am used to: first, its head was striped black and white instead of dull brown and white; and second, its face was clean white, down to and including the malar. There are many borderline, hard-to-identify meadowlarks out there, but this was not one.
Behaviors: The bird was not visible or audible for the first twenty minutes of our stay. Then it popped up on a fencepost across the field for us and sang for several minutes. It repeated one song type many times before switching, but switched song types at least four times before dropping back into the grass about ten minutes later.
Call: Bird sang at least four different song types during our observation, but was not heard to call.
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

Western Meadowlark is the bird that needs to be eliminated. Voice was the best clue here. The bird sang frequently for direct comparison; Western Meadowlarks can be heard in the background of the sound recording. Song in meadowlarks is apparently learned, so may not be diagnostic; however, of the three song types this bird sang, all were demonstrably Eastern. The phenotype of this bird was also demonstrably Eastern, with the black headstripes and very white cheek, including white malar, eliminating Western.
