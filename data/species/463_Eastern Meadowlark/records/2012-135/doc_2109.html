Description of the Bird

I heard, to my utter surprise, the bzzzrt of an Eastern Meadowlark coming from a snow covered, corn-stubble field. After listening to it for 5-10 times, I looked for, and found, the bird about 100 feet away. I put my Swarovski scope on it. There were actually two birds, rather similar in appearance, though only one was calling. Both birds were clearly meadowlarks. Their underparts were entirely yellow (excepting a veiled black "V" on the chest and the side streaking), including the sides, which were moderately intense buffy-yellow and fairly heavily streaked in dark brown. The yellow on the sides differed from the 100+ Western Meadowlarks in the vicinity (and the many I'd looked at over last week or two), which were whitish or pale buffy yellow on sides with less intense/dense streaking. The birds also looked darker overall, with darker head striping than the fall-plumaged (imm and adult) Western Meadowlarks present. The cheeks appeared paler than those of the Western Meadowlarks, most likely due less streaking on auriculars. The malar was dingy, not yellow, but not white. The birds flew in a flurry of meadowlarks, and I quickly lost it (them), not able to see the tail pattern.
Behaviors: See Description
Call: Loud, repeated Bzzzrt.
Plumage: adult fall

Similar Species Discussion

Western Meadowlark has different call note (this bird was heard 15-20 times), less yellow and less streaked sides, less blackish head stripes, generally darker auriculars, and often more yellow on malar (though some fall WEMEs have pretty pale buff malars). The call combined with the differences in side pattern, head pattern and overall darkness eliminate WEME.
