Description of the Bird

the bird was an obvious meadowlark, almost identical to nearby Western Meadowlarks. Only differences from that species as discussed below.

Visually, the head was more contrasting, with bolder crown stripes that were darker brown, and whiter supercilium and malar. The malar was completely white, with no trace of yellow.

The upperparts were more rufous tinged overall, and darker than those on WEME. The flanks were washed with a light buffy color, and the streaks on the side were continuous, and less spotted.

The tail appeared to have slightly more white than those of nearby WEMEs, but this was hard to judge.
Behaviors: the bird was mostly observed singing from a post on a barbed wire fence. Occasionally it flew out over a field, or sang from other perches. The second bird was heard counter-singing once on another post along the main road, but was not observed at all for most the observation
Call: The clincher, of course, was the song. This was made up of clear whistles, without the gurgling phrases of Western, as well as being higher pitched. The bird alternated between five or six song types, all of which had this quality. The bird was heard giving two call types, a buzzy "bzzzrt", and a high-pitched rattle that was higher pitched and drier than that of WEME.
