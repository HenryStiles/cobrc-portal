Description of the Bird

Note that the first part of this description is based on my 20-second observation at a distance of 175-200 feet. The bird was clearly a meadowlark: plump, short-tailed, and with a forward-leaning posture. It had a bright yellow breast. I tried to observe the bird’s face, and I could not discern any yellow on the bird’s malar. That is consistent with Eastern Meadowlark. However, at a distance of up to 200 feet (with 10x binoculars), I was probably not able to fully assess the exact color of the malar; still, the absence of yellow is consistent with Eastern Meadowlark. Just as important (and I think this is an under-appreciated field mark for Eastern vs. Western Meadowlark), I was struck by the bird’s pale-headed look overall--an effect contributed to mainly by the pale auriculars. The pale auriculars are most prominent on the Eastern subspecies lilianae, but they are also relatively pale on nominate magna.

The next part of this description is based on observation of the bird in flight. As it prepared to flush, I had closed into within 125–150 feet. As the bird flew away, I was struck by the extent of white on the bird’s outer rectrices; the bird seemed whiter than normal for a Western Meadowlark. Observation of the bird in flight was brief: about 2 seconds.
Behaviors: The bird was vocal and restless.
Call: Note that a sound recording and a sound spectrogram are included in this submission. When I first heard the bird's song, I recognized it as the primary song of the Eastern Meadowlark: clear, sweet (unmodulated), down-slurred whistles. I briefly considered the possibility that the bird was a Red-winged Blackbird. A common call of the Red-winged Blackbird is a clear, downslurred whistle--occasionally given in series. Then I began to approach the bird again, and it got flighty. I saw the bird as it was preparing to flush, but I could hear no flight call as it flushed this second time, and the recorder does not seem to have picked up a flight call, either.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Only Western Meadowlark.

Although the songs of the two meadowlarks are highly distinctive, there are rare instances of apparent song-switching in meadowlarks. So, given the possibility of song-switching, it is essential to consider other points that might serve to rule out Western Meadowlark. I can think of the following four:

1. Flight call. I was instantly struck by the high-pitched flight call. The next morning (May 24), I spent a great deal of time with Western Meadowlarks (I was at Gunbarrel Hill, Boulder County), as well as the morning after that (I was at the Fox Ranch, Yuma County). As I listened to the many flight calls of those Western Meadowlarks, I was constantly impressed by how much lower-pitched they sounded than what I had heard at Greenlee Preserve on May 23.

2. Facial pattern. Malar region probably not possible to accurately assess at 175-200 feet (see discussion above), but apparent lack of yellow is consistent with Eastern. Relatively pale auriculars also consistent with Eastern.

3. Tail. On flying lilianae, extensive white can usually be quantitatively assessed (white on at least the three outermost rectrices). I did not notice that much white. However, the tail seemed whiter than normal for the Western Meadowlarks I am accustomed to seeing.

4. Probability. Even in the relatively narrow zone of overlap (sympatry), meadowlarks that sing the “wrong” song (e.g., Western singing the song of an Eastern) are rarer (presumably much rarer) than meadowlarks that sing the “right” song. The two meadowlarks are almost unanimously treated as “good” species, with very little evidence for hybridization. Thus, even if this bird originated from the relatively narrow zone of overlap, it is more likely (much more likely?) to have been a “normal” Eastern than an aberrant Western singing an Eastern’s song. On top of that is the likelihood (probably high) that the bird simply came from somewhere else--that is to say, from outside the zone of overlap, where it could only be an Eastern. Put another away, while there is a small chance that an aberrant Western arrived from the zone of overlap, it seems overwhelmingly more likely that a normal Eastern came from the zone of overlap OR from anywhere else in the extensive range of Eastern.

Another consideration is lilianae vs. nominate magna. I raise this point especially in light of the fact that it is possible that the two populations will one day be “split” into separate species. Based on what I saw and heard, I don’t know for sure if the bird was lilianae or nominate magna. I would guess nominate magna given the relatively northerly latitude of Boulder County, combined with the not-overwhelmingly-white tail.

Resources Used

I confirmed my impression of flight call differences by consulting “Flight Calls of Migratory Birds: Eastern North America,” by R. E. Evans and M. O’Brien (OldBird.org, 2002). In preparing the “Similar Species” part of this report, I consulted the online Birds of North America accounts for Eastern and Western meadowlarks (Birds of North America Online, 2012). Finally, I note that I have had an informative online exchange with Bill Maynard and others about distinguishing between Eastern and Western meadowlarks.

Previous Experience

Extensive. I see and hear Western Meadowlarks many hundreds of times per year in Colorado. I grew up with Eastern Meadowlarks and know the species from my youth. I also have experience with meadowlarks in the zone of overlap--for example, during a fall 2010 visit to central Oklahoma. With members of the Oklahoma Ornithological Society, I pondered field identification of the two meadowlark species. Also relevant is that I spent three long field seasons (1992–1994) in southern New Mexico, where Eastern (lilianae) vs. Western is a constant problem. I finally note that I have given much thought to this topic here in Colorado--especially during visits to far eastern Colorado. For example, I and others heard and saw a presumed Eastern Meadowlark at the Fox Ranch, Yuma County, on 6 October 2007; of relevance is that we first suspected that it was an Eastern based on its flight call--which was also a key clue in my identification of the Greenlee Preserve bird under consideration here.
