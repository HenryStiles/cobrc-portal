Description of the Bird

In the meadow, north of the campground itself. This area had been wet in spring and had lush grass this fall. It was filled with meadowlarks (and for some reason flickers). A goodly number (15-20) of each were in a couple Russian Olives, and I was trying to obtain a good count. The Western Meadowlarks were calling frequently, occasionally singing. I heard a "Bzzzrrt" call about 10 times from the tree; I could not see the bird making the sound, but it sounded like a typical Eastern. I even had time to drag out my phone and play the Eastern call. Excellent match. I then heard the rattle call of an Eastern, which was drier/more mechanical sounding than that of a Western. The bird then flew from the side of the tree, where it had been just out of sight, with a Western. It gave the dry rattle again. The back looked darker than that of the Western, and when fanned, the tail had about 50% more white than the Western's tail did. The Western's tail looked pretty typical for that species. After a false landing or two, they flew about 300 feet away and could not be relocated. They may have crossed a fence onto private property.

Similar Species Discussion

Both call notes were used as the primary ID mark, much as was the case for the bird at Holyoke Cemetery. The back color is supportive, to an extent, but the amount of white on the tail would only occur in an aberrant WEME and was typical for EAME
