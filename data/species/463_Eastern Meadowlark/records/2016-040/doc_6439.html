Description of the Bird

I received information via Steve Mlodinow that Steven Gash had photographed a bird at the Rocky Mountain Arsenal which strongly suggested EAME.  In addition, Steven Gash reported the bird was singing a non-standard song for meadowlark (his experience at that time being confined to WEME).  I corresponded with Steven Gash and determined his bird was seen at the north side of Lake Ladora.

Jeff and I arrived and parked approximately 100+ yards from the bird's presumed location.  Almost immediately after leaving the parking lot we both detected the slurred song of an EAME in the distance to the north of us.

We arrived and saw the bird perched up singing steadily.  The song sounded like a series of slurred downward notes done generally in trios with last slur often being more drawn out - tee-ew tee-ew teee-eeew.  In addition the bird sometimes gave a rather electric bzrtt call as well as a chattery call hard to describe with letters.

The bird was clearly a meadowlark, but seemed to have a richer yellow chest that the nearby WEMEs and it had a very obvious bright white malar also not apparent on the nearby WEMEs.

Unfortunately, I was never able to get a clear view of the tail to determine how many outer tail feather were white (the bird only flew for me a couple of times and each time it was flat and at eye level).

The bird was visible while all recordings were being captures.

Similar Species Discussion

WEME is the only other remotely similar species.

The song is inconsistent with WEME.

The call notes and chatter are even more inconsistent with WEME.

The white malar indicates EAME over WEME.
