Habitat

at the base of the dam, first in tall rushes then in weedy native and non-native tall annuals near the eastern most of the three ephemeral tiny ponds below the dam/walkway

Description of the Bird

previously identified by David Tonnessen based first on sharp chip notes and later by viewing. When I arrived we used tape of SEWR call notes to get it calling. Called about 6 inches to 1.5 feet above ground. When visible, only part of bird seen each time it emerged from dense vegetation. A small wren with a buffy breast. Bill noticeably short/shorter than MARW and sort of thick based. Wings dark brown with much lighter barring. Could see short light-colored eyebrow. At times thin, light and dark streaking on dark back visible. Call notes identical to those on "tape" of SEWR calls.

Similar Species Discussion

Didn't think  much about other wren species except Marsh Wren which have been arriving in the area but that species only calling from cattail marsh areas with dense cattails. It would have showed longer, more narrow bill and less streaking on its back and with a longer, stronger white supercillium. A Winter/Pacific Wren would have a very short tail.  Photos show the SEWR tail to be much longer. 

Resources Used

Bird was I.D.ed by David Tonnessen before I arrived and btw it was a life bird for him and he knew the call notes weren't anything he knew. I did compare  photos in the Stokes guide with the ones I was able to obtain.

Previous Experience

This is my fourth SEWR at CBR. I found two of those and co-found the third. I watched one singing while perched in North Dakota. Saw a couple in northern Michigan on Isle Royle National Park where I worked for two summers.
