Habitat

The overstory was 10' tall Russian olives, some of the younger ones in the area, and there was a 100% ground cover of Canada thistle. There were numerous crisscrossing smaller brush near the ground where the bird prefered to hide.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Leica 10x42 Ultravids
I saw the bird once for about 2 seconds in my scope, a Leica APO with 32x ww lens while trying unsuccesfully to digiscope it.
Distance:  I got as close as 10 feet away while trying to photograph with my point-and-shoot camera
Light:  Variable. I kept trying to get a photo with the light from behind me but the bird was easier to see with the light slight in my eyes, although the sun was mostly overhead at this hour.

Description of the Bird

The bird was a wren by the dark-barred tail.

When I first saw its face, I was excited. The bird had a light buffy eyestripe, that contrasted with the darker brown crown. The crown was faintly streaked with fine whitish stripes. The bill was short as compared to the long bill of a Marsh Wren, and was orangish or yellowish in color with a slight down curve throughout its lenght.

I was able to see the bird's back a few times when it crawled around low to the ground. The back was orange-brown and it had noticeable dark streaks, that were not always seen, except twice in flight, and twice when it was creeping through the brushy sticks near the ground.

The best overall view for color was when it flushed from the ground. I saw it in flight at least 5 times, always short low flights where the bird's overall orangey brown dorsal side was unexpectedly bright. In flight I also saw a orange-brown belly.

I was able to see the bird's tail on a few occasions from underneath. It was brown with parallel darker brown bars through the length.

The eye color was blackish and there appeared to be a short darkish eyeline extending directly behind the bird's eye. There also was a light are in front of the eye. I was not sure if it was a light lore, my first thought, or a partially broken eyeline, with a darker spot just in front and above the eye.
Behaviors: This bird stayed low to the ground. While moving in the understory brush, it was never more than two feet above the ground. It was almost always partially hidden, so that the only time I had a view of the entire view at the same time was when it flew. I was very surprised that it returned to the exact same low branchy spot at least 5 times, if not more, where it moved into the densest tangles, but was almost always partially in view. The bird also ventured into both very dense Canada thistle and very dense tall sunflower, a species that grows tall in moist areas, but is not the species that is the roadside weed. When the bird was in those two habitats it was not visible, and seen only when I accidently flushed it.
Call: heard both one-note and two-note loud clicks
Plumage: uncertain

Similar Species Discussion

Marsh Wren is the only other wren species in the ABA Area with the parallel streaks on its back. Marsh Wren has a darker, unstreked head, a longer darker bill, and a richer rufous back. It also has a whiter, longer supercillium. It also has a big rufous patch on the wing that this bird did not have. I heard at least 5 Marsh Wrens a few hours before seeing this bird, and the Marsh Wrens scold notes were way different, as was the habitat. I have never seen a Marsh Wren out of the marsh habitat, but Sedge Wren can be seen in a variety of brushy habitat types, along with wetlands.
