Description of the Bird

In the field, I saw that the bird was small, with light buffy sides and a whitish throat, with an often up-raised, barred tail, and a visible eye line, though not apparently as strong as expected for a Marsh Wren. Although I did not see more detailed marks in the field, the photos show streaking on the top of the head, consistent with Sedge Wren, without the brownish crown of Marsh Wren. I did not see details of the wings, nor do I have photographs where these are clear enough to be useful.
Behaviors: The behavior of the bird was entirely consistent with Sedge Wren as described in Sibley. Though I was able to see the bird on the tree at the first sighting, this was the last decent look I got at it. The rest of the time I was listening to the bird and following it, it stayed low in the tall grass, though I could occassionally tell where it was by both the calling and movement of the grass. I spent, along with Nick Komar, whom I’d called, at least the next hour trying to photograph the bird again, and it stayed low in the grass nearly all of that time; we saw it quickly fly, low, only a handful of times. We were several times just feet from the bird, and it stayed down in the grass, often calling.
Call: The call was a series of loud, clear, speparated and relatively "deep" chips, with the occassional multiples of the same. The call was a near-perfect match for the "chat calls_ND" entry in the _Sibley eGuide to Birds of North America_, Android version. For comparison, the call never had the "rattle" character of the Marsh Wren.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

I was alerted to the bird by its call, which I did not recognize and thought perhaps could be a warbler or a sparrow whose call I didn't recognize. After seeing the bird, I considered only wrens. I eliminated House, Rock and Canyon Wrens (all expected there or nearby) quickly by call and plumage/coloration. The real competitor for me was Marsh Wren. Though I saw the eye line in the quick look I had, it did not appear as substantial as I expected for Marsh Wren. The overall coloration also seemed light for Marsh Wren. At the time I was able to look at the photos I had, and these led me to look up Sedge Wren. The coloration of the bird matched, which was reinforced by looking at the photos at the time of the sighting. Further, the call of the bird did not fit with what I recognize in Marsh Wrens, particularly this bird's call did not have a "rattle" character. I confirmed this by comparing them both at the time.

Resources Used

Sibley's eGuide to Birds of North America app for Android-based phones, including vocalizations. This was used extensively at the time of the sighting and afterward.

Previous Experience

I had never seen nor heard a Sedge Wren. I would say my experience with Marsh Wren is "intermediate", meaning I would expect that I would correctly identify one 99% of the time by sight and 80% of the time by ear. For House Wren, I think I would correctly identify one 99% of the time by sight and 90% by ear
