Description of the Bird

Shortly after 9 am, on a sunny morning, Mark Peterson, Brandon Percival, and I started walking up a low grassy ridge given the moniker ?Pipit Hill.? We had walked about 100 yards when Mark called out, ?Sprague?s Pipit? while pointing roughly to the northeast. I expected to see a bird flying away, but to my surprise, I saw a pipit descending in bouncing, step-wise fashion towards us. As it passed us, we had a mostly side/beneath view with the sun to our backs at about 15-20 feet. It flew on and landed about 100 feet from us. We walked to the area, failed to detect it on the ground, and it flushed upwards, gaining altitude rapidly in a step-wise fashion, and flew > 100 yards away. When it flew, it call several times and was easily heard. It was seen in flight for about 15-25 seconds.

Roughly size and shape of an American Pipit, appearing similar in size, but proportionately a tad shorter tailed. The bird?s underparts were easily viewed when it was close and in pretty good lighting. The face was relatively plain in pattern, excepting a bold white eyering. The background color was somewhat buffy. A series of fine dark streaks formed a pectoral band or necklace across the upper chest. The entire chest was moderately yellowish buff, and this buff extended onto sides. Most of the belly/vent was white or whitish as was the throat. The call was a zjheep-zjheep, with a bit of a squeaky quality. Not as emphatic as that of American Pipit. Neither note of the couplet was emphasized over the other.

Similar Species Discussion

The call really eliminates all other species, but especially eliminates sparrows. Separation from American Pipit is as follows:
1. Bold eyering but dull or absent supercilium
2. Buff chest, white belly, narrow chest streaking forms a combination not seen in American Pipit.
3. Call wrong for American Pipit, in quality, but also in emphasis.
4. Flight style when taking off typical for Sprague?s Pipit, unusual for American.
