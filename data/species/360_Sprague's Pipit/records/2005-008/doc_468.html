Description of the Bird

the bird was a small sparrow-like bird, identified as a pipit by the sparrow-like streaked brown plumage, but with a thin bill, smaller head compared to the body, and habit of running. It was slightly larger than a Savannah Sparrow. The bill was quite thin, and fairly short, though longer and heavier than expected for American Pipit. It was mainly flesh or orange colored, with a darker culmen. The eye was dark.

The plumage was primarily a medium sandy-brown. The head was pale, especially on the face, where a lack of bold markings gave the bird a "blank" faced look. The crown and nape were lighly streaked. The back was boldly streaked with very dark brown or black on the lighter sandy-brown. The bird had faint white wingbars. The tail was fairly short (for a pipit), and the outer two or three tail feathers were white, only visible in flight, with the inner tail feathers being brown. I did not see the underparts in the field.
Behaviors: The bird, when flushed, would rise into the air rapidly, fly a distance, and drop like a stone onto the ground. When landed it would run a short distance, and remain absolutely still in a clump of grass, almost invisible.
Call: In flight the bird was heard to give a squeeky "squeet" call a couple of times.
Plumage: probable adult

Similar Species Discussion

All sparrows were ruled out by thin bill, and smaller head compared to the body. Horned Lark was ruled out by smaller size, streaked appearance on the back, shorter tail and wings, and thinner appearance.

American Pipit was ruled out by "blank" face look, paler and without strong markings of that species, overall paler color, strongly streaked back, shorter tail with more white, and squeeky "squeet" call. Habit of flying high into the air and then plummeting to the ground also atypical of that species. The bill color, mostly flesh colored with a darker culmen, also supports Sprague's, as does it's slightly heavier shape.
