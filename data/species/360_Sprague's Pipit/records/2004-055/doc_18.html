Description of the Bird

A fairly small (roughly sparrow or Horned Lark size), warm brown prairie bird. The bill is fairly long and slightly decurved. Overall the bill is pale pinkish-orange, with a dark culmen. The face is pale and fairly featureless, with a large dark eye surrounded by a very thin, sharply defined white eyering. The auriculars are a warm pale brown, with a slightly contrastingly pale malar. The crown is has alternating light and dark streaks. The nape has pale sides and a streaked center. The back appears scaly, with light edged, dark centered mantle feathers. The scaly pattern extends into the scapulars and lesser and median coverts. The greater coverts are dark brown with light edges, giving them a striped look. Two thin buffy-white wing bars are formed by the white tips of the median and greater coverts. The folded flight feathers also have light edges, but the edging is thicker and not as white as in the greater coverts. The primaries only barely reach beyond the undertail coverts. The tail appears to be somewhat short, and the edge of the white outer tail feathers is visible in profile. The breast appears to be a warm beige color (colder-toned on at least one bird) with the sides and the upper center of the breast showing streaks of connected dark spots. The lower center of the breast and the belly are free of spots. The warm beige extends down the upper flanks. The lower flanks and belly are paler and colder-toned, almost white and unmarked. The leg feathers and undertail coverts also appear white. The legs look long and sturdy, and are pink. The toes also are pink.
When flying overhead or flushing away from me I got the impression of a lot of white in the tail. The width of each outer white side was roughly equal to the width of the dark center of the tail. The underparts appeared to be warm beige to white, without noticeable features in the underwing. The flying birds appeared to be sturdy and compact, with thick blunt wings. The tail appeared shorter than found on flying American Pipits.
Behaviors: These appear to be ground loving birds, mostly sticking to thick grassy areas but also feeding in a sprouted winter wheat field that was mostly bare dirt. Other prairie birds were around including American Pipits, Horned Larks, Vesper and Savannah Sparrows, McCowns and Chestnut-collared Longspurs, but the Spragues Pipits seemed to keep to themselves or with another Spragues Pipit. Birds were usually located by hearing them flying over or by flushing them into flight. The birds quickly gained elevation when flushed, and topped out about 100 feet in the air, sometimes flying directly or sometimes in wide circles. Some flying birds called a lot, giving single and or double squeet calls. Others called only once or twice and then flew silently. If they decided to land it was very abrupt the birds almost looked like they were suddenly dropping from the sky, only putting on the brakes by spreading their wings at the last moment. Once on the ground, the birds were difficult to find due to their coloration and their habit of staying in thick clumps of grass. When detected, though, the birds were seen running short distances, stopping to look and listen, running again, etc. When not looking around they stayed parallel to the ground, almost looking hunched over. Occasionally a bird would fly 10 to 20 feet within a foot of the ground, perhaps to quickly get to an area where it saw potential prey. A couple of times we observed the birds catching insects.
Call: The birds were quiet on the ground, but quite vocal in flight. They gave their squeet note singly and in doubles. We had several example audio files with us from the Flight Calls of Migratory Birds CD ROM by William Evans and Michael O'Brien and the sounds we heard matched them well.
Plumage: Unknown Perhaps adult photographed

Similar Species Discussion

The bill was too long and thin to be a sparrow or longspur. In addition to the bill being too long, the wing projection was too short and the vocalization was wrong for Horned Lark. American Pipits were also heard on our trip, and their vocalization was different (thinner, always doubled, etc.). Also, American Pipits lack the blank face look (instead having distinctive supercillium and malar patterns), have darker legs, have a longer tail with less extensive white sides, and lack the scaly pattern on their back and have less obvious wing bars.
