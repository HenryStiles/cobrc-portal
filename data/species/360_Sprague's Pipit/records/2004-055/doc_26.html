Description of the Bird

The birds we saw on the ground were pipit-shaped, with medium-length, thin bills (not sparrow bills), pale legs, and medium-length tails, a little shorter than American Pipit but close to that length. The birds were a pale sandy brown above and whitish below. They were densely streaked with darker brown on the crown, nape, and (I think) the sides of the breast. They didn't have bright white edgings to any back or wing feathers, though the buffy edgings to those feathers did create a faint pair of buffy wingbars as well as a complex, rather scaled pattern above. The birds' tails were dark, with white outer tail feathers visible when they flew. The faces of the birds were rather plain, without a lot of streaking on the auriculars, throat, lores or supercilium, and the black eyes looked quite large.
Behaviors: At least one of the birds flushed out of 6-8 inch grass on the side of a ridge, then was seen flying high to the east. This or a different bird, and a second individual, were relocated by the other half of our group on the ground in a field planted to winter wheat. The birds were walking and foraging on the ground. We observed them for about ten minutes through the scope, until they flew off together, calling. While we were watching the two birds on the ground, a third bird flew over us giving the "squeet" call. We heard other birds giving this call later, but were not able to see them or determine whether they were different from the three birds we had encountered earlier.
Call: The birds gave the "squeet!" call, which was how we located them and identified them in flight. One bird was never seen; it flew over, calling, as we were watching two others on the ground.

The call of the Sprague's Pipits we were hearing was a single-syllable, high-pitched squeak with rising intonation, often repeated. It was loud and emphatic.
Plumage: presumed adult

Similar Species Discussion

The birds in flight were identified by call. Also in the area were some species with rather similar calls, notably Horned Lark and American Pipit. The Sprague's calls, unlike the calls of the other two species, were almost always noticeably upslurred. The American Pipits tended to have monotone calls (often paired, tsit-tsit) and the Horned Larks have variable intonations, but most often slur their syllables down. The larks, to my ear, sound more musical than either of the pipits, which tend to sound a little more sibilant and toneless. Sprague's sounds louder, more emphatic, "more alarmed," I think because its call is lower in pitch than those of the other two species.

The visual ID was straightforward after my experiences with Sprague's in previous weeks (see below). The bill shape eliminated all sparrows and buntings. The pale legs and pale buffy face with a big black eye, along with the call notes, eliminated larks and American Pipit.

Resources Used

I do not recall that anyone had a field guide in hand during this observation, but we checked Sibley before (since Sprague's was the target bird on this trip) and after the observation. In addition, several of us had been listening to the recorded calls on the Flight Calls CD-rom during the week preceding, studying for this trip.

Previous Experience

This was my fourth time seeing Sprague's Pipits. The first was in October 2003 outside Lincoln, Nebraska--up to twenty calling birds, one spotted briefly on the ground. The second sighting was the weekend before this, near Ft. Pierre, South Dakota. I went to a location where many Sprague's were being seen and found a bird that had no fear of me. I spent half an hour within 20 feet of it. It absolutely refused to flush and eventually I was down on my hands and knees, looking at the thing from less than three feet away, while it foraged on the ground. (I know, I can't believe that either.) The third time seeing Sprague's Pipits was the day before this sighting, in Norton County, Kansas with Scott Seltman, the Schmokers and Henry Armknecht. Scott put us on several birds in one of his pastures and taught us what they sounded like, what kind of habitats they liked and how best to search for them.

I have seen and heard many, many, many American Pipits and Horned Larks.
