Description of the Bird

Norm Erthal organized a trip to the Hale area in search of Spragues Pipits, which have shown a tendency to regularly occur in mid-fall along a shortgrass ridge located east of Bonny Reservoir and north of the Republican River. As the group arrived at the site, we began walking the ridgeline, each birder separated by perhaps 50 feet. Within the first ten minutes, we had flushed a small group of Horned Larks and a single Chestnut-collared Longspur, which took off rather vertically across the landscape. Soon thereafterwards, however, a Spragues Pipit flushed very close to Norm and Norm and I immediately yelled Spragues. When in the air, the flushed Spragues was joined by another Spragues and they both circled nearby for a few minutes. Following that first encounter, we continued walking the ridgeline, flushing an additional bird twice.

Both flushed birds remained motionless and then flushed at close range. When each bird flushed, they skyrocketed vertically. In flight, the birds would rise to approximately 300 feet, flying in circles and calling a continuous tinkling, double noted call similar to a sewee, sewee, sewee. After a few minutes of aerial circles, the birds would dive back towards the ground.

Although we were not able to see the birds on the ground, field marks observed when birds flushed close included an overall buffy appearance, narrow profile, long dark tail with diagnostic white outer rectrices, wings buffy brown to brown with wing coverts edged pale, showing an indistinct wing bar, streaked upperparts, and a somewhat streaked breast barely visible when the birds flushed. In flight, from below, the underwing coverts and inner secondaries were dark, contrasting with pale mid- and central rectrices and primaries.

I was able to obtain some poor quality photographs of one of the birds that depict overall shape, coloration, underwing pattern, and, on one photo, a barely visible white wing bar.
Behaviors: See above
Call: A continuous tinkling, double noted call similar to a sewee, sewee, sewee when in flight.
Plumage: basic

Similar Species Discussion

The Spragues Pipits were separated from Horned Larks by lack of black areas on head and upper breast, longer tail, buffier appearance, and lack of pale underwings. American Pipit was eliminated by lack of buff-striped upperparts, generally paler coloration, and voice as expressed by Spragues. Separated from sparrows by slim profile, flight style, and call.

Previous Experience
