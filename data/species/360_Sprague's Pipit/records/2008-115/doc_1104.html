Description of the Bird

As the bird flew towards us, and then almost right over our heads at an altitude of about 40 feet, we got a pretty good look at it in binoculars. It was shaped right for a Sprague's Pipit, with a longer tail than a Horned Lark but a shorter one than an American Pipit, and its underparts appeared to be mostly whitish possibly washed with buffy, particularly across the breast, which also may have been lightly streaked. The face definitely seemed blank, without the dark markings of a Horned Lark.
Behaviors: The bird was observed only in high prolonged flight; we lost sight of it before it landed.
Call: The bird was detected first by its call, a high-pitched, clear, rising, Barn-Swallow-like "squeet!" that was repeated at least 8-10 times while the bird flew towards us from the west, over us, and then off to the south. All renditions of the call were identical, which alone is almost enough to rule out any longspur, Horned Lark, or American Pipit.
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

Longspurs are fairly easy to rule out given their chunky, very short-tailed shape and their distinctive vocalizations. No sparrows sound anything like Sprague's Pipit, and few would match the distinctive size and shape anyway. Mostly you have to rule out Horned Lark and American Pipit. Both can sound very like Sprague's Pipit once in a while, but neither would ever give the same one-syllabled Sprague's-like call many times in a row. The fact that the Sprague's made the exact same noise 8-10 times in a row is distinctive. In addition, the bird definitely did not have the black face and blackish tail of a Horned Lark, and its tail was too long. The tail appeared a little short for American Pipit, but other aspects of the visuals were close to that species; AMPI was ruled out mostly by vocalizations.

Previous Experience

I have seen and heard Sprague's Pipits on about 6-8 occasions in 5 states (SD, NE, CO, KS, AR). Hiking in the Rockies all summer for the past 3 years, I have heard many, many different vocalizations of Horned Larks and American Pipits, and I had seen and heard both species on this trip and recent trips as well.
