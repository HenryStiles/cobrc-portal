Description from eBird checklist on Day 1:  

I initially heard several call notes sounding vaguely like a distant Am Robin. After a short amount of time, I heard the calls again, seemingly getting closer but I saw no robins. It clicked that this could be the bird I was hoping for and flipped on Merlin which hit Sprague's immediately. I strained to get a visual on the bird but saw nothing and unfortunately the bird seemed to moving south and getting fainter until I couldn’t hear it. I continued walking and flushed a pipit (streaky brown, more slender shaped than HOLA or longspurs, a thin, sharp bill and with very white-edged tail) about 25 feet ahead of me. It flew low then up and out of view. It uttered a few of the same sharp calls as the first. No recordings of second bird as I was unsuccessfully attempting to obtain flight photos. Typical behavior and proper habitat for this species. Migration was forecasted to be good for the Front Range today with a front breaking to the north and winds overnight from the east/northeast. 

 

The day before this observation I had observed a small bird flying over calling in similar habitat within the county that gave me pause.  It was left unidentified on that list, but caused me to review flight calls of Sprague's Pipits later that night.  As pipits had been reported in Yuma county already, I decided to go to Coal Creek Open Space that next day with the intent of specifically looking for this species as well as migrating Longspurs (which I knew was a longshot but it worked out!). 

 

Photos obtained on Day 2 show streaky buff pipit with white outertail and slightly brownish auricular area (see eBird reports).  

Similar Species Discussion

Other birds that could be considered:

American Pipit - different call notes and streakier buff color in Sprague's; while several AMPI were heard flying over on that day calling, they are generally not in open grasslands flushing up and ducking back down.  

Longspurs -  different call notes from any longspur; while they are also generally brown with white in the outer tail, the bill was thinner and body was more proportionately pipit-like vs sparrow-like.  

Horned Lark -  different calls and has a different shape with black mask and tail usually appearing darker overall in flight.

Previous Experience

I first saw this species during Fall migration in Yuma county Colorado many years ago where we had specifically gone to find them at a newly discovered spot (Pipit Hill).  Similar behavior observed at that time, with birds flushing up from low open fields/stubble and flying a short distance before returning to the ground.  

Several subsequent experiences in North Dakota and Montana during summer where birds were breeding.  Much different behavior at this time of year (long aerial song displays) but I did enjoy scope views of them feeding on dirt roads giving excellent study of plumage, shape and size.
