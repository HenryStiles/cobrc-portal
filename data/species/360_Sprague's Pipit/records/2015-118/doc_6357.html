Description of the Bird

On 14 October 2015, I went to a shortgrass prairie section of Chico Basin Ranch containing rolling sand hills to see if Sprague's Pipits used this habitat type in El Paso County where they had not been documented.  When I drove into the appropriate habitat I heard a sharp 1-note call I was not familiar with so I got out and walked on the sand hills with shortgrass dominated by blue grama.  I flushed a few more, singles and pairs and at times they flew overhead.  At times, the sharp call note was doubled on the same high pitch. 


After five successful visits to the same spot, the pipits were observed to act differently on different days.  On two windy days, the birds were mostly inaudible and the wind blew flushed birds out of view and they did not hover.  On calm days, the pipits sometimes climbed high into the sky where they hovered or flew about in undulating flight at a high altitiude before flying straight to the ground on some occasions and flying to lower altittudes and hovering, or flying as if uncertain where to land.

Depending on which day, sometimes all of the pipits flushed from the shortgrass prairie (blue grama dominant) and often landed in slightly taller grasses that also had blue grama.  On 24 October, only one bird for sure flushed from the ground while others seemed to appear out of nowhere already in flight. I returned on 26 October and saw three more birds, one flushing from the ground nearby and then flying higher and higher where it was joined by a second bird, both flying out of sight, not seen returning to the ground. 

The big question remains, are the 24 and 26th October birds the same birds from 14 October, when I first found them.  Approximately the same number of birds were found at in the same location on all five days except for on 22 October when I only found one.  No birds were ever found where the dominant plant was sand sage or where there was disturbance of the blue grama grassland which had created rows of dense Russian thistle. The taller grasslands had Chestnut-collared Longspurs on occasion, but so far no SPPIs except when flushed into those taller grasses.

There appears to me to be more similar habitat on neighboring private properties and I would not be surprised if this species is in fact a regular October migrant in this habitat type in El Paso and other Colorado counties with similar habitat, very simlar to the shortgrass prairie found on "Pipit Hill" on the Colorado/Kansas border.  The eBird maps for these Chico SPPIs are all of the same group of birds I found originally and they are not in the correct location because getting to the location involves going through a locked gate.  In my opinion, the incorrect locations should be combined and placed in the correct location to allow data analysis to show there was one location for the pipits and not the four locations currently incorrectly shown on the eBird map.  

Similar Species Discussion

The photos show a pipit.  The big eye look on a somewhat blank face with a faint dark line below the eye can be seen on some flight photos.  On one photograph you can see the streaked head and back.  The tail was not pumped as it would have been in an American Pipit and the call notes were not multiple fast notes that AMPIs give.  The tail had noticeable white outertail feathers and maybe on the short side for the size of the bird.  The breast was light and had some buff coloration with noticeable streaking, but only on the upper breast and not on the flanks.  The bill was sharp-pointed with a yellowish lower mandible.  On the 20th of October, two of the birds flew very high and then descended straight to the ground.  On a very windy day, 15 October the birds behaved differently and never climbed as high and they were blown about by the stregth of the wind.  On that day they did NOT act very SPPI-like.  Only one bird was observed on the ground but it actively fed at the edge of shortgrass blue grama and taller grasses.  Besides American Pipit, there are no other birds to compare this bird to exept maybe Skylark which has a similar flight pattern at times but a different look. Horned Larks were the most common species present and were fairly easy to separate by different call notes and appearing much lighter in flight and with a darker contrasting tail.

Resources Used

I looked at the two book I have that have photographs for comparison with my photographs.  Stokes Field Guide to Birds of North America and the Crossley I.D. guide, the latter showing birds in flight and on the ground. 

Previous Experience

I drove to North Dakota to see this species and saw it there on two different occasions. I have seen a couple at TNC's Fox Ranch in October and one at Ramah Reservoir in October and my lifer looks were in the San Rafael Grasslands in SE Arizona in winter, but otherwise I have very little experience with this species until now. American Pipit I have seen on its tundra breeding grounds and more often in fall, but on occasion in spring migration.  They are sometimes very common at the shoreline of Big Johnson Reservoir.
