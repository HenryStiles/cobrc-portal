Description of the Bird

Found this a.m. along with Bill Maynard. Previously reported birds, there were at least four Sprague's Pipits in the short grass prairie. The only other species at this location that morning were Horned Larks and Chestnut-collared Longspurs. The pipits showed in an undulating climb that went much higher than the Horned Larks, and then they would dive down to the grass. There was an accompanying flight call. The birds had all the markings shown in the Nat Geo 6th edition including the smallish head and corresponding large eye. One photo (courtesy of Bill Maynard) is available on request.

Similar Species Discussion

The other two species at the location were Horned Larks and Chestnut-collared Longspurs. The flights of the lards were fairly low to the ground and of short duration. They were seen clearly and easily identified. The longspurs flew higher than the larks, but did not have the undulating motion of the pipits. American Pipits were not considered due to the flight pattern, flight call, and visible sightings including photos taken by Bill Maynard.

Resources Used

I used the National Geographic 6th edition in the field. At home I compared Bill Maynard's photo to the Sibley Field Guide to Bords of Western North America.

Previous Experience

I have many records of sightings of American Pipits across the western US, mostly in the October-March timeframe.
