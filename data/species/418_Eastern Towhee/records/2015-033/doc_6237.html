Description of the Bird

The bird popped up in typical brushy Towhee habitat although I see them more commonly a bit further west closer to the mountains. A few other Towhees of the Spotted species were observed in the same area, scratching on the ground and popping up in the bushes and then down and away. When this one popped up to a high point in the brush giving us a temporarily unobstructed view, Beth and I both remarked we were seeing something we didn't at first recognize - a Towhee for sure but different than others we had observed. There were no spots on the bird and it had a warm chocolate brown back, tail and head and a dark eye. Tail was brown on top with little to no white on edge of tail observable from the angle of view we had. The eye appeared black or quite dark with little to no distinction between iris and pupil.  There was a white patch on the wing but no spots. The location of the bird was just west of the main path in the Open Space near the Library-end entrance.  The library had feeders within 100 yards or less of these birds that were attracting House Finches,  House Sparrows, a few other common varieties of sparrow and finch, Chickadees, etc. and we were examining the brush for these birds as they left the feeder when we encountered the Towhees.  We did not observe this bird make any calls or song - no mew, no Drink Your Tea - but did see one of the Spotteds chipping and heard Spotted chipping in the brush - if there is a difference between Eastern and Spotted chip we had no recording device or knowledge at that time to sort that out.  We observed it, lost it in the brush, checked our field guide to confirm ID, refound it briefly but it continued moving away to the Southwest and was not refound. We did not get pictures.  We did not have cameras ready at first sighting and were busy looking at the bird because we found it unusual to us. After the initial sighting, we spent some time looking in field guides before trying to refind to try to photo.  We did relocate the bird briefly further back in the brush but could not get the camera to focus on the bird instead of the branches in between us and it before it moved further away into a dense reeded area at which point we didn't see it again. When seen the second time After reporting in Ebird Tony Leukering eventually asked us to report it here.  

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was observed near two Spotted Towhees and was eliminated as being the same by chocolate brown color of tail back and head, lack of "spots", little to no white on tail observed, eye black solid color not red, chest/belly unstreaked.  Since it was with Spotted Towhees we thought at first it was a female Spotted since they can appear a little brownish - but we reconsidered that because of this bird's dark eye, lack of spots, white patch, and the color on head, back, tail top being a consistent warm brown without tinges of black that we could see. 

Resources Used

We used Peterson's, Kaufman's, Sibley's guides and Internet photos for comparison.

Previous Experience

Beth and I both have seen Spotted Towhee frequently, but neither of us had seen Eastern Towhee before this occurrence. Since we had no photos, Beth and I made a point of paying attention to juvenile and female Spotted Towhees after this observation to mentally check against what we had seen.  Beth and I discussed this several times since and ended up thinking we had indeed seen Eastern Towhee. At the 2014 Sterling CFO convention I had good, close looks at another female Eastern Towhee on a CFO field trip with confirmation by group leaders.  This observation also increased my confidence in our ID in this first sighting, but admittedly our experience with Eastern Towhee is quite limited.
