Description of the Bird

I first observed this bird when it popped up to pishing.  It was foraging in the lower branches of a grove of Russian Olive Trees and the brush underneath them, hopping and scratching about both on the ground and then popping up to 4 to 6 feet off the ground.  There were also many American Robins feeding in the area (and a Cooper's Hawk flying overhead initially).  As soon as the bird was fully visible and I could see the body and wings I realized it was not a Spotted Towhee, as I was expecting, but a male Eastern Towhee!  The bird remained out in the open while I was pishing for several minutes allowing quite a few photos.  After a while it dropped back in the brush and moved away from me.  I continued to follow the grove of trees in the direction it was moving and saw the bird three additional times (views ranging from 30 second view time up to two or three minutes).  After it stopped responding to pishing I played an Eastern Towhee chip note on my phone and the bird immediately popped back up and started chipping.  The chip note made by the bird was identical to that playing from my iBird Pro for Eastern Towhee.  It continued to chip for an additional 10 minutes (it could be heard even when out of sight and after I stopped the recording on my phone), but never sang.  At one time the bird flew out of the tree grove and into some weeds in bright sunlight, however it fairly quickly returned to the brush under the trees.  At the end of my observation time the bird landed on some brush only about 12-15 feet from me for about 30 seconds before dropping back into deep brush. 

This bird exhibited a body size smaller than an American Robin, but larger than a White-Crowned Sparrow (with much longer tail than either of these species).  The  bird was consistent in body shape, color and size to belong in the Rufous-Sided Towhee complex.  It had a black head (coming to roughly a v-shape on the chest), with dark reddish-brown eyes, a solid black back, black tail feathers as viewed from the top, and mostly black wings.  The bird's flanks were a bright rust color and it had a prominent white belly.  The vent area was a paler buff color.  Wing pattern was solid black with a small white 'square' at the lower edge of the wing and thin white line showing at the lower edge of the wing.  The top edge of the wing showed three white lines along the top, but was devoid of any white spots on the scapulars.  The bird sat with its wings slightly drooped the majority of the time I was observing it. The bill was consistent in size and shape with a Towhee and grayish-pink in color (slightly duskier on top of upper bill).  Legs were pink.  The bird often had a slightly raised crest on the back of the head.  The tail was proportionately very long, also conducive for a Towhee.  When perched the under tail pattern was mostly white with black spear tip shaped marking closest to the vent area and projecting about 1/3 of the way down the tail.  A photo of the bird in flight shows the outermost tail feathers having the most white (roughly 1/2 of the way up) with the outermost tail feather having a solid white outer border, with the interior white going at an angle across the outer three tail feathers, and the middle feathers being solid black. 

Similar Species Discussion

Spotted Towhee and Spotted Towhee x Eastern Towhee hybrid were the primary similar species to be considered.

Spotted Towhee was eliminated by the lack of any spotting on the bird's scapulars and also by the under tail pattern, which showed white spotting extending higher than it would for all races of Spotted Towhee.  In review of a flight photo the white outer edge on the outermost tail feather is consistent for Eastern Towhee (Red-Eyed race), but not Spotted.

A hybrid was somewhat trickier to eliminate since they can look much like a pure bird, however almost all hybrids show at least some indication of mixed heritage (generally a few spots on the wings).  This bird did not show any intermediate traits between Spotted Towhee and Eastern Towhee to suggest a hybrid and repeatedly called exactly like an Eastern Towhee.

I suppose that a male American Redstart must also be eliminated, as the color pattern is superficially similar when viewed from the front.  American Redstart can quickly be eliminated by the substantially larger and heftier size of my bird, longer tail, and heavy grayish bill.  Under tail markings and wings did not show any orange as would be present on male American Redstart either.

Resources Used

Although I had not seen this species before in real life, I have viewed many photos of them in bird books and online.  Since this species is fairly distinct in appearance I had an immediate knowledge of what this bird was in the field.  I did however consult my iBird pro on my phone in the field.  Once I returned home I consulted several bird books (Sibley's and Stoke's) to confirm the field marks and searched for photos of the under tail online to make sure that the bird did not show any traits of being a hybrid. 

I posted a link to some of my images on cobirds and CFO facebook page and no one suggested that the bird was anything else (only encouraged by Rachel and Steve to make sure I reported it here) :)

Previous Experience

As indicated previous I do not have any experience with Eastern Towhee.

I do however have moderate experience with a number of different subspecies of Spotted Towhee, having observed them here in Colorado, but also in Arizona, Oregon, and British Columbia.

I have also seen about 3 or 4 hybrid Eastern x Spotted Towhees here in Colorado.
