Description of the Bird

We were pishing amont extensive willow thickets when David called out Eastern Towhee.
This bird was of the "Rufous-sided Towhee" type, with a black head and chest, black back and wings, and black tail excepting:
1- White square at base of primaries, full in size compared with that of a "normal" Eastern Towhee
2- Thin white edging extending posteriorly along edge of primaries forming a very narrow line backwards from white box
3- White tip to one one tertial
4- Extensive white spots under tail that very nearly reached the tip of the undertail coverts

Remainder of underparts where white, excepting deep "rufous" sides and butter-buff undertail coverts.
Eye red
Bill black
Head shape more rounded and bill seemed smaller than that of typical Spotted Towhee.
Though light not optimal, bird moved around enough that we could be certain that there were not white markings on back or scapulars (we were able to see the white tip to the tertial, so we were able to detect small white markings)

Similar Species Discussion

Spotted Towhee would have been spotted, lacked square at base of primaries

Hybrid Spotted x Eastern Towhee. This is more difficult as some hybrids can, essentially, lack Spotted Towhee spotting. In such hybrids, the white box is reduced or absent. Also, all of the Spotted x Eastern Towhees I've seen have been, presumably, montanus (Rocky Mountain) Spotted Towhee x Eastern Towhee. (Rocky Mountain Spotted Towhee is far more numerous than arcticus during summer anywhere in CO, including the eastern S Platte). I think that is why so many of the hybrids nearly lack spotting (hybrids between the more boldly marked arcticus Spotted Towhee and Eastern seem all to have noticeable white spots above.) The tail spots were full-sized Eastern Towhee tail spots. I've not seen a hybrid in CO with white tail spots nearly this extensive, presumably because the Spotted Towhee parent is of the Rocky Mountain subspecies, which has reduced white tail spots. An arcticus Spotted x Eastern Towhee could have large white tail spots, but should have visible white spotting above. 

So, to summarize the babble above -- It is certainly conceivable that, with enough back-crossing, a hybrid could look identical to either parent, and could even call like either parent, thus rendering the identification of any Eastern Towhee impossible. However, phenotypically, this bird showed no evidence for Spotted Towhee genes. It had normal Eastern Towhee wing pattern, lacked spotting dorsally and on wings, and had large white spots under tail. The structure also resembled that of Eastern Towhee
