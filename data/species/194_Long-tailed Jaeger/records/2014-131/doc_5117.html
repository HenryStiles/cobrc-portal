Description of the Bird

A dark jaeger was first spotted by Brandon Percival. When I arrived he and Margie Joy were watching it in scopes far to the west of South Shore Marina.  It eventually flew, a lazy flight across the reservoir and back and forth near the North Shore Picnic area.  We drove there for a closer view.  The bird was never close but on one pass it was photographed.  Photos, see below, showed a cold appearance of dark brown overall. The upper wing tips had two white outer primary feather shafts and a gray-brown collar that stood out. The head seemed small although without another jeager for comparison this was more of a thought than a positive I.D. character.  Later, from the end of Hobbie Cat Road the bird was observed flying back and forth along the low cliffs of the south shore.  There the small, rounded tails about equal to the length of the bill could be seen. Another morphology looked for and at was the area on the belly that protruded farthest towards the water which was the lower breast area. The bird was later photographed by Glenn Walbek and others that showed the bill length and size but I was never close enough to comment on the bill.  Based on the excellent drawings and photos in Skuas and Jaegers monograph, it was apparent to me the bird was an intermediate morph juvenile.  The two light shafts on the outer primaries are the best field mark for the time I was watching the bird along with its quite lazy, bouant (sp) flight.

Similar Species Discussion

Pomerine Jaeger was immediately eliminted based on the breast bulge and overall slender appearance, plus we looked for a double white flash on the underwing primaries and only one flash was observed.

Parasitic Jaeger - this was in the running as an option for some time, but Parasitic Jaeger has 3-8 silver shafts in the primaries, not the two visible ones, and it has a much warmer overall appearance.  Although the Pueblo Res bird did at times look similar to a Peregrine Falcon in flight the shape of the tails were round and not sharp pointed as in Parasictic Jaeger.  My South America experience with Parasitic was of a very aggressive bird harrassing many terns over and over. This jaeger ignored all the gulls and all other birds in the area.

Resources Used

I used Skuas and Jaegers by Olsen and Larsson an excellent book.

Previous Experience

I have seen them all on various summer jobs above the Arctic Circle but mostly adults and all three in Monterrey Bay but on pelagics they don't stay around for long study.  I have seen 5 or 6 LTJAs in CO, only a couple PAJAs in CO plus one in coastal Argentina and over 10 CO Poms but not enough to be an expert in their I.D.
