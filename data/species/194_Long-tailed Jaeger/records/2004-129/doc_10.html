Description of the Bird

Roy Halpin alerted me via e-mail that he, Larry Griffin and Cole Wild had seen a "Parasitic Jaeger" about 1.5 hours earlier at Fossil Creek Reservoir. They were doing a monthly bird survey of Fossil Creek and were on the south side of the reservoir, quite far from shore. The digiscoped picture (included with this report) taken of the jaeger by Larry Griffin illustrates their distance. This picture was taken approx 1.5 hours before I arrived.

The south shore of the reservoir is closed to general public access so I went in from the north shore through an empty lot. I walked down on to the beach and after scanning the east end I continued west, stopping and scanning the shores and open water very frequently. When I was near the west end I saw a jaeger quite well that I believe was a juvenile Long-tailed. I was standing on the north shore at the west end of the reservoir (past the center of the lake but not all the way to the west end) where water is still present but at low levels, facing SW, when the jaeger flew into my field of view from the west heading east. The sun was shining directly at me and on the water creating a strong glare. I watched the bird fly into a very strong headwind (there were white caps on the water.) It flew low to the surface, would flap a bit and then glide as it banked back and forth across the surface of the lake. The bird was flying at a fairly good rate of speed. At this particular point on the reservoir the width of the lake is rather narrow and because of this the jaeger was making frequent turns. This made it difficult to get sustained looks at either the upper or underside of the bird while it was out over the water. It was also difficult to accurately judge size, as there were no other flying birds near the surface of the water.

Overall, I would describe the bird as slender with a flight style that was smooth, steady and light. The jaeger had a pale head (but not "white") that contrasted with the mantle and upperwings, both of which looked true chocolate-brown. There was no "rufous" or "rusty" brown present on the upperparts of the bird (although the light was not to my advantage when I was looking directly at the back.) I saw a small (or restricted) white "flash" on the upperwing in the outermost primaries as the bird banked. The wings were narrow and pointed. I also noticed a tail projection but I could not really determine the actual shape or length, however it was short as compared to the tail projection on an adult Long-tailed Jaeger.

During this time there were plenty of gulls in the air in the same vicinity as the jaeger, fishing and actually chasing each other for food, however the jaeger paid no attention to any of the birds in the air.

The jaeger continued this pattern of flight when it suddenly turned again and this time headed directly towards me. It flew up on shore and was so close that I could not use my scope and had to step back to look at it through my binoculars. It was at this time that I was able to see the underparts of the bird. It had heavily barred upper and undertail coverts as well as barred underwings and flanks that were all "white & blackish" giving it a very gray appearance. The barring on the tail was even as opposed to the wavy barring you would expect on other jaegers. I noted the beginning of a breast band that was gray in color. The partial breast band also had a yellowish cast at the terminal edge where it met the breast. The breast and belly were paler than the breast band. The bird appeared to be heavy-chested angling back to a flatter belly. I was not able to get a satisfactory look at the undersides of the outermost primaries.

It continued to fly over the shore where it then landed on a far spit and briefly settled down. All of the gulls that had been on the spit immediately took to the air. Again, the jaeger did not show any interest in the gulls. Looking through my binoculars at that time, the bird appeared to be very gray overall with a rounded head. Unfortunately as I got closer it did not stay on the ground. I did not get a good look at the bill, nor did I get a close look at the back in better light. As it left, it flew east and I did not find it again.

One interesting point (and this is pure speculation on my part) is that I think that the bird came from the west end of the reservoir, past where the water ends. I think this is a possibility because I was standing at a point on shore where I could basically see the end of the water when I first saw the bird flying towards me. Previous to this I had carefully scanned the west shore and was in the process of scanning the surface of the lake starting at the west end and scanning east when the jaeger appeared.

I went out the next day to look for the bird again and this time I walked all the way to the west end. I did not see the jaeger but I noted that at the extreme west end of the lake there is a lot of mudflat as well as plenty of vegetation closer to the banks. Since Long-tailed Jaegers are basically insect eaters, it would seem to me that on the previous day, the bird could have been at the extreme west end of Fossil Creek hunting for food. Of course, I cannot guarantee that the bird just happened to fly in from some other location and entered through the west end of the reservoir, but I do think it is worth mentioning the habitat at that end of the lake. It would certainly make more sense for a Long-tailed to be at a waterless location (if in fact that is where it came from) then either a Parasitic or Pomarine.
Behaviors: I think the flight style as described above, the fact that it did not chase or act aggressive to gulls and that it flew close to me and over the shore edge for a quite a distance and then settled on land and are all behaviors that support the ID as LTJA.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

Pomarine Jaeger (POJA): Flight style very different. Pomarine tends to fly with a steady, heavy flight (described as "low and sustained" by Olsen) as opposed to the "light" flight as described in my narrative for the bird seen at Fossil Creek Reservoir (FCR). POJA has a "hanging belly" as compared to the larger chest angling back to a flat abdomen seen on the bird at FCR. POJA should not have a white "flash" on the upperwing. In addition, I would suspect that a POJA would have been aggressive towards the gulls at the lake and chased and attacked them. The bird that was observed ignored all of the gulls. POJA is a large jaeger and the bird that was observed did not give the impression of a large bird, especially when it had landed and was lying on the sand. POJA has upper and under tail coverts with "wavy" bars that are usually brown as compared to the "black and white" even barring I noted on the bird at FCR. The underwings are usually barred dark brown on POJA as compared to the "black and white" of the bird at the FCR. Also, the early date is more suggestive of either LTJA or PAJA but not POJA. It is also difficult for me to reconcile what a Pomarine would have been doing at the west end of the reservoir where there is little water.

Parasitic Jaeger (PAJA): PAJA shows a triangular head as opposed to the rounded head seen on the described bird and also, I think, evidenced by the photo included with this report. PAJA is described as having "falcon-like" flight as opposed to the intermittent flapping and gliding that I observed. PAJA are well known for chasing gulls, terns, etc., while the bird observed showed complete indifference to the gulls in the air and on the ground. The bird observed at FCR showed a white flash on the upperwing while in PAJA this is usually not the case. PAJA would be a warmer brown, even reddish or rusty where the jaeger at FCR showed no reddish tones at all. Uppertail coverts are wavy, "rarely distinct bars" (per Olsen) as seen on the bird at FCR. Juvenile PAJA have less distinctly barred undertail coverts. The bird seen at FCR had very distinctly barred undertail coverts. The underwing of a PAJA usually shows a rusty tinge as compared to the "black and white" coloration of the bird at FCR.

In addition, while I admit I do not have extensive experience with any of the 3 jaegers, LTJA is the only one of the three species that I have ever seen land on the ground and lay down. Every POJA and PAJA that I have ever seen has always landed in the water while not actively flying.

Great Skua and South Polar Skua: Large, heavy breasted birds with broader, more rounded wings than any of the jaegers. Skuas also have a shorter and broader tail with no feather projection. The jaeger seen at FCR did have a tail projection and did not have round, broad wings.
