Description of the Bird

A small, lanky dark-morph juvenile jaeger, the bird was a cold brown above with faint pale fringes to most upperpart feathers. The nape was unstreaked and the rump had fairly well-defined black-and-white barring. The tail was long and slender, with the round-tipped R1s extending at least a sixth in length again beyond the tips of the rest of the rectrices. The throat and chest were just about the same color as the upperparts, but the more distally the underparts were strongly and obviously barred with black and white, with the barring more obvious and pronounced in the vent and undertail coverts region. From below, the tail base was white, at least on the outer rects. The whole underwing was barred with dark and pale, being more obvious and more black-and-white on the axillars. There was a pronounced white patch at the base of the primaries, but of much less extent than is typical for the other two jaeger species in that plumage. The underside greater primary coverts had some white at the bases creating a very vague second white flash, but really only visible at very close range and really created by the dark-and-light banding, not by truly white-based primary coverts.

The wing base was fairly narrow, equal in length to only about 40% of the body length (bill tip to tail tip) and the bird flew quite buoyantly without the inveterate chasing of larids and quick, dashing or powerful flight of the other two species of jaeger.

The bird's bill was bicolored with a pale bluish-white basal 60% and a black distal 40%, with the black intruding into the pale along the cutting edge. The bill was fairly short, but also fairly thick-based.

In flight, the center of the bird's weight (lowest part of body) was forward of the wings giving it the typical chest and slim-bellied look of Long-tailed.
Behaviors: Flew almost languidly in great circuits of the reservoir, occasionally landing on the water's surface, apparently to grab some tiny, unsuspecting morsel. The bird would spend as much as 30 minutes flying circuits or partial circuits and never (while I was watching it) chased any larids, despite the presence of three species of gulls and two of terns.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

The other two jaegers can probably be ruled out by the bird's behavior of not chasing larids, but the cold aspect of the plumage, the distinct dark trailing edge to the wing, the lack of more than two truly white primary shafts (per wing), and the relatively limited white flash on the underside of the wing rule out the other two species.

Parasitic is also eliminated by the strong barring of black and white on the tail coverts and axillars, the rounded tips to the R1s, and the bill shape and pattern.

Pomarine is also ruled out by the narrow wing base and the conformation of the body in flight (very chesty rather than pot-bellied).
