Description of the Bird

the bird was a tad smaller than the other Long-tailed Jaeger that was present, when seen close together, and significantly smaller than a Ring-billed Gull. The wings were long and narrow, including at the base. The bird overall had a sleek, thin look, even compared to the other Long-tailed present. The back was a cold brownish color with a grayish tinge, not especially dark. The head had a "hood" of near the same color, but a bit warmer brown. The nape was paler brown than the hood, very hard to see on the bird unless is banked at close distance. The belly was white with a very few scattered brown marks, especially along the flanks. The undertail coverts were stongly barred with dark brown on white, as were the uppertail coverts. The tail was the same color brown, and the central retrices were blunt-tipped, but barely stuck out and were very hard to see. The tail overall looked fairly long. The underwings were very boldly checked with dark brown/black and white. The base of the primaries below had a bold white flash, but there was no "double flash" created with the coverts. The upperside of the primaries at most times did not appear to have any white on them, but the one time the bird banked when it was close I saw a very small amount of white on the outermost primaries, and I feel there were not more than two white primary shafts. Other than noticing the bill looked small I did not notice any details here.
Behaviors: The bird would circuit the reservoir, flying with fairly shallow wing beats at a fairly constant height. It rarely banked or dove to the water. Towards the end of the observation it was observed close to the other Long-tailed Jaeger present, and was observed to be smaller and even sleeker than that bird. This bird was never observed to harrass any bird, or to feed. It was only seen on the water briefly, once. This was used as supporting evidence in identification of this bird.
Call: not heard
Plumage: light phase juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

Parasitic Jaeger was ruled out by the black and white checkering of the underwings, rather than black and tawny checkering, by the blunt central tail feathers rather than pointy, by the lack of a bold white flash in the upper primaries. One of the most convincing details was the comparison with the Long-tailed Jaeger already present, as this bird was noted to be even sleeker and smaller, with similar wing shape and patterning. Behavior also played a role in ruling out Parasitic and Pomarine, as this bird never harrassed any other bird. This bird did not seem to fly as strongly as a Parasitic or a Pomarine, in my opinion, either.

Pomarine Jaeger was ruled out by the lack of a double flash on the underside of the primaries, the narrowness of the wings, especially at the base, the sleek appearance, without the chest, heavy look of a Pomarine, by the cold brown color rather than the warmer brown of a Pomarine, and especially by the small size when compared to the other Long-tailed Jaeger present, and to Ring-billed Gulls. This bird did not fly nearly as powerfully as I would expect from a Pomarine, or as compared to the Pomarine from Cherry Creek which I had seen a couple of days earlier. Behavior also played a role, as described above.

Resources Used

Both Sibley and National Geographic were consulted in the field, and helped us arrive out our identification.

Previous Experience

I have seen a few Long-tailed Jaegers on pelagics off the east coast, though never as close as this bird. I have seen two Long-tailed Jaegers in Colorado, a light phase adult and the dark phase juv. present at the same time as this one, and studied both closely.

I have seen many Parasitic Jaegers and Pomarine Jaegers off the east coast, and studied a few of them fairly well, including juveniles of both phases. In Colorado, I have seen two Parasitics and three Pomarines, and studied all of the them closely, including dark juveniles of both species.
