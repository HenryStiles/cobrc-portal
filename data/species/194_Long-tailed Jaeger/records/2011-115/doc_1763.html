Description of the Bird

The head pattern combined with the long tail streamers make this a jaeger. The tail streamers were dark gray, very long and flexible (photo), the length of them being greater than the greatest width of the wings. The face was white with a faint yellowish cast. The crown was dark contrasting stongly with the whitish face. The bill was short and dark.
In flight and from the dorsal view, the wings showed what I thought were two silver outer primary shafts possibly three. The underwings were uniform browinish with no light-colored crescents at the base of the primaries. The breast was white and stood out most when the bird was floating on the water. The bird seemed very buoyant, sometimes only the breast seemed to be touching the water and the tail was elevated at a 25 degree angle.
Behaviors: The jaeger was viewed floating in calm water and also it chased California Gulls and also was chased by them. The chasing was infrequent and did not seem to be very aggressive on the part of the jaeger. It flew a lot to different parts of the reservoir. In flight the jaeger never flew more than 40 feet above the water.

Similar Species Discussion

The skuas are much bigger bodied with much broader wings. The tail of an adult Pomerine Jaeger is with curled tips and the central rectrices are wider on that species. The body of a POJA is much more full bodied with deep chest appearance. The bill usually looks longer than in LTJA. The number of silver-based primary shafts is more than two on POJA, usually much more, 3-9. On the underwing surface, POJA has large white crescent and sometimes a 2nd smaller crescent, not seen on LTJA.
Parasitic Jaeger has shorter, triangular and sharply-pointed rectrices, unlike the very long and flexible thin rects of LTJA. Bill of PAJA is thinner.

Previous Experience
