Description of the Bird

An obvious juvenile jaeger (brownish with strong fringes on upperparts, long wings with white flashes in primaries), the bird was a light morph with a fairly pale head and neck contrasting slightly with somewhat darker body plumage. The bird was first found swimming and the bird's posture was reminiscent of that of Sabine's Gull, as it was very buoyant and sat quite high on the water with its rear end up in the air.

The bill looked mostly dark, but the bird was far away and I do not believe that I could have discerned a pale base to the bill. But, I believe that that is supportive of the ID, as either of the other two jaeger would have shown a more obvious pale base to the bill, even at that distance.

The nape was the palest part of the body plumage. The back and upper wing feathers were each fringed thinly with white (or, at least it appeared so, but the distance at which we saw the bird precluded determining the fringed status of every feather). I could not discern any fringing to the primary tips, but am not sure that I would have even had the bird been a widely-fringed Parasitic Jaeger.

We waited for the bird to fly to be certain of our tentative ID (based, primarily, on date) and when it finally did, I saw most of the key features. The bird's under and upper tail coverts and axillars were strongly barred in black and white. The secondaries were black and contrasted strongly with the upper wing coverts. The underwing flash (formed by white bases to the primaries) was not repeated on the upper side and looked to be of smaller extent than is typical for Parasitic Jaeger, though this was difficult to be sure of on the bird's flapping wings. I could discern the tips of the R1s projecting beyond the rest of the rectrices, but could not determine the point shape.

The bird had the heavy chest and slim belly typical of Long-tailed Jaeger, with the lowest point of the underparts in flight (the center of the weight) being in the chest and thus forward of the wings. The bird flew with more buoyant wingbeats, as is typical of the species, and atypical for Parasitic Jaeger.
Behaviors: Swimming by itself and then flying around north end of lake.
Call: none heard
Plumage: Juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

Both other jaegers can be readily confused with Long-tailed, particularly given the long views that we had of the bird. However, both are ruled out by the buoyant swimming posture and flight style, by the shape of the underparts in flight, and by the strongly contrasting secondaries. Parasitic is additionally ruled out by the strong pattern of black-and-white barring on the tail coverts and axillars. There were no other birds particularly close to it, so size was hard to judge, but the narrowness of the wing base certainly ruled out the wide-winged Pomarine Jaeger.
