Description of the Bird

Our birding group consisted of 11 persons, including myself, the trip leader. We were returning to Monte Vista from Cat Creek late in the afternoon when we saw a road-killed bird on the side of the road. I backed up the truck and we all got out to inspect it. The specimen was quite a challenge as we methodically examined all parts of the bird. The bird smelled on this 72 degree afternoon, and we guessed that it was killed in the past two days. But it was not consumed by bugs either and still manageable. Our group reached the concensus that it was a juvenile Jaeger species, and was either a Long-tailed or Parasitic Jaeger on first analysis. I took many photos to document the bird. Ten days later, I was able to examine the bird and made some critical measurements and examination along with assistant Lisa Clements who had stored the bird to be transferred to Adams State University which has a possession permit. I understand the bird specimen is currently at the Denver Museum of Nature and Science.

This narrative is lengthy in nature because it is of a specimen that can be viewed in hand. It also is lengthy due to the difficult challenge of identifying juvenile Jaegers, but is mollified by the fact that we have all of the observable features in hand! Measurable characteristics add to the strength of this determination. I feel it is imperative that we identify this specimen down to the species level. I believe this bird to be a light juvenile Long-tailed Jaeger and will present the supporting documentation. Identification to the species level is significant because there are, as far as I know, no records for any Jaeger species in the San Luis Valley.

I will be referencing certain photographs by number. These will establish key diagnostic criteria for this challenging group of birds. I also took measurements of certain characteristics of the bird. I received measureable paramenters for juvenile Jaegers from personnel at COASST, Coastal Observation and Seabird Survey Team, which does measurements and analysis of deceased seabirds on the northern pacific coast. I will reference COASST numbers in this report. 

We noted that the right wing of the bird was obviously broken at the humerus. This suggests that it was likely hit by a motor vehicle in a violent collision. It did not seem like predation was cause of death as a predator would have consumed it. The overall carcass was intact. This location is far from water. Communication with other birders have reported that this species will land far away from water and eat grasshoppers and insects. This location is dry desert about 8 air miles southwest from the Monte Vista National Wildlife Refuge. Specific GPS location is provided, though I had difficulty placing the "marker" on the electronic map (please use GPS coordinates that are included in this report). 

LENGTH OF BIRD: We did a quick field estimate of length and came up with 15 inches at the recovery site. Later, I directly measured the bird length at 36.0 cm, which equates to about 14 inches. The head was somewhat craned to one side so the measurment of a relaxed bird was not possible. May have been slightly longer. This length of bird best fits LTJA, according to Sibley (length 15 in vs 16.5 for Parasitic,  18.5 for Pomarine). The bird was slender with long narrow wings and a relatively small head.

OVERAL SHAPE, BODY DORSAL AND VENTRAL SIDES: The bird has a dark grayish brown back with lighter edgings on many back feathers (Picture 01). The underside (ventral) of the bird is light dusky gray with wings and undertail dark grayish brown with barred white edges on the feathers (Picture 02 Underside). Wings are relatively long and narrow and bird has a relatively long tail compared to other juvenile Jaegers. This best matches the shape of LTJA as described in Kaufman, 1990, whereby "Long-tailed has the slimmest body and smallest head of the three. The narrow wings contribute to the impression of a long tail, even without the central streamers." Our bird is more dark grayish brown with light colored edgings to the feathers. It does not have rusty edgings, according to National Geographic Third Edition for juvenile Parasitics, nor the distinctive rusty tones on the edges of the scalloped appearance.

HEAD AND BILL:  Head was relatively small, oval shaped and light gray to whiteish (Picture 03 Head and Bill). The bill was gull-like and we (Lisa Clements and I) later measured the culmen at 25 mm. The nail, or hard tip of the bill was 12 mm. This fits characteristics of Long-tailed Jaeger, where nail is 50% of total bill length.  COASST provided numerical measurements for pomarine, parasitic and long-tailed jaegers that they used in separating the species. I will use three measurable parameters that they provided for tarsus length, wing chord, and upper culmen. 

Poja: Upper Culmen 36-44mm; Wing Chord 34-38cm; tarsus 50-58mm.   
Paja: Upper Culmen 29-35mm; Wing Chord 31-35 cm; tarsus 40-49mm
Ltja:  Upper culmen 24-32mm; Wing Chord 29-33 cm; tarsus 38-46 mm

Our bird: Upper culmen 25mm; Wing Chord 28 cm; tarsus 48mm. The best fits are shown in bold. Upper culmen and wing chord meet LTJA. The tarsus was more like Parasitic, but only missed LTJA lengths by 2 mm.  

In addition, Sibley describes the culmen of Long-tailed Jaeger as being the "nail covering half the bill." This was validated by our bird having a 1.2 cm nail on a 2.5 cm bill which is roughly half as Sibley describes. Bill was dark gray in color with darker nail. Best fit is clearly LTJA based on this characteristic. Kauffman states "Close up photos that show the precise shape of the bill may provide the clincher for identifying a difficult young Jaeger." His book shows about a 50% ratio for LTJA and our bird meets that requirement. The gonydeal angle of the bill was difficult to establish on this bird due to the way the bill dried agape.  

WING: UPPER AND UNDER SIDES:  Wings are fairly long and narrow coming to a sharp point. Under sides were dark grayish brown with whitish edging on feathers giving a somewhat barred appearance. Primaries were dark grayish brown overall. There were "rufous tips" on the primaries, like would be present on Parasitic. Kaufman states "Long-tailed jaeger shows two primary shafts that are white for most of their length....The rest of the primary shafts are dark brown." He goes on to state  in Parasitic, the number of white shafts varies from 3 to 6. Pomarine usually has more than 5.  We need to examine photos 06a and 06b to see that this bird has two outer primary feathers that distinctly show the white shafts.  In 06a we see the topside wing primaries that are spread enough to see the distinctly white shafting of the first two primaries, next to darker shafted other primaries. In 06b, we see the topside and only see the second primary (which hides the first) and it is clearly white. The first primary is mostly hidden by the second, but you can still see a portion of the white shaft even on this picture.  The two primary feather white shafts strongly indicates LTJA.

Upper sides of wings and back are dark grayish brown with whitish to light buffy edges to some of the covert feathers giving a bit of scaly appearance (Photo 09). 

TAIL DORSAL AND VENTRAL: The dorsal and ventral sides of tail feathers are dark grayish brown. This includes two central longer feathers that show a very slight point. They project about an inch beyond the remaining tail feathers (Photos 04 and 05). Kauffman states " On Parasitoc juveniles, the central feathers extend more obviously; they vary from slightly to noticeably longer and taper to a sharp point.  On Long-tailed juveniles,  these feathers are noticeably long extending up to an inch past the rest of the tail. They may have fine spikey points but their overall shape is distinctly rounded at the tips." Our jaeger has rounded not tapered central tail feather tips and does have the "fine spikey point" described by Kaufman. Our bird best meets the diagram for the LTJA tail feathers in Kaufman. 

One final tail characteristic is described by Kaufman as "they (LTJA) often have whitish marks at the tip (of the tail central retrices) which are usually lacking in the other two species (of jaegers). Photo 04 clearly shows the whitish marks on the tips of the tail central retrices, meeting Long-tailed Jaeger. 

The Upper tail coverts are dark grayish brown and strongly barred with whitish bands. The undertail coverts are longer than upper tail coverts and both are barred with white and dark bands. As mentioned earlier, tip of the central two retrices are slightly pointed and edged in white. 

Legs are brown, slightly yellowish and brown. Feet are the same combination of colors and webbed. They seem like fairly small feet for this size bird, but they may have been dried a bit and shrunken.  

Similar Species Discussion

The closest species to compete are the juvenile Pomarine and the Parasitic Jaegers. The measurements we made strongly suggest Long-tailed Jaeger. The wing chord, culmen length, culmen to nail ratio all best fit LTJA and eliminate PAJA and POJA. Tarsus suggested PAJA but is close to LTJA by 2mm. 

I believe this bird to be light phase juvenile Long-tailed Jaeger. It is not a juvenile Parasitic Jaeger because our bird lacks the the warm brown and rusty colors that Parasitic juveniles often show. Parasitic have tapered pointed tails and our bird has round central tail feathers with slight nubby points. Sibley says juvenile LTJA "usually has blunt-tipped central tail feathers..." which I read as often but not always. He leaves room for some pointing on the two central tail feathers which our bird has and is consistent with Kaufman's description of nubby points on LTJA.  Our bird is not a PAJA because our bird has strong barring on the undertail and is smaller. Light juvenile Parasitic has weak barring on the undertail. POJA bill size is longer than LTJA and the ratio of nail to culmen is less than 50%.  Our bird is not Pomarine because our bird has a smaller bill length, and a nail to culmen ratio of 50%.   Our bird is considerably shorter in length than POJA. Juvenile POJA and PAJA would have more than 2 white shafts on the wing primaries. Our bird has distinctly 2 which best fits LTJA. According to COASST parameters, our bird best fits LTJA based on culmen and wing chord. Our bird best fits Sibley's guide for the Jaegers regarding bird length (15" for juvenile, vs PAJA 16.5 to POJA 18.5).  For the above reasons, POJA and PAJA are eliminated.
