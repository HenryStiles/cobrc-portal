Description of the Bird

The 1st winter Golden-crowned Sparrow was associating with a flock of White-crowned Sparrows.  We saw the bird several different times as we chased and relocated the mixed flock of sparrows.  The first thing I noticed was that the bird seemed to be a little heavier than the other sparrows, had a little different profile, and usually remained at the edge of the flock. When I saw it in the binoculars, the yellowish forecrown and faint eyeline were plainly visible.

Similar Species Discussion

The 1st winter Golden-crowned Sparrow was associating with a flock of Gambel's White-crowned Sparrows ( both adult and 1st winter birds).  There was no problem distinguishing the Golden-crowned from the black & white striped adult White-crowned Sparrows.  In comparing the 1st winter sparrows, the Golden-crowned had a yellowish forecrown, faint eyeline, and gray bill compared to the brown & tan stripes, strong eyeline, and flesh colored bill of the juvenile White-crowned.  Also the Golden-crowned seemed heavier and had more of a chunky profile than the White-crowned Sparrows.

Resources Used

There was a group of us (CFO Board members led by Mark Peterson), and once we had a good look at the bird in our binocs, there was no doubt as to the bird's ID.  I have both Sibley Guide to Birds that I use as a reference.

Previous Experience

I have watched Golden-crowned Sparrows at the Red Rocks feeders outside of Morrison in Jefferson County for a number of years.  One winter we had a 1st winter bird spend several months at Red Rocks.  I have also seen Golden-crowned Sparrows in the Northwest.
