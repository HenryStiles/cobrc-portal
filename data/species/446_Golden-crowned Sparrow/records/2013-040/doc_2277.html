Description of the Bird

The bird was fairly drab in its coloration. There wasn't much that stood out except the little bit of yellow on the forehead. The bird's size and shape reminded me of a White-crowned Sparrow's size and shape. The bill of the bird was gray. The crown had a yellow stripe running in line with the body and two brownish stripes that bordered the yellow stripe. Down the back of the head and the neck, the yellow and the brown stripes faded. The back was an overall brown with darker brown/black stripes typical of Zonotrichia sparrows running in line with the body. The rump was a grayish/brownish color. The tail was about the same color as the rump. The undertail coverts were whitish. The belly was a grayish with an overall rusty color mixed in. The feet were a pinkish color. The chest was a grayish color. The chin was slightly lighter than the chest. The bird had a malar stripe that was slightly darker than and contrasted with the chin. The malar area of the bird was about the same shade as the chin. The auriculars were gray. The eye was black. The bird had faint white eye arcs and a faint eyestripe. The supercilium of the bird was about the same shade of grayish color that the auriculars were.
Behaviors: The Golden-crowned Sparrow would fly in and feed with a few other birds and then fly out again. It was very skiddish overall.
Call: I didn't hear any calls or songs when I saw the bird. It was too skiddish to view from outside. However, a couple days before I viewed the Golden-crowned Sparrow, I heard and taped a call that I was unfamiliar with. Later on, I realized that it was the Golden-crowned Sparrow! I will attach the recording.
Plumage: I think first winter.

Similar Species Discussion

From what I have seen, Harris's Sparrow, White-crowned Sparrow, and White-throated Sparrow are the birds that would most likely be confused with Golden-crowned Sparrow. The only thing with that is that they are all different enough to be easily identified, at least that is what I have found. The drab color and general lack of patterning of the face pretty much rules out all of these options except very young versions of these birds. A young Harris's Sparrow should still have black on its chest and a contrasting white throat which the bird in question did not. A young White-crowned Sparrow shows a distinctive eye-line and supercilium, which this bird did not have. A young White-throated Sparrow also has a distinctive eye-line which this bird did not. No other Zonotrichia sparrow that I know of has a yellow spot in the middle of the top of its head and is so drab overall as the sparrow that I saw.
