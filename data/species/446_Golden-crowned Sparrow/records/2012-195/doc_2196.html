Habitat

Thickets of various deciduous shrubs probably largely Mountain Mahogany. Nearby rock face with thin sheet of water and grasses at the top margin. Feeder area with bare ground and interspersed shrubs. One nearby Gambel's Oak I believe.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski 8.5 x 42 and Canon 7D with 300 mm F4.0 and 1.4x teleconverter
Distance:  Rough estimate of 30 feet or less
Light:  Weather was clear, sunny skies with no clouds and there was no wind present. The sun was at an approximate 45 degree angle relative to the bird. Lighting was good and did not adversely impact any observation.

Description of the Bird

My immediate impression on seeing the bird was that it was quite large and stout. While not able to directly compare, I had been observing a single WCSP at the same location and this bird immediately stuck me as noticeably larger than the WCSP while having the same general characteristics of shape.

The obvious and striking crown pattern of this bird included a fairly bright yellow median crown stripe on the anterior crown becoming gray about the center of the crown with black, clearly demarcated and thick lateral crown 'stripes'. The bill was conical and generally appeared dusky although in one photo the mandible appears yellowish-gray. The nape was lightly streaked with dark gray. The sides of the breast and flanks had distinct tawny or buffy coloration. The breast was not streaked. The tail was moderately long and slightly notched. The mantle had dark markings forming streaking. The greater coverts had dark blackish central pattern with rust colored outer webs and the outer coverts had obvious white tips forming a partial wing bar.

Based on the head pattern it seems consistent with an adult in alternate, not basic plumage?
Behaviors: I was aware of the presence of this bird at the Red Rocks Trading Post feeder area prior to visiting. In fact, two days earlier I was unsuccessful in observing the bird. The sparrow was rather secretive, preferring to remain in the nearby shrubs, hidden. A wide variety of other species were present under the feeder on this date including a single White-crowned Sparrow and three Song Sparrows.

After approximately 30 minutes of observation I observed the GCSP briefly emerge and briefly forage for seed in the bare ground of the feeder area. It did not directly interact with any other species present. The bird hopped and did not do any scratching for seed as the Song Sparrows.

I estimate I only had perhaps 2-3 minutes of viewing/photo time before the bird disappeared into the dense shrubs.
Call: None noted
Plumage: Adult - apparent alternate?

Similar Species Discussion

The bright and prominent yellow median crown and prominent thick, black lateral crown eliminated other species.
