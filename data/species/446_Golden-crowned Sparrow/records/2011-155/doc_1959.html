Habitat

The feeders are are on a hillside adjacent to a large, very brushy area with some seeps. The Golden-crowned comes out from the brushy tangle to feed on the ground below one of the two apple trees or below the platform feeder, then scurries back to the thicket.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Vortex Stokes DLS 8 x 42
Distance:  estimate distance to the bird is 45 - 60 feet.
Light:  Sun is behind you at the Red Rocks feeders in the early morning and the light conditions were good to excellent.

Description of the Bird

Large sparrow of genus Zonotrichia with heavy body and long tail. Bi-colored bill with gray upper mandible and lighter colored lower mandible. Thick black supercilium with lemon yellow forehead, and gray hindcrown/nape. Mostly non-descript body with gray cheeks/auriculars and breast. Light brown on flanks, brown rump, and pink legs. Brown back with black zig-zag stripes. Wings are a combination of dark brown, black and olive green with 2 faint white wingbars.
Behaviors: The bird was seen numerous times at the feeders behind the Red Rocks Trading Post. The feeders are adjacent to a very brushy area with some water seepage. The Golden-crowned would venture out from the brushy area to feed on the ground for a brief period, then scurry back to the overgrown tangle.
Call: did not hear bird sing.
Plumage: adult nonbreeding

Similar Species Discussion

It's evident to an experienced birder that the bird is typical of the genus Zonotrichia. It was often seen with a first winter White-crowned Sparrow, which had a brown crown stripe bordered by tan supercilium. The bodies of the two birds were similar, but the lemon forehead of the Golden-crowned is distinctive.

Resources Used

I knew the ID of the bird as soon as I saw it. Did not need any books. However, I did use "The Sibley Guide to Birds" and "Sparrows of the United States and Canada" by David Beadle and James Rising to read and learn more about Golden-crowned Sparrows.

Previous Experience

I have seen Golden-crowned Sparrows in the Northwest and for several years at the Red Rocks Trading Post. Also have identified several in the winter on the Western Slope, where I used to live.
