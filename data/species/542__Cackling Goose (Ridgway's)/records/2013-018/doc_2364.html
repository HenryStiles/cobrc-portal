Description of the Bird

Tiny dark white-cheeked goose. Dwarfed by even Lesser Canada Geese (Bc parvipes) and smaller than nearby Richardson's Cackling Geese (Bh hutchinsii). Head rounded, delicate, bill small and stubby. When bird's head up and not preening, contour of bill smoothly transitioned into angle of forehead, not abrupt change as in Richardson's or somewhat angled, as in Aleutian Cackling Goose (Bh leucopareia). Neck and head black with white cheek patch that lacked indentation typical of Richardson's. Partial narrow gular stripe. At base of neck, anteriorly, thin white ring for about 20 percent of neck's total circumference. Breast shiny deep bronze, quite dark. The remainder of underparts similar in color, but a bit paler with some flank barring. Wing coverts highly patterned (for white-cheeked goose) with each feather having blue-gray base, dark brown subterminal band and crisp white terminal band. Back brown with narrow white bars. Undertail coverts and vent white. Bill and legs black.
Behaviors: Sleeping on ice, occasionally preening or looking about, standing with Richardson's Cackling, Lesser Canada, and Moffitt (mostly Moffitt, presumably some Giant Canada genes as we are in CO) Canada Geese.
Call: not heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The glossy dark breast, small bill, rounded delicate head combined with tiny overall size eliminate other Cackling Geese. More specifically (or subspecifically), a dark glossy breast is rare in taxa other than minima and leucopareia. Leucopareia has longer bill, more angular forehead, larger size, and lacks the wing-covert patterning of this bird. Additionally, the wing-covert pattern of this bird is very rarely seen in individuals of other Cackling Goose subspecies, and is used by n. California birders to pick the minima out of the leucopareia flocks most readily. Richardson's Cackler seems to me the most variable of the Cackling Geese, some birds dinky, some birds darkish, and bill size/shape all over the place. However, I've never seen one with a truly glossy breast, and if such individual exist, they must be exceptionally rare. The same is true of the blue-gray/blackish/whitish wing covert feathers. Richardson's is essentially eliminated by this combination. Add that to that the darkness and color of breast, the head and bill shape. Taverneri would be larger, much plainer on wing coverts, not glossy on breast and hefty of beill.
