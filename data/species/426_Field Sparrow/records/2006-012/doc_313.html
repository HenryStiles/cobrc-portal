Description of the Bird

This bird was noticeably smaller than the juncos it was feeding with and its bright orange-pink bill, bright white eye-ring, and plain gray face with a bright rufous crown immediately made me think Field Sparrow. The wings had two rather narrow white wingbars and the scapulars were rufous tinged. The tail was relatively long. The underparts were mostly light gray with a light orangy-rufous wash on the upper flanks and part of the breast. There was no dark central mark on the breast. The face was light gray and lores were unmarked gray. The eye with its bright white eyering was completely surrounded bythe gray face. The crown was a bright rufous and did not extend down to the eye. Behind the eye there was a bright rufous line the extended back to the nape. The nape was mostly gray. The back was striped with some rufous tones, but generally of medium tones. The legs were light colored, but I did not see them well. I did not see the rump.
Behaviors: It was feeding on the ground with a flock of juncos
Call: not heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

This bird differed from an American Tree Sparrow in several ways. First, the bill was not bi-colored. Second, the eyering was very prominent on a plain face and the lores were plain and unmarked. Third, The breast and flanks were too orangy-rufous and lacked any central spot. Fourth, the bird was too small. Since this bird appeared to be the eastern subspecies (pusilla), it was quite different from the form called Worthen's Sparrow.

Previous Experience

Field sparrow is a common bird in Ohio and they nested near my home there before I moved to Colorado. I have seen American Tree Sparrow many time in many places including Colorado and Ohio. I have never seen a Worthen's Sparrow.
