Habitat

Habitat atop North Table Mountain consists of native shortgrass prairie (blue grama, etc) on volcanic soil with interspersed yucca and a few patches of woody plants (snowberry etc) and very few small trees in the upper drainages. Bird sang from the largest tree in the immediate vicinity which was at the top of a large rocky drainage with ample woody vegatation, a 15 ft cedar and patches of tall thick grass being much taller and denser than most of the mesa. During observation the bird never left the cottonwood (aka singing perch). A small area along the north fork of the same drainage has a few Juniperus sp. among the rocky, grassy habitat which is somewhat similar to their local breeding habitat in North Central Texas (ie LBJ National Grasslands).

Description of the Bird

While conducting point counts on top of North Table Mountain just north of Golden, I heard a song that was immediately outstanding among the numerous Vesper Sparrows I had heard all morning. I spotted the singer perched roughly 10-20 feet up in a sparsely vegetated cottonwood at the top of a rocky drainage. A small slender sparrow, longish-tailed and noticeably more petite than the numerous Vespers in the area. I watched it sing in clear view and noted the smooth unmarked grayish-white underparts with a faint tawny wash to the sides of the upper breast, bright orange-rufous crown and transocular stripe, and pale gray eyebrow matching the rest of the underparts. Relatively bright (compared to the Vespers) streaked brownish upperparts with thin white wingbars. Immediately recognized as a Field Sparrow with which I am very familiar as a common winter resident and local breeding resident in my home region of North-central Texas. I considered this bird to be a rather bright individual, which surprised me since I figured the 'western race' ought to be grayer and lacking the orangey wash to the head and sides of breast.
Behaviors: Bird was not particularly active during the brief observation. Detected by loud clear song, spotted the bird half to two-thirds of the way up on a bare limb of a sparse cottonwood. It moved a couple times to different branches of the tree while it sang but generally was still and deliberate. Only watched it briefly as I was in the middle of conducting point counts and was not aware that the species was so unusual in the area, although I was sure to confirm the ID and note it as I had not seen any in the area that morning or on the previous 3 mornings of surveys on North Table Mountain (mesa).
Call: Bird sang several times over the period of about 5 minutes. Song consisted of four descending whistles leading into a slightly accelerating trill - "tew tew tew tew tewtewtototototo" - all notes having a similar quality being clear and descending (lacking any buzzy tones of the Vespers singing nearby). No calls heard. I have heard dozens of Field Sparrows sing on their breeding grounds and even during winter in north Texas as they are one of the more common sparrow species there. Although the song quality was familiar as Field Sparrow - clear plaintive and especially distinctive in the descending quality of the notes - the pattern was a bit different from what I'm used to hearing, in this case the trill being slightly abbreviated from the drawn out bouncy-ball song.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The other sparrow species observed during my week long survey of the mesa were Vesper (common), Grasshopper (a handful), Cassin's (two or three), Lark Sparrow (one), and Spotted and Green-tailed Towhees. No real contender for ID of this bird as the the most similar looking species (ie Chipping, Brewer's and Aimophila sparrows) have very different songs.

Resources Used

No ID resources used at the time, although later that day I checked Sibley for any description of song variation and racial variation in the extent of pale rufous wash to the face/sides of breast.

Previous Experience

I have seen hundreds of Field Sparrows on their wintering areas and in their local breeding areas of Texas, and have heard them calling and singing not infrequently on their breeding and wintering grounds (on nice warm sunny winter days!). I have also seen all other North American Spizella and most of the somewhat similar-looking Aimophilas.
