Habitat

Nov 5, 2025, entrenched active floodplain with dense desert riparian comprised of willow, tamarisk, Russian Olive, and Fremont Cottonwoods supported by perennial flowing stream. Jan 2, 2026, dense, expansive greasewood stand on terrace above active floodplain.

Viewing Conditions

Description of the Bird

Nov 5, 2025 (https://ebird.org/checklist/S283022263): While birding the dense riparian of Lower Yellow Jacket (LYJ) from a terrace elevated 10-15 feet above the creek, I heard an unknown vocalization from a thicket of Russian Olives with abundant ripe berries about 75 feet away. The sound somewhat resembled an agitated AMRO or WEBL, which were both present, but the repetitive pattern seemed unique. I recorded with my phone, did not think much of it and had forgotten about it until processing saved recordings on Dec 6. With headphones it did sound rather unique, so I spent some time researching and found an excellent match to a Nov CRTH recording from Nevada (https://macaulaylibrary.org/asset/644878080). On Dec 8, I sent the Nov 5 recording to Nathan Pieplow who replied, "I agree this seems like a perfect match for the audio you found from Nevada. And the habitat and location seem plausible. I haven't personally heard Crissal doing that exact thing, but I can hear and see the resemblance to sounds I know, and the Nevada recording seems to elevate this to a pretty high level of confidence." Nathan approved my submission on eBird and recommended I notify the CObirds listserv and submit a CBRC report. 

Jan 2, 2026 (https://ebird.org/checklist/S291995988): After Nov 5, I birded the general area 12 times with CRTH as a target during 5 of those visits following the findings on Dec 6. During those subsequent visits, I detected zero hints of CRTH in the active floodplain. On Jan 2, after a few hours of quiet birding in the active floodplain, I decided to test the large, dense greasewood stand which I usually avoid due to its difficult viewing and unpleasant walking conditions. While pishing up a mixed flock of sparrows in the maze of greasewood, I first heard faint whining noises on the other side of a thick hedge completely out of view, maybe 30 ft away at ground level. I recorded the faint weird sounds which are the first few recordings in the Jan 2nd eBird list. As I approached the sounds a small flock of GAQU loudly flushed from the same hedge. That left me disheartened, believing the noises I was hearing were just something weird from GAQU. Just in case, I played the Merlin CRTH song from Texas at about half volume. Immediately the strange noises resumed, and it was only then that I was confident I was hearing CRTH. I played the song softly a few more times, well-spaced in time, and the bird responded positively by coming closer, about waist high but staying in the thick hedge until about 5 feet away. The last audio recording in the list was at its closest. The bird was still rather difficult to hear in person, but my phone picked it up pretty well. Eventually the bird flushed across an opening, and I assumed I'd never see it again. In flight I caught a glimpse of the rusty undertail and started formulating my eBird CRTH description in my head. But shortly after, I heard it whisper singing again, shifted positions and saw it perched in the top of a tall greasewood about 40 feet away allowing for photos and video. According to the timestamps of my audio recordings and photos, this was about a 23-minute encounter from 3:32 to 3:55pm. The photos and video clearly show the diagnostic features of a plain breasted thrasher with a long, decurved bill and rusty undertail coverts. The audio recordings also are a perfect match for CRTH songs inclusive of mimicry of local breeding species including WIFL, ATFL, WBNU and CAKI.

Other CRTH observations at LYJ: Almost a year prior to the above observerations, on Jan 31, 2025, I recorded audio of a shy unseen bird making a 2-note call from a greasewood thicket. Later that evening I interpreted the call as LOSH with uncertainty. A year later, on December 29, 2025, I re-evaluated that recording and eliminated LOSH based on the shapes in the spectrogram. With the context of the November 5 CRTH observation, I compared the audio and spectrogram to CRTH and concluded the 2-note vocalization is a very good match for CRTH. This realization in part led to my visiting the greasewood habitat on Jan 2, 2026, which successfully located CRTH. I sent the Jan 31, 2025, audio recording to Nathan Pieplow who replied, "I do agree that your audio from Jan 31 sounds like Crissal as well. I will be very interested to discover whether we have an ongoing population or whether the Montezuma birds disappear the way the Baca birds apparently did." I am not officially including the Jan 31, 2025, observation or recording as part of this CBRC submission, but mention here as it does strongly suggest CRTH have been present for at least 2 consecutive winters at LYJ (https://ebird.org/checklist/S211408195).

Similar Species Discussion

At this location, CRTH is most easily confused with Curve-billed Thrasher which has a shorter less decurved bill, spotted breast and lacks rusty undertail coverts. CBTH is clearly eliminated by the photos.

Other similar species include California Thasher and LeConte's Thrasher which are extremely unlikely due to their limited range and are easily eliminated by photos.
