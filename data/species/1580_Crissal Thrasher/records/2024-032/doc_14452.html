Description of the Bird

Finders of the first bird were on November 30th, 2024 were Luke Pheneger and Brian Genge.

There were two birds for certain.  I have a really awful photo with two birds in it that I did not include in my eBird report that I will upload here.  There was a third thrasher present that was the same size as the other two but it stayed separate and I did not refind after my initial sighting.  I saw all 3 birds at once at first.  They flew away from me with 2 staying together the entire 45 minutes I was there with the 3rd bird peeling off in a different direction.  Hopefully, subsequent birders will be able to confirm exact number. The coordinates I chose on the map were as close as I could get to the ones I added to my eBird checklist.  The birds circled me 3 or 4 times diving deep into the oak scrub as well as perching on top of the scrub and pinon pines and junipers.  They seemed curious about me.  Nervous and skulking like they do, but curious nonetheless.  A delight of an experience to have by myself though I kept hoping someone else would show up.  At least one was seen by 6 others on December 7th.

My immediate impression of these birds were that they clearly thrashers and that they were all larger than our typical Curve-billed Thrashers.  Both birds I have photos for showed the extremely long and substantially curved bills.   Both birds had the requisite dark rufousy-red undertail coverts that were obvious many times over.  The birds were lacking any of the round spots on breast/belly that a CBTH would have.  They were uniformly grayish brown over their bodies with plain faces and dark lateral throat stripes.  The dorsal side of the tail feathers toward the terminal ends were a smidge darker than the body terminating in lighter tips.  One bird was an adult for sure with the light iris.  I speculate that the other is a juvenile as its irises were still darkish even in good sunlight.  My photo of that bird is probably not good enough to confirm as juvenile or at the very least not good enough to eliminate a possible melanin issue.  Nothing else about the bird made think there was a melanin issue but I had to consider it anyways.

Vocalizations given by both birds while I was there included the diagnostic "churry" call.  I captured audio of one of the birds and it is located on my eBird checklist here. I attempted to upload the audio in the upload media section but it gave me an error that maybe the file is too long.  All photos are mine. Either way, find it here:

Similar Species Discussion

Curve-billed Thrasher was the immediate consideration.  Eliminated by size, lack of spotting on breast/belly, the presence of rufousy-red undertail coverts, length of the bill and the significance of its curvature.

Sage Thrasher-same reasons as above.  There was a Sage Thrasher present with these Thrashers and the size and field mark differences were clear and obvious.

Canyon Towhee-Thought about it for .2 seconds as they are similar in color but nothing else and were present in the canyon. 

Lack of intense streaking on breast/belly immediately eliminated Brown/Long-billed Thrashers 

All of these were eliminated by field marks and the vocalizations I heard and subsequent photos and audio I captured. 

Previous Experience
