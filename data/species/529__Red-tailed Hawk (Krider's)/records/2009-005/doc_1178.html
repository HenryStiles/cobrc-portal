Description of the Bird

I immediately recognized this bird as an unusual, pale juvenile Red-tailed Hawk. There were two other Red-tailed Hawks soaring in the vicinity with the same relative size and shape, but not coloration.

Dorsal surface: The Krider's back and wing was brown with buffy mottling overall, with light patches on the back and upper wing coverts. There were very bright wing panels. The tail is fully barred, but fades as it nears the base. Overall, the head is very light for a Red-tailed Hawk. It had a white crown with light brown marks, light brown marks on the cheeks, and a clear white throat.

Ventral surface: The Krider's had faint, but noticeable patagial bars and comas on the under wing, which means it is a Red-tailed Hawk. It was very pale underneath with a lightly dark-brown spotted belly band. The wings were brownish-black tipped and had brown with buffy edges on the trailing edge of the wing. The tail was white at the base and was buffy with dark brown barring. The tail is fainter in color towards the distal edges. It had yellow legs.
Behaviors: This bird was riding thermals with prey in its talons. Along with the prey, it had picked up vegetation, which was still clenched in its talons and trailing behind. Two other typical Red-tailed Hawks and two adult Bald Eagles were also soaring in the area. The Krider's Red-tailed Hawk landed in a medium-sized tree on a branch that was swaying in the wind just on the other side of the road behind a fence. It landed on the backside of this tree where I could not clearly view any more behavior.
Call: Did not vocalize.
Plumage: Juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

Bill Schmoker sent the photos to Brian Wheeler, Tony Leukering, and Jerry Liguori. Below are their comments.

Quoting Brian Wheeler:

"This is nearly a full juvenile Krider's Red-tailed Hawk. If a Harlan's, then there would be barring on the outer primaries and all barring on remiges would be much thicker. There is rufous tinges on underwing markings, which would not occur on a harlani. The tail banding and all dorsal markings are borealis type; however, the faint leg barring is a bit unusual for a bird this pale.

On the top of the tail, the banding gets less and nearly absent on the base of the tail. A juvenile pale harlani has full banding on all of the tail. These bands are neat and typical of Krider's, they get less defined on the base of the tail. The faint coloring on the distal tail is more indicative of Krider's, too. This bird is virtually full Krider's with perhaps a bit of calurus blood in it, which they do intergrade on the northern and western Plains, especially Alberta.

There are lots of tawny feather edgings on the upper parts. This is common on borealis (which Kriders' is) and all other typical redtail juveniles; not found on juvenile harlani redtails."

Quoted from Jerry Ligouri: "100% pale Eastern or "Kriders"... didn't even have a question on it. Classic bird..."

Tony Leukering agreed too that this is a Krider's.
