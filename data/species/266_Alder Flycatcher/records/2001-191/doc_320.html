Description of the Bird

Chris Wood and I had just finished a floating low-elevation riparian transect (part of the Monitoring Colorado's Birds program at RMBO) and we were floating downstream to our take-out point at Ft. Lupton when Chris noted an Empidonax flycatcher singing. Since no species of that genus is known to breed in the vicinity and because it sounded like an Alder Flycatcher, we beached the canoe to go find the bird. It took us a couple of minutes, but we found the bird, all the while it sang occasionally.

The bird was an obvious empie with an olive green crown, nape, back, and rump; a narrow partial eye ring (mostly behind the eye); a fairly distinct white throat contrasting with the dark auriculars; an olive-gray chest band; pale belly (whitish or very, very pale yellow - depending on the light); and a dark tail with pale outer webs to the R6s. The wings were blackish, with whitish wingbars and a fairly sizable primary projection (about 55-60% the length of the longest tertial). The maxilla was dark and the mandible was almost entirely orange-yellow.
Behaviors: Foraging in shrubs and tall grass and singing sporadically
Call: The bird sang at least ten times, giving the typical "f-bee-yo" with the initial syllable very short and the emphasis on the second syllable.
Plumage: I do not know

Similar Species Discussion

Willow Flycatcher quite similar, but head features (eye ring, auriculars/throat contrast, color) reasonably useful in species separation. Of course, the song was different from the two-syllabled primary song of Willow Flycatcher. Other empies ruled out by plumage and song, as were both wood-pewees.
