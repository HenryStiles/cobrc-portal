Description of the Bird

The bird's song was the classic three-syllable rrRH-BEE-uh of Alder Flycatcher, very harsh and heavily modulated. Each time, I heard the three notes, the first one creaky and grinding, the second one explosive, the third one relatively faint and fading.

I saw the bird very briefly in a tall sapling, and could only tell that it was an Empidonax flycatcher. Then I lost it. Despite lengthy searching, I and my companion (David Gillilan) could not re-find it. The trees around the ranch house are very dense, and birds often get holed up in there for long periods of time.
Behaviors: Other than hearing it sing, no behaviors noted. The bird was briefly seen near the top of a tall sapling; then it flew away, possibly staying within the grove.
Call: This was the basis for the ID. The bird was singing its rrRH-BEE-uh song in the productive grove of trees near the Fox Ranch headquarters. It sang about 5 times, then disappeared for good.
Plumage: presumed adult

Similar Species Discussion

Willow Flycatcher.

I am well aware of the problems of overlap between the songs of Willow and Alder Flycatchers. (I grew up in a region of w. Pennsylvania where the two species were sympatric.) I know about the weird 'tweener songs, but this bird seemed to be giving a good "rrRH-BEE-uh" for Alder Flycatcher.

My brief glimpse of the bird was good enough only to say that the bird was an Empid.

Of note is that David and I heard a WILLOW Flycatcher (FITZ-bew) the next morning. It wasn't in the grove of trees with the Alder, but rather about 1/4 mile away, along the Arickaree River. Hearing this Willow Flycatcher provided us with a good opportunity for mental comparison with the ALDER Flycatcher the evening before.
