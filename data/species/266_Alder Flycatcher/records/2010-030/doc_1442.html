Light:  Variable but usually strongly overcast. I was usually east of the bird with the sun being to the south and high overhead. The flat light was good for photography and seeing subtle shades of color, such as the greenish cast to the upper parts and green glow on the flanks. Rarely was the light "contrasty" and full of shadows.

Description of the Bird

This flycatcher was clearly in the genus Empidonax on the basis of its overall small size, modest primary extension, and greenish hugh. My first impression of this empid was that it was very green above, fairly large for this genus, and thick-tailed.

The head shape varied, depending on the angle and disposition of the bird. At times it was fairly rounded and unpeaked, at others it had a slight crest toward the rear of the crown. At no time did the crown appear highly crested or "ragged". The bill was moderately long and bicolored: the top mandible was dark gray, the lower was entirely yellow-orange (no dark tip to the lower mandible, even on the underside). A white eyering was evident, although not prominent. The eyering was white, evenly thin, with a slight break at the front and a very slight crescent at the rear. The crescent at the rear was not nearly as exaggerated as would be typical of Cordilleran or some of the other empids. The throat was white and clearly the lightest shade of the bird's underparts. The head color was an even olive-brown, and did not contrast with the rest of the upper part of the bird (i.e., basically the same shade as the nape, back, upper tail, and scapulars). The wings had two prominent whitish wingbars, the upper being ever so slightly narrower and browner than the lower in subdued light. In bright light, both wingbars appeared white and broad, contrasting sharply with the bordering wing feathers. The secondaries were blackish with the outermost secondaries showing fairly wide, contrasting, white edges. The primaries were black. I would characterize the primary extension as moderate. The breast was light gray, only slightly grayer than the throat. The flanks in good light showed an olive glow. The belly was light yellow. The tail was thick, fairly long, and straight-sided. With back-light, the outer retrices appeared white, but this may have simply been a function of sunlight coming thru them that was blocked for the other retrices. The underside of the retrices was dark gray. The legs and feet were very dark gray, bordering on blackish.
Behaviors: The bird was very actively flycatching for midges within 10 feet of the ground along a stretch of unpaved street (Atlanta Avenue) that went up a slight hill, with both sides of the street being heavily vegetated with small woody trees, shrubs, and tangles (i.e., catbird habitat (see below)). The wind was blowing briskly on this day and the area was mini-refuge for the insects, thus, attracting the steady presence of the bird. It left this area on occasion and foraged perhaps as far away as 50 yards on either side of the "alley" but kept coming back. The bird occasionally flicked its wings and tail, but not prominently, and I would describe the first motion of the tail as upward. The bird used willows, mullein, and a barbed wire fence for perches. It was chased off on a number of occasions by a territorial Gray Catbird but persisted in coming back to feed.
Call: Only the call note was heard, but heard well and repeatedly: a one-syllable "pip" note, with the "p" sound distinctly at the beginning and end.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The large size of this empid, its thick and straight tail, the limited eyering, bicolored and longish bill clearly made this a "Traill's" Flycatcher. Other features pointing to this duo were the upward tail flick (eliminating Gray). The bill length and bright yellow-throughout-its-length lower mandible, normally proportioned head in comparison to the rest of the body, bright white throat, and lack of contrast between the head and rest of the upperparts eliminated Hammond's for sure and most Duskies I've been sure of. It was too big and too green for Least, although the eyering evenness was somewhat suggestive of this species. Primary extension, head shape and call seemed to eliminate Acadian. Eyering color was too white for Yellow-bellied. Thus, it seemed to come down to Alder vs. Willow and the call note was the deciding factor there, plus the eyering was too prominent for most Willows I've seen, at least in the West.

Additional Information

Comments: In all respects this bird seemed to be a classic Alder Flycatcher. It was very cooperative, allowing me every chance to see and hear what I needed for positive ID and documentation. Hereford is a logical "trap" for migrants on the Pawnee Grasslands. The date, in the prime time for eastern migrants in northern CO, is perhaps a bit early but overall good for this species.
