Description of the Bird

David Dowell and I were driving west of Laird with the windows down listening and looking for land birds in Yuma County. We saw a variety of shorebirds and ducks on a mudflat along a small stream  just north of the road and got out of the car. As soon as we got out I heard "rreeBEEa" and then a "pip." I said to David that we have an Empidonax over hear. We got on the bird and noticed the following; Empidonax in size and shape, very faint eye ring (at first we didn't even see it until it flew to another perch and in direct sunlight), medium primary projection, a darker grayish cheek and crown which emphasized the white chin and two buffy wing bars. It had a olive cast to the bird with rich crisp feather edges. The head shape seemed more rounded. The underparts were warm light yellow. The bird had a noticable light grayish chest. The bill didn't strike me as either short or long but average in length. The upper mandible was dark and the lower orange. It would occasionally flick its tail. The bird was actively feeding and singing. It would take off after an insect, catch it, fly back to its same or very near perch and eat it. Several minutes later it would sing "rreeBEEa" and once or twice just "eeBEEa". The sound was burry or buzzy with an accent and slightly higher pitch on the middle inflection. We also heard several "pip" calls. These were very dry or short with a distinct and sharp beginning and end. The bird looked in excellent condition and feeding actively.


David Dowell was able to make a sound recording and shoot a few pictures which I will include in this report. What a fun experience!

Similar Species Discussion

We considered a Willow Flycatcher. It was eliminated due to the following;
1. The song heard of "rreeBEEa" and "eeBEEa" is much different sounding than the Willows RITZbew, rrrEEPyew, rrrIP. the Alder had a different sound at the end of the song that was not a "w". A "w" is a weak ending that sounds lower in pich and not a clean ending. We heard the "a" which has a very slight rise to the end. This was the main distinguishing factor used to seperate our Alder from the average Willow.
2. "Pip" is much different than the Willows "whit." "Pip" is much drier and has an accented beginning and end to the sound. The Willows "whit" has a burry unrefined beginning to the sound with a sharp "t" at the end. The "whit" also has a slight crescendo to the beginning of the sound. we did not hear that! This was the second main distinguishing factor used to seperate our Alder from the average Willow.
3. The Willow has a broad base to the bill which we did not think it was that wide. (Not a great distinguishing factor.)
4. The primary projection was medium in length which might help seperate between the two species. Willow can sometimes look a little shorter. (Not a great distinguishing factor.)
5. The cheek seemed a little darker than most of the Willows I have seen in the state. (Not a great distinguishing factor.)

Resources Used

First off I used my knowledge of bird calls. I guess originally I learned it from various published recordings. Secondly, we looked up the bird after studying it for a very long time. We used the Sibley 2nd edition. We saw the discription of the calls, primary projection, bill, and overall coloration of the cheek. Finally, I listened to the bird calls on my Sibley app on my phone. Without meaning to, the bird responded to the song by flying one tree closer just after playing the sound. I didn't intend the bird to hear the song but just wanted to check on the actual sound.

Spending the last 11 years in Colorado, Ihave become familiar with the Willow Flycatchers. Since I have spent my whole adult life as a band director, I pay special attention to bird calls, songs and their distinct nuances.
