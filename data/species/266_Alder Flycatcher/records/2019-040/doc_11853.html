Description of the Bird

Both were initially heard calling "pip" which alerted us to their presence. The birds were widely separated, eliminating the possibility of recounting. Of the close to a dozen "Trail's" Flycatchers observed that day at Tamarack, as many as four appeared to be good for Alder, but only two were confirmed with vocalizations. Both were foraging in willows and other thickets low to the ground in typical Willow/Alder fashion. These were rather bulky empids with relatively short, thick bills (for a Trail's). Upper mandible dark while lower mandible was orangish-yellow. Back and crown were noticeably olive-green. Both birds sported narrow but complete white eyerings when seen well (at times very conspicuous, other times thin and hardly detectable). The white throat contrasted noticeably with grayish-green cheeks. Underparts pale with yellow-olive wash. Black tertials with contrasting white edges. The head was rounded or flat, but never appeared to take on the crested appearance of a Willow Flycatcher. Primary projection fairly long for a Trail's Flycatcher. Strong white wingbars tinged with olive. 

Similar Species Discussion

Immediately identified as a Trail's Flycatcher based on the rather thin eyering, larger size, and strong green upperparts. Hammond's, Dusky, and Gray Flycatchers quickly eliminated by aforementioned traits. Yellow-bellied would be much more greenish-yellow throughout and would be smaller and more compact. Least Flycatcher eliminated by thick, wide tail, larger size, narrower white eyering, and distinctive "pip" calls. Acadian would have less noticeable contrast between white throat and head, a longer bill, and different calls. Willow Flycatcher was carefully eliminated for both birds based on multiple traits plus vocalizations. First, the head shape was consistently flat or rounded, distinctly different from the crested appearance of a Willow Flycatcher. This head shape was maintained throughout the period of observation. Additionally, the primary projection was noticeably longer than that of a Willow. Its bill was short and thick, whereas that of a Willow's would have been longer. When seen in good light, both birds were very green overall (especially on the back and head) with strong contrast between the white throat and olive-green head. Willow Flycatcher would be more muted grayish-green with less strong auriculars-throat contrast and a grayer head. The eye ring was white and complete, being fairly noticeable at times, versus the much more faint eye ring of a Willow. In addition to these physical field marks, the call solidified the identification. Both birds were giving the characteristic "pip"/"peet"/"pleep" calls, distinctively different from the plain "whit" of a Willow Flycatcher. 

Resources Used

Several days before visiting Tamarack I reviewed material on the identification of Alder Flycatcher in case we saw any. Such material included the Cornell Lab's page for vocalizations, a Flickr album by Steve Mlodinow on Alder/Willow Flycatcher ID, and various field guides. In addition to these sources, I consulted the Sibley Guide to Birds (Second Edition) after the observation to confirm what I had seen.

Previous Experience

I have much experience with Willow Flycatchers from breeding birds in the Colorado mountains as well as from migration along the Front Range. I also have a fair amount of experience with other empidonax flycatchers such as Least/Hammond's/Dusky which it might possibly be confused with. I have relatively minimal experience with Alder Flycatchers, but had observed them previously in Minnesota.
