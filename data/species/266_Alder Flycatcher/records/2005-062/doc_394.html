Description of the Bird

A somewhat large empid, with very greenish-olive upperparts. Crown also greenish-olive but slighter darker than the back. Dull white eye-ring somewhat broader posteriorly but not tear-shaped. Lores a dull white. Dark eye. Sides of face and nape grayer than the back. White chin and throat very much contrasting with the face. Whitish upperbreast. Distinguishable breast band about the same color as the back but faded in the center. Belly had a yellowish wash. The rest of the underparts yellowish white. The bill appeared wide and somewhat long and was completely yellow-orange on the lower mandible and blackish on the upper mandible. Fairly long wings, grayish-black in color with two bold obvious contrasting white wing-bars. Well-defined tertial edges with white on outer web, secondaries also edged with white, and primaries tipped with narrow white. Long primary extension. Broad, medium length tail. Upper tail similar in color to wings but not quite as dark. Legs and feet blackish.
Behaviors: This bird was actively flycatching in the woods at Dixon. It repeatedly returned to a medium to low exposed perch, but not always to the same tree. While perched the bird was generally quiet with little movement other than looking around.
Call: not heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Wood-pewees have no eyering, are larger with longer wings that extend farther down the tail, longer primary extension, and a noticeable peaked crest. Hammonds Flycatcher has a grayish throat, a lower mandible that is usually partly dark and more contrast between their gray head and olive back. Dusky Flycatcher would appear more gray-olive above, and have a noticeable dark tip to the lower mandible, shorter primary projection and longer tail. Least Flycatcher would appear smaller and more large-headed; have a much bolder eyering, shorter and more triangular bill, and shorter primary projection. Gray Flycatcher dips its tail when perched. It also has a paler face and would not show as much contrast between the face and chin/throat. Lighter gray upperparts, shorter primary projection a longer, narrower bill and an obvious dark tip to the lower mandible all help eliminate Gray Flycatcher. Cordilleran has a broken eye-ring with a distinct teardrop shape posteriorly, and shorter primary projection. Yellow-bellied Flycatcher would appear much more yellow underneath with a broad yellow eyering. Buff-breasted can be ruled out by coloration and structure. Acadian Flycatcher has a larger bill, pale yellowish eye-ring, paler face, grayer throat, buffy vs. white wingbars and longer primary extension. Willow Flycatcher is eliminated because typically, and most importantly, there is no eyering or a very indistinct eyering. Willow is somewhat paler with browner upperparts, less contrast between the crown and back, less contrast between the face and throat, and generally has duller wingbars.
