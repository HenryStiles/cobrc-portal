Description of the Bird

Description
Excuse me if I borrow some of the description verbatim from the description of Alder #1, as there was much overlap in markings. Notes were written in field, for both birds, before referring to any sources/books.

This was a moderately active Empid, seemingly larger than the Leasts I'd seen earlier (by impression anyway, including tail, which seemed longer and wider). The back was moderately bright green, brighter than that of any Willow/Dusky/Hammond I'd seen this spring. Some Leasts were probably about as bright. However, there was not the distinct contrast between back and nape that Leasts show. I did not carefully scrutinize color of nape and crown, but they were at least somewhat green, without prominent gray or brown hues. The wing was quite blackish, like a bright Least. The wingbars were bright, but had a slight yellow-buff tinge. The tertial edges where bright white. The primary extension was long, longer than any Willow or Dusky I'd seen but probably not as long as that of a Hammond's.

The underparts were entirely whitish, except some dinginess about the chest. The face was greenish with a narrow, circular, complete white eyering, distinctly narrower than the eyering of Hammond's/Least/Dusky for comparison. The bill was largish, but seemed a tad narrower than that of a Willow, which was backed up by nearly direct comparison (there was a Willow Flycatcher "whitting" nearby that came into nearly the same field of view as the Alder). The lower mandible was completely pink.
Behaviors: moderately active
Call: Vocalizations:
Call something between a "Peek" and a "Jeek." Not quite like a Hammond's Fly, which I think of as sounding rather like a LB Dowitcher. The song was wee-BEE-o, with the second syllable emphasized. The song blended together as one, without a distinct break. About half of the time, the first syllable could not be heard. The "o" at the end was a bit drawn out (not was brief was "wee-BEE-o" might imply) with a faint roughness to it. I've heard a number of peculiar songs from Willow Flycatchers, including those that went "wee-BEE." These had a louder first syllable than that of an Alder, with a different tonality that I have a hard time putting into words. "Sharper" is the term that comes to mind.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Identification
Much as with bird number one, excepting that the song is an additional ID mark:
The call and song itself should eliminate all other Empids. I have heard the occasional Willow give an Alder-like call and song (see above). The odd Willow call-notes tend to have a burriness to them. Occasionally, I have heard otherwise typical western Willows utter a call quite similar to that of an Alder. However, the evenly colored green upperparts (eastern Willows tend to have contrasting napes like Leasts; western Willows tend to be more uniform above, but duller), the eyering, the primary projection, and call in combination, effectively eliminate Willow Fly. I've seen a number of Alders (100+ ) and 1000+ Willows in eastern and western North America. I feel comfortable with identifying calling Alder Flycatchers seen well, and feel comfortable identifying this bird as an Alder.
