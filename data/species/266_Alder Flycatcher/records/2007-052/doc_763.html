Description of the Bird

Empidonax flycatcher by size, eyering, structure, including bright orange mandible and wings shorter than Contopus pewees.

Large Emip with noticeable small round whitish eyering. Crested head with large broad bill with undersides of mandible orange, although bill appears shorter on one photo due to foreshortenning. Nape and back greenish brown. Bird in shade but through scope bird appearded noticeably greenish. Wings short with medium primary projection. Tail appears longish and dark gray contrasting with back color but the same dark gray as the wings. Buff broad wingbars contrast with dark gray wings. Throat whitish and contrasted with darker light breast and strongly contrast with sides of head. Tibia/tarsus dark gray as were feet.
Behaviors: Foraged low in Russian olives with cattail and weedy border. Short flights from one low perch to another. Moves into middle of tree when flushed by me.
Call: none heard
Plumage: junvenal

Similar Species Discussion

Willow Flycatcher and especially the eastern subspecies is almost identical to Alder Flycatcher and the differences are somewhat subjective. The bill of Alder is slightly smaller. At the time of this sighting I had not seen any Willow Flycatchers around but later did and this bird was not as brown-backed at Willow and I looked for and thought that Willow had a big honking bill, noticeably larger than the Alder. Also in some postures the Alder seemed less crested than the Willows. Although I studied the wing lengths of this bird and another one from CBR in El Paso County and compared them to the numerous Western, they appeared to be approximately the same length, shortish. The two birds that I photograhed and called Alder were identified as such by comparing them to the known Wilows that lacked eyerings. The two Alder were always rounder headed, lacking the stronger crest of Willow, greener-backed but still with brown tones, and slightly shorter billed.
