Description of the Bird

This was a large Empid with long wings and large wide bill caught amid short Russian Olives at Chico Basin Ranch and the RMBO Banding Station.

I was not present for the measurements but there was a brownish-backed Willow Flycatcher caught too that was noticeably different.

The entire head and back was olive-green (although this green does not show as much in the photo, at least on my screen). The eye ring was complete, whitish, and narrow. The wings had two very broad contrasty wingbars. The primary projection was longish. The bill was long and wide and the lower mandible was mostly orangish. In the hand, it did not seem crested. When it was released, it did flew away fast so i could view it perched.

The mouth lining was bright orange.
Behaviors: It was in the hand of a bird bander. No noice from it open mouth. When it was released it flew low and directly away to the west until out of sight.
Call: none heard
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

The big Empids are Gray, Willow, Alder, and Acadian. The green head and back eliminate Gray. The measurements conclusively rule out Acadian. There are apparently four critical measurements. Three of these were outside the range for WIFL, and fourth measurement in the range of both ALFL and WIFL.

My experience with CO and NM WIFLs is that it is a mostly brown- or gray-brown backed bird with either no or an incomplete eye ring. This bird was noticeably green with a complete eye ring. Based on measurements, eye ring, and coloration it is a ALFL and not WIFL.

Resources Used

I consulted the measurements given in the tables of the Birding magazine article called Identifying Empidonax Flycatchers The Ratio Approach in the March 2009 issue and I read all the details in Peter Pyle's Identification Guide to North American Birds Part I for these two congeners.

Previous Experience

Most of my experience with this species is ironically on Chico Basin Ranch where they are an annual spring and fall migrant. Because in spring they often migrate during the same dates as WIFL, and becasue both species get caught in the banding station, I have tried to see the subtle differences. When I first was showed this bird, I thought it was a ALFL and the measurements proved it to be.
