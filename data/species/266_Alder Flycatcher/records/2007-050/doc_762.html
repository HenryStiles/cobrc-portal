Habitat

Mostly giant ragweed with nearby pasture with wet sedges and innundated with lots of Canada thistle, and very closeby some giant sunflower. Scattered Russian olives nearby which flycatcher perched in a few times.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Leica 77mm APO scope with 32x WW lens
Distance:  as close as 50 feet
Light:  Excellent early morning light on bird. I photographed with light over my left shoulder.

Description of the Bird

This bird has the two crisp, unworn buffy wingbars of a hatch year bird. This bird is still mostly in juvenal plumage and will retain this plumage until October. (Pyle)

Head slightly crested and somewhat grayer than the back.
Small round whitish eyering.
Long and large wide bill with orange undersides to mandible.
Throat immaculate white, strongly contrasting with sides of head and breast.
Breast not visible in any photos but appeared dull creamy white in person.
Long rictal bristles.
Rather short primaries and tertials strongly edged yellow, fresh plumage.
Tail relatively long and dark gray.
Wings appear quite short, slightly longer than the base of the tail.
Moderate primary projection.
Back dark brown with greenish tones on upper back.
Behaviors: Foraged quite low in giant ragweed, Embrosia trifida. Perched for long periods of time on upper and lower strand of barbed wire fence near ragweed and giant sunflower. At times could hear bill snap.
Call: none heard
Plumage: juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

The bird that is almost identical to this flycatcher is the Eastern race of Willow Flycatcher.

I believe that an Eastern Willow Flycatcher would have a smaller eyering. Although the bill on this bird is long, Willow Flycatcher's bill is even longer. I studies about six Western Willow Flycatcher bills on 22 August 2007 and with close scope views, the length of the two species' bills are different lengths. Willow Flycatcher has a brown back like this bird, but the light on the back of this bird slightly washed out some of the green tones.
At the time of observation, the wings of this bird seemed quite short for most Empids, except for Least Flycatcher which has a much shorter bill, wider white eyering and much shorter tail.

Another character of this bird, that I noticed only after observing the Western Willow Flycatcher is this bird's lack of whitish lore, that is very noticeable on the Willow Flycatchers that I observed.

The almost entirely orange bottom of the mandible and size of the bill eliminates all but the Eastern Willow Flycatcher and the large Acadian Flycatcher.

Acadian Flycatcher was eliminated by that species' very long sickle shaped wings at rest and longer primary projection.

Additional Information

Comments: I used a large number of digiscoped photos for the description of the bird. which helped tremendously with the details of the plumage of the bird. I strongly feel that Eastern Willow Flycatchers would have even narrower eyering if any eyering was present. The comparison between this bird and photographs of Western Willow Flycatcher with no eyering visible are in close up, quite revealing in subtle difference.

I showed a series of photographs to three people and they all said that it was Alder Flycatcher. One of these persons showed the photos to Christopher Wood and Marshall Illif and they both said that this bird is an Alder Flycatcher.

I am including a photo of a Western Willow Flycatcher for comparison. This photo should not be confused with the photos of the Alder Flycatcher.
