Description of the Bird

At about 9:15 a.m. on the morning of 28 August, Tracy Clark and I were working along the creek immediately north of the Crow Valley Campground. This area still had water and was flanked by patches of thick brush and dense willow thickets. I was standing on the muddy edges, pursuing a dragonfly, when an Empid sallied upwards and back. It landed atop a willow, in clear view. I was struck by how green its back seemed. Other field marks, as discussed below, quickly pointed me towards Alder Fly. I watched the bird for about 3 minutes (during which time it sallied to and fro several times), at a distance of about 30 feet, with the sun mostly to my back, while using Swarovski EL 10x42 binoculars.

Description
The bird was of the general size and shape of an Empidonax flycatcher and behaved generally like one. The back was a moderately bright green as was the crown (and at most angles the nape, thus making the uppersides from back to forecrown bright green.) At some angles, the hind-neck seemed grayer or browner, typically when bird's head was turned sharply. The cheeks were grayish, the throat bright white. There was a narrow eyering that looked white and even in thickness throughout. The bird had a vague grayish loral spot. The belly and vent were washed in moderately bright, but pale, yellow. After the bird had left, I was not certain about the chest color. That is, I was not sure whether it formed a grayish transition from yellow belly to white throat, or if it was simply washed in yellow. My recollection is the former, but such was not directly noted in field.

The wings were blackish with two broad buffy wingbars. The tertial edges appeared white at times, pale yellow-green at others, but these edges always contrasted markedly with the main portion of the tertials. The primary extension was long, resembling the primary projection of many Hammond's Flycatchers. The tail was dark, color not really noted in any detail. Rump was not seen in field. Tail shape was consistent with "Traill's Flycatcher," being proportionately larger than Hammond's/Least types. The bird never looked crested, but rather it was consistently flat crowned. The bill was medium to large for an Empid in both length and width. The bill looked entirely yellow-orange when viewed from beneath.

The bird did occasionally flick tail and wings, sometimes just tail, consistent with my experience of "Traill's" Flycatchers. Three times the bird uttered a "peep" call note, consistent with Alder Flycatcher in my experience, but not with Willow Fly, including some its peculiar variations.

Similar Species Discussion

This bird initially stood out due to its green upperparts. This, plus bill shape/size, tail proportions, narrow and even eyering, and call eliminate Least Flycatcher. The bright plumage, primary extension, and eyering eliminate western races of Willow Flycatcher pretty easily, but Eastern Willow Flycatcher is more tricky, as it is similar in back color and primary extension. Beyond call, Eastern Willows tend to have a duller nape and dull crown color, both contrasting with the greener back. This bird's crown was as bright as its back, and usually its nape (or hind-neck) appeared equally as bright as well. The eyering was typical for Alder, though I think some Eastern Willows can have a similar eyering. The call, of course, eliminated Eastern Willow. Hammond's Flycatcher has a similar (though different) call, but has a much smaller bill, more prominent eyering, and grayer head (though in fall can be quite bright in back and belly color).
