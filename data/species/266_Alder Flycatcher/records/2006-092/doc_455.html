Description of the Bird

This bird was a very large flycatcher in the genus Empidonax. I had been seeing many Empids all May including other Alder Flycatchers that were not as cooperative to photograph. This bird was identified by call but a description (see photo) is included because this bird would probably have been identified as Willow Flycatcher if it did not call, as the back was not as green on its back, as other Alder Flycatchers seen in May at the same location, Chico Basin Ranch. Willow Flycatcher were also heard (sing and call) and seen the same day but not at this exact location.

Features that stand out are the complete small eyering, the peaked crown on the dark head, the large wide, orangish bill, the longish winged, longish tailed Empidonax. Once again this bird was identified by call which could be heard at close range (see photo for closeness).
Behaviors: Flycatching low in willows and also a medium height in plains cottonwoods and siberian elms. Foraged alone. Perched often in plain view but mostly within a circle of branch so that although it often perched in the open it was surrounded by foliage in the bigger picture.
Call: loud pip heard as many as 8 times, one very loud explosive call note possibly the one described by Sibley as a low, clear, pew
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Eastern Willow Flycatcher - this subspecies is extremely similar to Alder flycatcher. Without hearing this bird I may have thought that it was a WIFL, Extimus race I studied for a summer while working for Dr. Sartor Willians, III, when I was in charge of the survey efforts for this subspecies in New Mexico.

Acadian Flycatcher -- I thought about this species which has the narrow eyering, peaked head and white throat that this bird also had, but I don't think that thew wings were long enough for ACFL but the one LOUD call note does fit with the explosiveness of the ACFL calls and song.

Additional Information

Comments: Please consider Acadian Flycatcher as an alternative. I don't know the loud flat peek call note of this species and if it could be confused with a loud pip of Alder that I described. I have a photo of the second bird that was also at Rose Pond, which has a yellowish narrow eyering. I think this bird is an Alder, for sure not a Willow, but possibly an Acadian.
