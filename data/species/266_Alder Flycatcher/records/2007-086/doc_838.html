Description of the Bird

the bird was a small flycatcher, pretty much the exact same size and shape as Willow Flycatchers seen nearby, and for all intensive purposes the same size and shape as other Empidonax flycatchers seen in the area. It had the typical flycatcher shape, with a large head, long tail, fairly long wings, and upright posture. It was identified as an Empidonax flycatcher by the small size, bold white wingbars, complete but very thin eye-ring, and primary extension not long enough for a Wood-Pewee. The primary extention appeared to be of moderate length, and did not strike me as particularly long or short.

The bird's bill was fairly broad-based for an Empid, and the lower mandible was completely flesh colored. The head of the bird was dark dusky colored with a noticeable greenish tint; the bird had a slightly contrasting "helmet" that was duskier than the rest of the head and back. There was a small but noticeable pale supraloral spot. The bird had a very thin but complete and noticeable eye-ring that was white. The remainder of the head and the back were dusky green, but noticeably greener than the "helmet" on the head. The bird had a white throat that contrasted strongly with the greener head. The underparts were paler, with a dusky area across the chest. The wings were black, with two bold white wing bars. The tail was dark, but I did not notice the exact color.
Behaviors: The bird was seen foraging at about eye-level; it would sit on a small branch and wag it's tail slightly, and occasionally swoop out to catch a fly. After doing this a couple of times it dissapeared into a bush and could not be relocated.
Call: The bird was heard calling a couple of times; it gave an emphatic "pip" note, quite differant than the "whit" of Willow Flycatcher.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than Empids were ruled out by the combination of shape, bold white wing bars, eye-ring, and overall greenish color to the upperparts.

Hammond's, Dusky, and Gray Flycatchers were ruled out by the larger and broader based bill with no dusky tip to the lower mandible. Furthermore, Gray Flycatcher was ruled out by the far greener upperparts than expected for that species and very different call note, as was dusky flycatcher. Hammond's Flycatcher was ruled out by the greener plumage, and different proportions without the "big-headed" look typical of Hammond's.

Acadian Flycatcher was ruled out by the shorter primary extension, darker overall look, especially head, and sharper call note.

"Western" and Yellow-bellied Flycatchers were ruled out by the duskier coloration of the upperparts and whiter coloration of the underparts.

Willow Flycatcher was ruled out by the "helmeted" look of the head, greener back than expected for most subspecies of Willow, complete eye-ring, and, especially, by different call note.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen many Alder Flycatchers, and know their voice quite well from when I lived in the east. I know Willow Flycatcher very well, as I worked with them during the summer of 2005, and am very familiar with just about every vocalization they give. I am also familiar with all the other similar species.
