Description of the Bird

On the morning of 29 May, under high cloud cover, around 9 am, I found an Empid that caught my eye. Structurally and behaviorally, it made me think Willow Flycatcher. However, its back was greener than any Willow I've seen, the wings showed a great deal of contrast between coverts/tertials and their edges, and the bird had an eyering, albeit a very narrow one. Alder Flycatcher came to mind, and I checked on other ID features (see below). After about 30 seconds, the bird was nice enough to start calling.

This was a moderately active Empid, seemingly larger than the Leasts I'd seen earlier (by impression anyway, including tail, which seemed longer and wider). The back was moderately bright green, brighter than that of any Willow/Dusky/Hammond I'd seen this spring. Some Leasts were probably about as bright. However, there was not the distinct contrast between back and nape that Leasts show. I did not carefully scrutinize color of nape and crown, but they were at least somewhat green, not with prominent gray or brown hues. The wing was quite blackish, like a Least. The wingbars were very contrasting white, as were the tertial edges, like a bright Least. The primary extension was long, longer than any Willow or Dusky I'd seen but probably not as long as that of a Hammond's.

The belly was washed lightly in yellow. The throat appeared quite white. The chest color was not specifically noted, but was dingy. The face was greenish with a narrow, circular, complete white eyering, distinctly narrower than the eyering of Hammond's/Least/Dusky for comparison. The bill was largish, but seemed a tad narrower than that of a Willow (perhaps because I was expecting such... I must admit). The lower mandible was completely pink.
Behaviors: This was a moderately active Empid
Call: Call: given about 8 times, about 5 seconds apart. Call something between a "Peek" and a "Jeek." Not quite like a Hammond's Fly, which I think of as sounding rather like a LB Dowitcher.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

The call itself should eliminate all other Empids, excepting Hammond's (which is obviously eliminated by other marks, such as wing contrast, bill size...). I have heard the occasional Willow give an Alder-like call. These tend to have a burriness to them. Occasionally, I have heard otherwise typical western Willows utter a call quite similar to that of an Alder. However, the even green upper parts (eastern Willows tend to have contrasting napes like Leasts; western Willows tend to be more uniform above, but duller), the eyering, the primary projection, and call in combination, effectively eliminates Willow Flycatcher. Bill size, call, tail shape/size, eyering and other details eliminate Least. I've seen a number of Alders (100+ ) and 1000+ Willows in eastern and western North America. I feel comfortable with identifying calling Alder Flycatchers seen well, and feel comfortable identifying this bird as an Alder.
