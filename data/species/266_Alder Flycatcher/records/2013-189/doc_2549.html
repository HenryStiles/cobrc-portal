Description of the Bird

Shortly after arriving at Last Chance, I noticed a rather green backed flycatcher with a flattish head. It was initially in superb light, as close as 10 feet, with sun behind. Later observations were at greater distance, with skies then overcast, causing the brightness of all colors to dim. 

The bird's back, scaps, crown were all moderately bright green, with the green extending solidly down through the auriculars (where the green was washed with some gray). The head was not as bright as the back, but not sharply contrasting. The auriculars did, however, contrast sharply with a white throat. A narrow white eyering was present. The bill was moderately large for an Empidonax, pink/orange below and black above. The chest was dusky and the belly was washed with pale yellow. The wingbars and tertial edgings were bright white, contrasting brightly with the dark centers of those feathers. The primary extension past tertials was long. The tail was rather large, consistent with the Traill's group of Empidonax. 

After following it about for 5 or so minutes, I resorted to using a recording. I played Willow Flycatcher about 10 times... and the bird did not react. When the ALFL song was played, the bird flew straight towards me after about the 3rd song, landing about 10 feet away, having started 30 or so feet away. After landing close, it went "pleep" (or something similar) several times. I stopped the tape, trying to get more photos, but raising the camera caused the bird to fly away again, this time to some distance.

The size was c/w that of an Empidonax flycatcher. The head shape was, to me, stereotypical for Alder, with a bit of steep forehead, a rather flattish crown, and then a rounded rearcrown. During the entire time of observation, this head shape was evident, and never did the bird appear to have any crest.

Similar Species Discussion

Least and various Willow Flycatchers are the most similar. The call note eliminated all of these. Please note, that I've spent much time listening to peculiar calls of Willow Flys in WA, and am familiar with various atypical, Alder-like, call notes that they can give. This bird's calls were not within that range.

Least should have a bolder eyering as well, shorter primary, extension, narrower tail, different head shape.
All Willows typically show a different head shape. Primary extension is shorter, and contrast on wing is duller, back is plainer on western Willow Flycatchers
Eastern Willow Flycatchers typically have more contrast between back and head, less contrast between cheek and throat, and show some degree of crest. At any given moment, the head shape difference might not be apparent, depending on what bird is doing, but over time these differences in head shape are rather apparent. Also, this bird had a rather prominent diffuse gray loral spot. This seems to be present on many ALFL and less distinct on WIFLs of all stripes. 

Note that in photos submitted, those taken in dim light show the bird to appear substantially grayer than it did in life.
