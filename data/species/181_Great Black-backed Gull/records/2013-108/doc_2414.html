Habitat

The gull was standing on the ice next to a large flock of Ring-billed Gulls that were approximately 500 feet from the shoreline at Tower Loop in Cherry Creek Reservoir.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10 X 42 Binoculars; Spotting Scope - smaller Swarovski with a fixed 30X lens
Distance:  500 to 600 ft.
Light:  Partly cloudy; winter sun made lighting a bit difficult in addition to the reflection off the ice. We were facing southwest while standing on the shoreline. The gull was almost facing us.

Description of the Bird

This gull was standing on the ice next to a large number of Ring-billed Gulls. Its very large size and very dark mantle were immediately evident to our group. Using several field guides, we looked at all the gulls with dark mantles that possibly could be in our area during January. We were able to eliminate the gulls that had darker mantles, but that were just slightly larger than the RBGU (Ex. California Gull) because this gull seemed so massive. We figured that the Slaty-backed Gull was too rare and also was pretty close to the size of a California Gull as was the Western Gull. Our two remaining choices were the Lesser Black-backed Gull and the Great Black-backed Gull.

We could see that this gull seemed to have a fairly clean head and nape that made us think it might be the GBBG since the LBBG usually shows heavy streaking in the head and nape areas in non breeding plumage. In the photo I took through Karen von Saltza's scope, the leg color of the RBGU shows up slightly whereas the leg color of this gull seemed washed out. Since the leg color of a LBBG is the same color as the RBGU, we thought this might be another field mark in favor of a GBBG. The head and bill of this gull seemed very large unlike the more rounded head and smaller bill of the LBBG. The mantle seemed very dark, almost black, on this gull. The mantle color did not appear to be the dark gray color of the the LBBG.
Behaviors: This gull remained on the ice for approximately 5 to 10 minutes allowing us time to study the field guides and discuss our findings. It then took flight heading southeast over the ice. I can remember this part vividly because I am a volunteer for HawkWatch on Dinosaur Ridge, preferring to watch raptors rather than gulls. The large broad wings of this gull powering itself above the ice with slow, but massive wing beats was a sight to behold. Its movements reminded me more of an eagle in flight than a gull. I still remember exclaiming, "Unbelievable!" when it took off. This was the largest gull I had ever seen since I began birding in 2009.
Call: No call or song was heard
Plumage: Adult Nonbreeding

Similar Species Discussion

Using the field guides, we examined all the gulls that might possibly be in our area during January that had darker mantles and were larger than the RBGU.
California Gull: The gull in question seemed much larger than a California Gull. The nape seemed cleaner than a non breeding CAGU and the leg color appeared paler than that of a CAGU.
Herring Gull: The size of the gull in question appeared larger and the mantle was much darker than that of a Herring Gull.
Lesser Black-backed Gull: The dense dark streaking on the head and nape, the bright yellow legs and the dark gray mantle of a LBBG did not match what we saw on the gull in question.

Resources Used

The main field guide used was Sibley's Guide to Birds. I know we had at least 2 other field guides, but I don't recall which ones they were.

Previous Experience

At this time, I had only been birding for 2 years. The three main gulls I had studied were the ones that are often seen in this area: Ring-billed Gull, California Gull and Herring Gull. This gull did not look like any of these gulls I had studied. This was the first time I had ever seen a Great Black-backed Gull.
