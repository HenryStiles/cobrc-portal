Description of the Bird

A large gull, substantially more massive than nearby California Gulls, with pure white head and underparts, dark gray upperparts except for narrow white tertial crescent, and a trace scapular crescent, and small white mirrors on the outermost two primaries, with trace white tips to other outer primaries; primaries black. Tail not seen well. Legs viewed briefly when bird stood up from sitting position were very pale flesh colored (pinkish gray). The white mirror on the outermost primary (the longest primary, P10), seen from underneath, extended to tip of the feather, a key feature that helped identify this bird as a Great Black-backed Gull. Finally the bill was large and heavy, of a yellow color with a small red gonydeal spot and a gray smudge on the mandible just above the red spot. The gray smudge and the tiny white tips to primaries suggest a bird slightly younger than full adult, i.e. a bird at the end of its fourth annual cycle.

Similar Species Discussion

Lesser Black-backed Gull would be smaller, smaller-headed and smaller-billed, with slightly lighter shade of gray on the upperparts, yellow legs. Western Gull would have pink or pinkish-yellow legs and would have a black mark between the white tip and the white mirror of P10. Yellow-footed Gull would have yellow legs. Slaty-backed Gull would be slightly paler shade of gray and bright pink legs. Kelp Gull would be blacker on the mantle with greenish legs. None of the field marks were out of the range of variability for Great Black-backed Gull, so there is no indication of hybrid status.
