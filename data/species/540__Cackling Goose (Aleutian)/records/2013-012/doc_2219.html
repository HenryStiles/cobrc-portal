Description of the Bird

Cathy Sheeter and I were standing on road that runs along Union Reservoir's n. side. We noticed a lone, rather dark breasted white-cheeked goose. Studying it from some distance, based on white neck collar and extensive black gular patch, plus bronzy breast, I suspected the bird was an Aleutian Cackling Goose. We drove to the closest point, putting us 100 to 150 yards from the bird with fair light.
The birds dark bronzy breast, seen face on (which makes breast look palest, or "true color") was quite dark, as were the remainder of the underparts (obviously, when viewed from side), excepting white undertail coverts and vent. The underpart color was not as dark as most minima, but richer and more bronzy-brown than darkest Tavs. The bird had a typical black neck and head of white-cheeked geese. The cheek patch was very narrow, and without indentation throughout observation. The gular patch was broad, so broad that it could be seen in strict profile views as it came up onto cheek. The bill was relatively long and narrow, the forehead fairly steep, but the bill to forehead angle was not as sharp as typical for Richardson's Cackler. Again, this head shape held true throughout observation, as apparent Cackling Goose head shape can vary from moment to moment depending on activity and alertness. The crown itself was rounded, not angular or flattened. The wing coverts were medium brown for a Cackling Goose, but with bright pale buff edging. No blue or gray hues present. The flanks were moderately barred with diffuse dusky markings, as is often seen in immatures. A thin white collar at base of black neck encompassed about 2/3 of the neck circumference, not easily seen in attached photos. This white collar was subtended by a dark brown band, darker than that of chest below. The bird had a rather chesty appearance that is typical of Aleut and Taverner's, but not Richardson's or minima Cackling Geese. No other geese were present for size comparison, but shovelers and Mallards passed by from time to time, and size was c/w Cackling, not Canada Goose.
Behaviors: Standing, then walking, without apparent aim, along muddy edge of Union Reservoir
Call: not heard
Plumage: immature

Similar Species Discussion

Size and shape too small, short-necked, short-billed for Canada Goose. Richardson's Cackling Geese rarely have such dark underparts, especially with chestnut hue. Also, when watched over time, an indentation is usually seen in Richardson's Cackler and the cheek patch is wider. Gular patches of such width are exceptionally rare in Richardson's, and though a fair number of Richardson's have neck collars, the darker collar beneath the white one is also exceptionally unusual. The head shape was not as angular as typical for Richardson's, and the bill shape was not typical, though within the broad range of Richardson's bill shapes. A minima would typically have blue-gray on wing coverts, a glossy and more purplish chest color, shorter bill, more rounded head. A Taverner's would be stouter billed, with a rounded head, and Taverner's rarely show rich brown color to underparts. Gular patch would also be highly atypical for Taverner's.
The broad gular stripe, white neck collar, and bronzy chest color form a triad that, when combined with proper head/bill shape, are considered diagnostic for Aleutian Cackling Goose.

Additional Information

Comments: Notes taken immediately after observation. Bird appeared ill to me, by its slow seemingly aimless movement and separation from flock of Canada/Richardson's Cackling Geese on Union. Interestingly, from late Oct into mid-November, Washington had an unprecedented landfall of Aleutian Cackling Geese on outer coast extending into Puget Sound (southbound Aleuts usually make landfall in s. Oregon coast, with rare bird seen on outer coast); this was thought to be due to unusual and persistent weather system over n. Pacific Ocean.
