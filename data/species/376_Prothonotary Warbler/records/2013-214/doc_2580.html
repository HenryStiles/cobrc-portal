Habitat

The bird was in a juniper tree. The juniper trees were surrounded by sagebrush. Cottonwoods, willows and Gambel's Oak are nearby.

Distance:  40 feet
Light:  The light was great; the sun was at our backs

Description of the Bird

The warbler had an entirely yellow head.  Immediately, I checked to see if there was any other color on the crown or back of the head; there was not.  The head appeared bright yellow without any markings on the auricular.  (In fact later, Barb Byron told me that she initially thought the warbler was a yellow cottonwood leaf.)  The chin of the bird was also yellow.  The back of the bird was a bright green.  The wings were gray and lacked wing bars.  The upper side of the tail was mainly gray.  When the bird fanned its tail, the upper side of the tail showed extensive white.  The bird was facing away from us for the most part, but I did notice that there was white on the underparts.  (I didn't get to chance to see exactly where the yellow on the bird transitioned to the white on the lower part of the warbler.)  
     I had quickly determined the bird was a warbler due to the bill shape, size of the bird, overall shape of the bird, and activity of the bird.  The bill color was not noted.  However, the bill was consistent with a warbler's bill, thin and pointed.  The bird was warbler-sized, somewhere around 5 inches in length.        
     No calls were given.  The bird was foraging very actively in a juniper tree the entire time we saw it.  The bird was out on the edge of the tree, probing the needled branches in such a way as to be concealed within the branches at times.
   

Similar Species Discussion

     Hermit Warbler was the only species that I needed to rule out.  While a Hermit warbler's  head would be bright yellow, the posterior of the crown and the nape would have been another color (black or green).  In addition, wing bars and a black throat would have been evident on a male or adult female Hermit Warbler.  (A first fall female Hermit Warbler would have had olive on the crown and auriculars in addition to white wing bars.)  Consequently, Hermit Warbler was ruled out during the time the bird was still in view.  Because the warbler we saw had a bright yellow, unmarked head without an eyeline, auricular pattern, or crown patch, the identification was rather straightforward.  


Note:  Barbara Byron saw the bird several seconds before I got on it.  She found the bird and alert me to it.      

Resources Used

References didn't need to be consulted for an identification to species, but I did consult several books to determine gender, age, and plumage of the bird.  Those references included:  The National Geographic Guide to the birds of North America, The Sibley Guide to Birds, and A Field Guide to Warblers of North America by Jon Dunn and Kimball Garrett.  Peter Pyle's Identification Guide to North American Birds was consulted to assist in determining the age and gender from the amount of white in the tail (on the upper side).   

Previous Experience

    I have adequate experience with this species.  I've seen the species in New Mexico several times.  Here in Colorado, I saw one at Pastorius Reservoir (found by Jim Beatty) on 10/3/01.  I have seen many Hermit Warblers in breeding plumage, mostly in Arizona. 

Note:  The location of this sighting of Prothonotary Warbler was approximately 75 feet from the Prothonotary Warbler found at Pastorius Reservoir on 10/3/01.
