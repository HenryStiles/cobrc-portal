Description of the Bird

On the morning of September 4, 2005, I birded Bonny Reservoir. My first stop was the first wooded area east of State Highway 385 on County Road 2, where the road bends to the south, but an old trail extends eastward into the cottonwood gallery forest. After a few minutes in the woodland, a Red-shouldered Hawk began calling incessantly as it was quite agitated. The call, which I have extensive experience with, was a loud screaming kee aah accented on the first syllable and then dropping strongly in pitch and volume. As Blue Jays can quite adeptly mimic the call of Red-shouldered Hawk, I obviously wanted to get a visual on the bird. But the calls were very loud and powerful, which did not seem quite right for Blue Jay. As I cautiously moved eastward through the trees toward the calling bird, the bird took flight and flew further eastward perhaps another 60 yards. The first view I had was of a long-winged Buteo, roughly mid-sized between that of a Broad-winged and Red-tailed Hawk. Other than a mostly brown body, the only other trait I noticed on the first observation was of a tail containing numerous black-and-white bands. Moving slower towards where the bird seemed to have perched, I finally found it in the upper third of a cottonwood tree. It was still calling often. Through binoculars, I could see a crow-sized or slightly larger hawk with a relatively long tail and legs. The head was brown with some darker streaks. The bill was black with a bright yellow cere. There was a bit of darkening through the malar. The breast was an overall rich rusty coloration with the rusty-cinnamon pattern barred horizontally and vertically. The rusty underparts lightened gradually towards the vent. The legs were yellowish. As the bird turned at times, I noted that the shoulder -lesser coverts- was a richer rusty reddish. While facing me, the tail extended well beyond the tip of the primaries. From that view, the tail appeared barred with black-and-white terminating with a narrow white band. It seemed that there were at least three or so white bands exposed from this view. After roughly a half a minute, the bird again took flight and flew northwards across a small cattail marsh. When in flight, I noted that from beneath, the breast, chest, axillaries, and underwing coverts were all rusty colored. The flight feathers were diffusely banded with gray and black, though the outer primaries were noticeably darker. Quite evident were lighter rectangular windows across the base of the inner primaries. I was able to get a better view of the tail as it flew and it seemed that I was seeing at least four white bands bounded by an equal number of black bands. Upon entering the other island of trees, I was unable to refind the bird.
Behaviors: Bird was calling often. I saw it in flight and perched.
Call: A loud screaming kee-aah accented on the first syllable and then dropping strongly in pitch and volume.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Red-tailed Hawks, besides generally lacking a rusty breast (except for rufous morphs), have reddish tails (as adults), rusty shoulders, and having a different call, are larger, more stocky and have relatively shorter tails. Broad-winged Hawks are smaller, stockier, have shorter tails that are only have two or so grayish-white bands bordered by black, lack the rusty shoulders, and also differ by call. Ferruginous Hawks, which do have rusty upperwing coverts, are much larger, have a whitish and light rust tail, and have rusty leggings.

Previous Experience

Have extensive experience with this species. For seven years during the 1980s, I ran a Red-shouldered Hawk breeding biology study in central Wisconsin in which we captured and banded over 100 birds. I also was involved with a banding study of wintering birds in Texas between 1993-1997. I have seen many while birding across the eastern U.S.
