Description of the Bird

An medium to large immature hawk flew past us and landed below the group on a stump on the edge of the creek.  It was originally identified incorrectly as a Cooper's Hawk but Bill Kampfer and myself both immediatly said, no it was a buteo. The shoulders were too broad for Coopers Hawk.  After about one minute, it flew away from the group and we were able to observe the upper wing and spread tail.


The bird was overall brown aboveThe wing was brown with tawny crescent shaped wing panels at the base of the primaries (again as seen from ABOVE).  The tawny panels are unique to Red-shouldered Hawk per Brian Wheeler.  The spead tail was barred with perhaps 7 dark brown bars and light brown in between.  These bars were broader and more distinct than Red-tailed.


The bird then flew out of sight and the group split up and worked up towards where the hawk and flown to, on each side of the creek.  After about five minutes the hawk flew west into the Cottonwood stand.  At that point I was able to get a 15 second fly by and again noted the red tail and wing panels.  With this second look, was initially hesitant of ruling out Red-tailed Hawk, but Chuck Huntermark got some pictures that I've sent to Brain Wheeler.  Brian states that it is undoubtedly a Red-shouldered Hawk, most likely from the Arkansas/East texas group.


I'm sure Chuck will include his photos in his submittal.

Similar Species Discussion

Red-tailed Hawk immature and Broad-winged Hawk immature.  Neither of this immature hawks have tawny crescents at the base of the primaries.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen hundreds of Red-shouldered Hawks, but most of those have been adults.  I've seen maybe 10 immature Red-shoulders in my life time.
