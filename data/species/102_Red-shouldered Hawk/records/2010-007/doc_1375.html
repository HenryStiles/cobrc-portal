Description of the Bird

The bird was a medium-sized hawk, clearly a member of the genus Buteo but on the small side, smaller than any of the Red-tailed Hawks in the area, comparatively more slender, with a proportionately longer tail and a relatively small bill. It was fairly dark brown on the back, with uneven white spotting; it had a pale-creamy background color to the underparts, with distinct vertical brown streaks, especially on the upper breast. The head and throat were dark brown, almost solid, but there was a distinct buffy supercilium. The tail was dark brown with 5-6 thin pale bands visible when the bird was perched (the last pale band was at or near the tip of the tail). With good views, I could see one adult belly feather just right (bird's left) of the center of the breast: this feather was pale with a vertical light reddish bar down the center and light reddish horizontal bars coming off of it. When the bird flew, the underwing coverts showed a distinctly reddish hue; the flight feathers were white with fairly subtle horizontal black barring; the trailing edge of the wing was traced in thicker black. There was no contrasting patagial bar as in a Red-tailed Hawk. The upperside of the wing in flight showed strongly contrasting buffy wing crescents near the base of the primaries. From below when the bird flew directly over my head, the base of the primaries were slightly translucent, in contrast to all other parts of the wing.
Behaviors: The bird was reasonably active, perching in trees at various heights (1 to 18 meters high) for periods of 1-2 minutes, then dropping to the ground to pursue prey or flying to another nearby tree. In flight the bird tended to stay low and fly through trees, even when open meadow was available; it would swoop up to a perch at the last second. Wingbeats were slightly faster than a Red-tailed Hawk's.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: presumed first-winter

Similar Species Discussion

Several Red-tailed Hawks were in the area for direct comparison. They were ruled out by size, shape, underwing pattern, and (especially) by the buffy wing crescents above, rather than the pale window across all the primaries that Red-tails have.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen Red-shouldered Hawks on numerous occasions in California, Oregon, Arkansas, Minnesota, and several other states. I have seen them more than half a dozen times in the last year. I see hundreds of Red-tailed Hawks each year.
