Description of the Bird

While hiking along main trail, I encountered a foraging flock on a somewhat isolated snag that still bore cones. I counted 8 birds total on the tree, clearly crossbills, as I could see the crossed bills in the bins. The birds were mostly brown and streaky, but two were adults, one quite reddish and the other yellowish. All the rest seemed young. They were fairly quiet, only occasionally chipping. Nevertheless, I've been getting in the habit of recording any and all crossbills that I can, although opportunities are scarce as I seem to encounter flyover groups more often than not, and cannot get my recording gear started in time before they're gone. This time seemed like an ideal opportunity, since they were stationary. My initial guess for ID was that these were Type 5 Red Crossbills, which would be expected in that habitat, and the chip sounds seemed consistent with that guess to my ears.

I'd been using Merlin as my recording tool the past couple months, as an ongoing experiment. I was startled to see however that Merlin IDed these crossbills immediately as Cassias, never once calling them Reds. I'd learned to treat Merlin's IDs with a fair amount of skepticism during my experimental phase, although I also noted that on many occasions Merlin was consistently accurate with many more mundane identifications, even with what I would have thought were hard-to-register sounds.

In any case, I continued to watch and record the crossbills for several minutes as they kept feeding on the cones, occasionally chattering with each other, flitting to nearby trees but always staying close. Finally, after about 10-15 minutes they gathered and flew off, chipping repeatedly as they departed in usual crossbill fashion. I obtained recordings for this episode as well, but because I was still doing my field work, I knew I wouldn't be able to analyze these recordings for another 2-3 days.

Three days later I saw an eBird alert from Luke Pheneger and Will Anderson that they had recorded crossbills very near where I had been on July 19 and had those recordings confirmed as Cassia Crossbills by the "finch folks" at Cornell. I immediately realized that they had likely encountered the same birds I had, except that I had been sitting on my earlier recordings the whole time, waiting until I was more free to dive into the problem. I finally began to study Cassia Crossbill calls online and compare them to the spectrograms of my own recordings. Based on my limited study it seemed to me like I'd had Cassias too, but for good measure I sent email inquiries to Matthew Young at Cornell and asked him what he thought of the recordings (which are included in this report). After a day or so he replied that all the crossbills in my recordings were Cassias.

Similar Species Discussion

Red Crossbill was really the only other possibility, specifically Type 2 or Type 5. Other types are also possibility, although less common in Colorado like Type 4 or 3. Visually I had no way of eliminating other Red Crossbills from contention, so I was entirely reliant on the spectrograms of the chip calls to separate them out.

Cassia Crossbill (formerly "Type 9") chip calls are most similar to those of Type 5, but are consistently in a lower register than those of Type 5 (the Lodgepole). I used the extensive description provided here:

https://ebird.org/news/crossbills-of-north-america-species-and-red-crossbill-call-types/

which depicts spectrograms the typical flight calls of all the crossbill types (1 through 10, including 9 which is now Cassia).

Previous Experience

This was my first experience with Cassia, that I know of. (I've heard Type 5 RECRs many times in Colorado, but I now wonder how many times I may have heard Cassias without realizing it!)

I've heard many other crossbills over the years in Colorado. My main familiarity is with Types 2 and 5 though.
