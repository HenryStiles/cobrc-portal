Description of the Bird

I was the only observer. I camped at this location on the night of July 15-16th. I heard crossbills on the evening of 7/15, but only managed to get poor cell phone recordings. I assumed the birds were the expected Type 5 "Lodgepole Pine" RECR because of the predominance of Lodgepole Pine across the landscape and because the birds didn't sound like the Type 2 "Ponderosa Pine" crossbills that I have the most experience with. 

When I woke on the morning of July 16th, I could hear crossbills in the nearby forests. I immediately got my Tascam DR-100mkiii recorder and Senneiser ME66/K6 shotgun microphone set up to record the crossbills hoping to obtain good recordings of Type 5 RECR. I spent a solid 25 minutes with a small group of crossbills at close range, at one point <5m. The group consisted of at least one adult male, an adult female, and a juvenile. Other crossbills were heard nearby, but these three were the target of my recordings.

I recorded several tracks of the birds, including some that were 5+ minutes long. The birds were foraging in the Lodgepole Pines. Most pines in the area had two generations of cones, some old, brown cones and fresh, green cones. I was able to capture a variety of calls including typical flight calls, alarm "toops" calls, what I think may be juvenile begging calls ("chew-wheer"), and various feeding chatter. The flight calls, in particular, are diagnostic among crossbills and provide the most compelling evidence of the specific ID. The male sang some half-hearted songs. 

The plumage was typical for a Red/Cassia Crossbill. The male was a dirty red color, and the female was streaky but with some greenish hues. The juvenile was more heavily streaked. All had "cross bills". 

Although I recorded these birds in July 2021, I did not get around to downloading the files and analyzing them until November 2021. It was at this point that I realized that these were Cassia Crossbills, and not the expected Type 5 Red Crossbill. The details of the spectrograms are unoquivocal. The recordings have been reviewed by Tim Spahr and Matt Young and they confirm that these are Cassia Crossbills (formerly Type 9 Red Crossbill).  

Similar Species Discussion

Cassia Crossbill was only recently accepted as a valid species (AOS 2017). It is identical to the Red Crossbill except that it has different vocalizations, ecology, and genetics. The flight calls are the most diagnostic vocalization. I am basing my ID on the recordings and the visual characteristics of the flight calls on a spectrogram. 

Resources Used

My primary source is: https://ebird.org/news/recrtype/

I also consulted Nathan Pieplow's (2019) "Peterson Field Guide to Bird Sounds of Western North America".

I sent the recordings to Tim Spahr and Matt Young, the leading experts in crossbills, and they confirmed the ID.

 

Previous Experience

I have no experience with Cassia Crossbill. I have ample experience with many types of Red Crossbill across North America. I am a crossbill afficianado, so I spend a lot of time recording their calls when I can in an effort to identify types. I am very familiar with the Type 2 Red Crossbill and can typically tell when I'm listening to a non-Type 2. That was the case here.
