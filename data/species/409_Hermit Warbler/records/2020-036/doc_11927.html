Description of the Bird

This warbler had a yellow head, except for dark smudgy marks on the cheek and dark gray that extended from the back of head to the crown.  (Forehead was yellow.)  Bill was pointed.   Chin and throat were white, but there was a very faint dusky patch below the throat.  (Because this dusky patch was not seen in all views, I wonder if this appearance was due to dark bases of those feathers.)  Back appeared mostly gray (with perhaps a hint of olive) and with sparse, diffuse streaks.  The bird had gray wings with 2 distinct white wing bars (with the upper appearing wider).  The upperside of the tail was gray with extensive white to the outer tail feathers.  Underparts were white, except there were a few sparse, dark streaks on either side of the chest.   The vent was white.  The absence of a black throat indicated a first-fall bird.  On review of Jim Beatty's photos, the dark centers of the median coverts also supported aging the bird as a first-fall individual.   

 

Similar Species Discussion

When I initially saw the bird, I had the impression of a Hermit Warbler.  Because the bird had smudges on the auricular area, I felt the need to rule out an immature or an adult female Olive Warbler.  In seeing the bird's face well, I realized that an Olive Warbler (of all ages and genders) would have had a more defined auricular patch rather than just smudgy marks on the auricular patch.  A more well-defined auricular patch on an Olive Warbler provides more of a masked appearance.  An adult female Olive Warbler would also have had a yellow chest.  After Jason St. Pierre arrived and questioned me about the lack of a black throat, we studied the bird further to eliminate a Hermit Warbler x Townsend's Warbler.  Since the bird was lacking a yellow chest and well-defined streaking on the sides and flanks, we concluded the bird was not a hybrid.     

Resources Used

 I was just out on a walk near my neighborhood.  I had no references or camera equipment with me.  I was relying on my memory when I identified the bird.  Later in the day, I consulted A Field Guide to Warblers of North America by Jon Dunn and Kimball Garrett to ensure that hybridization with Townsend's Warbler was ruled out.  That reference and The Warbler Guide by Tom Stephenson and Scott Whittle were consulted to see if gender could be determined.  I'm not sure that gender could be determined for this individual.     

Previous Experience

I have seen Hermit Warblers fairly often, mostly in Arizona.  I've also seen them on the Pacific Coast.  I have seen Olive Warblers often, mostly in Arizona, but also in New Mexico.
