Description of the Bird

At about 7:45 am on the morning of 16 April, I flushed up a Hermit Thrush at Crow Valley Campground in Weld County. The bird was nice enough to perch atop a brush pile about 60 feet from me. When I looked at it, its ID was clear enough, pumping its tail and making a "chack" noise. I was a bit surprised at how brown, and fairly warm brown, its back was... this caused me to pause a bit before calling Chris Nunes' attention to the bird. Before doing so, I noted that it had some buffy wash on face and upper chest, and that the flanks were washed in buff as well. The wings and back looked rather uniform, unlike the auduboni-type Hermits I am used to seeing in eastern Washington (and was expecting in CO). I double-checked to make sure the tail really was rusty, and then called to Chris, "Hermit Thrush."

He looked at the bird, and responded, "Cool." He was about 50 feet from me (not certain what his distance from the bird was.) The sun was largely to my back, and the lighting on the bird excellent. I was using Swarovski EL 10x42 binoculars. Chris quickly turned away from the bird to look at some sparrows and apparently did not hear my babblings about the buff wash at various places that were not consistent with Western Interior Hermit Thrushes.

Description
The bird appeared to be within size range of Hermit Thrush, not obviously appearing at the largest or smallest ends of the spectrum for this species. The lack of comparison with other birds made further size distinctions difficult. The wings and back (and presumably crown, though I did not specifically note it) were moderately warm brown, with no hint of gray. I did not look for wingbars and could have missed weak ones. The folded primaries were rustier than remainder of wing, which was briefly noted. The tail was bright rusty, contrasting with back. Rump/uppertail covert color not specifically noted. The tail was held cocked much of the time, pumped intermittently. The eyering was moderately pronounced and white or whitish. The face had a weak buffy wash as did the upper chest. The chest was streaked/spotted in blackish brown, with individual marks seeming somewhat spot like, but loosely forming rows. Below the chest, the streaking/spotting faded into weaker spots, the extent of which was not noted. The flanks were unspotted and washed in a faint, but distinct, buff... not the pure gray I was expecting. The bird flushed, popping up for a couple more brief looks. My initial view was 45-90 seconds. Additional views were 2-3 in number, and 10-15 seconds in length, but at a distance of about 40 feet.

Similar Species Discussion

Identification
In Washington, I am used to scrutinizing Hermit Thrushes, looking for "Dwarf" Hermit Thrush in the e. deserts or Interior Hermit Thrushes on the "wetside." So, I reflexively went to subspecific level upon looking at this bird. I was expecting a bird with a gray back and gray wing-coverts contrasting markedly with a bright rusty tail and somewhat with rust of folded primaries. Plus, I was expecting broadly washed gray sides, with no hint of buff there, or on the face. This bird looked more like "Dwarf" Hermit Thrush, form the Pacific Coast, than like a Western Interior Hermit Thrush-in large part due to the lack of gray hues to the back. The upperparts of this bird, however, appeared warmer brown than the Pacific Coast birds. This bird fits well with Sibley's drawings of "Taiga" Hermit Thrush, though the brown on back seemed brighter than in Sibley's depiction of an adult (the Crow Valley bird's back color, to my estimation, was more like what Sibley shows for 1st year Taiga Hermit.) The buff flanks eliminate the other two subspecies groups, and the brown back eliminates the Interior Mountain group. Also of note, the birds call note was more of a "Chack" and less of the Varied Thrush-like "Chuck" that I am used to from both western taxon-groups.
