Habitat

Montane willow carr along Middle Boulder Creek. Dominated by several species of willow, thinleaf alder, and river birch.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Eagle Optics Ranger, 10X30
Distance:  15 feet.
Light:  Clear and sunny. Sun was over my left shoulder, behind me, as I was looking at the bird.

Description of the Bird

Bird was yellow underneath and green-yellow on upperparts. Black streaks on sides of body. Black lines on sides of neck. Black lines on head; one through the eye and one below the eye, that merged.
Behaviors: Singing, perched near top of birch, alders, and willows. Moved from shrub to shrub, generally staying high and singing.
Call: It was singing all 3 times encountered. The song first lead me to believe that it was a different warbler. I was not familier with the song. After 1st time heard, went home and listened to tapes and my memory matched the song of the prairie warbler. This was reconfirmed during the second and thrid trip.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The song was the first thing to make me think it might be something different. But I realize that is not always fool proff. For example, near this same sight there is a MacGillivray's warbler that sounds very much like a common yellowthroat. But the song I was hearing matched the recording of the prairie warbler.

Yellow warbler has continuous orange streaking across the breast and does not have the black marking on the side of the face.

Wilson's warbler oes not have black on sides of body nor on sides of face.

Townsend's warbler has black throat, white wing-bars.

Canada warbler (which I saw in similar habitat about 1 mile from hear in the 1980s) has grayish upper body, white eye-ring, necklace across neck.

This is the first time I have ever seen or heard a prairie warbler. Have good experience with Wilson's warbler, Townsend's warbler, yellow warbler, and one previous experience with Canada warbler.
