Habitat

Riparian with mature cottonwood and box elder and lots of low dense shrubbery. This habitat is situated within a transition zone between high prairie to the east and ponderosa pine-dominated foothills to the west. There are lots of open meadows with tall grasses, steep slopes with pine, juniper and mountain mahogany. The most striking feature in this area is called the "hogback." It is a spine of rocky sandstone cliffs and canyons that run from Lyons to the north all the way south to Colorado Springs. The bird stayed in the riparian vicinity.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Pentax 10x42 binoculars
Distance:  about 15 to 20 yards, but not measured.
Light:  The sun was high and and slightly east of us. Since we and the bird were in the shade of the creekside foliage, the sun wasn't much of a factor.

Description of the Bird

The bird appeared to be about 4 1/2 inches or so, with a small compact appearance. It was bright yellow on the face with a black eye-line through the eye and a black "comma" or curved line beginning at the eye line and curving down below the eye. The bill was dark and narrow in typical warbler fashion. Its throat, breast and belly were also bright yellow with very pronounced black streaking (broken lines) along the edges of the breast and flanks. Wing and upper parts were dull olive/yellow. I don't recall distinctive wingbars, but what bars were there appeared to be pale double bars in buff or very light yellow. I have seen the similar Pine Warbler several times, and this bird was noticeably smaller with more brightly-colored plumage, and the black markings much more highly contrasting.
Behaviors: The bird was in a large mature cottonwood tree between a large open meadow and a creek with riparian understory and mature cottonwood and box elder. He was very animated and quick in his movements, hopping or flying from branch to branch and foraging for insects among the newly unfurled spring leaves. (Spring is late here in Colorado). He worked this one tree for at least a couple of minutes, so I got several brief, but good looks at him when he wasn't disappearing among the leaves. Although I had my camera with a 400mm lense, I could never quite get a good focused shot off due to his quickness and the abundant foliage. He finally flew away to another thicket much farther away. I was never able to find it again. This prairie warbler was seen roughly at the same time my birding partner and I also spotted the unusual Black-throated Blue Warbler (which I did manage to get pictures of).
Call: The bird was not vocalizing at the time.
Plumage: Bright yellow with black markings

Similar Species Discussion

Pine Warbler (observed several times) is noticeably larger and less brightly colored. The streaking is also much more subdued. It also has pale whitish lower underparts that the Prairie Warbler lacked. The common warblers I see around here are Yellow, Wilson's, Orange-crowned, Virginia's, Common Yellowthroat, Audubon's & Myrtle, McGillivray's, and none of these birds even remotely resemble what I saw.
