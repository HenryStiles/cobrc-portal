Description of the Bird

A small warbler actively feeding in the willows along the creek.  The bird was observed from a footbridge that crosses the creek.  The head was olive green towards the front and then turned to an olive green/grayish in color down the back of the head and continuing to the nape, back and rump. The supercillium was a light yellow/whitish color creating a type of crescent over the eye and extending in front to the base of the bill.  There was also an inverted crescent to the upper one in the auricular area of the same color.  The olive green coloring bisected the crescents creating the appearance of a line through the eye.  The bill was pointed short gray/black color.  The throat was yellow and extended down through the chest to the belly.  There was a dark blackish gray smudge spot on the side of the neck.  Extending down the flanks from that spot were two lines of not well defined, or blurred, blackish gray.  The wings were a dark gray with an olive tinge and the feathers had an edging similar in color to the crescents around the eye.  The the ends of the median and greater covers also had this lighter coloration giving the slight appearance of wing bars.  The upper tail was dark gray with white tips on the outer couple of feathers.  The undertail coverts and underside of the tail were white.  The legs were black.  I do not recall any calls or chips during the observation but that could have been drowned out by the excited whispering going on between the observers.

This would be a first county record for this species.

Similar Species Discussion

Keying on the obvious lines along the flanks: a female Cape May has a similar crescent over the eye but the under crescent is much larger outlining the entire auricular area.  It is also grayer in color and white edging on the feathers; a female Magnolia has stronger lines, a gray head and white on the coverts;  a female Pine has olive streaks, smaller better defined crescents and is more whitish yellow than yellow from the throat down to the belly.

Resources Used

There was concensus between Gene and myself immediately upon observation that the bird was a female Prairie Warbler.  As I recall, Phil pulled up Prairie Warbler on his phone after the bird had moved on and the plumage of the bird observed was discussed relative to the image on his phone.

Previous Experience

I have previously observed a female Prairie Warbler in Colorado.  More recently, two weeks before this observation I was birding south of Nashville with local birders and observed Prairie Warblers of both sexes.
