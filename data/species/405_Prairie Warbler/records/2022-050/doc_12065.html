Description of the Bird

Heard singing regularly between 6:40am and 7:00am from open habitat southwest of the Audubon center parking lot very soon after getting out of car. Series of rapid “zeets”, increasing in speed and rising upwards toward the end (sound recorded). Only got one distant look at the bird, so couldn’t make out much detail, but I did see a small, entirely yellow warbler with dark streaking on flanks, ruling out Yellow Warbler. Went silent around 7:00am and as unable to relocate through 7:30, when I had to leave.

In the sound recording uploaded to this report, the bird's song is a little faint, so volume should be turned up high. One bout of song is heard between seconds 3 and 4.5. The recording has also been uploaded to eBird, which shows a spectogram of the song, here: https://macaulaylibrary.org/asset/449800471.

Similar Species Discussion

Yellow Warbler (YEWA)-- While the song I heard would've been highly atypical for a YEWA, I have once heard YEWA sing a pretty decent Prairie Warbler (PRAW) song in the Lower Colorado River Valley in Arizona. However, the dark streaking on the flanks rules out this species.

Townsend's Warbler (TOWA)-- The Sibley app contains a recording of TOWA that is similar to PRAW. However, the ending of TOWA song is usually a little more "jumbled". The recording on the Sibley app does have an ascending quality but then seems to slow down or level off toward the end. This is unlike the song I heard which seemed to asscend upward and speed up at the end without leveling off. Adult male TOWAs (assumed age/sex based on time of year and singing behavior) do have a yellow breast and dark streaks on the flanks; however, they also have a white belly, dull oliver/grayish upper parts (i.e., not entirely yellow), black throat and cap, and dark cheek patch. While the view I got of the bird was brief and distant, the facial patterns of a TOWA would have stood out pretty clearly and there would've been more contrast between the upper and lower portions of the bird. I was trying very hard to look for the black semicircle under the eye that is typical of PRAW so was really focusing on the face; however, I was struggling to see even that small black mark at the distance I was at (i.e., face mainly just looked entirely yellow. Was maybe seeing that dark semicircle, but couldn't be positive).

Other warbler species with at least some yellow lower parts and dark flank streaking include Kirtland's, Cape May, Magnolia, Pine, Yellow-throated, and Grace's. However, none of these species have songs similar to PRAW, nor are they entirely yellow. There is generally more contrast between upper and lower parts for these species, with the exception of Pine. However Pine Warbler streaking isn't usually as bold as what I saw on the bird, plus habitat was perfect for PRAW and not so much for Pine (though I realize habitat isn't always a good indicator of species during migration).

Resources Used

Have experience with this species so instantly recognized the song and knew what to look for when trying to view. Also consulted Sibley App to confirm and attempt to rule out other species.

Previous Experience

I grew up in NJ and went to college in Vermont, so have become very familiar with eastern warblers by sight and sound. I have seen PRAW and most other eastern warblers many times while living on the east coast and during multiple visits back east. I have also lived in the western US since 2010 so have become familiar with western warbler species and have seen many of the similar species I listed on multiple occasions. YEWA are abundant at the right time of year in almost all places I've lived, and TOWA are pretty common in a few places I've lived, including Arizona and California. I've only seen Kirtland's Warbler once, but 1) it's really not all that similar to PRAW other than having some yellow bellow and dark streaks, and 2) that would be an incredibly good sighting for Colorado (way better than PRAW). Also, despite living out west for the last 12 years, I feel that my eastern warbler song ID is a little bit better than my western, with PRAW being one of my favorite songs (and one of the easiest to identify in my opinion).

Furthermore, this is the second PRAW I've seen in Colorado, the first being a bird I found at Chatfield State Park in the fall (though that bird was not singing and was in a duller plumage).
