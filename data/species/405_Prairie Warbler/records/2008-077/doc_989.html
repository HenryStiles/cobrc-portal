Habitat

In dense, low willows along a muddy lakeshore. Tall cottonwoods immediately behind. The bird stuck to the willows. During my 3rd observation (heard only) of what I assume was the Prairie Warbler, the bird had moved 250 feet west, but it was in identical habitat--dense willows along the lakeshore.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Nikon 10X premier binoculars
Distance:  15-50 feet away.
Light:  Good light conditions, with uniform cloud cover causing uniform dispersion of light. Light drizzle was falling during the 1st and 2nd observations, but my binoculars and eyeglasses were clear. More problematic was the effect of the drizzle on the bird; it was somewhat bedraggled-looking in the rain, and might have been a little crisper, had it not been raining. (My uncertainty about the bird's plumage--see elsewhere--might have to do with the bird's rain-induced appearance.)

Description of the Bird

Here is the unedited description that I posted to CoBirds on the date of observation:

I first saw the Prairie Warbler in the mist/drizzle earlier today. It was foraging low in the willows along the north shore of Waneka Lake, and I was right away struck by its small size, active demeanor, bright yellow underparts, and olive upperparts. My immediate guess was Blue-winged Warbler, but then I put my binoculars on the bird, and saw its muted, but distinctive facial pattern: olive-green cap, broad yellow supercilium, thin brown trans-ocular line, and broad yellow subocular patch bordered below with brown.

The bird had a long tail with large white tail spots, and it twitched its tail frequently. Its wing-bars were indistinct. Head on, the bird had an unmarked yellow breast with fine black streaks along the sides. The bird's flight call was a thin, pure-tone seet. After the bird had disappeared from view, I heard what I believe was its call note: a repeated, musical chep. I briefly saw the bird about 1 hour after the original observation, and I believe I heard its call note again shortly thereafter. I think it was a male, although a rather muted one--maybe a one-year-old with some retained formative plumage?

Before consulting Andrews & Righter, by the way, I would have guessed that spring records of Prairie Warbler would have been earlier than today's bird, but that's not the case: 3 of the 5 records are later than May 20th, including both Boulder County records given by A&R (May 30-31 May 1975 and 5-6 June 1982).

Similar Species Discussion

Oddly enough, the only bird that I think it could have resembled is a first-cycle female Townsend's Warbler, especially one with hybrid genes. I have never heard of these species treated as confusion-species, but I do think there is possibility of confusion! For example, see Hybrid (e) on Pl. 14 of the Dunn & Garrett warbler guide. However, there are many points of distinction for the Prairie Warbler that I saw:

1. Exact facial pattern, especially dark trans-ocular with yellow subocular patch below.
2. Indistinct (almost lacking) wing-bars.
3. Small size. (Prairie is, I believe, the smallest Dendroica.)
4. Relatively long tail.
5. Active foraging demeanor, low down in shrubbery.
6. Distinctive tail-twitching behavior.

Resources Used

I used none at the time of the observation. After the time of observation, I read through the description in Dunn & Garrett's warbler guide, basically just as a refresher. I also carefully studied the description in D&G of Townsend's Warbler and "HeTo" hybrids. I also consulted the Pyle Guide for molt information.

For status & distribution, I checked out Andrews & Righter (see relevant comments under "Description," above).

For vocalizations (flight call and call note), I consulted online recordings and sonograms; these didn't alter my in-the-field assessments of what I was hearing, but rather served to confirm my in-the-field assessments.

Previous Experience

I lived for many years in Pennsylvania (and elsewhere in the East), where I have seen and heard many hundreds (probably thousands) of Prairie Warblers. I also have extensive experience with the species that are classically thought to be similar (Magnolia, Palm), and I have extensive experience with Townsend's and even hybrid "HeTo" warblers (see above).

I have experience with one other extralimital Prairie Warbler--seen briefly at Percha State Park, New Mexico in the early 1990s. I submitted a description to the state records committee, and I do not know what decision was arrived at by the committee.
