Description of the Bird

HY (male?). This bird was a surprise for its rarity and on the early end of the spectrum for a fall migrant. It appeared in Gambel oaks and provided excellent views as it foraged along the outer edge of the oak patch more or less at eye level, and was in view for about 6 minutes. I took a series of photos with my iPhone. https://www.flickr.com/photos/135558325@N07/? Sadly, they are lousy images, but in studying them I can discern several features that support the identification. My initial glimpse of the bird got me onto the ID right off based on: (1) the unique face pattern that was like a subtle gray ghost pattern of the adult male, (2) diffuse dusky streaks on the sides and flanks, (3) the extensive yellow underparts becomeing pale whitish yellow on the undertail coverts, (4) the yellowish wing bars, and (5) frequent tail pumping and flicking showing a lot of white. Over the several minutes of observation I was able to study it in detail between trying to get some photos with the iPhone. This was a small warbler with a proportionately long tail. The head was pale grayish with some yellow tones on the crown and nape, and grayest on the auriculars. A short whitish supercilium was tinged slightly with pale yellow and began over the lores and extended back to just behind the eye, arcing down a bit there to meet a fine dusky transocular line that ran from the lores to just a short way posterior to the eye until it there blended into the gray of the auriculars. Below the eye there was a relatively broad and distinctive semicircular arc of whitish tinged slightly with yellow, bordered below by a dusky gray moustacial line that extended along the malar area, wrapping under the broad pale eye arc to connect to the gray auriculars. This pattern was like a ghost of the pattern of the adult male which shows yellow (whitish on this young bird) and blackish (dusky to pale gray on this young bird). This pattern itself was distinctive and eliminated everthing else. The back and wings were a dull greenish-grayish yellowish, with two diffuse yellowish wingbars that did not contrast strongly with the rest of the wing. The posterior wingbar was more yellow than the paler whitish-yellow anterior bar. The flight feathers were somewhat more dusky. The rump was greenish. I did not see any streaks on the back. The underparts were fairly bright yellow with some indistinct dusky streaks on the sides of the breast extending back onto the flanks. There was a subtle gray protrusion or bar coming onto the top of the sides at the bend of the wing, a feature also distinctive for the species. The dusky streaks flowed back from this protrusion of gray. The yellow underparts became whitish yellow on the undertail coverts. The tail was dark with extensive white outer rectices. The bill and legs were dusky or blackish. The bill appeared small. No calls were noted. The bird was actively foraging in Gambel oaks, often near the outer surface of the crown or down within the crowns about a foot. These oaks were essentially large shrubs 5-10 feet tall, so the bird was often just 3-7 feet off the ground. As it foraged It frequently pumped its tail and flashed the white of the outer tail feathers as the tail was flicked or maybe spread quickly.

Similar Species Discussion

Yellow Warbler - lacks distinctive face pattern of Prairie, has yellowish eyering, streaks do not extend onto flanks, and is yellow in the undertail and the tail is yellow lacking white. Does not pump tail in same fashion.

Imm. Magnolia Warbler has different pattern of white in tail lacks patterned face and had narrow band of gray across breast. 

Pine Warbler - unexpected on date, I think. Different and much more subdued face patternlacking broad arc below eye, I think more obvious wingbars, somehwat larger size and larger bill.


I don't think there is really anything else to consider given the marks that were noted.
