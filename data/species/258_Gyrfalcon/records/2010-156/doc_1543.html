Habitat

Wide open, intermontane park; grasslands with some distant (1 mile away) pines and spruces to the west. The bird seemed partial to the crossbars of the power/telephone poles in the area (there are a number of these).

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Brunton Epoch 8.4x44mm binolculars
Distance:  ~60 feet (estimated)
Light:  The bird was found about 30 minutes before local sunrise (behind me as I looked at the bird). The bird flew about 5 minutes before local sunrise. By then the light was evenly spread, but sunlight was not directly on the bird.

Description of the Bird

The bird's size was just about the size of a Rough-legged Hawk (Buteo lagopus). I had just seen a juvinile Rough-legged Hawk about two mile back. Upperparts of crown, nape, back, wings and tail were all very dark brown. Underparts dirty white with very bold, dark brown streaking starting just below the white throat and extending down to about the belly. The bill looked grayish, but the light was still fairly poor. Short, hooked beak with dark tip, the color of the cere, gape and eye-ring could not be easily determined in the low light. The bird displayed a distinct, buff supercillium and a fairly wide-looking, dark brown moustachial mark (not as wide as a Peregrine's). Chin and throat white. The tail was dark brown and fairly long, extending much farther than the folded wings and could be easily seen extending below the phone pole crossbar. Upper wings: The primary and secondary wing coverts were dark brown with very narrow pale buff edges. In flight the upper wings were even dark brown with no discernable color variation between the back, primaries, secondaries, wing coverts and tail. Under wings: The primaries and secondaries were pale white with darker terminal edge giving the bird's wings a darker trailing edge. The under wing coverts were again fairly boldly patterned or streaked with brown and noticeably darker than the base of the primaries and secondaries. I did not notice any pattern to the dark brown tail, but most of the tail was obscured by the crossbar and again the light was still poor.
Behaviors: Large dark falcon with a long tailed perched upon a telephone pole crossbar. I passed this bird, its back to me, doing 65mph and thought, "That's a long tail on a DARK bird....GYR!" I drove a short distance further before turning around so as not to alarm the bird. I was able to pull up on the east side of the highway almost next to the telephone pole (pole was on west side of the highway). I watched the bird sit here for 25 minutes. About 6:50am the bird starting moving its head side to side and up and down, almost convulsive like, and it regurgitated a pellet! Several cars were passing by and slowing down for some cow elk crossing the highway about a quarter mile further north. I think the bird may have been a little nervous about this and it took off to the west flying with stiff, shallow wingbeats. The bird dropped from the crossbar about 5 minutes before local sunrise and flew VERY low over the landscape and at the last second "shot up" and landed on another phone pole crossbar on a second set of phone lines running parallel to the highway's phone lines about a quarter mile to the west. At this point I left to call/text a few people and requested that Glenn Walbek post the sighting to COBIRDS. Two and a half hours later Joe Roller, Glenn Walbek, Loch Kilpatrick, Tim Smart, Maggie Bosewell and a friend arrived and we searched for the bird for about 3 hours without success.
Call: No vocalizations were heard.
Plumage: Juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

American Kestrel & Merlin - Much too small. this falcon was similar in size to a Rough-legged Hawk.

Prairie Falcon - Noticeably smaller, medium to light brown back (not dark brown); streaking (juv) and spots (adult) are not as dense; underwing displays black axillaries; rest of under wing pale without discernibly darker underwing coverts and not trailing edge to the underwing.

Peregrine Falcon - Noticeable smaller; adults with medium brown or medium gray upperparts (not dark brown) and Peregrines have a distinct "helmet" that his bird lacked. Underparts are mostly barred and not heavily streaked. Juviniles with medium brown wings (not dark brown) and depending upon race, either a distinct helmet or large moustachial mark (similar to this bird). Underparts typically buff rather that white. Tail not near as long compared to wing length.

Previous Experience

I have only seen one Gyrfalcon prior to this sighting. Chris Wood and I relocated a gray-morph adult seen on 01/07/1995 in Colorado near Union Reservoir near the Weld/Larimer County line.

I have seen MANY Peregrine & Prairie Falcons as well an NUMEROUS Kestrels and Merlins in my 26 years of birding in Colorado and throughout the country.
