Description of the Bird

Large raptor, generally about the size of Red-tailed Hawk, observed in flight about 25 feet above ground, flying with strong wingbeats in a fairly straight line, while being harrased by a Belted Kingfisher. Size was very much larger than the Kingfisher, perhaps 10:1. Wings were long (longer than accipiter and most buteos; about the relative length to the body as for Ferruginous Hawk), and more pointed than Buteo wings. The only plumage details that struck me during my brief observation was 1. Overall concolorous plumage, generally a cold buff coloration, or light brown, or light gray-brown, and 2. Distinct, crisp, fine black barring on underwing coverts. No obvious patches of black feathers or white feathers were observed. The head was relatively large (like a buteo or large falcon) but otherwise unremarkable; The tail was not observed well and presumed to have been unremarkable.
Behaviors: Bird flew briskly and directed (i.e. in a straight line) with powerful but fairly slow, irregular wingbeats, unlike the "flap-flap-glide" of an accipiter, and unlike constant, regular flapping of Prairie or Peregrine Falcon. It was first flying toward us more or less in parallel with the road we were standing on, then banked and flew perpendicular to the road, flying through a fairly narrow strip of forrest below the treetop level, but probably continued flying over a large unforested clearing (snow-covered meadow).
Call: unknown
Plumage: immature

Similar Species Discussion

Peregrine Falcon always has distinct black hooded appearance, and patches of bright white on underparts. Prairie Falcon has obvious black axilars. Both these falcons have quicker wingbeats and are smaller relative to the Kingfisher. Northern Goshawk has rounded wings, bright white eyeline and undertail coverts. Buteos have more rounded wings. Northern Harrier has more noticeable long tail and buoyant flight style. None of these similar species have the concolorous pale buff appearance with crisp black markings on underwing. No other falcon from other continents would match the large size of this bird.

Previous Experience

I have seen Gyrfalcons in the wild on two previous occasions, both in Colorado. The first (in 1998) was never seen in flight. The second was seen at a much greater distance flying low over the ground (possibly hunting) with continuous, quicker wingbeats on 5 Nov 2006 in Jackson County (see additional comment below).
