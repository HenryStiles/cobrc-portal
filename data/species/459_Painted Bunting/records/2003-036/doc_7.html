Description of the Bird

Stephanie and I were camping in Cottonwood Canyon so that I could work on revising the ABA birdfinding guide for Colorado. After waking up on the 25th I soon heard a bird singing that I believed was a Painted Bunting (having heard singing Painted Buntings less than 10 days earlier). I soon spotted the bird and there was no doubt that it was a Painted Bunting. Several included images accurately reflect the plumage. While Painted Bunting occurrences in some parts of the world (e.g. California) are often treated as suspect (particularly those of adult males), those in Colorado have never faced much scrutiny. In this case, the bird showed no signs of captivity and it is much more likely that this species is slowly continuing its spread to the north, than that this bird escaped and ended up in this rather off-the-beaten-path locale. This bird moved around quite a bit (at least 800 meters) and could have been responsible for reports of multiple birds present. I feel quite confident that on the 25th and 26th there was only one male Painted Bunting. It is possible there was a sneaky female around, but this bird certainly never interacted with one.
Behaviors: Bird was perched in a tree.
Call: The song was similar to that of Indigo Bunting but not containing the paired notes typical of that species. In that respect the song was more like Lazuli Bunting, but without the thinner and higher quality of that species. It sounded somewhat like an Indigo Bunting singing the rollicking song of a Blue Grosbeak (but without the husky quality of the grosbeak).
Plumage: adult

Previous Experience

I have seen and studied several hundred Painted Buntings (mostly females and immatures). Most of these have been in Mexico, but I have also seen them in Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, Arkansas, Missouri and Florida. This was my first (FINALLY!) in Colorado.
