Habitat

The bird was singing from an aspen grove in an area that is a patchwork of aspen groves, stands of Gambel Oak, and grassy mountain meadows, with occasional stands of Ponderosa Pine in the area.

Description of the Bird

The following is a verbatim excerpt from my original paper writeup, dated March 19, 2003:


We never saw the bird. (We never even looked for it, not really realizing the significance of the bird at the time). We only heard it singing a constant che-BEK! from the aspens about 50 feet from the road. We listened to it for maybe a full minute before driving on. The bird vocalized incessantly during that time.
Behaviors: The bird called incessantly without much in the way of pauses.
Call: A constant, dry, two-syllabled snappy "che-BEK."
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

The following is a verbatim excerpt from my original paper writeup, dated March 19, 2003:


Separation from similar species:
Obviously, separation was entirely on the basis of sound. I cannot think of any other bird likely to make an incessant dry CHE-BEK!
Just for argument’s sake, there are certain elements of Hammond’s and Dusky Flycatcher songs that are somewhat similar, but they would never be isolated from the other song elements and continually repeated. (We heard both of these species numerous times that same morning).
I suppose House Sparrow could sound somewhat similar, but this bird had a much drier and more two-syllabled call. Furthermore, it was calling from an aspen stand beside a grassy meadow, miles from any human habitation (i.e. House Sparrow habitat).

Previous Experience

After I started birding in South Dakota, Least Flycatcher was the first Empid I learned to ID, and its call was one of the familiar sounds of spring migration there. The species was abundant and vocal, so that I practically have its call hard-wired into my brain.
Ditto for House Sparrow. :)
