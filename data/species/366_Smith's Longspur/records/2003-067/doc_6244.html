Description of the Bird

This document should be considered additional material to support recirculation of 2003-67.

Fall-migration timing in Smith's Longspur:
Various publications were referenced in previous circulations of this report (particularly the books on the avifauna of South Dakota and Nebraska) to define the fall-migration timing in the Lower 48 states as occurring from late September on, thus calling into question the ID of these birds on 11 September.  However, much of the area hosting the fall migration of the species is lightly (even very lightly) birded, such that any generalizations of temporal and geographic occurrence must be used cautiously.  In fact, there are numerous records of Smith's Longspur from the Lower 48 during the first half of September that have been accepted by relevant BRCs and/or eBird reviewers.  While canvassing all of the relevant BRCs is a daunting task, canvassing eBird data is simple and quick (http://ebird.org/ebird/map/smilon?neg=true&env.minX=124.1709490716355&env.minY=17.39075742630653&env.maxX=4.288136571635505&env.maxY=75.07251815471734&zh=true&gp=false&ev=Z&mr=on&bmo=9&emo=9&yr=all&byr=1900&eyr=2015).  Among the eBird data are the following records from the Lower 48 from the first half of September:

Prebasic (PB) and preformative (PF) molts in Smith's Longspur:
The other argument advanced by members of the CBRC voting to reject the report in previous circulations was that early-September Smith's Longspurs would still be in alternate plumage, a matter easily checked by referring to Pyle (1997; though terminology in that publication is outdated).  In that eminent reference, Pyle states:

"Molt--HY partial (Jul-Sep), AHY complete (Jul-Sep)....  The PBs probably occur on the summer grounds, although the 1st PB [modern terminology has this as the PF] may be completed [italics mine] on the winter grounds; more study is needed."

Thus, the summer/fall molts of the species are completed by sometime in September.  Additionally, high-latitude passerine breeders typically do not make many (or any) subsequent nest attempts after a first-nest failure.  That is because there is little suitable time in which to shoehorn both breeding and molting before departing on fall migration.  Thus, Smith's Longspurs that failed at breeding can be expected to initiate and complete their molt sooner than successful breeders, allowing them to initiate southbound migration earlier, escaping from deteriorating foraging conditions on their arctic-tundra breeding grounds.  These various facets, then, suggest that adults that were unsuccessful at breeding in a given summer are, perhaps, more likely to occur early in the fall-migration season south of the breeding grounds.  Regardless if that is accurate or not, southbound Smith's Longspur migrants are in basic (adults) or formative (immatures) plumage before they initiate fall migration.

I heartily recommend use of eBird and reference to Pyle (1997, 2008) when considering various facets of bird biology in reference to Colorado occurrence patterns of species on the state's review list.
