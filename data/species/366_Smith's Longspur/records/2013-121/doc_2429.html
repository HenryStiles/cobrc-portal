Description of the Bird

This bird was noticed for a brief period as it stood on a paved road. The structure of the bird was that of a sparrow-like passerine with a long-tailed, and not particularly chunky, profile. The bill was not of a particularly noteworthy shape or size like a grosbeak or blackbird and no significant head pattern (e.g. like a breeding male Smith's Longspur) was noted. The size of the bird was smaller than the numerous Horned Larks in the vicinity.

The field mark that stood out prominently was that the whole of the under parts, breast and belly, was strongly colored in a shade of mustard or saffron. This color was neither the bright yellow of a meadowlark breast nor a beige or sandy color. In addition the breast and flanks were not marked with strongly noticeable streaking. The under parts of the bird were lighter and brighter than the upper parts that appeared generally a darker brown. No wing bars were noted.

Shortly after I noticed the bird, it took off and flew past and behind us. In flight it displayed distinct white outer tail feathers that were prominent and broad enough to be two feathers wide on each side and contrasted with feathers of a dark central tail.
Behaviors: The bird stood still (i.e. not tail wagging or pumping like a pipit) on a wet paved road, then it took off and flew strongly past us and to our rear. It flew well off the ground, perhaps 20 or 30 feet or more in the air, and it flew for several hundred yards.
Call: NA
Plumage: adult female or male molting from winter

Similar Species Discussion

White outer tail feathers rule out most other small to medium sized passerines that might have a roughly similar body color like Brambling, Henslow's Sparrow, Dickcissel, White-collared Seedeater or Bobolink. Lark and Vesper Sparrow have white outer tail feathers but have under parts that would be either white or pale brown and streaky. Pipits would have been tail-wagging, have shown more strong streaking especially on flanks and breast and would be a dingier color. Horned Larks would also have pale tan to white bodies and would have a noticeable face pattern. Behavior (strong flight pattern) was not like a warbler or flycatcher.

Of the other three longspurs, none would show the bright body color of this bird. In addition the tail pattern was totally wrong for McCown's and Chestnut-collared. Lapland Longspur would show more streaks and thinner white on the tail in flight.
