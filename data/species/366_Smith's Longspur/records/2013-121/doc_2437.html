Description of the Bird

I did not see this bird as well as Bill Kaempfer. I got onto it just as it was taking off. The bird was in a flock of Horned Larks, McCown's Longspurs and Chestnut-collared Longspurs. It was the same size and shape as the other Longspurs. It appeared to be of mostly uniform plumage, that I would describe as straw colored with not very distinguishing marks.
However, the tail had white outer tail feathers: I believe there were two each side as the white was very clearly visible. The tail pattern and uniform color was in contrast to the other longspurs present. Later I saw a Lapland Longspur, which was clearly patterned with rufous in the wing, some black in the throat, and a white belly with obvious side streaks.
Behaviors: In a roadside with Horned Larks and other Longspurs when deep snow covered the fields. It took off from in front of of me, flew to my side and then went for several hundred yards behind me.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Not breeding male

Similar Species Discussion

McCown's and Chestnut-collared Longspurs. All seen had some amount of black on their breasts, and their pattern of white in the tail was very different from this bird.
Lapland Longspur. Has some rufous in the wing, some black in the throat, and white belly with side streaking. This bird appeared to have more white in tail than a Lapland, but this is subjective.
Vesper Sparrow: One seen earlier that afternoon.
Has breast streaking and a white belly, which contrasts with the darker back. The bird I saw was quite uniform in color with little contrast.
American Pipit: Also shows contrast between a dark back and a lighter belly with streaking.
Juncos: All have too much contrast for this bird.

Resources Used

National Geographic and Sibley field guides.

Previous Experience

Have much experience with all these species except Smith's Longspur. I have only seen it in breeding plumage at Churchill, Manitoba, and along the Denali Highway in Alaska.
