Description of the Bird

Very rare: first detected by rattle call as it flew up from below path (apparently) heading up towards peak above Medicine Bow; call fairly reminiscent of LALO but to my ear was slightly more liquid; I immediately recognized as Longspur due to its relative similarity to LALO in vocalization; no other calls given and only heard twice; can CCLO make a similar call? (I have not heard similar call from them or found recordings suggesting that;

I watched the bird fly up by me in my binoculars; struck me immediately as a medium sized sparrow like bird; undulating flight; as it flew past I had my first good look at the tail which showed an obvious white outer edge and a dark central tail; the white, while obvious, was not extensive throughout tail; the one useable image seemed to confirm that white was restricted to the two outer tail feathers but a difficult field mark to confirm;

I could see obvious wing bars with a particularly obvious upper white wing bar (lesser coverts) but this was more obvious in the one usable photo I obtained; I'm not entirely sure this feature can't be seen in CCLO?;

the bird had an overall buffy and warmly colored appearance, especially below; the chest had some fine streaking which appeared to sparsely (at least) cross the breast; I have no prior field experience with SMLO; ID largely helped by single photo but field impression and audio support the photo; additional opinions very much appreciated from those with more experience;

the bird did not seem to be lingering and I visited the spot twice after this with no luck; it was in view for about 3 minutes; landed briefly twice but only for a short time and always out of view in grasses; last seen flying high over highest point above Medicine Bow trail headed east;

Similar Species Discussion

Other longspurs considered; McCown's and Lapland eliminated by prominant broad white upper wing bar; CCLO more difficult to eliminate but the more limited amount of white restricted to outer tail feathers (rather than a broader wedge of white) seemed to suggest Smith's more strongly.  Call was also much more closely aligned with Smith's.  I have not heard CCLO give a similar call but I am  not certain that the do not have a similar call?; I felt that the warm buffiness and fine streaking to the underparts was also more indicative of Smith's rather than CCLO.
