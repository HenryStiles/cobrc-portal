Description of the Bird

the bird was a sparrow sized bird, about the size or slightly larger than Vesper Sparrows seen before and after the observation. The overall color was brownish, with darker brown colored wings with two bold white wingbars. Below the bird was buff brownish colored. The tail pattern was seen very well, and was brown with two completey white outer tail feathers in a pattern very similar to a Vesper Sparrow.

The clincher in the ID was the call heard, which was a fairly long, dry rattle, with the last few notes dropping in pitch.
Behaviors: The bird was flushed from the ground numerous times, and would fly a certain distance fairly low to the ground before landing again. We were unable to ever see the bird on the ground.

Similar Species Discussion

Most species of sparrow were ruled out by the white in the outer tail feathers, in addition to the longspur-like call. Vesper Sparrow was ruled out by the darker and buffier coloration, especially below, by the bold white wingbars, and especially by the call.

McCown's Longspur was ruled out by the darker and buffier coloration, by the very different tail pattern with much less white, and by the longer rattle call with the descending final notes.

Chestnut-collared Longspur was ruled out by the bold white wingbars, by the different tail pattern with white restriced to the two outer tail feathers and the rest of the tail brown, and by the longer and drier rattle call with descending final notes.

Lapland Longspur was ruled out by the different tail pattern, with two tail feathers distinctly all white instead of only one all white and some white on the second, by the buffier coloration on the underparts, and by the different long rattle call, with distinctly descending notes at the end.

Previous Experience

I have seen a few Smith's Longspurs previously, some in non-breeding plumage in Kansas and one heard in Colorado, and a couple in breeding plumage in Illinois. I have seen many of all the similar species and am familiar with the flight calls of all of them.
