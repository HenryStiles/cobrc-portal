Description of the Bird

I heard a call that I did not recognize.  That call was given three times and then the bird gave a longspur rattle — long (6-7 notes) and somewhat mechanical like that of LALO. I exclaimed in my head at that point, as the first call was not something that I’d heard from LALO and the rattle sounded higher-pitched and more twittery than typical of LALO.  The bird(s?) gave 2-3 more of the first call and then rattled twice more, confirming both the distinctness of the first call from my understanding of LALO calls and the slightly different quality of the rattle.  The first call was a short, somewhat-musical, plaintive, down-slurred call.  It differs from a somewhat-similar LALO call in a few respects, most notably in that it was not obviously two-noted, definitely sliding downward into the second pitch.  Visually, the two birds flying by me (from which the calls originated) were horribly backlit, but had the round-headed, shortish-tailed, deep-bellied appearance of longspurs (as differing from the long, svelte, angle-headed look of HOLA).  I watched the two land about 150m S of me just off the road in wheat stubble.  Unfortunately, I could not find them when I got down there.  I report both seen birds as SMLO, though I have no certainty that both were of the same species.  However, no other calls emanated from the two, they were widely separated in space from other birds (larks, other longspurs) flying in the area, and they were quite close to each other.  Unfortunately, I did not have time to bring my camera to bear on this close approach (<30m).  I heard numerous LALO calls (both the ‘tu’ call and the rattle) subsequently, so was able to further confirm the different quality of the SMLO rattle.  I spent much of the rest of the time here endeavoring to get pix and audio of longspurs (there was a decent W-bound flight going on), but I heard no further SMLO calls, and the only longspurs that I photographed were, I believe, all LALOs.  The sound-descriptive notes here were entered shortly after I heard the SMLO calls, while the more-general notes (and organization) were entered much later.  The birds were somewhat more than a mile N of Hwy 96.

Similar Species Discussion

The only really similar birds are the other longspurs. CCLO is ruled out by the long rattle, as is MCLO, though that species' longest rattle approaches the length of the longest rattle of the two arctic-breeding longspur species. MCLO is also ruled out by the first call heard, as I do not believe that the species produces anything like it. LALO is a close contender, both in rattle length and because there were a lot of LALO around shortly after hearing and seeing these two birds. My entire ID of the birds as SMLO rests solely on vocal evidence, particularly the seemingly distinctive down-slurred vocalization that I heard well 5-6 times.

Resources Used

After I lost the birds and wrote my initial notes about the two call types that I heard (provided in description), I checked my Cornell Guide to Bird Sounds: Master Set to hunt down the vocalization that I'd not heard previously. I found a good match for it there and the same source had no example of a LALO vocalization that was particularly close. I also checked the eBird/Macaulay audio archive and found an exact match to the vocalization that I heard (https://macaulaylibrary.org/asset/131481#_ga=2.201554385.1999829048.1540758378-744088459.1446400897).

Previous Experience

I have knowingly seen only three individual SMLOs previously, 1 in Alabama, 2 at Barr Lake.  All were in basic plumage.  I heard the Barr Lake birds give the rattle call on multiple occasions, but that has been more than a decade.  I have extensive experience with all other longspur species at all seasons and with the most-common small bird found in ag:  HOLA, having seen/heard >3500 CCLO, >2500 MCLO, >15,000 LALO, and >500,000 HOLA.
