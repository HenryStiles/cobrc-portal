Description of the Bird

On 12 May, after finding a Mexican Duck with Ted Floyd, I was birding the gravel pits near Firestone. Another startlingly dark Mallardoid duck appeared in view. This time, the distances were not quite as far (200-300m) and the lighting was considerably brighter (though no actual sunlight). I watched the bird for about 15 minutes before it walked up onto the grass, and sat down, largely out-of-view. I observed the bird through a Kowa 88mm telescope, with a 20-60x eyepiece. Digiscoping was done using a Nikon D300 body and Sigma 50-500 lens, with lens between 50 and 100mm (scope was at 20x).

Description
The bird was quite similar to the Boulder bird. The body was uniformly dark, without a darker chest or paler vent/undertail coverts. The line on the neck demarcating pale vs dark regions was sharp (this line of demarcation appears higher in the photos of this bird than it did (most of the time) in life, or on the Boulder bird, likely because of the alert posture in photos) The tail was grayish brown overall, similar in darkness to the body, but also having narrow dusky grayish edges to the outer tail feathers. The apparent dark chin in the photos is due to shadowing. As with the Boulder bird, the legs were bright orange-red.

Notable differences between Firestone and Boulder birds include (on the Firestone bird) the absence of a silvery or gray patch on tertials and presence of narrower white borders to the purplish-blue speculum. The rear border was quite thin, and the anterior border was distinctly thinner than that of a Mallard. Also, this bird, though apparently a male, was a bit smaller than the male Mallards present.

The remainder of marks can be discerned from the photos. Dark centered contour feathers with dark chestnut fringing was present... and is somewhat blurred into a rich dark chestnut in the photos.

Similar Species Discussion

See Boulder Mexican Duck (below) report for discussion. This bird is even "more" like a "pure" Mexican Duck given the lack of tertial contrast and narrower speculum borders.

(Boulder Mexican Duck: For Mexican Duck, a huge part of the identification hinges on the definition of “What is Mexican Duck?” vs “What is Mallard?” My basis for proceeding in this regard is based largely on http://azfo.org/journal/mottled_duck.html, an on-line article about Mexican Ducks in Arizona by Richard Webster. Additionally, the presence of “pure” Mexican Ducks in the United States is based on the 1998 AOU Check-list and on the Mexican Duck account in Ducks, Geese, and Swans (ed. J Kear, published by Oxford Univ Press in 2005.) Notably, the latter book elevates Mexican Duck to full species status, with Mottled Duck being a closer relative to Mallard than Mexican (this taxonomic relationship is why Webster’s article refers to Mottled rather than Mexican Duck.)

This bird fits descriptions of “pure” Mexican Duck well, excepting perhaps the tertials and the thickness of the anterior white speculum wing-stripe. The bird’s body was not as dark as that of an American Black Duck, and the wing pattern eliminates Mottled Duck, as does the fine streaking on chin/anterior neck.
