Description of the Bird

The most striking feature of this duck was its uniform olive bill that was slightly lighter at the edges. The head and face was distinctively marked (more than a female Mallard) with a dark, crisp line through the eye and the dark, almost black crown. The face and neck were warm buffy-toned and not very gray. The body overall was fairly dark and warm-toned with slightly rufous-brown coloration. The belly was fairly dark brown. At times the speculum was partially visible and it appeared to be greenish-blue with a very narrow white line on the trailing edge. The tail did not show any white. The legs were a bright orange.
Behaviors: This duck was associating with a small group of male and female Mallards in a muddy and marshy area next to open water. It was feeding and walking around in the area. It did not fly while we were observing it.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Since this bird was with female Mallards, it was easy to observe directly the differences between these two subspecies: first, bill was olive and not mottled yellow and dark; second, the eyeline and crown were much darker and almost black; third, the face and neck were warmer-toned, more buffy-brown, and not as grayish; fouth, the body color was darker and warmer brown with a dark belly; fifth, white trailing edge on speculum was very narrow; and sixth, the tail showed no white.
The differences from a female Mottled Duck were more subtle: first, there was no black spot at the gape of the bill; second the eyeline and crown were much too dark, almost black; third while the throat and foreneck were warmer-toned than a female Mallard, they were not nearly as bright buffy as a female Mottled Duck; fourth, bird seemed very close in size to accompanying Mallards while Mottled would have appeared slightly smaller.
