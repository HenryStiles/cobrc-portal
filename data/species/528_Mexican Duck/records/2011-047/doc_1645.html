Description of the Bird

The same size and shape as nearby "normal" (Common) Mallards. Fairly dark brown body contrasted with paler, grayer neck and head; demarcation between breast and neck obvious. Visible tertials on perched bird formed a somewhat paler, somewhat grayer panel, contrasting somewhat with overal brown body; this panel showed two faint white stripes. Bill fairly bright, uniform yellow, with small dark tip. Crown dark; thin transocular line dark. Neck with dark, very fine streaking. Rectrices dusky brown, contrasting little with rest of body; adult male Mallard standing nearby had much whiter rectrices. Feet deep orange-red. Speculum, seen well on bird with wings raised up on several occasions, was purplish-blue with a thin white trailing edge and a somewhat thicker, white leading edge.
Behaviors: Feeding, preening, and resting along a muddy lake shore. Seemed indifferent to the presence of other ducks, neither avoiding them nor actively seeking them out; basically, just another bird in a loose flock of (Common) Mallards, Gadwalls, and 3 teal species. After about an hour's observation, the bird flew off to the east.
Call: None heard.
Plumage: basic definitive sensu Pyle

Similar Species Discussion

The duck was clearly a "Mallard-type" based on direct comparison with "normal" (Common) Mallard types nearby. Its basically unmarked yellow bill made it a male; lacking a green head and overall pale body, the bird clearly was not a "normal" (Common) Mallard. Details of the speculum (showing narrow to medium white borders), bill color (fairly bright yellow), and overall body color (medium-dark) ruled out American Black Duck. Mottled Duck ruled out because this bird was colder and grayer overall (especially on the neck and head), and its speculum had white edges that were probably too prominent for Mottled Duck.

Additional Information

Comments: I think this bird was pretty straightforward for a phenotypically "good" Mexican Duck. On first seeing the bird, at a distance of ~800 feet, I was inclined toward calling the bird an intergrade. On seeing the bird closer (~400 feet), the bird seemed much better for a phenotypically "good" Mexican Duck. All marks matched up great for Mexican Duck, except perhaps for the visible tertials on the bird at rest. They seemed a bit paler and grayer than I might have expected for a "100%" Mexican Duck (is there such a thing??). If I were voting on this bird, I'd call it at least mostly a Mexican Duck, with the eternal caveat that you just can't know about these birds without genetics.
