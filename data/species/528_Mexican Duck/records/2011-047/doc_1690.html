Description of the Bird

On a gray dreary morning, Ted Floyd and I visited the northern end of Boulder Reservoir, where that had been mudflats all spring, visited at times by a various interesting birds. Shortly after arriving, I picked out a much darker Mallard-type duck with a contrasting paler head and neck. I pointed this out to Ted. After studying this bird at some distance, we worked our way closer. Our initial observation was at 300 meters or more. Eventually, we were approximately 100 meters away, and much of our observation time was at this distance. Total time of observation was probably 45 minutes during an hour-long period, half of which was at the most proximate distance. I was using a Kowa 88mm scope with a 20-60x eyepiece. The bird was in the presence of Mallards and Gadwall, mostly. It was not clearly paired with any other duck, but its size was more c/w male than female Mallard, and so the bird was presumably a male.

The bird was roughly the same shape as the nearby Mallards. Its body was uniformly dark, appearing blackish with a warm brown cast. Observed more closely, the individual body feathers were blackish-brown, with dark chestnut edgings (and internal markings?). The chest, belly, undertail coverts were all the same color/darkness. The folded wings were similar in color, except that the tertials formed a vague silvery patch, with one whitish line at uppermost edge on each side (left and right, seen while bird perched). The tail was dark grayish-brown, similar in darkness to the body, excepting narrow dusky gray edges to the outermost tail feathers could be discerned. The tail feathers showed no "curling" like a male Mallard. The line between chest and neck was quite distinct, at least from side and frontal views. The neck and head were a pale gray-tan (this color is hard to describe, but was much like the photo of male "Mexican Duck" in Kaufman's Birds of North America.), excepting a very blackish crown/nape (hind neck color not noted), and a quite dark narrow transocular stripe, that met (or nearly so) the blackish crown/nape posteriorly. No hint of green could be detected on the head. The auriculars, chin, and anterior neck were finely streaked in brownish. The speculum was purplish-blue, with a narrow white stripe posteriorly, and a thicker (more Mallard like) white stripe anteriorly.

The legs/feet were bright red-orange. The bill was bright yellow, with a slight greenish tinge, the intensity of which varied with angle and distance. The small nail to the bill was black.

Identification
For Mexican Duck, a huge part of the identification hinges on the definition of "What is Mexican Duck?" vs "What is Mallard?" My basis for proceeding in this regard is based largely on http://azfo.org/journal/mottled_duck.html, an on-line article about Mexican Ducks in Arizona by Richard Webster. Additionally, the presence of "pure" Mexican Ducks in the United States is based on the 1998 AOU Check-list and on the Mexican Duck account in Ducks, Geese, and Swans (ed. J Kear, published by Oxford Univ Press in 2005.) Notably, the latter book elevates Mexican Duck to full species status, with Mottled Duck being a closer relative to Mallard than Mexican (this taxonomic relationship is why Webster's article refers to Mottled rather than Mexican Duck.)

This bird fits descriptions of "pure" Mexican Duck well, excepting perhaps the tertials and the thickness of the anterior white speculum wing-stripe. The bird's body was not as dark as that of an American Black Duck, and the wing pattern eliminates Mottled Duck, as does the fine streaking on chin/anterior neck.

Finally, Ted Floyd agreed with the identification, with the caveat that some Mallard genes could be present.
