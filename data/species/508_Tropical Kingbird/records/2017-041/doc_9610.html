Description of the Bird

It was cold and rainy day, so instead of taking a walk we decided to take a drive around and do some birding. At 2700 and CC Road we noticed two kingbirds (not expected at this time of year). After taking a look one kingbird was most certainly a Cassin's Kingbird (rare for our area and recieved our attention). Little did we know the other kingbird was the real show (assuming it was a late migrating Western Kingbird). After being alerted by eBird filters that this bird could be a Tropical/Couch's Kingbird we checked the photos and decided to see if the bird was still around. That evening we found no kingbirds.
The next morning we tried again around 10:00 AM and we did find one kingbird. As luck would have it, it was the kingbird we wanted. It was very cooperative and Brenda took many photos.
After many photos were taken we did play both Tropical and Couch's songs and call notes (which we rarely do). The kingbird showed no interest and did not respond. In a couple of minutes a falcon (possible Merlin) flew over and the kingbird did give a warning call, a high chittering trill several times. We spent 4 winters in Mexico and know Tropical Kingbird calls and this sounded much like their call which they do constantly. This is the only sound we heard from the bird.
The kingbird had a very yellow breast, belly and undertail covert. The tail was not black but brown and deeply notched and did not have white outer tail feathers. The back was greenish gray. The size was nearly the same as the Cassin's Kingbird (photo of the two species submitted). The bill was very thick, long, heavy and black (see photo). There was a mask through the eye but not very dark which may indicate the bird could be a young bird (hatch year bird).

Similar Species Discussion

Western Kingbird (which is what we first thought) is a very common kingbird in our area. They should be gone by now. This kingbird was too yellow and the bill was too thick. The tail was not black and the call notes did not match.
Couch's Kingbird is a much more difficult species to eleminate. Bill of our bird looks long and heavy. The call did not match Couch's either. We have spent much time in eastern Mexico and Belize as well as Texas and do know the call notes and song of the Couch's Kingbird.

Resources Used

Sibey's Guide to Birds, National Geographic Field Guide to Birds, Audubon Handbook of Western Birds, A Guide to the birds of Mexico and Northern Central America.
eBird records were looked at for both Couch's and Tropical Kingbirds. The location of our kingbird is only 27 miles from the Colorado/Utah Line. Nearly all records of the two kingbird species are Tropical. Based upon geographic range only Tropical is a logical choice.

We have had a very unusal weather pattern on the last several weeks. High winds from the southwest have presisted day after day which is very rare in September and October. There are several other rarities in the area, Vermilion Flycatcher, Cassin's Kingbird, Brown Thrasher, Gray Catbird and a very likely Costa's Hummingbird.

Previous Experience

Brenda and I have spend 4 winters in Mexico and have visited nearly all countries in Central and South America. We have seen all kingbird species but only Western and Cassin's are present in western Colorado in spring and summer, the two species we know best.
