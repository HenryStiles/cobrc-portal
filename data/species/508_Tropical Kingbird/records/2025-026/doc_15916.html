Description of the Bird

Ethan Cleveland and I came across a yellow-bellied kingbird perched on a mullein and noted that the bill seemed awfully long for a Western Kingbird.  So we started to consider it might be a Tropical/Couch's and proceeded to try to observe (and document) the relevant field marks.  We followed the bird as it perched in various places and took short foraging flights, and we observed the following features, all of which were well photographed by Ethan:

- As mentioned, the bill was long for a Western.

- In flight, the bird's tail was uniformly brown (not black as in Western) and lacked the outer white tail feathers of a Western.

- The yellow on the bird's breast extended up closer to the throat than as expected in a Western.

At this point, we believed the bird to be a Tropical/Couch's (and I put out a message to the Discord RBA), but I was concerned that even really good photos of the bill would be insufficient to confirm to species in a vagrant context, so we knew we needed to hear calls to be sure which one.  I played calls of each of those species to try to elicit a vocalization from the bird, but nothing really happened -- the bird seemed to perk up a bit at the Tropical call, but it remained silent.  Fortunately, a couple minutes later, a Western Kingbird flew in and mobbed the mystery kingbird, and it elicited a trill call of a Tropical Kingbird.  (This is the call that is described as "Chitter" in Nathan Pieplow's Peterson Guide to Bird Sounds).  Both birds were then perched next to each other, allowing a really cool opportunity to study their differences in real time.  A couple minutes later, the Tropical Kingbird flew across the trail and perched on top of a Russian Olive and repeated the same trill call for nearly a minute.  I was able to record some of those vocalizations.

About fifteen minutes later, other people started to arrive to see the bird.  It remained in the same area, continuing to feast on bugs, and more trill calls were heard by those present.  Ethan and I stayed until I think about 9:00 before we moved on to bird other areas of the park.  The bird was reported by others throughout the rest of the day.

I am uploading the audio I recorded, and Ethan will upload the photos he took.  Our eBird report and media are available here:  https://ebird.org/checklist/S255661935 

Similar Species Discussion

Western Kingbird was eliminated because of the bird's larger bill, solid brown tail (not black with white outer feathers), and more extensive yellow up its breast nearly to its throat.

Cassin's Kingbird was eliminated because of those same features, plus the bird's grayer face.

Couch's Kingbird was eliminated by calls -- the bird gave trill calls consistent with Tropical Kingbird, not any of the "pip" or burry "breer" calls of Couch's.  Reviewing photos, I suspect that the bird's bill is long enough to confirm Tropical over Couch's visually as well, but I would rely primarily on the audio for the ID.

Previous Experience

I have seen Tropical Kingbirds often -- they are annual in late fall on the Pacific Coast in Washington (Neah Bay, Ocean Shores, etc.), and I have seen them in other states (California, Arizona, Texas) and many times internationally as well (Costa Rica, Mexico, Argentina).

I have seen Couch's Kingbirds on two trips to Texas.  Only one of those trips was in an area where both Couch's and Tropical overlap (Rio Grande Valley), but I found Couch's to be very vocal and relatively easy to separate from Tropical by sound.  (I did spend time on that trip trying to get familar with the differing bill shape of the two, but I think they are too similar to rely on bill shape a vagrant context).
