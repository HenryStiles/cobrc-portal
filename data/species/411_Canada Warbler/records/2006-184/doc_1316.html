Habitat

The bird was seen in the Grape Creek canyon just downstream of the DeWeese Res. dam. The canyon is about 50 ft deep and 150 ft wide at this point. Grape Creek is about 15 ft. wide. The vegetation is shrubs and forbs growing along the creek and on the cliffs. A photo of this location is submitted.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10x42 binoculars
Distance:  15 to 30 feet
Light:  Sunny conditions. Sometimes the bird was in open shade. Sometimes in direct sunlight. The position relative to the sun varied as the bird moved around a lot. Generally it was seen at or below eye level.

Description of the Bird

The bird was comparable in size and shape to other warblers nearby, i.e. Wilson and Yellow-rumped. The upper-parts (back, head and upper tail) were blue-gray. The under-parts (throat, breast and belly) were bright yellow. There was a faint necklace of dark streaks across the breast. It had a complete white eye-ring. There was yellow on the lores. No wing-bars.
NOTE: I did not spend any time making detailed observations of this bird. After I recognized that it was a CAWA, I spent the rest of the time attempting to get usable photographs of it.
Behaviors: The CAWA was actively feeding in the shrubs and plants along the creek and on the cliff. The photos were taken when the bird was feeding in plants on the cliff.
Call: None heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The complete white eye-ring and lack of wing-bars eliminates any of the Dendroica, Vireo or Parula species with yellow under-parts. The necklace of streaks and blue-gray back eliminates a Nashville Warbler.

Resources Used

National Geographic’s Birds of North America was referred to on site. Later, Sibley and Peterson.

Previous Experience

I lived and birded for many years on the East Coast where I saw this species frequently.
