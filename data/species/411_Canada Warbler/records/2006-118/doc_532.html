Habitat

Thick deciduous woods, mostly cottonwoods and willows, with a dense undercarpet of bur bushes and other weeds and some pockets of dense underbrush.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Pentax 8x42 binoculars
Distance:  estimated 20 feet at closest approach
Light:  The first time I saw the bird, when I got very poor looks, but thought I saw the necklace streaking extending down the flanks, the bird was extremely backlit and partially hidden behind some vegetation. My good 30-second views, however, were unobstructed views at eye level in partial sun (sun was behind a cloud just then, and well off to my left).

Description of the Bird

A slender warbler, kinda longish-tailed; uniform slate-gray above: no wingbars or streaks. Underparts yellow. Yellow throat set off from below by faint necklace of dark streaks, stronger towards sides, where I think they extended a short distance down the sides. Distinct circular white eye ring. Bill, as seen from below in foreshortened view, had a distinct flesh color.
Behaviors: The bird was very active, foraging from 10-20 feet up in thick deciduous woods in the company of a great many other small passerines. It liked the thicker vegetation and switched trees often.
Call: The single chip note I THINK came from this bird was thick and noisy, rather like that of a Wilson's Warbler.
Plumage: believed to be adult female

Similar Species Discussion

My original concern, especially with my poor original looks, was eliminating Magnolia Warbler, as I thought I saw streaks extending down the flanks, a character good for Magnolia and not for Canada. However, the good looks I got at the bird later left no doubt in my mind that it was a Canada, as Magnolia was eliminated by the completely uniform gray upperparts, completely gray wings with no wingbars, and prominent white eye-ring. I also did not see any white spots in the tail.

Resources Used

I looked at Sibley after writing up my field notes during lunch that day. Sibley reminded me of the yellow lores and white undertail coverts of Canada Warbler, field marks I forgot to take note of in my excitement. He also caused me to reconsider my original impression that streaking from the necklace may have extended a short distance down the flanks.

Previous Experience

Canada Warbler is my nemesis bird. I had only seen one prior to this, and that was an adult male in Connecticut in 2001.
