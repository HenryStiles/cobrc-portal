Description of the Bird

The warbler was first seen with a small flock of Mountain Chickadees and Yellow-rumped Warblers (Audubon's) in a small pine. The yellow throat, extending from the bill to the upper breast and bordered by the black mask and breast streaks was evident. The black mask around the eye extended from the base of the bill, behind the eye, and broken by a small white crescent below the eye with black leading to the breast (forming an upside down triangle on the face). A white stripe extended from the base of the bill, above the eye and back and was slightly wider at the back. A white stripe extended down and behind the black triangle patch. Two white wing bars contrasted with a gray-blue mantle and head (darker forecrown). The lower belly was white with dark black streaks extending down the flanks. The underside of the tail was white with black tips on each side. The bill was rather long and thin and black.
Behaviors: The warbler fed in the pine for some time before flying off to a low brushy tree, back to the pine (usually staying fairly high), and eventually away towards a cottonwood and not seen again.
Call: called several times. A soft chip reminiscent of other members of the dendroica genus, not as solid as a yellow-rumped call.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Grace's Warbler is the only other warbler species with a yellow upper breast and throat contrasting with a gray-blue body and dark streaks on the flank. The white supercilium extending behind the eye, the white patch behind the black face patch, and white crescent below the eye are features characteristic of Yellow-throated Warbler and not Grace's. The long bill also is suggestive of Yellow-throated over Grace's.
