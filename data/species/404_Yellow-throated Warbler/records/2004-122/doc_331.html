Description of the Bird

A distinctive warbler, the bird had a black forehead, with the black bleeding up onto the forecrown. The rest of the crown, and the upperparts down through the rump were blue-gray. It had a distinct and well-defined white superciliary with a hint of yellow in the supraloral part of that feature. There was an obvious black eyeline that connected to black auriculars. A single white eye arc was positioned under each eye and the area immediately behind the auriculars was white. The chin, throat, and upper breast were bright yellow, the rest of the underparts being white with the sides have bold, black streaking extending onto the flanks.

The wings and tail were dark, with the wings being marked by bold, white wingbars (tips to the greater and median coverts) and blue-gray edges to most feathers; and with the inner webs of the outer 4-5 pairs of rectrices being primarily bright white and forming large, obvious tail spots.
Behaviors: foraging on the trunks and larger branches of cottonwoods, somewhat like a creeper or nuthatch; also perched for a while on a power line and foraged on the roof of the shed at the banding station
Call: It sang once while I was present (more when I wasn't present) -- a series of sliding whistles that descended in pitch until the end when it ascended again.
Plumage: Alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Grace's Warbler is superficially similar, but that species easily ruled out by song, white superciliary and post-auricular patch and much longer bill.

Resources Used

none (though I checked Pyle (1997) to make sure that the subspecies albilora can have some yellow in front of the eye

Previous Experience

I have seen 100s of Yellow-throated Warblers and heard >75 of them. I have also heard >30 Grace's Warblers and seen at least 50 of them.
