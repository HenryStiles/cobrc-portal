Description of the Bird

This warbler was found by a person unknown at Crow Valley Campground, Weld County on April 8, 2010. Bob Shade and Warren Finch were birding there and when their attention was directed to the bird, Bob identified it (pers comm). He called me and I went up there about 3 PM and photographed it.

The bird's shape, size, and behavior were compatible with a Dendroica Warbler (5-6" long, about the same size as a Yellow-rumped Warbler). No other warblers were nearby for comparison. It was seen well in sun on the trunks, large branches and twigs of a Chinese Elm tree from 5-20 feet above ground, at close distances, perhaps 15-30 feet away from me with 8.5 power Svarovski binoculars. Little wind; temperature about 65 degrees. A photograph made with an Olympus 400 mm equivalent lens was sent to Larry Semo.
The upperparts were gray and there was a bright yellow throat and breast, confined laterally by black lateral throat-sripes. The yellow color did not extend up into the face as one would see with Grace's Warbler. There was a white post-auricular spot, an intricately patterned face, with white around the eye and black auriculars. From this and the black lateral throat-stripe, a black streak extended down the sides of the breast, merging into a few blackish flank streaks.

Of interest was a white eyebrow stripe, and there was a faint yellowish wash in front of the eye, (see photo), but the eye-brow stripe (super-cillium) was white behind the eye. There were two white wing-bars.
The bill and legs were black.
Male sex was favored, based on darker black around face and brighter yellow on throat and breast, dark blackish flank streaks.

This individual was seen well enough to rule out other North American warblers, including Grace's, as the description above supports. When I studied the photo, I ruled out Grace's, Black-and-white (which has a similar behavior), Blackburnian male and female, Black-throated Gray, Audubon's, N. Parula,Townsend's Warblers, some of whose field marks overlap with Yellow-throated Warbler.
I had some ideas about which subspecies this might be, but did not identify it to subspecies conclusively. I consulted DUnn and Garrett's "Warbler," Sibley's Guide to Birds and internet photos of YTWA. I am familiar with this species from previous sightings in CO, and from birding in central Missouri, where they both migrate and breed. I am familiar with Grace's Warbler and the other birds this individual might be confused with.
Behaviors: The bird actively gleaned from the trunk and large branches of a tree like a "hyper" nuthatch, but at times was on smaller twigs. Few warblers feed like this. Grace's does note as far as I know. It did not interact with other birds.
Call: silent (or at least I could not hear it).
Plumage: Alternate
