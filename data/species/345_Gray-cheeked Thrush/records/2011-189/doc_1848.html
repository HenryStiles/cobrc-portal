Description of the Bird

The three birds were quite similar in appearance, except that one had an extremely worn tail, with much of the middle rectrices broken off and with the outer rectrices frayed and short. I describe them here as one individual, noting differences in individuals where appropriate.

Fairly large Catharus thrush with cold-colored head and relatively cold-colored back, though back was slightly warmer-colored than head. Crown gray-brown, loral area pale grayish, auriculars gray with vague dark barring. Bird 1 had essentially no eye ring, except a bit at the back of the eye that was noticeable only at very close range. The other two had thin and incomplete pale grayish eye rings. A vague pale ear surround separated the auriculars from the neck, which was the same color as the nape and crown, though, perhaps, just slightly warmer colored.

The rest of the upperparts became gradually warmer from the nape's brownish-gray to the rump's grayish-brown. The tail was ruddier than the back, being brighter and warmer-colored than the tails of nearby Swainson's Thrushes, but nowhere near the orangish color of the tails of Hermit Thrushes. Bird 3 was warmer-colored overall than was Bird 1, which were the two full-tailed individuals.

The chin and throat were white, except for thin, black lateral throat stripes. The chest was pale with a bit of buff ground color on Birds 1 and 3, with all birds having dark brownish to blackish spots that became more vaguely defined at the lower edge of the chest. The sides and flanks were washed gray on Birds 1 and 2, but on Bird 3, the sides were washed brownish-gray changing to gray wash on the flanks. The belly was whitish.

The wings were mostly brownish-gray, though the primaries had distinct warm brown fringes, creating an ill-defined wing panel on the folded wing like that of Hermit Thrush, but without quite the distinctness nor orangeness of that species. In flight, a distinct yellowish wing stripe was noted, formed by pale bases to most of the remiges.

The eyes were dark, the legs were pink, and the bill was dark, but with extensive yellow at the base of the mandible, with each bird varying slightly in the extent and placement of this yellow.
Behaviors: Birds were variably in trees, on the ground, or on headstones, foraging. They were somewhat skittish, not permitting close approach most of the time. On the ground, they usually hopped to locomote, as is typical for Catharus thrushes.
Call: None heard
Plumage: basic

Similar Species Discussion

Bicknell's Thrush is nearly impossible to rule out, except that that species tends to have more extensive yellow on the mandible and is noticeably smaller than Swainson's Thrush (SWTH); the birds in question were the same size as nearby SWTHs. SWTH ruled out by warm-colored tail and wing panel, lack of large, obvious, and buff eye rings, and other bits of buff on the head. Hermit Thrush (HETH) -- particularly auduboni HETH -- ruled out by lack of distinct complete whitish eye ring, lack of typical tail-lift behavior of HETH, and lack of strong contrast in color between rump and tail.

Additional Information

Comments: I was working at home this morning and got a call that there were multiple Gray-cheeked Thrushes in e. Colorado this day (at Crow Valley, Chico Basin Ranch, etc.). So, though I didn't have a lot of time to spare, I went over to the cemetery in hopes of finding my own. While I am reporting three birds present here, I suspect that there were actually four -- at least, but I could discern only three demonstrably different individuals in my pictures. I wrote this description from brief notes written after returning home from the cemetery and from reference to my many pictures of the birds. Finally, a wrote an essay on the ID of Gray-cheeked Thrush in Colorado for the In The Scope column of Colorado Birds primarily using pictures of the birds described here.
