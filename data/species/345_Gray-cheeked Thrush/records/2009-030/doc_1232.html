Habitat

In a dense tangle of willows and understory shrubs. The bird was in the eastern tip of this tangle, which seems to be a magnet for rarities.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Nikon 10X EDG binocular.
Distance:  When perched, about 25 feet. I paced off the distance.
Light:  Mostly sunny. It had been overcast, but was clearing up. I was in a shady clearing. There was good ambient light in there, but the bird was not in direct light. The sun was behind me and to my left as I was watching the perched bird.

Description of the Bird

The bird was seen briefly, but at very close range. First, it flew right in front of me (~10 feet away), giving its distinctive queeah! flight call as it passed. Right away, I suspected it was a Gray-cheeked. It landed in a shrub about 25 feet away from me. First I saw it dorsally, and I could see that it was a cold, neutral gray-brown from head to tail. The back and tail were concolor, with little if any reddish tinting to the tail. On landing, the bird kept its tail in line with the body. (Hermit typically does a tail-rise on landing.) This dorsal view lasted for about 5 seconds. Then the bird turned sideways (body pointed to my left, relative to angle of observation). The face was quite plain and "blank," lacking the buffy or orange highlights on the lores and spectacles and below the auricular region. I did not see the breast very well, but I was able to discern fairly heavy dark spotting against a background color that I do not recall (or did not see).
Behaviors: As the bird flew past, I heard its flight call: a shrill, "urgent," slurred or disyllabic "queeah!" Immediately, I suspected it was a Gray-cheeked. This flight call is variable, but in its "typical" manifestation, it is distinctive, and probably diagnostic. I would say that this particular flight call was typical.

When the bird landed, in plain view, it simply stayed put. It didn't fidget about or raise its tail or anything like that.

Then it disappeared, not to be seen again. That's the way with Gray-cheeked Thrushes.
Call: Flight call heard. This was, in fact, the initial basis of the identification. See below.
Plumage: undetermined

Similar Species Discussion

Other -Catharus- thrushes. I've touched on this above. Briefly:

Flight call is usually distinct from either Swainson's or Veery. (Veery tends to be very harsh, low, and "drawling." Swainson's tends to be more musical, more mellow, "sadder," and less disyllabic. Hermit is quite different: pure, sweet, and monotone.)

Low-contrast face with little marking and little buff/orange separates from Swainson's Thrush. Uniform upperparts, with tail and back concolor, plus no tail-raising separates from Hermit. Overall cold, neutral tones, with decent spotting underneath separates from Veery. (Bicknell's not relevant in Colorado.)

Previous Experience

Extensive. I see and hear hundreds of Swainson's Thrushes per year in Colorado, plus a few Veeries (mainly on the breeding grounds) per year, and I have seen and/or heard several Gray-cheeks in Colorado. I have observed hundreds of Gray-cheeks and thousands of Veeries and Swainson's elsewhere. I have made extensive study of the flight calls of these species, too.

Additional Information

Comments: This bird was seen during a period of an intense incursion of "Eastern" and "Midwestern" species into Boulder County and elsewhere in the Front Range region. During the same morning, and in the same patch of shrubbery, for example, I observed 2 Veeries, 2 Blackpoll Warblers, and at least 85 Clay-colored Sparrows. Many other observers also reported good numbers and diversity of Eastern/Midwestern species in the region that day and the 2 days prior.

At the time of the observation, I had thought that the sighting was unusually early. (I had thought that Colorado Gray-cheeks are mainly from the 2nd half of May.) However, I checked the eBird database for 2009 through 10 May (the day before the sighting), and noticed that the main front of migrating Gray-cheeks had already made it to the Lower Great Lakes by that date. So the date of the sighting is not as notably early as I had thought; I would say it was about "normal" for the latitude. (Note: I will upload the eBird database for 2009 Gray-cheeks through 10 May.)
