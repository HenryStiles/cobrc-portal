Habitat

In a fairly low willow tree along the Arickaree River. Nearby were other, taller willows, tall cottonwoods, and tall grasses.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10x Nikon Elite binocular.
Distance:  Initially ~100 ft. I closed in to ~20 feet, near the close-focus limit of my binoculars.
Light:  Overcast, with a low cloud ceiling. Some fog/mist along the eastern and southern horizons. I observed the bird from the south, meaning the sun was to my right; however, the sun was completely obscured by the clouds. Given the overcast conditions, the light was good, being evenly dispersed.

Description of the Bird

A "spot-breasted thrush," gray-brown above, paler and gray-white below. First seen mainly facing away from me; the bird was a uniform cold gray-brown above, and, to the extent that I could see the face, I was struck by the large black eye against a cold brown-gray background.

Angling around for a look at the bird in profile and then nearly head-on, I got very good looks at the face, throat and breast; I noted the brown-gray to gray-white background color of the throat, breast, and face. The uppermost part of the breast (i.e., directly below the unmarked gray-white throat) tended toward more of a weak buff-gray background color, which is normal for Gray-cheeked Thrush. Otherwise, the bird's face, throat and breast lacked warm or buff tones altogether.

The visible primaries were long and nearly black. The bird imparted a strong-legged, long-winged appearance overall. Its upperparts were uniform in color; when it finally flew directly away from me, I got great looks at the tail-to-back contrast, of which there was none. The bird was alert during the entire period of observation.
Behaviors: The bird was perched in a low willow for 10 minutes. During the period of observation, it moved around 3 or 4 times within the willow. Its movements were probably related to my close approach. The bird did not call while in the willow. After 10 minutes it flew away, and it did not give a flight call as it departed. At no time did the bird flick its tail up then lower it.
Call: none heard
Plumage: unknown

Similar Species Discussion

Swainson's Thrush and Hermit Thrush are the confusion species.

Separated by Swainson's Thrush by lack of warm buff tones about the face, throat, and breast. In particular, the bird lacked a buff eye-ring, buff lores, and buff below the auriculars. The throat was gray-white. A small amount of buff was across the upper breast was noted, and that is normal for Gray-cheeked Thrush; otherwise the fairly dense spotting on the breast was against a gray-white background.

Separated from Hermit Thrush, in particular the interior (auduboni) populations, by dark gray-brown upperparts; interior Hermit Thrush is grayer above. Breast-spotting was denser than on Hermit Thrush. Black eye stared out prominently on brown-gray face; eye of Hermit Thrush tends to be less prominent, being encircled by a narrow gray or gray-white eye-ring. Upperparts showed no contrast between tail and back, unlike Hermit Thrush, which shows moderate to strong tail-to-back contrast; this mark was seen well as the bird flew away. Perched Hermit Thrushes frequently raise and then lower tails; this behavior was not shown by this bird.
