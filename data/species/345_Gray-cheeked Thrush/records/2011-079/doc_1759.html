Description of the Bird

On the evening of 4 June, Tony Leukering, Mark Peterson, and I were birding Van’s Grove. I flushed up a Catharus thrush, which given the date and location, immediately aroused my interest. I called to my companions, and we worked to relocate the bird. Tony relocated it, happily saying, “Gray-cheeked Thrush.” The time was about 7:05pm, the bird was 50-60 feet distant from me in moderate to deep shadow, and I viewed it for 2-3 minutes as it hopped around the bases of trees (thus providing various views and lighting angles).

The bird had uniform dull brown upperparts, lacking warm hues, or contrast between back and rump/tail. The chest was heavily spotted dark brown. The throat was unspotted as was the belly and flanks. The cheek appeared gray and certainly was not buffy. The eyering was relatively weak (by comparison with Hermit or Swainson’s Thrush) and was broken anteriorly. The sides appeared washed in gray, certainly not bright buff. The bird gave a chek-chek call, repeatedly, that was different from any Hermit or Swainson’s Thrush (or Veery) that I’ve heard. I’ve not heard a Wood Thrush in some time.

Similar Species Discussion

The combination of back and flank color, cheek/eyering pattern, moderately heavy chest spotting (not HEAVY like a Wood Thrush) eliminates all other possibilities except Bicknell’s Thrush. The unlikely-to-occur Bicknell’s Thrush would typically show more warm tones, particularly in folded flight feathers and uppertail coverts/base of tail. Note that the call does not fit descriptions in guides for any thrush, but I was able to find a recording from British Columbia that matched well. Unfortunately, almost all recordings are from breeding grounds.

Previous Experience

I've little experience with Bicknell's having seen but a handful. Over my life, I've seen dozens of GC Thrush, but none recently. Recently (last 5-10 years) I've seen dozens of Veery (mostly more similar western race), and hundreds of Swainson's Thrushes
