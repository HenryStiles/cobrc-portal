Description of the Bird

this bird was foraging on an extensive mat of vegetation debris (leaves, wood bits, etc) caught by some tree trunks in the recently flooded stream bed, where it was joined by a Swainson's Thrush, several Yellow-rumped Warblers, and Common Grackles. It was about 50 feet away, and when I first viewed it through binoculars I observed features that indicated it was not one of the more common thrushes. So after watching it for a short time I went to get my spotting scope from the car. It kept coming back to the same mat of debris and I had great views through the scope as it fed on the mat and perched low in the adjacent trees. The skies were mostly clear and the bird was sometimes in sunlight and otherwise in bright shade. Views were mostly unobstructed when it was on the debris mat or when it perched low in the trees beside the debris. I was able to view it for about 6-7 minutes over a period of about 18 minutes, occasionally being distracted by the many other birds in the area. My initial views that made it appear different compared to the adjacent SWTH were of a less warm-colored bird, with darker spotting on the breast without buff tones and a lack of a noticeable eyering. The bird showed a gray face that lacked warm tones, especially noticeable in the loral and malar areas and toward the rear of the auriculars. Compared to the SWTH the auriculars were grayish and not warm brown. The eyering was weak and not at all pronounced. In my binocular views I did not really notice it, but in the scope it was weakly evident, and showed as whitish or pale and not buff-pale as on SWTH. The difference with the obvious eyering of the nearby SWTH was clearly evident. The breast was well marked with blackish spots, the spots being somewhat darker and more distinct than on the SWTH. The base color of the breast and sides was cool pale grayish in tone and lacked the buffy suffusion of SWTH. The upperparts were gray-brown, with the tail being just a bit more brown (less gray) so there was weak contrast with the back. The flanks had a pale grayish wash nicely evident through the scope when the bird was in the bright shade, lacking buff or warm coloring. The bird appeared the same size as SWTH. The legs were flesh. The bill appeared similar to SWTH in coloration and shape. I did not notice much tail or wing flicking. I did not hear any calls from this bird, but with the stream flow it would have been hard to hear any that may have been given.

Similar Species Discussion

Hermit Thrush was eliminated by this bird's slightly larger size, less contrasting tail color, and less pronounced eyering. It also did not flick tail and wings oftren as HETH usually does. 

Swainson's was eliminated (in direct comparison) by the overall cooler coloring, clearly grayer face and upperparts, blacker breast spotting, weak eye ring, and gray tones on the flanks. 

Veery was eliminated by the same features as SWTH and even more so. 

Bicknell's was eliminated by known patterns of occurrence in CO and assumption.

Resources Used

I had been generally ready for the ID based on advance study this season in anticipation of possibly encountering a rare thrush. Directly after the observation I conferred witht the Sibley guide, and upon returning home I looked at photos of this and other species online.

Previous Experience

My experience with this species is limited to observtions of several along the east coast several years ago. This was my first in CO or the west. 

I have extensive experience with Swainson's and Hermit from 33 years of highly active birding in CA and now CO, and extensive work in their breeding habitats as a field ornithologist. 

I have encountered Veery about 20 times. I've never seen a Bicknell's.
