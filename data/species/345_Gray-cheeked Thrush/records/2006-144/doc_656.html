Habitat

The bird was captured at the banding station located in the riparian forest, consisting of mainly cottonwood and willow tree species, surrounding Barr Lake.

Description of the Bird

The upperparts were grayish olive, and the underparts were white. The flanks were also grayish olive and not contrasting with the white breast and belly. The upper breast had a slight buffy wash. The olive-brown breast spots, which were restricted mainly to the upper breast, were more distinctive in the center of the upper breast whereas the other spots clustered together to form blotches. The bill was dark except for the base of the lower mandible, which was pale with a yellow tinge and only extending just past the nares. A distinctive, yellow gape was also present. This bird also had a slight hint of a pale eye ring. The wing chord was 100 mm and the tail 70 mm. This GCTH belonged to the subspecies C.m. minimus.
Behaviors: Normal behaviors of the Gray-cheeked Thrush were not observed during its capture and banding.

Similar Species Discussion

The wing morphology of this bird matched that of a GCTH, as well as a VEER, but differed from that of the other Catharus thrushes. The difference between the lengths of the longest primary covert and p10 was 8.4 mm, and p9 was longer than p6, which was slightly emarginated. The difference between the lengths of p8 and p6 was 4.9 mm; the differences between the lengths of p8 and p5 was 12.3 mm, and p8 and p1 was 30.0 mm. The plumage of this GCTH did not fit the plumage criteria of the other Catharus thrushes. It lacked the typical characteristics of a VEER: the reddish upperparts and tail, the distinct pinkish wash of the breast, and the distinct contrast between the flanks and belly. However, the most obvious and distinguishing feature of this bird was its dark bill, which is strikingly different from the pale bill of a VEER. Its plumage was also dissimilar to a SWTH due to the lack of distinct, buffy eye rings, lores, and wash to the upper breast and throat. The tail of this bird was not reddish and did not contrast with the back, as in a HETH and BITH. The bird's dark bill and olive-brown upperparts and tail were more similar in coloration to a GCTH, although the breast spots were less numerous and distinctive than a typical GCTH.
