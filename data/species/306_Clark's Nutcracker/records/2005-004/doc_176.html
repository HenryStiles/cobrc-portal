Description of the Bird

While on a hiatus from work, I occasionally bird about the Interlocken business district in southern Broomfield county. On the afternoon of January 20, 2005, I spied a relatively large bird perched atop a large cottonwood tree. I first thought Northern Flicker, as it was roughly that size and that would be the usual suspect in the area. However, this bird was perched more horizontally so I headed toward its direction for a clearer view. Upon getting closer, the bird flew to another cottonwood and I quickly identified it as a Clark's Nutcracker, a potential first for Broomfield county. The occurrence of this species in Broomfield county during the winter of 2004-05 was not unpredictable as other Front Range counties had experienced a movement of Clark's Nutcrackers as well.

The bird appeared slightly larger than a Blue Jay and somewhat bulkier. It was mostly gray overall, except for jet black wings and clear white inner secondaries (when perched). The head was somewhat flattened in shape and the bill was longish, very pointed, and all black. The undertail coverts were white. When in flight, the contrast between the black wing coverts and primaries and white secondaries stood out. The black wings also contrasted strongly with the gray body plumage. The tail was mostly white, except that the inner couple of rectrices were jet black. The flight was flappy and crow-like. After following the bird for a few minutes, it flew towards to the southeast and out of sight. I contacted Eric Zorawowicz and David Ely, two other Broomfield county listers, but they were unable to refind the bird.
Behaviors: Seen perched atop trees and in flight.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Gray Jays, which would be even more unusual at this elevation, does not show the contrasting blacks and whites in the wing and tail. That species also has a shorter bill and longer tail. The two regular North American shrikes (Northern and Loggerhead) are smaller, show a black patch through the eye, and have shorter, blunter bills. The Northern Mockingbird, which also would be unusual in winter at this location, is smaller and more elongate, has a shorter, more decurved bill, has a white wing patch on the greater primary coverts and middle primaries, and has a mostly black tail with white outer rectrices.
