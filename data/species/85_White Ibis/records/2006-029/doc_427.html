Description of the Bird

The bird was a long-legged wader with a long, decurved bill, clearly an ibis. Plumage all white; some very faint grayish/orangey staining on a few of the wing coverts, but I think this is more likely mud staining than retained juvenal feathers. Front of neck was pretty well orange-stained; this was the part of the neck that the bird was dipping most frequently in the water. Bright red legs; bright red facial skin extending up around eye and across forehead above bill; basal quarter of bill also red, rest of bill black. Bird was slightly larger in the body and heavier than adjacent Mallards. Red facial skin extended farther up the forehead and behind the eye than facial skin on Plegadis ibis, and back away from the bill in the chin area also. Once when the bird was scratching its chin, its wings separated slightly and I caught a brief glimpse of what appeared to be black feathering at the wingtip, but this was not visible when the bird had its wings folded normally.
Behaviors: The bird was foraging in the shallow region of a prairie pothole pond, usually up to above its knees, dipping its bill in the water rapidly and actively in a sewing-machine motion, coming up every minute or so with a quarter- or silver-dollar sized piece of food. One seemed to be a small 1 inch fish.
Call: The bird was silent.
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Other ibis are not white; albino Plegadis ruled out by more extensive facial skin and black primary tips (which Scott Hutchings saw, and I thought I might have seen). Sibley says the bill is thicker and the bird is larger and heavier than Plegadis, but no other ibis were present for comparison. Pelican and egrets ruled out by downcurved bill and long red legs.
