Description of the Bird

While scanning a flock of mostly Franklin's gulls on a lake shore the ibis flew into my field of vision and landed near the gulls. It sat for ~5 minutes before flying due North low to the ground (15-20 ft above ground level). I took several photographs.

The bird was mostly white with a long decurved reddish bill, red legs, and black tips to the outer primary feathers that were most obvious in flight. The bird sat for about five minutes then flew off to the N.

Similar Species Discussion

White-faced ibis and glossy ibis - this was a virtually all-white ibis except for black outer primary tips and redish soft parts.

Great egret - this bird had a strongly decurved reddish bill - not a straight yellowish bill.

Snowy egret - this bird had a strongly decurved reddish bill - not a straight (and shorter) black bill.

Little blue heron, imm. - again, this bird had a strongly decurved reddish bill - not a straight dull colored bill.

The reddish legs also separate this bird from any heron or egret likely to be seen in Colorado.

It was too small and had too little black to be a wood stork. Also the long and decurved reddish bill means it could not have been a wood stork.

The bird was much smaller than an American white pelican and only the tips to the primaries were black (not most of the primaries and some of the secondaries black). Also the bill and legs were red (not yellowish or yellow-orange).

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I worked for the National Park Service in the Everglades studying fire ecology impacts on plants and birds for three years and white ibis were common - I saw them most days. I also got to work doing wading bird counts several times and saw this species and many other waders on the SE U.S. I'm familiar w/glossy and white-faced ibis from birding and working in the east and west, respectively.
