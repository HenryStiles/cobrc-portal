Description of the Bird

This bird had
  • a curved culmen on a shorter beak than is expected from Cassin's Finch
  • buffy sides (not always visible in the photos)
  • darkest brown on its auriculars
  • no eyering
  • thick, dark streaks on the white breast and sides
  • unmarked undertail coverts
  • and overall color darker than surrounding House Finches

  • David Dowell heard the bird call in flight multiple times and described it as a "pik".
  • Female-type individual

Similar Species Discussion

House Finch was considered and easily eliminated by broad white eyestripe, white chest background with heavy brown streaks, overall darker brown and more contrasting plumage.

Cassin's Finch was considered and eliminated by beak shape being curved in the culmen, very white background to the heavy brown streaks on the chest and flanks, unmarked undertail coverts, buffy flanks, and bold white eyestripe.

Resources Used

Prior knowledge, Sibley Guide to Birds, Crossley ID Guide, and feederwatch.org

Previous Experience

House finch has been common at my feeders throughout my life (25 years of birding) in Pennsylvania, Illinois, and Colorado.  Cassin's Finch has only been a more recent addition to my understanding with limited individuals in Boulder and Larimer counties in the last 7 years.  Purple Finch was an uncommon winter resident in central Illinois where I lived for 3 years and had ample experience with the eastern subspecies of this finch.  I have NO memorable experience with western Purple Finch.
