Description of the Bird

I saw 10 individuals, 8 brown birds and 2 adult males. Larry saw an 11th bird that I missed.

The two red birds had dark bills, though paler mandible than maxilla, and dark eyes and legs. The crowns were reddish-pink with no strong contrast to duller nape. There was a very vague paler superciliary, also of reddish-pink. The auricular were brown, contrasting with the reddish-pink malar region. The birds lacked whitish eye arcs. The backs were brown and vaguely streaked darker. I did not note anything about the tails due to the shortness of the sighting of these two individuals. The chins, throats, and chests of the two birds were about the same reddish-pink color on the head, with that color devolving into reddish-pink streaking on the lower sides and flanks; the bellies were white and the vent regions were virtually unstreaked.

The eight brown birds were mostly quite similar, though there were at least two individuals that I describe below as slightly different. Most were cleanly marked individuals with strong contrast of brown to white or whitish. The crowns and upperparts were medium brown, neither warm nor particularly cold, with the backs also sporting darker streaking. Distinct bright white supercilia and malar regions contrasted strongly with the brown crowns and auriculars. There were no contrasting whitish lower eye arcs on any of these birds. On one of them, I noted that the very forehead was contrastingly white. They sported nearly blackish lateral throat stripes that were the darkest apparent part of the body plumage. The underparts were white with distinct brown streaking extending down the sides to the upper flanks. The lower belly and vent regions were, on the individuals on which I noted the region, were nearly unstreaked. The bills, eyes, and legs were like those of the adult males.

The two somewhat different brown birds differed in that they exhibited a distinct somewhat warm brown wash on the chest but that was obviously paler than the darker streaking. This is a regular variant of Purple Finch plumages that I have seen and suspect that it has a bearing on age and/or sex, but I know not what it means.
Behaviors: Perching, calling, and one singing in a mixture of pines and some short deciduous tree, though I detected the first individual when it flew up off the ground (foraging?) with four American Goldfinches.
Call: Dry 'pik' notes heard at least 5 times; the short, two-noted whistle 'whee-yu' heard at least thrice; one bird gave full song, a bright warbling without the typical harsh ending of House Finch song
Plumage: definitive basic and others

Similar Species Discussion

Cassin's Finch is really the only similar species, but it is readily ruled out by the various birds' face patterns, particularly the strong white supercilia and malar regions, the mostly unstreaked vent regions, and by the call notes. The lack of eye arcs on all birds is also a strong separator.
