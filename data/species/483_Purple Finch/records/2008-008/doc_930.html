Description of the Bird

The birds were roughly the size of House Sparrow. On the males (2), the bills were conical, short, and heavy with the mandible being dark gray and the maxilla being horn-colored basally and dark gray distally. The heads were rounded. The males were purplish-red across the crown and back, blending into pink across the breast, flanks, and uppertail coverts. The auriculars were slightly browner than the remainder of the head. There was also a small grayish spot anterior and slightly ventral to the eye. The back and scapulars were also lightly streaked with brown. The wings displayed two narrow wing bars of brownish red. The bellies and undertail coverts were white, while the tail was a darkish red-brown above and whitish-gray beneath. The tail was relatively short and forked. The legs were a dark gray.

The females (9) had a similar overall shape to the body and bill as with the males. The crowns were dark brown. Diagnostic brown auriculars were bordered dorsally by a broad clean, white supercilium. The malar was white and was bordered ventrally by a distinct brown moustachial stripe. The throat was whitish-gray. The back was brown with distinct darker streaks. The wings were brown, with the primaries being darker. The edges of the coverts and tertials were edged in white. The breast was white with dark, thick streaking extending to the belly and posteriorally along the flanks. The undertail coverts were clean white with no streaking.
Behaviors: The birds foraged beneath the canopy.
Call: A few birds would occasionally give a short krick call. A couple of birds also sang, a rich American Robin-like liquid song somewhat resembling that of a House Finch’s, but lower pitched, less sustained, and less obviously phrased.
Plumage: Definitive basic

Similar Species Discussion

Cassin’s Finches are slightly larger with a relatively less stubby bill. The overall red on male Cassin’s is paler, although the crown is of brighter shade that forms a cap, which contrasts with the more brownish remainder of the upperparts. The throat and breast are paler and pinker on male Cassin’s and the undertail coverts usually are streaked. Female Cassin’s Finches are grayer, have less defined facial patterns, have finer and sharper breast and flank streaking, and have streaked undertail coverts. House Finches differ by being smaller, have a more restricted and brick-red plumage to the males, have a more decurved culmen, and the lower breast, belly, and flanks thickly streaked with brown. Female House Finches are paler above than Purple's and have no definite facial patter.

The males, although somewhat similar in shade, are smaller than Pine Grosbeaks, have a different color pattern, and bill shape. The uncrossed mandibles on the subject birds distinguished them from crossbills.
