Description of the Bird

The birds were seen well on numerous occasions. The males were extremely distinctive, like House Finches but a little plumper, and with the bright raspberry-red stain continuous all over the head and down the breast, with darker auriculars and white belly and ventral area. Females were pretty distinctive also, also plump finches with very sharply defined facial patterns, unlike House Finch: a strong white supercilium and strong white moustachial stripe setting off the dark brown crown, auriculars, and submoustachial stripe. The females were also more crisply streaked below than House Finches, with white bellies and ventral areas like the males.
Behaviors: The birds were flying around the area in small flocks and landing in the tops of tall trees. They would make their distinctive calls while perched, though the louder versions were made in flight.
Call: Birds made the typical Purple Finch flight call, an extremely brief high-pitched "tip" or "tp" call that is distinctive and very different from the flight calls of Cassin's and House Finches. For what it's worth, the flight call of Purple Finch is not unlike the flight call of Lawrence's Goldfinch, though a little less musical.
Plumage: male and female

Similar Species Discussion

House Finch was ruled out by vocalizations, the much more extensive purplish-raspberry color of the males, and the strong facial patterns of the females. Cassin's Finch was ruled out by the purplish-raspberry color of the males (not brightest on the forecrown as in Cassin's) and by vocalizations (the key distinction for females). Pine Grosbeak was ruled out by size, coloration (especially for females), and vocalizations.

Previous Experience

I used to see Purple Finches regularly, especially in winter, when I was growing up in South Dakota and going to college in Massachusetts. In recent years I have seen them only a few times, e.g., in spring migration in Arkansas.
