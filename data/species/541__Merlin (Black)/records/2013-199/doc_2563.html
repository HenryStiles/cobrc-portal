Description of the Bird

I observed this bird near Union Reservoir. It was sitting in a tree half asleep. It had obviously recently fed (full crop) and watched me throughout the 24 minutes of observation with minimal concern and periodically falling asleep (many of my photos it’s eyes are closed).

The bird was a small Falcon, larger than American Kestrel and smaller than Prairie Falcon. Size was consistent with Merlin. This is a sub-species report, as obviously Merlin are not rare in the state, however records of F.c. suckleyi, I believe, are fairly unreported and unknown in the state of Colorado.

This Merlin’s back was the color of dark chocolate, with the chest area being heavily streaked with dark brown against a tan colored base with pronounced white spotted feather markings on the mid-abdomen.  Due to this bird's full crop the chest streaks were somewhat less visible than expected with more of the tan showing through, however still observed.  The face too was very dark brown, with only a slight hint of facial mask pattern and a fine pale supercilium. The tail showed several thin buff colored bands, much more restricted in thickness and less pronounced than the Merlin species I was more familiar with.  All of these traits were consistent with the suckleyi sub-species of Merlin.

Similar Species Discussion

There is no other falcon species that I am familiar with that shows this color pattern and fits the small size of this bird. Although specied such as Gyrfalcon can be dark, they would be substantially larger than this bird was. The Taiga race of Merlin can be dark at times, however the overall tone of this bird was much darker with a much less discernable facial pattern, heavier streaked chest with less tail pattern, and the spotted feathers on the abdomen with dark brown base are classic for F.c. suckleyi.

Resources Used

I consulted with Steve Mlodinow, who is familiar with the species from living in Washington for many years.  I also consulted with Jerry Ligouri who confirmed the ID.  He also told me that "Black" Merlins are annual and expected during migration in Utah, where he lives.  I did additional research online looking at many photos of the different races of Merlin.

Previous Experience

I am unfamiliar with this sub-species, only having seen photos of them prior.  I am quite familiar with "Prairie" Merlin, which are regularly seen in Colorado, and slightly familiar with "Taiga" Merlin in this state.
