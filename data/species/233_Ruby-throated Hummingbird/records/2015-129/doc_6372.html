Description of the Bird

First noticed the hummingbird interacting and fighting with a female Black-chinned Hummingbird in a tree near our hummingbird feeders.  The bird stayed for four days and visited several of our feeders during that time.  It hung around our feeders most of that time, even when it was not eating.

All the other male hummingbirds that were present during the summer had migrated by this time, and only females/immatures remained.  The Ruby-throated was the only male hummingbird coming to our feeders at the time.

The field marks used to identify the bird included a solid green back, a black tail without orange markings, a black throat that glowed red in some lights, and orange, yellow and green in other lighting conditions, and a black stripe under the bill above the gorget.  The flanks were colored gray-green; the breast was white.

Similar Species Discussion

Rufous Hummingbird was eliminated because this male lacked orange plumage.
Broad-tailed Hummingbird was eliminated because the bird had no orange in tail and because the gorget shone green.
The Calliope Hummingbird was eliminated because the gorget was not striped.
The Black-chinned Hummingbird male was eliminated because the gorget shone red, orange and green in different lighting conditions instead of purple.

Resources Used

The National Geographic Field Guide to the Birds of North America.

Previous Experience

We've been birding in Colorado for approximately three years and have seen and identified over 300 species.  We've had hummingbird feeders for several years and have observed all the common species.  This looked nothing like the other males.
