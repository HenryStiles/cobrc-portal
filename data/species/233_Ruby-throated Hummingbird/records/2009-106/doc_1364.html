Description of the Bird

Briefly, the bird was a smallish hummingbird, green above, with a red-orange gorget, white breast, green-gray belley, with a small and straight bill, black wings, black forked tail, and black wings.

Specifically, the forecrown was a deep green with grayish overtones. This same coloration was on the crown, nape, back, and rump. The tail was entirely blackish-brown, with the retrices showing slightly lighter brown feather margins (feather wear?). The tail showed no white spots on the corners or other colors on the main portions of the shafts. The tail was deeply forked (see attached photos). The eye was black, the lores dark blackish gray. There was an inconspicuous light gray postocular spot. The bill was dark and short, mostly straight with a very slight downward curve evident only at close range, and its length was about equal to the width of the head when viewed from the side. The gorget overall appeared red with strong orange overtones, being most orangish on the lower part and blackish along its top edge. The breast below the gorget was bright white. The belly was evenly green-gray from its border with the white breast down to the undertail area. The legs were dark gray bordering on black, as best I could tell. The wings were black.
Behaviors: This male was coming into a standard hummingbird feeder (I do not know the brand), usually to the same port, but not always, about every 10 minutes and feeding for 2 minutes or so. It's flight was relatively silent, compared to black-chins or broadtailed males.
Call: none
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

The gorget color was distinctly red-orange compared to the rose red typical of the other expected hummingbird in this area, broad-tailed hummingbird. The bill length was much shorter than black-chinned and the forecrown was green, not gray like black-chinned, not red like Anna's. Also the bill straightness was not like black-chinned. The tail was deeply forked and black, without any other coloration which combined with the gorget coloration eliminates anything else. The tail was longer than the wings by a considerable margin (eliminating calliope) and the 3 retrices showing when at rest is good for separation with black-chinned.
