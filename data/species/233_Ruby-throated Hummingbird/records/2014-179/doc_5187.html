Description of the Bird

During a CFO fieldtrip, our group observed a hummingbird at the Lion's Club Fishin' Hole on the east side of Holyoke.  During a period of roughly 5 minutes, the hummingbird visited a trumpet vine flower on the south side of the pond several times and twice perched on nearby trees.  The hummingbird was never in one location for more than 10 seconds during the period we observed it.  I did not hear any sounds from the hummingbird, but noise pollution would have made it difficult to hear it.

Through binoculars, we were able to see plain white undersides including the throat, and a thin, pale rufous strip on the flanks that was visible when the bird was hovering but obscured by the wings when the bird was perched. Other features are noted from two photos (included in this report), low quality but showing consistent features. Key features are (1) short, thick, and rather straight bill; (2) gray auriculars with white spot behind eye; (3) wingtips that are narrow and straight; and (4) tail projecting beyond wingtips. All of these characteristics are typical for an immature female Ruby-throated Hummingbird. The combination of gray and green on the crown is less typical for Ruby-throated Hummingbirds but is still found in some (e.g., photo by Bill Schmoker at http://www.schmoker.org/BirdPics/2007/Hummers/RTHU_fem3.jpg).  Since the viewing angle relative to the sun was not optimal and the photo resolution is poor, it's unclear if the photos of the Holyoke hummingbird accurately depict the crown colors.

Similar Species Discussion

The candidate species are the following small, plain-looking, immature/female hummingbirds:  Anna's, Costa's, Black-chinned, Ruby-throated, Calliope, and Broad-tailed.  Regional ebird reports would indicate that among these species, Ruby-throated, Calliope, and Broad-tailed are most likely at this location.  Since the tail projected well beyond the wingtips, Calliope and Costa's can be ruled out.  Broad-tailed is eliminated because the observed bird did not have the spotted throat and more extensive rufous undersides characteristic of this species.  Anna's is eliminated by the lack of gray/green flanks.  Black-chinned Hummingbird is ruled out by the short bill and the narrow wingtips.  The location of the observed hummingbird is also well outside the range of Black-chinned Hummingbird, a species not known to wander.
