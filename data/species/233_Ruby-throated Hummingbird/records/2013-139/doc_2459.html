Habitat

Foothills desert shrubs with some willows where water runoff creeks had formed.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  8 X 42 Bushnell Binoculars
Distance:  estimated 50 feet at furthest point and 10 at closest
Light:  Observation was almost entirely during sunny conditions with the sun directly behind me as I was looking at the bird. While trying to confirm field marks I moved to various positions along a 180 degree semi-circle to try and get different views. Thick shrubs prevented me from completing views from all angles.

Description of the Bird

Hummingbird with metallic green back and head, olive green on face around the eye with no white showing. Dark eye and black patch under the eye and throat. A slightly larger reddish patch on the throat under the black patch. Sides were an olive-brown color. Breast and belly were and off-white color. Undertail coverts were not able to be seen during the observation. Tail was black - did not see any other coloring despite multiple views from different angles. Size was indeterminate - could not ascertain if it was bigger or smaller than broad-tailed's seen earlier in afternoon.
Behavior: Bird would sit at the crown of a set of shrubbery for about 2-3 minutes and then take off and shoot straight up into the air, dive almost directly down towards the small runoff creek and then land in the exact same spot. While perched it twist head to each side in quick twitchy scanning motions.
Call: No recognizable call or song
Plumage: Breeding

Similar Species Discussion

I started by observing the general head markings of the bird and upon noticing the black patch under the eye and the red throat patch elminated black-chinned hummingbird. I then spent time trying to get different angles of the bird to see if the bird had any white patching near the eye and it did not - I also got multiple angled views of the tail which was consistantly black from all angles. Based on these observations I eliminated Broad-tailed. The bird observed was not consistent at all with Calliope. The lack of any rufous coloring eliminated most other hummingbirds. I could not eliminate the possibility of a hybrid cross.

Resources Used

I referenced my National Geographic field guide for initial reference while observing the bird. I further referenced photos from multiple websites to get a better confirmation of whether I was mistaken in my ID. With the exception of the coloring of sides of the bird it matched what I saw in my field guide and photos.

Previous Experience

I have seen numerous broad-tailed and black-chinned hummingbirds and other non-similar species in the Arizona Desert, but I have never seen a ruby-throated before.
