Description of the Bird

I photographed 3 hummingbirds in the willows and cottonwoods at the north end of the Lamar Community College woods and then identified them afterwards.  One was a female (and presumably immature) Ruby-throated Hummingbird, and the other two were Black-chinned Hummingbirds.  The contrast seen in the photos of the two species was helpful in identifying the Ruby-throated Hummingbird described here.

Some characteristics seen in the photos are:  (1) a rather high-contrast head pattern including dark mask and white spot behind the eye, (2) bright green crown and slightly green forehead, (3) short bill relative to Black-chinned Hummingbirds, (4) narrow and straight wingtips, and (5) tail projecting beyond wingtips.  The bird is identified as an immature female by the clean, white throat.

Similar Species Discussion

The most similar species is (immature female) Black-chinned Hummingbird.  I obtained photos of both Black-chinned and Ruby-throated Hummingbirds on the date of these observations.  The bird in this report is identified as a Ruby-throated rather than a Black-chinned Hummingbird based on the relatively short bill, the bright green crown, the primary shape (narrow and straight), and the high-contrast head pattern.

Previous Experience

Although I used to live where Ruby-throated Hummingbirds were common, I never looked at them closely.  During the last 2 years, I have gained a lot of experience observing the common hummingbirds in Colorado:  Broad-tailed, Rufous, Calliope, and Black-chinned.
