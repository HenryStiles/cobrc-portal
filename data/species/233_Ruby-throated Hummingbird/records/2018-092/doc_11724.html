Description of the Bird

There were at least two RTHUs present, both adults, though I believe that a third hummer was present, but I saw it only briefly and poorly, so could not be convinced that it was not one of the birds that I'd already seen.

The first bird that I saw was a female, which came into Janeal's hummer feeder next to the house three times while I was visiting, but its visits were brief (total time for about a minute).  I saw the bird well only in shade, so the coloration was muted.  Regardless, the upperparts were still obviously bright green and that feathering seemed to lack pale fringes (thus indicating age).  The whitish underparts contrasted strongly with the upperparts and with the obvious black mask that curled down a bit behind the eye and was counter-pointed by a bright white post-ocular spot.  The throat was white and apparently unmarked.  I never saw the flanks well enough to discern color.  The tail appeared dark, but with large white tips to the outermost rectrices.  At one point, I could see the wing tips well, and the outermost primaries were fairly pointed (shown reasonably well in one pic).  The bill was black, straight, and of only moderate length.

The second bird, which had been found by Janeal earlier that day, was a male (she had been unaware of the female that I found), and it came in to the feeder twice, both for relatively extended visits (total time approached five minutes). Its upperparts were quite bright emerald green, and its head appeared similar to that of the first bird, except that the throat was nearly entirely an iridescent ruby red color when viewed at the right angle (though it varied from dark to orange to red); the very upper edge -- the chin -- was black.  The tail was entirely dark, though I could not discern its shape, as it was not visible either time that I saw it perched.  The outermost primaries were fairly pointed, and the bill was black, straight, and shorter than that of the female.

I saw neither bird wag its tail in hovering flight, though I saw the male in hovering flight only very briefly.

Similar Species Discussion

I did not really consider any other species, as they were both obviously RTHUs at first sight.  However, an ID of BCHU for either bird can be ruled out by aspects of bill length and shape, throat color, upperparts color, and outer-primary shape.  ANHU is ruled out by the white underparts and aspects of facial pattern.
