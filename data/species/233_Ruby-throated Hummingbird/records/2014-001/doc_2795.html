Description of the Bird

Firstly the supporting photographs I believe are the main part of this discription so I'll keep the description brief.
This Hummingbird first drew my attention by it’s extremely aggressive behaviour at the feeder and what seemed like a pronounced notched tail. Although I realise both the above features are not indicative of an immature RBHU it made me look more closely at the bird. My initial impression was of an immature or female type Black-chinned Hummingbird, a species which had been a scarce but regular attendee at the feeders. But this bird seemed somewhat brighter and contrasting about the “face”. It also did not display an insistent tail pumping action whilst hovering, something I have come to find quite common and unique among Black-chinned.
Realising good quality photo's of the birds primary and tail formula/pattern would be an excellent way to deal with an immature/female hummingbird, I obtained some decent shots to review on screen.
When I looked closer (on screen) at the primary formula I began to think it had something going for it in terms of it being a Ruby-throated Hummingbird. 
Thinner and bunched P1 to P6 contrasting with a thicker & more widely spaced P7 to P10 confirmed it to be a Archilochus .This bird also had a distinctly slimmer and tapered tip to P10, quite markedly differing from the thicker P8 & P9. A feature strongly suggestive of Ruby-throated. Black-chinned should have broad spatulated primary tips right up to and including P10. Other plumage features the bird had which favoured RBHU over BTHU included the contrasting dark auricular and a greener crown. The bird also appeared a bluer, bottle green than BTHU. A pointed P6 and spotted throated confirms it to be a male. (All the above features can be seen in the photos)

Similar Species Discussion

All possible Hummingbirds on the North Amercian list were considered, the photo's of the primary formula narrow it down to two options BTHU & RBHU, the thinner tip to P10 contrasting with the wider tips to P9 & P8 would lead us to the conclusion it can only be a Ruby-throated. 

Resources Used

Sibley guide, the interent and circulating the images amongst other birders that included: Nancy Newfield, Sheri Willaimson & Pete Pyle.
They all agreed the bird was an immature male Ruby-throated Hummingbird.

Previous Experience

From May until October hummingbirds were present on the feeders, throughout August in very high numbers. These included, Rufous, Black-chinned, Broad-tailed & Calliope.
I have seen Ruby-throated in the Eastern part of Nort America.
