Habitat

Habitat was a mix of open fields, large cottonwoods, roadside vegetation including juniper and toward latter observation period the bird was feeding around a large private pond with shoreline cottonwoods and willows. The bird typically perched up in a snag or on top of whatever nearby tree was there but was also observed perching in the pond side willows and even once on the roadside fence.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski EL 8.5 x 42 and Swarovski 20-60x 85 mm scope.
Distance:  Variable but as close as 10 meters including when first observed.
Light:  Heavy overcast in the beginning and moderately heavy rain in latter observation period. So white/gray skies but not too impactful to identify this beast.

Description of the Bird

Found and identified by Linda Chittum and Ada Jones.  First observed perched in a snag at approximately 10 m distance when we were driving down a country road.  We stopped and got binoculars on the bird.  Obvious kingbird and from slightly above and viewing the bird from the back the dark blackish cap immediately stood out.  The lack of any white terminal tail band immediately removed any consideration of EAKI as did apparent bulk and size.  The bird turned it's head and body and immediately the huge, thick black bill and yellow coloration ventrally grabbed my attention whereupon I immediately exited the vehicle and proceeded slowly down the road to improve my angle and started taking photos.  The bird moved to a different tree nearby and shortly thereafter we heard it vocalizing.  For the next approximately 3 hours we watched the bird as it moved around the area, perching in different trees and periodically calling.  I'll spare a description of the call and attach audio file.  After calling Dave Price, a local birder living fairly close, we kept on the bird until he arrived.  However, it just disappeared for about 45 minutes until I eventually relocated it further west on the same road after hearing it's call.  Additional local birders were notified and Linda and Ada stayed around watching the bird until other birders arrived and getting them on the bird. 

Identification features include very large, thick black bill.  Blackish cap, darkest around the eye, transitioning to more olive-brown at rear of crown and solid olive-brown coloration dorsally.  Flight feathers more brownish color with very thin pale margin on tertials.  The tail was of similar coloration.  White throat transitioning to light gray and yellow upper breast.  From there smooth, fairly bright yellow through undertail coverts.  The underwing coverts are yellowish.  

After reviewing photos this is clearly an adult bird, despite the brighter yellow coloration ventrally.  The outer primaries (P8 - P10) are emarginated though I lack experience and knowledge in judging whether this degree might be sufficient to conclude a male.  Of interest is the ongoing secondary molt with some slightly brown/rusty secondaries, feathers still in sheath, and partial growth of new secondaries. 

Similar Species Discussion

All kingbirds initially in consideration but the black cap ruled out WEKI, Tropical, Cassin's, Couch's, and Gray.  As previously noted, EAKI was easily eliminated due to lack of white terminal tail band, grayer back, smaller size and bill.  Initially also considered Gray Kingbird but as soon as I saw the yellow belly that was eliminated from consideration as did the blackish cap.  The vocalizations confirmed it as a TBKI.

Resources Used

In the field didn't use any references other than listening to the different TBKI calls in the Merlin app.  While waiting for others to show up briefly looked at Sibley and National Geographic field guides.

Further review later including Birds of the World and Vagrancy in Birds, not so much identification resources but just trying to learn a bit more about the species.

Previous Experience

Linda has seen this species once in Arizona 9 years ago.  Ada has not previusly seen TBKI.  Very limited experience.

Both have extensive experience with expected kingbirds - Eastern, Western, and more limited experience with Cassin's.  Both have observed Tropical and Couch's Kingbirds outside the country.
