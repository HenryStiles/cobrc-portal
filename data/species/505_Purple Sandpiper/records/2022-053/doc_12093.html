Description of the Bird

The bird was initially found by Mark Peterson the prior day and photographed in bright sunny conditions.  

The bird is an immature bird given the crisp feather edgings to all the upper body feathers giving a scaly look.  The bird sported orangish legs and about 1/3 of the distal end of the bill being orange.  Further description can be see best in the photos I am attaching.  "A picture is worth a thousand words"...I'm submitting several thousand "words" :-)  

Similar Species Discussion

Debate was whether this bird is an immature Purple Sandpiper or an immature Rock Sandpiper.  Both can be VERY similar.  Norm and I were leaning towards Rock Sandpiper, but have learned much since relocating, photographing and consulting about this bird.

Rachel Hopper had Kevin Zimmer (Alaska tour guide experience) weigh in and he felt the bird was a Purple Sandpiper.  Also, Norm Erthal contacted Paul Lehman (has spent MANY a fall in Gambel on Saint Lawrence Island and has seen MANY Rock Sandpipers of both races in immature plumage) and his impression was Purple Sandpiper (Paul's response is included below):

 

From: Paul Lehman <lehman.paul@verizon.net>

To: "nerthal@comcast.net" <nerthal@comcast.net>, "greatgrayowl@aol.com" <greatgrayowl@aol.com>

Date: 10/10/2022 6:35 PM

Subject: Re: Sandpiper in Colorado

Norm,

I am perfectly happy with it as a young Purple Sandpiper. The color of the bill base and legs is great for Purple and too orangey for any subspecies of Rock Sandpiper. Some duller Purples are extremely hard to tell from Rock, but not this bird! I'd also add that Purple is infinitely more likely to occur than Rock in Colorado, or anywhere inland--even farther west. There are only a couple published reports of inland Rock Sandpiper ever, from Yukon and BC areas, and it is my humble opinion that those records should be re-evaluated just to be sure!

Paul
