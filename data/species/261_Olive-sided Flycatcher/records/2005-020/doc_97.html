Description of the Bird

My initial feeling regarding shape was that of a large Flycatcher, with a short tail compared to Myiarchus flycatchers. The bird had an entirely dark head and auriculars, contrasting with a clean white throat. The breast had a distinct "vest" pattern with dark sides and a white center. The belly was also clean white. Undertail coverts were dark, with short tail projection past the coverts.
Behaviors: The bird was seen perched on an aspen limb, singing. It remained at that perch for the duration of observation.
Call: The song was composed of three parts. First was a short chip, or whip type note. The second and third parts were longer ascending then descending whistles. This matched the classic "quick three beers" call I have heard from other OSFLs.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Intial elimination of other species was by the distinctive song. Size eliminated empidonax flycatchers. Eastern Phoebe was eliminated by breast pattern, head shape, and tail length. Greater Pewee was eliminated (post-observation) by breast pattern, as well as the strong contrast between the throat and the cheeks and the throat and the flanks.

Resources Used

Stokes Guide to Bird Songs was used to confirm the song and the Sibley Guide to Birds was used to refresh my memory on field marks for both Olive-sided Flycatcher and Greater Pewee. I did not have any resources present during the observation.

Previous Experience

I have observed approx. 10 Olive-sided Flycatchers, all in New England. Several of these birds were observed singing. I have seen many Eastern Phoebes in the East as well as in Colorado. I was able to observe a handful of Greater Pewees on a trip to W Mexico this past March. The majority, if not all of these birds were heard singing also.
