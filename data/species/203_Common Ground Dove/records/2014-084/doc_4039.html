Description of the Bird

I was just past the roofed overlook area about 100 yards from the parking lot at the Navajo State Park Wildlife Viewing Area. While walking from gazebo towards the bridge over the Piedra River, I spotted a smallish dove flying the opposite direction and away but at eye level, at a distance of about 10-15 m. I’m accustomed to seeing flushing Columbids like Mourning Dove and Eurasian Collared-Dove, and at first I recognized that this was another such bird, based on the speed of its flight and the very general proportions — that is, I knew right away it wasn’t, for example, a passerine or a raptor. But then it wasn’t so much the size of the bird that caught my attention (I am very careful not to prejudge the size of birds if I don’t have any reference objects nearby) but rather the noticeably red ends of the rounded wings on this dove, clearly viewable during its flight whether flapping or gliding.

So within just a second or two I guessed that this was a Common Ground Dove, or perhaps another Columbina dove. Any Columbina dove in Colorado is noteworthy, so I was immediately excited and followed it over to where it appeared to have landed. I hurried back to the sheltered area and when I looked down at the riprap rocks that buttress the berm on which the footpath sits, I saw the dove very clearly in good midday light, with the sun mostly behind me. I drew up my bins and noted the light, scaled neck, the spots on the ruddy wing coverts, and the proportionally short tail. I didn’t note the color of the bill, but I am sure that if I’d taken more than just a few seconds to look at the bird in the binoculars, I’d have seen that it was reddish with a black tip. What I knew is that the overall diminutive size in comparison with the other regular Colorado doves, in combination with the in-flight views of the wing color and shape, this was a Common Ground Dove.

Similar Species Discussion

As mentioned in the earlier description, the most similar species to consider would be another Columbina dove, like Inca Dove or possibly Ruddy Ground Dove. Inca Dove however is much more scaly in appearance, and clear well-lit views through my bins showed a bird with scaliness only on the nape -- elsewhere, the bird was plainly marked except for spotting on the folded wing coverts. Ruddy Ground Dove was eliminated via the presence of at least some scaliness on the bird (female western Ruddy GD would be very plain in color and lack all scaliness), combined with the lack of any ruddy coloration below the neck or even any visible contrastiness in the coloration between the head and the rest of the body (as a male Ruddy GD would show).

The view of red outer primaries of the bird in flight eliminates all other regular Colorado columbids.

Resources Used

As mentioned in the earlier sections, I actually IDed the bird on sight as it was in flight and then in the couple moments after I viewed it through my Zeiss T*FL 7x42 binoculars. I purposefully delayed looking at the Sibley guide that I had handy in the car until I was able to compile some notes (which were written up later that day), so as not to bias my memory of the sighting. Unfortunately I was not able to obtain any photos of the bird, although I did get back to my car to retrieve my Canon DSLR and ran back to the place where I'd last seen the bird. It had left by that time, and several hours of searching the area afterwards were fruitless in refinding the bird.

Previous Experience

I've seen and photographed Common Ground Dove on several occasions over the past decade including some in the continental US, in Florida and Arizona. Most recently I found and photographed a few very cooperative individuals at close range along a roadside in Puerto Rico this past March (2014).

I've seen Inca Dove on a few occasions in southern Arizona as well as in Rocky Ford some years back.

I've seen Ruddy Ground Dove only in Central and South America (never in the US), most recently in 2011 on multiple occasions in Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru.

I also have experience with other congeners in South America like Croaking and Scaled Ground Doves, as well as other similarly proportioned ground-doves in other genera like Metriopelia and Claravis.
