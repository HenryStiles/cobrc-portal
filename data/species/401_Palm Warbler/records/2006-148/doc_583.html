Description of the Bird

the bird was about the same size as nearby Yellow-rumped Warblers, and had a similar shape. The tail was slightly shorter in comparison to the body, though. The bill and legs were black, and the bill was typical for a Dendroica warbler, thin and pointed.

The bird had a brownish cap contrasting with a bold pale supercilium. There was a bold dark eyeline that contrasted with the rest of the face. The remainder of the face was pale brownish gray, a bit paler than the cap. The upperparts were the same color, and faintly streaked with darker streaks on the back. The wings were brownish with some yellowish coloration on the edges of most of the feathers. There were two very faint brownish wingbars. The underparts were dingy colored with some faint but noticeable darker streaks. The undertail coverts were bright yellow and contrasted with the remainder of the underparts. The uppertail covers and rump were yellowish green, which contrasted somewhat with the brownish back, but not as much as the yellow rump of a Yellow-rumped Warbler. The tail was grayish brown, with bold white tail corners, but these tail corners were smaller than those on a Yellow-rumped Warbler.
Behaviors: The bird spent most of its time foraging on the ground, hopping along with small hops and insessently wagging its tail down. It would occasionally fly to the lower branches in various trees and hop around in a manner fairly typical of a warbler.
Call: none heard
Plumage: western race basic

Similar Species Discussion

Most species of warbler were ruled out by the habit of foraging on the ground and tail wagging. The yellowish rump contrasting with brownish upperparts ruled out all species other than Yellow-rumped, Cape May, very dull Orange-crowned, and Magnolia. All of these species were ruled out by the bold eyeline and supercilium, dingy underparts with faint streaks, contrasting cap, and habits and behavior.
