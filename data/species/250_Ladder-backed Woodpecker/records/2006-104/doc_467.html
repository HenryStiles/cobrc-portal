Description of the Bird

The bird was a small Picoides woodpecker, about half the size of nearby Northern Flickers (the only other woodpecker I observed that day at that location). The bill was typical of a woodpecker, medium length, straight, and with a somewhat blunt tip. Both it and the eye were dark.

The cap and nape were black. The bird's face was white with a bold black "bridle". The underparts were a off-white color, with some spotting across the chest, not too bold, as well as spotting down the flanks. The belly appeared to be unspotted, but I did not see this area too well. The back was black with bold and unbroken white bars across the length of the back. The wings were black with bold white spots on the coverts and wings. The central tail feathers were black, and the outer tail feathers boldly spotted black and white.
Behaviors: The bird was seen tapping on a large cottonwood about halfway up, as well as moving along the trunk and a few larger branches. At the end of the observation I watched it fly downstream.
Call: The bird was heard to give a soft "pik" a couple of times, but was mostly silent.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Most of the species of woodpeckers were ruled out by the small size, black and white plumage with no red, black bridle on a white face, black and white barred back, and spotting on some of the underparts.

Hairy Woodpecker was ruled out by the smaller size and barred back, as well as differing face pattern with a black bridle. Downy Woodpecker was ruled out by the same features (other than size), as well as the spotting below, longer bill, and more extensively spotted outer tail feathers.
