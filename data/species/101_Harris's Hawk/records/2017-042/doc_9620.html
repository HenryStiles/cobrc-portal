Description of the Bird

After the bird flew from Running Deer Natural Area, it crossed Prospect Road and landed in a small tree along the open field and plowed field off of the frontage road where it sat for 15-20 minutes. No calls were given. After about 20 minutes, if took off to the west and landed in another tree closer to Summit View Road. It sat there for another 3-5 minutes. The bird (facing us)was dark brownish from head to the start of the undertail coverts with patches of warmer brown. The undertail coverts were a snow white. The tail was obscured by a limb, but looked dark throughout. After about 15 minutes, the bird turned to face away from us. The shoulders were a warmer brown, but the bird was still dark throughout with the white sides to the undertail coverts showing and a white rump. The tail again was dark throughout. The tail was distinctly longer than the wing tips. The legs were bare of any feathers and yellow. There were no jesses nor bands on the legs.

The bird then flew from the second perch and started to soar and glide heading west and north. While gliding, the bird had the clear arched form of the wings, curved downward.

Similar Species Discussion

The rufous form of western Red-tail Hawk was eleiminated by the white undertail coverts and rump and by the all-dark tail that did not appear to me to be banded. I did not see the end of the tail to check for a white tip.

Rough-legged Hawk was eliminated by the white undertail coverts and white rump and also by the bare legs. Also the appearance of a dark unbanded tail would eliminate Rough-legged.

Dark-phase Ferruginous Hawk was eliminated by the white undertail coverts and white rump transitioning into the all-dark tail. Also eliminated by the bare legs.

Female Northern Harrier was eliminated by overall darker appearance, dark unbanded tail, and white undertail coverts, as well as typical "hawk" face.

Both eagles were eliminated by the smaller size of the bird.

Resources Used

My memory of seeing Harris' Hawks in Texas and Arizona. I refreshed my memory about the legs when I got home with the Peterson Field Guide "Hawks" by Clark and Wheeler.

Also, David Wade and Rachel Hopper and Cole Wild took pictures of the bird showing the white undertail coverts and white rump and (in the case of David's picture flying) the warmer chestnut shoulders and white tip to the tail.

Previous Experience

I have seen Numerous Harris' Hawks during my three trips to south Texas and six trips to Arizona. I have seen several (4-5) rufous Red-tailed Hawks here in Colorado. I have seen numerous Rough-legged Hawks here in Colorado. I have seen one dark-phase Ferruginous Hawk.
