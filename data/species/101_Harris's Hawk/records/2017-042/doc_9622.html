Description of the Bird

Bird was initially seen perched in a small tree to the west of my viewing point, views here were brief and somewhat obstructed by vegetation. Shortly after locating the bird (say 2-3 minutes later) it flew north across Prospect Road and perched in a stand of large cottonwoods. During the flight, my view through my camera was virtually unobstructed. Furthermore, upon landing, the bird was in a realtively unobstructed poisiton within the tree and was observed for no more than 5 minutes through the spotting scope. At this point the bird flew east into a second stand of trees and remained there, almost entriely blocked by vegetation, until my departure. 


The bird had a farily heavy bill that was noticable at a distance both due to its size and it being nearly entirely yellow from the base towards the tip. Photos show the front 1/4 (or so) of the bill to be a light grayish color. The head was uniformly dark brown which extended down the back towards the tail and down the front through the breast and belly. The lesser and median coverts showed a bright rufous color. This coloration also included the underwing coverts and axillaries. The wing flight feathers retained the dark brown color of the body feathers. The upper- and under-tail coverts were pure white and contrasted markedly with the rest of the plumage. The retricies showed a wide dark band and white tips. Legs were bright yellow and unfeathered. 


The bird stayed relatively low and never soared so wing and tail shape were difficult to judge. When perched, the legs appeared long and were clearly visible and the bird was fairly upright. I did not note the degree of primary projection. The bird did not vocalize. eBird report: http://ebird.org/ebird/view/checklist/S41052577

Similar Species Discussion

The combination of white tail coverts, rufous "shoulders", solid brown body feathers, and large yellow bill rules out all other regularly occuring ABA raptors. Below are the species I would consider most similar and the characteristics that eliminate them from consideration:

Ferruginous Hawk (Dark morph): Underside of wing flight feathers would be pale white and the tail would show no dark bands. Legs would be short and fully feathered. The HASH had dark flight feathers, a single tail band, and long unfeathered legs. 

Rough-legged Hawk (light morph): In flight this species would show a large white rump patch similar to the HASH however, the underwings would be well patterned with white primaries and dark markings near the "wrist". Tail would show a single white and dark band. The observed bird showedall dark primaries and a white band at the tip of the tail feathers with a band band in the middle. 

Red-tailed Hawk (dark adult): Perched this species could appear to share the dark body feathers and hints of rufous in the shoulders; however once in flight the solid red tail and the contrast between pale underwing flight feathers and underwing coverts would not match the dark underwing primaries and white and dark tail of the observed bird. 

Common Black Hawk: In less than ideal lighting this bird could be confused due to its all dark appearance, realtively large, yellowish bill, long yellow legs and largely dark wings. The observed bird clearly showed bright rufous "shoulders" and a distinctly brown body color, rather than the solid blackish color of the Common Black Hawk. 

Red-shouldered Hawk: The extensive patterning of the upper primaries and secondaries, small size, and multi-banded tail eliminates this bird. 

Golden Eagle: Juvenile eagles would show white markings on the tail but the observed bird showed a white band at the tail, not expected on an eagle. The observed bird was also significantly smaller and showed long unfeathered legs. 

Northern Harrier: This bird would show a white rump patch but the habits of the observed bird were contrary to the typically whimsical low flight of a harrier. Also, Young harriers would show patterning on the underside of the wing and multiple narrow bands on the tail. 

Resources Used

No resources were used at the time of the sighting. I was confident in the ID based on the observed field marks and my previous experience with this bird. The Sibley Gudie to Birds of North America was used to write the species comparison section of this report. 

Previous Experience

This is the first HASH I have seen in Colorado; however I have seen two individuals in South Texas in the spring of 2017. Save for the Common Black Hawk, I have had regular encounters with all of the other species listed in the "similar species" section.
