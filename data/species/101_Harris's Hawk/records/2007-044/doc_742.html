Description of the Bird

This is a dark buteo-like (but not) hawk with a long tail, upright posture, with reddish wing patches. The cere was boldly yellow on a hooked beak. The face was dark chestnut but there was a faint supercillium above the eye and there appeared to be a light eyelid surrounding the dark iris. The back of the head and nape was also dark chestnut brown but with light verticle streaks in it. The upper breast, seen on the perched bird was dark with wide white verticle streaks, but the light streaks were less wide than the surrounding dark "streaks". This was visible on the perched bird. The long yellow legs were visible along with yellowish talons.

In flight the bird had three areas of white underneath. The first was large white patches or windows on the inner primaries larger in size as compared to a Rough-legged Hawk. A second area of white was the lower belly and undertail coverts. It too was sizable and contrasted strongly with the birds upper belly breast (which didn't look as streaked in flight, face and much of the bird's tail However the tail appeared more like that of an adult showing as being dark for three quaters of its length with a small terminal white tip and a wide white bar at the proximal end of the tail.with a wide white subterminal band. At rest, the long tail was very frayed at the distal end and the coloration as described above was not noticeable on its tree perch. At rest the legs were noticabley longer than any North American Buteo and unfeathered tarsi.

On the perched bird the rufous areas were confined to the shoulder, but was not too visible on the angle I had but was much more visible when the bird flew. The rufous leg feathers were barred with thin white bands. The other area of rufous was just above the white lower belly and undertail, but this could actually have been the long leg feathers hanging down.

The posture of the perched bird was significant in that it showed a hunched back at times, but when alert before moving, it had an upright posture.
Behaviors: I watched the bird for over an hour where it was partially hidden from view while perched in the top center of a large tree. It hopped out of view twice and once it held its wings half closed and fell straight down and out of view. The bird was later seen flying low in a direct flight for about 200 meters where it landed on a roof top for about five minutes before flying out of sight, for me. It then was flushed by two birders, one who hadn't seen it yet and the bird ended up in the darkening skies, perched in another large tree, about 1/4 mile away from where I had first see it. The one time the bird disappeared (found again perched lower in the same tree) it is possible that it was hunting something on the trunk or lower branches of that tree.
Call: none heard
Plumage: in its 1st year apparently molting

Similar Species Discussion

The only two birds that I know that have the rufous tones with some dark chestnut are Savanna Hawk and Black-collared Hawk.

Black-collared Hawk has very broad rudous wings but a shore tail with two thin dark bands sparated by a broader, but fairly thin dark tail band. It has a white head and the cere is grayish.

Savanna Hawk comes closest, but can be eliminated by the lack of dark chestnut on its face, nape, and breast, and the rufous continues down through the whole ventral surface. It tail bands are much different than the Pueblo West bird also.

Additional Information

Comments: I also used Brandon Percival's photograph in addition to mine to study the plumage of this bird.

The big question is one of origin. According to Wheeler there are 7 accepted records of HAHA for CO, from Dec. (6) and Oct. (1). He says that five of the CO records are from Jefferson County (I am guessing this might five reports of that same bird), and one each from Larimer and Otero Counties. There is one breeding record from KS, in Meade County in SW KS in 1963, a nest that failed due to a hail storm. He does state that there is at least six records from NV and says that they are from the Spring but doesn't give any location information, nor does he put any dots for the NV birds on his map. Other accepted records he lists are from NE, two, one from Jan. '94 in Scott's Bluff County, and then in OK, at least 7 records from Oct. - Jan, but Birds of North America Online says that there are many records from OK but doesn't talk about any.

All of the authors that I have read describe the juvenile birds as looking very similar to the Pueblo bird. Steve Howell says that juveniles attain adult plumage after their first prebasic molt but doesn't say how long it takes before the first prebasic molt. One author says that juvenile birds attain adult plumage after one year.

Wheeler says that both juveniles and adults have been found out of range.

All authors say that HAHAs breed anytime of year depending on food supplies. Wheeler says that AZ birds often breed in early spring and again in late summer. He says that AZ juveniles are often nest helpers but not HAHAs from other states. He says that HAHAs are increasing in SO CAL, but declining in TX.

Most authors say that HAHAs have to be near water and drink and bathe at least once a day. They eat a lot of quail according to one AZ study, plus small mammals including cottontails, birds, and lizards, but no records of eating snakes. There are a lot of quail in the neighborhood.

I am trying to find out if falconers are required to put leg bands on their captive birds.

Most authors says that HAHAs are tame, except in NM, and that they hunt from open perches (Pueblo West rooftops) and will use the shade of trees when it gets hot. They hunt most in early morning and late in the day.
