Description of the Bird

The bird was a medium-sized buteoine-like hawk, but slightly slimmer and with a longer tail. It was dark brown above and below with chestnut shoulders and thighs. The chestnut on some of the scapulars were dark-brown centered. There was some white feathering acoss portions of the breast, belly, and sides of neck. The throat was also mostly white. The bill was a bluish-gray, darker toward the tip. Evident was the large, bright yellow facial patch and gape. There was a thin yellow eye-ring surround the dark brown eyes. The long tail was mostly black, except for hite bases to the rectrices and a broad white terminal tail band. When in flight, you could see the brown primaries and secondaries and that the bird was in active molt. The undersides of the wing were mostly dark, except for rusty coverts and a whitish patch to the inner primaries.
Behaviors: Bird was nervous and stayed hidden, prched in a Siberian Elm tree. It later flushed from that tree and relocated within the confines of an Austrian Pine near Brandon Percival's house.
Call: None heard

Additional Information

Comments: From Terry Ireland in response to Bill Maynard's question about falconry birds - "I was quite sure the bird should be banded and permitted if it was a captive bird but wanted to check with more knowledgable people. I called our local LE contact but he didn't return my call so I called our Migratory Bird Office and talked to someone a few minutes ago. The bird should be banded with a yellow or seamless band if the bird was reared from captivity. If it was a wild caught bird a black nylon band should have been placed on it by the CDOW (through FWS authorization). Since the hawk has no band it may be totally wild or slipped the band somehow or escaped before a band could be placed on it. A migratory bird, especially a wild
one, cannot be possessed without a permit. The person I talked to was Linda Downey in our Migratory Birds Permit Office, at 303-236-8170.

I (Larry) sent an email to the President of the Colorado Falconry organization. I asked him if anyone in that organization or within neighboring states had noted an escaped Harris' Hawk. He did some digging and told me that no one had lost a bird. All were accounted for. Unfortunately, I do not have that email anymore.
