Description of the Bird

The bird was a fairly large sized loon (about 5-10 percent smaller than the nearby Double-crested Cormorants). The crown, face, and nape were a dark slate-gray, with no contrasting paler gray nape. The throat appeared black because of the distance and poor light, and color could not be determined. The sides of the neck adjacent to the throat had thin but bold vertical white stripes over the dark slate-gray, extending almost the length of the dark throat. The oarts of the bird's chest that were visible were white, with thin vertical black stripes of the sides of the chest. The back of the bird was black, with large and bold sqaure white spots. The bird had a bold white flank patch, that when the bird rode high on the water was white over most of the length of the flank, and had a white "bubble" at the rear of the flank that rose above the rest of the flank patch. When the bird rode low on the water, typically after diving (when almost the entiretly of the body was under water), only this bubble of white was visible, but there was never a time in the three hours I saw the bird when this white bubble was not visible and bold, no matter how low the bird rode on the water. When the bird rolled over when preening I was able to see the entirely white belly. The vent area of the bird was also clean white, with no markings. The undertail coverts and tail were black.

The bird's bill was black, and fairly large, approaching the size when compared to the head of some smaller billed Common Loons, and noticeably larger when compared to the head than any Pacific Loon I have seen. The head had a bump just above the eye, and a flatish crown, giving the bird a lumpy headed appearance, also more reminiscent of Common Loon rather than Pacific Loon. The overall head shape of the bird did not have the "puffy" look that Pacific Loons often have, especially in alternate plumage.
Behaviors: the bird spent much of it's time just swimming around the reservoir, especially when I first found it. Later on it was diving actively, and moving quite quickly to different parts of the southern end of the lake. Towards the end of the observation the bird spent a lot of time preening, and flapping its wings.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

All other loons other than Pacific (and all other species, for that matter) were ruled out by the distincive alternate plumage with gray head, dark throat, white stripes on the side of the neck, black back with bold white spots, etc.

Pacific Loon was ruled out especially by the white flank patch with the "bubble" at the rear end of the flank, that was always visible even when the bird barely had it's body above water. Other marks used include the complete lack of any vent strap, the evenly dark gray head that was both darker than I would expect for a Pacific Loon, and without the paler contrasting nape I would expect for that species. The head shape was lumpier and less "puffy" than I would expect for Pacific Loon, and it did not have the delicate look that Pacific Loon usually has. The bill was larger than that of any Pacific Loon I have seen when compared to the head.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted during and after the observation to confirm ID, as were pictures of the Arctic Loon from Franktown in 2002

Previous Experience

I have only seen one Arctic Loon previously, the Franktown bird in 2002, which was in basic plumage, which I studied extensively and closely.

I have seen many hundreds of Pacific Loons in basic plumage, mostly on the west coast, but many in Colorado. I have only seen two Pacific Loons in alternate plumage, one a flying bird in Colorado, and one a swimming bird I studied closely in Washington.
