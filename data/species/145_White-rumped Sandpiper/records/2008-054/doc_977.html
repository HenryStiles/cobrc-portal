Description of the Bird

the bird was a small shorebird, the same size as an adjacent Baird's Sandpiper. The overall shape was also very similar to that of the Baird's Sandpiper, with fairly chunky body proportions, with a fairly short neck for a shorebird, but with fairly long (and black) legs. The wings appeared to be slightly longer than the tail, but this was hard to judge at the distance we were at, and was mostly determined by the similarity to the Baird's with this feature. The bill was fairly long (about as long as the head), thin, tapering slightly to a blunt tip, and black.

The crown of the bird was medium brown, with a slight rufous tinge, with indistinct streaks. The supercilium was whitish, contrasting with the darker crown. The chest of the bird was sparsely but evenly streaked with dark streaks on an otherwise white background; the overall appearance of the underparts was very pale, due to the sparseness of the streaks. There was no area on the chest where the streaks were more concentrated, such as would be expected for Semipalmated Sandpiper. The streaks on the chest extended downwards along the flanks as well.

The back of the bird was pale grayish brown, with some inconspicuous rufous highlights that were not concentrated into any discernible stripes or patches. The wings, in flight, had a white stripe at the base of the flight feathers; the flight feathers were otherwise blackish. The rump was clean white, with no dark stripe through it. The tail was brown, with a short black stripe down the middle.
Behaviors: the bird always associated closely with a Baird's Sandpiper. I first spotted it flying in with the Baird's; it then foraged on the small muddy are before disappearing behind the taller grass.
Call: none heard
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Most species of shorebirds were ruled out by the small size, similar to that of Baird's Sandpiper. Least Sandpiper was ruled out by the larger size when compared to Baird's, the relatively longer legs creating a less dumpy look to the bird, the black legs, the white rump, and the much paler underparts. Western Sandpiper was ruled out by the larger size than expected when compared to Baird's, the lack of strong rufous tones in the head and back, the white rump, and the streaking down the flanks. Semipalmated Sandpiper was ruled out by the larger size than expected when compared to Baird's, the longer legs, the paler underparts, the streaking on the flanks, and the white rump. Baird's Sandpiper was ruled out by the much paler and whiter underparts, the completely white rump, by the overall paler color, and by the subtle rufous tones on the back.
