Description of the Bird

Rachel Hopper and I were sitting in my car, trying to unfreeze after looking at a first-winter Little Gull that I'd found earlier. Nick Komar and Cole Wild pulled up, and we pointed to the flock of Bonaparte's Gulls (not far off) in which the bird was feeding. After a few moments, Nick (I believe it was Nick) said, "There it is." Cole quickly located the bird, saying "I got it." One of them said, "Nice adult." To which Rachel and I said, nearly in unison, It's not an adult, its an immature." Nick responded, "Well this bird is an adult, with black underwings."

We quickly jumped out of the car, and there it was, a lovely adult basic-plumaged Little Gull. The mantle was entirely pearly gray, excepting a white trailing edge to the wings that extended out to outer primaries. The tail was entirely white, and fan-shaped (ie, not wedge-shaped like a Ross's Gull). The underparts were entirely white. The underwing appeared entirely slaty black, excepting a white trailing edge to the wings that extended to outermost primaries (the small amount of white that should be on underwing coverts was not seen, but then again, I've never noted that on a Little Gull in past, so ...) The head was mostly white, but fine details were hard to ascertain at the distance involved (about 100 yards or so). There appeared to be some grayish smudging on top of crown and/or on rear crown. The bill appeared small and all black.

Overall, the bird was smaller than the nearby Bonaparte's Gulls with more rounded wings.

The bird was observed for a total of about 5 minutes over a 20-30 minute period at distances of 100 - 500 yards.
Behaviors: See above. Feeding in flock of Bony's with similar dipping to surface feeding style
Call: not heard
Plumage: adult basic

Similar Species Discussion

Ross's Gull would not be so dark on underwing and would've had wedge-shaped tail. Also, wingtips tend to be more pointed and white trailing edge does not extend onto outer primaries
