Description of the Bird

1 Adult: Very large, easily double the size of the nearby Canada Geese. A large, long-necked, white bird, with black eyes and black bill (see photos) is obviously a swan. The adult bird appeared to be uniformly very white, with blackish eyes and a long, straight bill. The all black bill eliminates Whooper Swan and Mute Swan, but not Tundra Swan and Trumpeter Swan. The bill was longer and straighter than Tundra Swan. A front view of the bird revealed the black facial skin tapering to a point (V) between the eyes, not the straighter (U) of a Tundra Swan. A profile of the bill shows the edge of black facial skin extending from the eye to the bottom of the lower mandible to be a smooth, gentle arc lacking the curve at the gape of a Tundra Swan. The black facial skin tapers at the eye to about the diameter of the eye, whereas the taper on a Tundra Swan is decidedly narrower than the diameter of the eye. The back of the bird was more evenly rounded than Tundra, and the tail was lower to the water than Tundra Swan tail.
1 Juvenile: The juvenile bird appears to be slightly smaller and with a shorter neck than the adult, and its bill is not so long and straight as the adult. It is obvoulsly not an adult because it has no bright white anywhere, and its bill is not all black or black with yellow (if Tundra). The bill is mostly black, which eliminates Whooper and Mute. The base of the bill has considerable black, whereas the base of the bill of a juvenile Tundra Swan would be pink. The neck, head and upperparts are gray-brown, and the lower parts are lighter gray, giving a two-toned effect. I think a Tundra Swan would be whiter, but I'm not sure about that on a 25 November bird. As with the adult, the tail is lower to the water than a Tundra Swan. I think distinguishing juvenile Trumpeter from Tundra for this bird is a challenge, but I believe it is juvenile Trumpeter, all things considered, and especially because of the bill coloration.
Behaviors: Both the adult and juvenile were swimming and feeding underwater during the entire observation, and stayed fairly close together.
