Description of the Bird

The bird was immediately suspected as being a flaviventris (Yellow-bellied) based on its very bright yellowish-green plumage relative to other Empidonax species in Colorado. The short, the head was big for the body, but short and very rounded (not elongated). As a long-distance migrant, the bird had moderately long wings and short tail (short primary extension). The primary tips were quite pointed unlike the more rounded primary tips of Western Flycatchers have one additional primary than Yellow-bellied which creates a more rounded terminal shape.
The top of the head, back, rump, and upper tail coverts were green, tinged with yellow. The crown was slightly darker than the back. The face was rather uniformly colored. The rounded eye was blackish encircled by a pale yellow, thin, and fairly uniformly shaped eye ring; you could not discern any rearward elongation to the eye ring (like Western Flycatchers). The bill was short and wide-based, being blackish above and pale orange below. The throat was pale yellowish-gray throughout. The breast, abdomen and vent were a bright yellow, being brighter across the mid- and upper breasts and undertail coverts; the flanks were a dull yellow-gray. The undersides of the tail were a dark gray, bordered by thin white edging to the outer rectrices. The upper tail was a dark blackish-brown. The legs were short and black. The wings were dark blackish-brown contrasting with distinct yellow wing bars. The anterior wing bar was pale yellow but slightly washed with buff, as evidenced on at least one of the attached photographs. The posterior wing bar was whitish with a yellowish was, being brighter toward the tips. The tertials, although not visible on the attached photographs, had relatively broad pale edges to the tips.
Behaviors: The bird was perched on small branches. It would occasionally make a foraging sally and then return to the same or nearby branch.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

The greenish upperparts, yellow, rounded eyering, yellow lower mandible, and yellowish underparts eliminate all other congeners except Western Flycatchers (E. difficilis and E. occidentalis) and eastern Willow Flycatchers (E. traillii traillii/E. traillii campestris).
Gray Flycatcher differs by being overall light gray in color, has a long tail, and short primary extension. The short, blocky stature of the subject bird differs from the elongated and larger look to the “Trail’s” flycatcher group. That group also either lacks eyerings or the eyerings are often indistinct. The bill of the subject bird is also too short overall for a member of the Trail’s group. Acadian Flycatcher is quite similar to eastern Willow Warbler and can be eliminated for similar reasons. They have longish bills, more of a peaked crown, has a duller throat that contrasts less with the greenish head, have greenish-white flanks and whitish breast and belly. Acadians look more similar to wood-pewees with their longer profile, larger bill, and relatively long wings. The thin, rounded eyering on the subject bird eliminates “Western” flycatchers that have an elongated eyering. Western Flycatchers also have narrow tertial edging, unlike the broad tertial edging of Yellow-bellied. Western Flycatchers also have longer tails and shorter primaries. Hammond’s and Dusky Flycatchers have an olive-gray back with a dirty white breast separated by a dusky band. Hammond’s would have a long primary extension and short tail, whereas Dusky has a short primary extension and long tail. Least Flycatchers, which are similar in shape to Yellow-bellied Flycathers (though generally not so pot-bellied) have an all white throat and breast compared to the bright yellow of Yellow-bellied.
