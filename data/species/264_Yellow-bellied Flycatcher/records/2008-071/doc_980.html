Description of the Bird

the bird was a small flycatcher, identified as an Empidonax by its small size (about the same size as a warbler, though it appeared larger due to its longer tail and upright posture), by its bold wingbars, an eyering, and by its "dumpier" shape and smaller size when compared to a Western Wood-Pewee.

The bird had very little in the way of a crest, and the head as a whole appeared quite rounded. The primary projection was moderate in legth, for an empid, but not nearly as long as I would expect for a Wood-Pewee. The tail appeared a little bit proportionally shorter than I would expect for Cordilleran Flycatcher, though this was hard to quantify without direct comparison. The bill was fairly wide, and the lower mandible was completely orange. The legs were black.

The head and back of the bird were bright olive with a definite yellow tinge. The underparts were bright yellow, with an olive wash across the chest, contrasting slightly with the clearer yellow throat and belly. The wings were black, with no brown or olive coloring, with two bold white wingbars. The tail was olive green. The bird had a thin, complete eyering that was even around the eye, and pale yellow in color.
Behaviors: the bird was first observed foraging low in dense, young willows. A little later it was slightly higher in a larger willow, where I recorded the calls. It was never seen associating with a flock, and was never seen or heard again for sure after the initial observation.
Call: The clincher was the call, which was a slightly falling then rising "pwee", more like a call I would expect from a Pewee than from an empid. I heard the bird make this call twice during a two minute interval. Furthermore, I later heard a "che-bek" call twice, separated by a ten minute interval, that sounded off to me for Least Flycatcher. I believe that it may well have been the Yellow-bellied Flycatcher singing, but I never did see the bird singing, so I cannot confirm that.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Species other than Empids were ruled out by the small size, upright posture, bold wingbars, and distinctive shape. Wood-Pewees especially were ruled out by the much bolder wingbars than expected, by the shorter primary projection, by the brighter coloration, and by the dumpier shape.

Most species of empid were ruled out by the bright yellow coloration of the bird; most of those same species were ruled out by the completely orange lower mandible. "Western" Flycatchers were the hardest to rule out; I ruled them out by the thin and complete yellow eye-ring, by the much blacker wings with stronger contrast with the wingbars, by the yellower coloration than I would expect even for those species, and by the slightly more rounded and dumpier shape. All other species of empid were especially ruled out by the distinctive call note.
