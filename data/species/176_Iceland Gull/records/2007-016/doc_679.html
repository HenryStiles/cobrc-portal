Description of the Bird

A gull about the size of average California Gull (estimated 21 inches in length), based oon close proximity of this bird to several California gulls of various sizes, numerous Ring-billed Gulls, and singletons of immature Herring Gull and Lesser Black-backed Gull. Head all white with dark eye and smallish mostly black bill with flesh-colored base. Mantle whitish with faint pale brown mottling. Underparts whitish with faint brown barring on vent. Wings whitish with faint brown mottling on coverts, and secondaries. Tertials and primaries appeared (at a great distance) to be pale gray centered with broad white edges, without obvious barring or mottling. Pink legs. When sitting, primaries extended moderately far beyond the tail (more than for Herring Gull, less than for Lesser Black-backed Gull).
Behaviors: standing, floating
Call: none
Plumage: first cycle immature

Similar Species Discussion

Plumage resembles typical first cycle "Kumlien's" Iceland Gull, although some molt on the mantle would have been expected by this date. Other taxa differ as follows:

1. "Glaucoides" Iceland Gull should show entirely white tips to the primaries, rather than pale gray centers.

2. Thayer's Gull (L. thayeri) in first cycle has much darker markings on wing coverts, tertials and primaries, and brownish head and underparts. A Thayer's Gull appearing like this bird would be a double anomaly, anomolous in plumage (an extremely bleached individual or leucistic) and a runt.

3. Herring Gull (L. argentatus) has blackish primaries and a solidly dark tail; even a bleached individual would not be as white on the primaries as this bird. All Herring Gulls are larger than this bird.

4. California Gull (L. californicus) has blackish primaries and a solidly dark tail. Bill mostly pale rather than mostly dark from early first cycle onward.

5. Hybrid. A hybrid is difficult to rule out. Thayer's X Glaucous could result in a similar combination of plumage features, although one would expect any bird with Glaucous genes to be larger. California XX Glaucous might result in a bird of this size, but would have a mostly pale bill with a daark tip (feature of both parent species) rather than a mostly dark bill.
