Description of the Bird

I initially spotted an obviously pale-winged gull flying and diving near the distant east end of the lake.  It flew toward me and landed on a nearby ice shelf, where I photographed it.  Eventually, the gull stretched its wings and flew back to the other end of the lake.  As it did so, I did not see any darker secondary bar.

This gull is identified as a second-winter Iceland (Kumlien's) Gull based on the following characteristics:  (1) overall size larger than Ring-billed Gulls but not as large as Herring Gulls that were also present; (2) small, rounded head; (3) smallish (for a gull of this size) bill that is bicolored; (4) pale iris; (5) medium pink legs; (6) light gray scapulars on an otherwise brown and white body; (7) medium brown primaries; (8) lack of secondary bar; and (9) tertials that are quite speckled.  Note that the photo of the Iceland Gull standing with other gulls provides the more realistic depiction of the folded primaries, consistent with what I observed in person, since the gull is evenly lit.  In contrast, in the photo of the gull by itself, the primaries are shaded and unrealistically dark.

Many gulls were present, some vocalizing, but I'm not sure if the Iceland Gull was or wasn't vocalizing. 

Similar Species Discussion

Based on overall size and structure, bill size, plumage, and leg color, the ID can be narrowed down to either second-winter Iceland (Kumlien's) Gull or second-winter Thayer's Gull.  Multiple factors point to Iceland rather than Thayer's.  First, the pale iris is much more common in Iceland than in Thayer's.  Second, the scapulars are light gray, expected for Iceland Gull, but lighter than expected for Thayer's Gull.  Third, the lack of an obvious darker secondary bar when the gull flew away is a characteristic of Iceland rather than Thayer's.  Fourth, the medium-brown color of the folded primaries is a better match for Iceland than for Thayer's, albeit at the dark end of the spectrum for Iceland.  In contrast, second-winter Thayer's Gulls typically have very dark primaries.  Fifth, the very speckled tertials are a characteristic of Iceland rather than Thayer's Gull.  The observed gull looks very much like the second-winter Kumlien's Gull, observed at the same time of year (Dec. 1999), shown in photo 35A.20 Howell and Dunn's book.
