Description of the Bird

On the morning of 13 Nov 2011, at about 9 am, I spotted a somewhat concolorous biscuit-colored gull smaller than nearby Herring Gulls at Timnath Reservoir. Both Bill Schmoker and I suspected that it was an Iceland Gull, and so drove to the north side of the reservoir where we could approach the bird more closely.

Overall, it was a pale brown (biscuit color is the term I’ve come to use) with an overall frosty appearance. From front to back, the bird was rather concolorous on the whole, though patterned in detail. The head and underparts were uniform (except a slightly darker area from behind eye up towards crown), without much in the way of internal markings, excepting the the whitish vent/undertail coverts, which were barred brown. The back and scapular feathers were brown fringed in hoary white. The folded wing coverts were speckled brown and white. The tertials were more coarsely speckled/barred in brown and white, and marked as such throughout (not seen well in distant photos taken); they were also edged in white. The folded primaries had pale brown centers with broad white edges.

In flight, white uppertail coverts marked heavily with brown bars were noted. The tail was uniformly biscuit brown, not forming a tail band. The upper wings were rather uniform, without a secondary bar present. Dorsally, the outer 4 or 5 primaries were a bit darker on the outer webs and whitish on the inner webs. From beneath, the wing-coverts were brown, and the secondaries/primaries looked nearly white, except for extremely limited dusky tips to the outermost primaries.

The bird’s shape was delicate, almost Mew Gull like, appearing finer in structure than nearby Thayer’s Gulls. Head seemed proportionately small and gentle, wings rather long. The bill was also delicate, with dull pink to the base. The extent of this was difficult to determine.

Eventually, the bird flew off to the ENE, following a steady trail of gulls that had departed in that direction.

We watched the bird for 10-15 minutes at approximately 100 yards. I was using a Kowa 88mm scope with a 20-60x eyepiece. The light was mostly to our backs.

Given the direction of departure, we headed to the N Weld County Dump, where we relocated the bird at about 10:30 am. Though the distance was greater, somehow the bird was more sharply seen. We watched the bird there for 5-10 minutes, and had no doubt that it was the same individual.

Similar Species Discussion

The bird’s build and size and general coloration eliminate all gulls except Thayer’s and Iceland (which includes Kumlien’s). The pale coloration, well-marked tertials, lack of secondary bar, broad pale edges to folded primaries all act to eliminate Thayer’s Gull. Also pointing towards Iceland was the pale base to the bill and the proportions, which were delicate even in comparison to companion Thayer’s Gulls. The darkish outer webs of the outer primaries would suggest the more likely Kumlien's race of Iceland Gull
