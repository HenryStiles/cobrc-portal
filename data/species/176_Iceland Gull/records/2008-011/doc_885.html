Description of the Bird

Overall pale creamy brown 1st-winter gull. Size a bit larger than Ring-billed Gulls nearby but noticeably smaller than nearby Herring Gulls. Overall pale creamy brown tone uniform throughout entire body including wing feathers. Bill all black, moderately short, giving it a stubby appearance. Upper and lower bill surfaces nearly parallel, very slight gonydeal expansion. Eye dark. Legs pink. Wing projection moderate giving the bird a fairly slender, attenuated appearance (not chunky like Glaucous or Glaucous-winged.) Head appears domed (not flat like Herring.) Scapulars and upper wing covert tracts patterned with nearly white bases, creamy pale brown distal half (or so), and narrow white outer edge, giving upperparts a neatly patterned appearance. Tertials centered with same pale creamy brown color, with nearly white outer edge and wide whitish tip containing pale creamy brown blotching. Primaries centered with same pale creamy brown coloration as the rest of the body, with thin nearly white edging and tips. On spread wings, secondaries are seen to have the same pale creamy brown coloration in their centers, with nearly white edging and tips, while primaries are seen to have paler inner webs, the outer webs of the outer primaries being about the same creamy pale brown as the rest of the body and covert feathers. The general impression of the spread wing is a uniform pale creamy brown throughout with no distinctly darker areas, only lighter highlights in the coverts and remiges. Tail also uniformly pale creamy brown, not appreciably darker than the rest of the body or wings. I feel the brown on this bird is on the dark end of the continuum for Kumlien's Gull, but referable to individuals depicted in Howell and Dunn's Gull's Of The Americas: The Photographic Guide, figures 35A.8, 35A.12. The 1st-cycle individuals pictured in flight all appear a bit lighter, but the individual in figure 35a.36 is quite similar, particularly the coloration of its tail and outer primary webs.
Behaviors: The bird briefly rested on the ice with numerous Ring-billed and Herring Gulls before flying east, presumably to the Erie Landfill.
Call: not heard
Plumage: darker 1st-winter Kumlien's

Similar Species Discussion

Glaucous Gull larger than Herring Gulls (this bird was smaller), and has stronger-looking bill, flatter head, bicolored bill, proportionately shorter wing projection. The real tricky bird here is Thayer's Gull. This bird would be on the light end for Thayer's but probably not impossible. Five main separators point to Kumlien's, however. First, the tertials and primaries should look at least a bit darker than the rest of the brown coloration on the upperparts, and in this bird, the brown appeared concolor thoughout the primaries, tertials, and upper parts. Second, Thayer's should show a stronger contrast between inner and outer primary webs- on this bird the difference here was more subtle. Third, Thayer's bills look thinner due to the fact that they are longer, while in this bird, the bill looked more stubby. Fourth, in the spread wing there should be a darker secondary bar on Thayer's, which wasn't seen in this bird. Finally, on Thayer's the tail should show a broad dark distal band, which wasn't seen in this bird.

Separated from Iceland Gull, nominate ssp. mainly by darker overall color.

Additional Information

Comments: David Waltman has additional photos, and a few show contrastingly darker primaries and tertials. I'm not sure how to explain this since I didn't observe this in life or in my photos. I wonder if he switched inadvertently to one of the Thayer's Gulls also frequenting the lake. Upon reviewing David's photos, John Vanderpoel said the bird was a straightforward Thayer's. Whatever this bird is, I would argue that it is anything but straightforward. Howell and Dunn clearly write and show that Kumlien's Gulls can be as dark as this bird, although they also acknowledge that some research suggests that Iceland, Kumlien's, and Thayers are co-specific anyway.
