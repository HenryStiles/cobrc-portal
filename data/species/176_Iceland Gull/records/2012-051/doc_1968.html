Description of the Bird

First, I note that an earlier written description appears here: http://tinyurl.com/7vwbc9k

I first saw the bird in the presence of a "Thiceland" Gull, i.e., a gull with characters intermediate between Thayer's and Iceland. Like the "Thiceland" Gull, the bird in question was a "large white-headed gull" (LWHG), but definitely on the dainty side for an LWHG. In particular, the bird at rest on the water appeared more buoyant than would a larger LWHG. In direct comparison with nearby Ring-billed Gulls, the bird was clearly larger.

Sitting on the water, the bird was notably pale/white, especially on the exposed wingtips. The mainly or entirely black bill was small and thin for an LWHG.

On several occasions, the bird engaged in leisurely, low, soaring, circling flight, providing prolonged views of the upper wing surfaces. Most notable were the uniform, quite-pale-gray wingtips, concolor with the clearly visible secondary bar.
Behaviors: On several occasions, the bird picked at the water's surface. Of course, any gull can do this, but I consider that behavior to be consistent with the overall "dainty" build of Iceland--relative to other LWHGs.

The bird alternately swam/floated on the water's surface and engaged in low, mainly soaring flights close to shore.

I'm unclear as to whether the bird can be said to have "kept company" with the other LWHGs that were out there. Initially, it consorted with the "Thiceland" Gull, but then it seemed to do its own thing.
Call: none heard.
Plumage: fairly late first cycle

Similar Species Discussion

From other LWHGs by overall small size/build. Thayer's vs. Iceland is, of course, a very difficult separation. This bird was quite pale, but exceptional Thayer's can probably be as pale; however, the combination of the uniform quite-pale-gray wing tips (as seen in flight) and quite-pale-gray secondary bar (concolor with wing tips) would be quite unusual for any Thayer's and is perfectly consistent with Iceland.

A good example of a pale Thayer's in flight is given in the March 2012 Birding, p. 39, Fig. 1d. Even though the bird depicted appears quite pale, it shows darkening toward the wing tips and a secondary bar notably darker than the primaries. In contrast, the bird I'm reporting showed notably pale-gray primaries concolor with a notably pale secondary bar.

Briefly, I note that other LWHGs (Herring Gull and "on up," size-wize) differ in generally bulkier build and bill structure, and in larger size overall.

Previous Experience

Because I live and bird and watch gulls in Boulder County in the wintertime, I've had a lot of experience the past 10 years with the Thayer's-Iceland problem. In Colorado in the past 10 years, I've seen a few (fewer than 5) apparently "good" ("pure") Iceland Gulls, at at least 10 "tweeners" (i.e., "Thiceland" Gulls), and 100+ Thayer's Gulls. I've also pondered the problem in the field in such places as the Great Lakes, the East Coast, and coastal California.

Additional Information

Comments: Two comments:

1. From what I can gather, it was an exceptional winter for Iceland Gulls and "Thiceland" Gulls in northeastern Colorado. Thus, this bird at Panama Reservoir may have been part of a larger mini-invasion of Iceland Gulls (and Iceland Gull genes) into the region.

2. Second, as always, the committee will be faced with the ontological question of whether this bird was "really" an Iceland Gull. I don't have DNA, and a photo wouldn't help in that regard. The best I can offer is that this bird was as good as any candidate Iceland Gull I've seen in Colorado; also, this bird would pass for sure as an Iceland Gull back East.
