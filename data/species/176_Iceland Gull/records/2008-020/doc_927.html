Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized gull, noticeably smaller than nearby Herring Gulls and slightly larger than nearby California Gulls. The overall shape was similar to, but less bulky than Herring Gull, with a more rounded head, slimmer overall shape that was accentuated by the slimmer neck and proportionally longer wingtips. The bill was relatively petite, with very little gonydeal angle. The color of the bill was a dull greenish-yellow color, noticeably different than the brighter yellow-orange bill of practically every other gull in the flock. There was a small red gonydeal post, which also appeard to be a bit paler than on nearby gulls. The legs were bright pink, on the intense side compared to Herring Gull. The orbital ring and gape were pinkish red. The irides were yellow, though perhaps with a very slight dusky color apparent at very close range.

The head and underparts of the bird were completely white. The mantle was at least a couple of shades paler than nearby Herring and Ring-billed Gulls. The bird had a fairly large white tertial crescent. The wingtips were almost completely white, with very faint pale gray markings visible only at close range. On the spread wing these markings appeared to be near the tips of the outer primaries only, with the largest marking on p8 or p9. The tail of the bird was completely white.
Behaviors: The bird was found on the edge of a large flock of gulls loafing on the edge of Boyd Lake. It was seen both perched and swimming, and very briefly flying, for about an hour total. The bird apparently flew after we left, and could not be found in the area despite much searching.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Most species of gull were ruled out by the completely white wing tips, as well as the petite proportions for a medium sized gull, with long wingtips and small bill. The paler mantle color also ruled out most large gulls.

Glaucous Gull was ruled out by the small size, by the long wingtips, by what would be very petite proportions for that species, by the bill color and small size relative to the rest of the bird, and by the dusky markings on the wing-tips. A Glaucous X Glaucous-winged hybrid, which could have dusky markings on the wings, was also ruled out by the small size, small proportions, long wings, and bill morphology.

Other hybrids with Glaucous Gull, including Herring X Glaucous hyrbid, were ruled out by the same characteristics, especially the completely white wings that were also proportionally longer than expected for any hybrid combinations that could produce whitish wingtips.

Thayer's Gull was ruled out by the white wingtips, with no black or dark gray whatsoever, as well as the smaller bill that was also duller than expected for that species. Supporting marks include pale eyes, paler mantle color than nearby Herring Gulls rather than similar or very slightly darker, and by the slightly smaller size than expected for Thayer's Gull.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Kumlien's Iceland Gulls in the east, including many adults in Nova Scotia, and am familiar with that species. I have also seen many Thayer's Gulls in Colorado and the west coast, and am reasonably familiar with that species. I am less familiar with various hybrid combinations, but as this bird appeared fairly clear-cut that was not an issue.
