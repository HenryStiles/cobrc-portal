Description of the Bird

A gull about the size of large California Gull (estimated 22 or 23 inches in length). Head all white with dark eye and smallish mostly black bill with flesh-colored base. Mantle mostly gray but not clean, possibly due to mixture of worn and fresh feathers (this bird would be engaged in its pre-alternate molt of body feathers). Underparts white on the chest, pale gray-brown on the lower chest to belly, with faint brown barring on vent. Wings whitish with faint brown mottling on coverts, and secondaries. Tertials white with faint thin brown bars. Primaries all white. In flight, wings all whitish from below. From above, a pale secondary bar was apparent. Tail heavily marked with thin gray-brown bars, in contrast to a bright white rump, possibly including the base of the rectrices. Pink legs. When sitting, primaries extended moderately far beyond the tail (more than for Herring Gull, less than for Lesser Black-backed Gull).
Behaviors: Flying, circling in a thermal, bathing, standing, walking.
Call: none
Plumage: immature

Similar Species Discussion

Plumage resembles typical immature "Kumlien's" Iceland Gull, probably in its second cycle (worn 2nd basic plumage). Other taxa differ as follows:

1. "Glaucoides" Iceland Gull probably cannot be differentiated in this plumage.

2. Thayer's Gull (L. thayeri) has non-white primaries and much darker markings on wing coverts. Heavily faded/bleached individuals are unusual. Most Thayer's Gulls are larger than this bird. A Thayer's Gull appearing like this bird would be an anomaly, either a very rare bleached 2nd basic bird, or a leucistic individual.

3. Herring Gull (L. argentatus) has blackish primaries and a solidly dark tail; even a bleached individual would not be as white on the primaries as this bird. All Herring Gulls are larger than this bird.

4. California Gull (L. californicus) has blackish primaries and a solidly dark tail. Bill mostly pale rather than mostly dark from early first cycle onward.

5. Hybrid. A hybrid is difficult to rule out. Thayer's X Glaucous could result in a similar combination of plumage features, although one would expect any bird with Glaucous genes to be larger.

Additional Information

Comments: These notes were aided by a photo taken by Rachel Hopper.

I believe the extensive gray on the mantle and the white rump favor a second-cycle bird. A photo of such a bird with a mostly dark bill is published in Olsen and Larsson. First-cycle birds begin to molt mantle feathers beginning in December so it is possible that this bird has an advanced molt on the mantle and rump, approaching first alternate plumage.

A second apparent Iceland Gull was also observed and is described in a separate record.
