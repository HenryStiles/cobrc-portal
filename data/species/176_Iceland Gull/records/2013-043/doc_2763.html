Description of the Bird

The Iceland gull (presumed Kumlien's) was feeding on dead fish along with about 200 other gulls.  The intermingling of different gull species (also Ring-Billed, Herring, Thayer's, and Lesser Black-Backed) made numerous size and plumage comparisons.  It was very windy (40+ mph) while I was there, so it was difficult to hear sounds clearly, and the birds had a somewhat disheveled appearance.

The Iceland gull was much smaller than the Herring gulls, slightly smaller than the Thayer's gulls, and slightly larger then the ring-billed gulls.  It appeared small-headed and had a small, black bill.  The overall color was a low-contrast mix of white and tan.  The back had a somewhat frosty appearance.  An excellent photo by Glenn Walbek of the bird from the back in good light (http://www.pbase.com/gwalbek/image/148577856) shows the low-contrast nature of the plumage quite well.  Notably, the tail is light colored, and the secondary bar is light colored.  A photo I obtained of the shaded side of the bird, with light shining through some of the feathers, also reveals some key features.  Patterning in the tertials is modest, at the low end of the observed range for Kumlien's gulls.  More notably, the pale bands at the tips of the primaries are wide.  Furthermore, there are darker subterminal marks near the centers of the primary feathers.  These features are typical of midwinter first-cycle Kumlien's gulls, as shown in photographs in Howell and Dunn's "Gulls of the Americas".  

Similar Species Discussion

First-cycle Glaucous-Winged, Thayer's, and Iceland (Kumlien's) gulls were considered based on plumage and bill color.  Glaucous-Winged gull was eliminated based on overall size and bill size.  The following characteristics indicate that the bird was an Iceland (Kumlien's) gull rather than a Thayer's gull:  (1) the lack of a dark secondary bar, (2) the lack of a dark tail band, (3) the wide pale bands on the primary tips and darker subterminal marks, (4) pale overall color, and (5) small overall size.
