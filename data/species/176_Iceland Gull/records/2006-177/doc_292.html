Description of the Bird

A small, large gull, about the size of nearby California Gulls and obviously smaller than nearby Herring Gulls, the bird was neither very dark nor very pale for that age class of Iceland Gull; in other words, fairly typical. The entire bird was, generally, the same very, very pale brown, much paler than nearby immature Herring Gulls and about the same color tone as the very pale end of Thayer's Gull plumage, and without the darker primaries of both Herring and Thayer's gulls. The back and scapular feathers looked to have been replaced with First Basic feathers, though it was tough to be certain given the distance of the bird.

The tertials were well seen and were basically whitish with a few brownish bars and completely lacking any suggestion of solid dark centers. The tail was a bit darker than the rump. The primaries on the folded wing were fringed extensively in white, much more so than is found in Thayer's Gulls of similar plumage, with the white fringe accounting for, perhaps, 1/4 to 1/3 of the width of either web -- this was no thin, white fringe typical of Thayer's Gull. The underparts were unremarkable. The bill was nearly entirely black, but with a a paler base to the mandible. The legs were bright pink, brighter than those of nearby Herring Gulls.

The bird's shape was quite different from any of the neighboring gulls, being quite long-winged with long primary projection (the tip of P7 fell just about at the tail tip), short-legged (which made the bird look a bit dumpy), and round-headed (which made the bird look "soft" and "cute"). The head was small enough to make the eye look nearly centered in the head and the bill was quite short, nearly as short as those of nearby Ring-billed Gulls.
Behaviors: Loafing and preening
Call: none heard
Plumage: First Basic

Similar Species Discussion

Thayer's Gull is the most similar species, and I ruled it out due to the overall pale coloration (though the palest of Thayer's can be the same color), the primaries being concolorous with the rest of the plumage (not darker as in Thayer's), the tertials being white with somewhat distinct darker bars rather than dark-centered with a pale fringe as on Thayer's), and the very short bill (shorter than on any Thayer's I've seen). The other very pale large, white-headed North American gulls (Glaucous-winged and Glaucous) are ruled out by size (both species are much larger than California Gulls and most individuals of the two are larger, even, than Herring Gulls), bill size and pattern, and very long wings. Leucistic individuals of California and Herring gulls are ruled out by bill size and pattern (both should have bi-colored pink-and-black bills by the date of occurrence), primaries not darker than the rest of the plumage, and the very long wings. Ivory Gull is eliminated on plumage pattern and leg color.
