Description of the Bird

As I walked along the South Platte River trail, I noticed a gull with very pale primaries on one of the nearby gravel pits / ponds.  It was an adult gull with a pale gray mantle and a small, mostly yellow bill (photos also show a red gonys spot).  Through binoculars, the gull's folded primaries appeared mostly white with pale gray below the tips.  No other gulls were nearby on the pond, but nearby ducks provided a size comparison.  The gull of interest was longer than the Common Goldeneyes and approximately the length of a Red-breasted Merganser.  A little more than a minute after I spotted the gull, it took off and circled around before flying off to the southwest.  As it circled around,  I alternated between observing it through binoculars and snapping photos.  The closest photo is included with this report.  These and other photos show a typical wing pattern for adult Iceland (Kumlien's) Gull.  The undersides on the wings were entirely pale except for gray along the edge of the outer primary and at the extreme tips of the outermost primaries.  The uppersides of the wings had limited gray on the primaries.  Light smuding on the head and neck indicated the gull was in nonbreeding plumage.  Photos also show that the gull had pink legs.  As the Iceland Gull flew off, it was joined by first-year Herring Gull, and the former was clearly smaller than the latter.

Similar Species Discussion

Iceland and Thayer's Gulls are the small-billed, pink-legged gulls found in this region.  Thayer's would have shown slaty gray color on the primaries (extensive on the uppersides and noticeable near the tips on the undersides), unlike the observed gull.
