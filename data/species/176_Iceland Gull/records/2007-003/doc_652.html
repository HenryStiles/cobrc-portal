Description of the Bird

This gull was distinctly larger than a Ring-billed Gull and smaller than Herring Gull with direct comparisons available. A frosty looking gull with gleaming white underwing. Head someone small and pigeon-like, whitish with brown streaks. Bill appearing mostly blackish with some pinkish showing through the black at the base of the "lower mandible". Bill looked relatively short compared to Herring Gull. Iris color dark. In my opinion there was some dark smudging posterior to the eye, a character more consistent with Thayer's than will Kulien's Gull. Enitre body, including primaries and tail appeared apporoximately the same color, a light tan. Tertials barred light brown, NOT COMPLETED DARK, as would be expected for Kumlien's and not as expected for Thayer's Gull. Primaries long, extending well past the tail, with noticeable white edging to both inner and outer edges. Undertail coverts whitish with large light brown circular spots. Feet and legs pinkish.
Behaviors: Mostly at rest on ice. No specific behaviors exhibited.
Call: none heard

Similar Species Discussion

The biggest question is: what are the extreme range of variation in plumage and soft part coloration clinally from Western through Eastern Kumlien's Gull, compared to the extremes of Thayer's Gull. For sure this bird is not a nominate gluacoides Iceland Gull, as the primaries and tertials are nowhere near pale enough to fit that taxon. As stated in Olson and Larsson, Kumlien's Gull has the greatest range of variability than any other gull. The Pueblo bird easily fits inside the darkest range of Kumlien's Gull, but in my opinon is in the darker end of the spectrum, especially when compared with Kumlien's Gulls from winter birds inNewfoundland as seen on Bruce MacTavish's website. (He also has a dark Kumlien' that is much darker than the Pueblo bird on his website).

The three key features to look for in separating dark Kumlien's Gull from light Thayer's Gull, according the Dunn and Vanderpool are,

presence vs. abscence of smudging around the eye, tertials with bars or squiggles compared to dark centers, and coloration of wingtips compared to rest of body color.

The Pueblo bird meets two of the three criteria for it to be a Kumlien's Gull as compared to a Thayer's.

1. Pueblo bird's broadly light-edged primaries are approximately the same color as the rest of the bird, which is good for Kumlien's Gull

2. Tertials are NOT completely dark-centered as in Thayer's Gull, but consist of a light background with light brown bars.

3. The Pueblo bird DOES have smudging around its eye, especially posterior to it, a character more consistent with Thayer's Gull.

Although, I have looked for photos of Thayer's Gull looking like the Pueblo bird, I could find none.

While the Pueblo bird could be a hybrid between glaucoides Iceland Gull and Thayer's, (which Olsen and Larsson think a Kumlien's Gull may actually be), I have seen no photos labeled hybrid between Thayer's and Kumlien's so I am uncertain what such birds look like. If Kumlien's gull is a good supspecies of Iceland Gull, I believe that the Pueblo bird is most likely a darkish representative of that taxon.

Although a dark tail is not common in Kumlien's Gull it appears dark on approximately 10% of Kumlien's Gulls. I did not get a good look at the tail as the bird was never seen in flight, but from my photos it appears that the tail is about the same color as its mantle.
