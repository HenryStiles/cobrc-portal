Description of the Bird

the bird was about the size of a Ring-billed Gull, maybe slightly larger. It was also similar in shape, but with broader based, broader, proportionally longer wings and a heavier build. The bill looked fairly large for a Jaeger, and was pale based with a dark tip on the outer third of the bill. The overall shape looked heavy for a Jaeger, with a bulky build and relatively large head.

The upperparts of the bird were medium chocolate brown, with no rufous or buffy fringes on the mantle. The cap was a bit contrastingly darker than the nape, which in turn was contrastingly slightly paler than the cap and back. The uppertail coverts were mostly white with some small brown checks. The tail was fairly square for a jaeger, with barely noticeable extension of the two central tail feathers.

The bird had a medium brown "hood" that was slightly paler brown on the throat and upper chest. The belly was whitish with a pale brown tinge. The undertail coverts were strongly barred with medium brown.

The upper wings were the same medium chocolate brown as the back. The outer primaries had white shafts; at it's closest approach I was able to count at least 4-5 white primary shafts. The primaries themselves were uniformly dark from above.

The underwings were checked with whitish, though medium brown was the slightly predominate color. The base of the primaries were white. I was unable to observe a "double flash" in the primaries below, but was assured by an observer who saw the bird much closer than I did that it was there.
Behaviors: The bird was mostly observed flying around low over the water, where it flew with a direct, almost falcon-like flight, continually harassing Ring-billed Gulls and at one point a Laughing Gull. After harassing the gulls for a while it flew off to the north, then gained a significant amount of altitude. For the next ten minutes or so it soared in circles at a constant elevation, rarely flapping. It then rapidly descended to the reservoir and after flying low over the surface for a minute or so landed on the water.
Call: none heard
Plumage: juvenal light phase

Similar Species Discussion

Species other than Jaegers were ruled out by the overall dark coloration combined with white checkering on the underwings, white flash at the base of the primaries below, barred undertail coverts, and especially the shape - broad and long wings, bulky proportions. The behavior of harassing gulls was also used to rule out other larids.

Long-tailed Jaeger was ruled out by the large size, bulky proportions, more than two white primary shafts, less bold checkering on the underwings, more direct and powerful flight, and behavior of continuously harassing the gulls.

Parasitic Jaeger was ruled out by the larger size and bulkier proportions, by the lack of buff or rufous edging on the upperparts, by the more aggressive flight style and behavior, by the white uppertail coverts and strongly barred undertail coverts, and by the lack of a bold white flash in the outer primaries above.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted after the observation for details on light phase Parasitic and Pomarine Jaegers. Glenn Walbek was called while observing the bird so I could discuss the ID with him.

Previous Experience

I have seen a number of Pomarine Jaegers before, though not very often of this age and morph. I have studied the species closely in Colorado a number of times.

I have seen good numbers of both the other Jaegers previous to the observation.
