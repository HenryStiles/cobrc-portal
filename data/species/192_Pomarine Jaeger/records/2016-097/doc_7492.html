Description of the Bird

observed mostly sitting on the water, but it flew around a few times.  Dark backed jaeger, golden nape and cheek area, not real bright golden color, modestly stout bill mostly pale with dark tip, dark breast band with white belly, dark undertail and 2 central tail feathers projected beyond rest of the rects, pale base to outer couple primaries only (not a really bright white base of the primaries, but no appearance of a "double flash" as in parasitic).  It chased Ring-billed gulls a couple times upon my viewing and drove one under water (photos don't show that well, but it was awesome to watch!).


I was watching it from the handicapped fishing pier out in Jeff Co waters (pretty sure) and it flew south and out of view (prob best seen off swim beach).  I eventually relocated it chasing a gull and still going south and gone, but I actually expected it to come back north towards me, and low and behold it came almost right over my head chasing a gull.  I was prepared for the photo opp and managed a series of shots from below.  That was AWESOME!  Then it proceeded to put that aforementioned gull under water).

Similar Species Discussion

Parasitic is most similar, but PAJA has a smaller bill, pale vent, and pointed and shorter central rects, and has the described double flash of white at the base of the primaries.  This bird didn't have much white at the base, well relatively indistinct, but quite noticeable but less so than a PAJA.  Long-tailed is smaller and generally grayer overall with a dainty bill.  I've seen all jaeger species several times, and various color morphs, but never extended good views, so this was a fun study (said no one ever about jaegers!)

Previous Experience

I've seen all 3 jaegers on a few occasions, pom and parasitic off ATL and PAC coasts and Long-tailed off PAC coast on pelagics with Dave Pereksta and Steve Howell.  I'm by no means an expert on this suite of species, but this bird was seen well on the water and immediately over my head.  Good study with respect to the other species.  Well documented by other birders as well via photos.
