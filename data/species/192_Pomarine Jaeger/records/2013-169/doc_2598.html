Description of the Bird

I first discovered the bird as I was driving in to the south entrance campground/boat ramp. I could see several gulls flying about as I was trying to figure out where to park. Then, probably no more than 7-10m ahead of my windshield, I caught a view of a much darker 'gull-like' bird passing in profile, which had a very visible extension to its tail.

I parked as soon as I could, knowing that I had something very unexpected here. It took me 10-15 minutes of scanning the reservoir and its shorelines before I realized that the bird was no longer flying, but instead had landed on the beach adjacent to the main camping area very close to me and was alone there, facing out into the westerly wind coming off the reservoir. The bird remained there for the rest of the time that I observed it (probably about half an hour). Lighting was excellent, as I set up facing south with the bird illuminated in profile by the less-intense late afternoon June sun.

The bird had a very obvious dark 'cap' that extended below the eyes, and below the cap was a whitish nape and collar. The bill appeared to be completely dark too, of moderate length, but no obvious coloration pattern to my eyes. The body (mantle, scapulars, and wings) was fully dark brown, with no obvious mottling or marking. The tail extension that I saw briefly when I entered the main camping area was very noticeable, and in the scope I could see a twist pattern to what appeared to be two inner rectrices extending past the rest of the tucked-in tail feathers. The twist rendered the two feathers at a 90-degree angle with respect to the rest of the tail. (The tail coloration was fully brown too.)

As mentioned before, the bird did not move or relocate during the entire time I observed it on the beach, and may have been settling in for the night. There were no discernible vocalizations or sounds made. No other birds approached it or passed anywhere near it that entire time.

Similar Species Discussion

I mostly considered the other jaegers, since it was clear to me that its dark complexion ruled out virtually every conceivable other gull-like bird. Parasitic and Long-tailed Jaegers in breeding plumage can be fairly similar in overall impression, but when I was able to study the bird on the beach, I was able to see the main distinguishing characters. Primarily, the 90-degree twist in the inner rectrices is unique to the Pomarine Jaeger, and that twist was very obvious in the scope (and hopefully in the attached photo). Additionally, the whitish neck collar is less broad in the Pomarine than in the other two jaegers (see Sibley), and this bird's collar was much more consistent with Pomarine illustrations I'm familiar with than the alternatives. The very presence of that neck collar, incidentally, rules out any of the larger skuas, which lack that collar in every plumage.

Lastly, the color of the upperparts was a very rich dark brown, whereas the other jaegers' upperparts in breeding plumage should be somewhat paler, even moreso for the Long-tailed, which is the only other jaeger that would show any kind of extension to tail rectrices like this bird. But again, the obvious 90-degree twist mentioned before rules out the Long-tailed, whose extended tail feathers would be completely straight when present, and would appear parallel to the ground when the bird is in a seated position (Pomarine's tail feathers are perpendicular to the ground).

Previous Experience

I'd only ever observed Pomarine Jaeger twice before: the first time on a pelagic boat tour with Shearwater Expeditions in 2000 on Monterey Bay, and more recently in October 2006 with an immature bird at Chatfield Reservoir in Denver. I've observed Parasitic Jaeger on a couple occasions, including at sea and along the Pacific Coast when I lived in California a decade ago. My only sighting of a Long-tailed Jaeger was at McIntosh Reservoir in 2009.

On that aforementioned pelagic tour, we also had a very close and spectacular overflight of South Polar Skua, providing me with an excellent sense of the difference in body contours and flight behavior between skuas and jaegers, and between those birds and true gulls.
