Habitat

Typical water storage reservoir, minimal shore vegetation, DOW recreational lake (i.e., it is stocked with fish) and it also contains good supply in most years of gizzard shad.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  8 x 42 Bausch and Lomb Elite binocular, Questar scope (owned by Lisowsky)
Distance:  Most of the time, at least 50 yards
Light:  Variable. Most of the time we were on the east side and looking west, but as I recall most of this period was overcast and the light seemed good for gauging color (i.e., flat, not contrasty)

Description of the Bird

This bird first caught my eye while sitting on the water because of its dark brown color and gull-like appearance.  As I scanned past, it lifted off the water and pursued a gull in typical jaeger fashion. This was its routine the rest of the time we saw it over 5 visits spanning 6 days.  While on the water it either bathed by dunking it head in the water, or it scanned for gulls with fish.  When in pursuit of a gull, it was steady and relentless, using its strong flight ability to reel in gulls from behind with amazing ease.

The bird was dark chocolate (not milk chocolate) brown.  It frequently showed a double crescent of white on the underside of the wings, with the brighter arc being the base of the primaries and the second arc (about half as bright in intensity) being in the wing coverts.  In top view, the bases of the primaries were somewhat paler than the rest of these feathers, giving the appearance of a single, subtle arc.  It showed a pale rump.  Long retrices absent, giving the tail a short, blunt appearance.  Distal one-third of bill dark, rest appeared gray.  Face darkest in lores region.  Bulky, chesty build.  Fairly deliberate wing beat, but top speed achieved in remarkably short time.  Undertail coverts patterned with straight (not wavy) horizontal stripes, with alternating light tan and dark brown.  It was about the same size as Ring-billed Gull.  Neck thick.

Similar Species Discussion

This was a jaeger, not bulky enough to be a skua, so the only other two realistic possibilities were Parasitic and Long-tailed.

Bird was just too bulky to be a Long-tailed, with its flight being impressive as to top speed but appeared "heavy" and relentless, not particularly acrobatic.  It bill was too long and thick for Long-tailed.  The double-wing arcs would seem to eliminate both Parasitic and Long-tailed.  The crisp, straight barring on the undertail, with the dark coloration (on the tail and elsewhere, for that matter) was "cold", with no warm brown tones.  Parasitic tends to be warmer brown, Long-tailed can be cold like this bird was.  But Long-tailed is eliminated per above discussion. We were never sure whether this was a "dark" morph or "intermediate" but I would lean toward "intermediate".  Extent of dark bill tip OK both Parasitic and Pomerine, not extensive enough to be typical of Long-tailed. 

Resources Used

Standard field guides plus Olsen guide to skuas and jaegers.

Previous Experience

This was my first Pomerine for Colorado.  I had seen perhaps a half dozen on pelagic trips off the West Coast.  I have seen one Long-tailed in Colorado prior to this, a few off-shore.  Ditto for Parasitic.  I had no experience with skuas at the time of this observation.
