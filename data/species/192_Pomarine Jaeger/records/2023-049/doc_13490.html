Description of the Bird

Overall brown seabird about the size of California Gull.  Bluish beak with black tip (1/3) and hooked tip.  Underwing brown with two pale arcs at base of primaries (wide) and the second one narrower, paler and more basal.  Undertail brown and white banded with bands being fairly wide.  Upper side of outer 6-7 primaries (P4 or 5 thru 10) with white outer edge at tip, with P10 being the brightest. Legs bluish. Breast and belly dark brown.  This was an "intermediate" bird, darker than "light" morph, paler than "dark" morph.  Most medium brown feathers with paler brown edges, with rump being paler than back.  Tail black on dorsal surface and wedge-shaped (no long retrices).  Bird was silent.  Rarely chased gulls because (1) gulls were in low numbers and (2) gulls were not actively feeding.  While swimming alone, usually far out on reservoir, it frequently picked at small items on the water surface.  I guessed these were chironomid midges.  When closer to shore this was confirmed.  Midges were found by the bird along the floating, anchored logs in the northwest corner of the reservoir and during one episode when it actually swam over to the west shore, then south along the shore, then over to the northernmost fishing pier where it was observed at a distance of 2 meters directly BELOW me gleaning midges from the pier pilings. On a few occasions it swam over to the logs occupied by Ring-billed Gulls, one Herring Gull and Double-crested Cormorants and unaggressively tried to climb up on the logs.  The cormorants kept it off or acted mildly aggitated and swam away.  I think the jaeger was more after midges on the edge of the log than trying to rob and/or displace the log birds,.

 Finder of the bird was Cole Wild.

Similar Species Discussion

This bird was bigger than the other two jaegers (Long-tailed and Parasitic).  The wings were wider at the base.  The bird appeared "pot-bellied" in flight.  The double-arc on the underwing was unlike the other two. The banding on the undertail coverts was wide and the pale stripes brighter than the other two jaegers.  The bill tip was only 1/3 black instead of 1/2 black like the books say is typical for pomarine but I guess that could be variable.

Previous Experience

I have seen maybe a half-dozen Pomarine Jaegers on pelagic trips and this was my third in Colorado.  The two previous Colorado birds were 25 and 27 years ago.  I have seen two Parasitics in CO and three Long-tails in CO.  So, I don't have a ton of experience with jaegers and every one seems to look different than the others.  This was by far the closest I have ever been to a jaeger, which made it much more satisfying than most of them which are brown birds flying way off.
