Description of the Bird

When sitting on the water, the bird had a gull-like posture, but perhaps a little more corky and buoyant looking. A bright white flank showed most of the time, which contrasted with a brownish-gray back. The white flanks also contrasted with a dark gray or brownish-gray breast and bib. An extensive yellowish nape that transitioned to a pale grayish-white throat was distinctly visible. The top and sides of the head were pretty much just black. The bill was medium-lengthed and fairly robust. It was mostly pale, but appeared to have a dark tip when seen in the occasional direct sunlight. Direct size comparison on the water with other gulls wasn't possible (other gulls maintained a large buffer zone even when the jaeger was on the water), but in general the jaeger appeared about the size of a Ring-billed Gull, although chunkier.

When seen from underneath in flight, the clean white belly was very prominent. The vent area and undertail coverts appeared dark gray with some light speckling. The legs weren't very noticeable, presumably because they were dark. The underside of the tail appeared nearly black, without obvious markings. The tail was a bit wedge-shaped, tapering slightly towards the base. The 2 central tail feathers were markedly longer, and appeared quite worn and tattered (feather shafts about all that was left), so presumably they were much longer when fresh. They projected beyond the main tail end about as long as the bird's bill. Going forward from the belly, a dark gray collar is visible. The transition from clean white belly to the dark collar is narrow, but not clean or abrupt. Dark speckling is visible in this transition. A light throat is visible in front of the collar, but not quite as clean white as the belly. Yellow from the nape was visible along the sides of the neck. The border separating the black upper head parts angled forward from the upper nape to just behind and under the bill base. In flight the bill appeared compact and fairly robust, and mostly kind of gray. Without direct sunlight the dark bill tip wasn't always discernable. Going sideways from the belly, the clean white gave way to dark speckling in the axillaries. The underwing coverts were mostly dark gray, with two faintly contrasting paler gray bands visible parallel to the leading edge of the wing. The lightest part of the underwing coverts appears to be the base of the outermost tract. The bases of the flight feathers were the palest part of the underwing, appearing pale gray. The distal portion of the flight feathers (perhaps about 1/2 of the exposed part) were darker, but not quite as dark as the bulk of the underwing coverts, except for the primary tips, which looked about black. The primaries also appeared to have lighter outer edges, lending some definition to these tracts.

When it was closing in on a hapless Ring-billed Gull, the size of the bird was easy to compare. It was perhaps only slightly larger in overall dimension, but appeared more robust in body and showed much more powerful flight than the Ring-billed Gulls it was after.
Behaviors: The bird spent the majority of the time resting on the water, bobbing along in a buoyant fashion. Periodically it would fly off in pursuit of gulls (mostly Ring-billed), overtaking them with powerful flight and deft maneuvering to stay on the tail of the victim. This harassment would immediately break off when the gull being chased regurgitated a fish. At least once, the jaeger caught the recycled fish in flight, but at other times it dropped down to pick up its prize off of the water. When the jaeger was flying around, any gulls in the area would scatter and try to gain elevation to avoid engagement with their antagonist. After several successful chases the jaeger just rested on the water without chasing any more gulls.
Call: n/a
Plumage: Alternate II or III

Similar Species Discussion

The flight style, wing shape, bill shape, and buoyant posture in the water suggested a bird belonging to Family Laridae. The robust body and bill, flight style, and extent of dark coloration in the wings and dorsal surfaces eliminated terns. Darklooking juvenile gulls would not show such a white belly, and the long central tail feathers plus yellowish nape and dark head eliminated other gulls. To my knowledge the combination of dark head, yellowish nape, and long central tail feathers is unique to jaegers. Long-tailed Jaegers would lack the dark collar, have a more delicate, dark bill, have a more gray belly, etc. Additionally, the bird appeared much too large to be a Long-tailed Jaeger when seen directly behind Ring-billed Gulls. The aggressive behavior of the bird doesn't seem as consistent with Long-tailed Jaeger as the other two jaeger possibilities. Parasitic Jaeger is a trickier bird to eliminate. However, the bill on this bird looks too stout for Parasitic Jaeger, and no white spot at the upper bill base/face junction is visible. Additionally, the black of the face clearly extends below the gape line, which shouldn't happen in Parasitic Jaegers. The collar appears a bit dark and complete through the front of the breast for Parasitic Jaeger as well. I think that the row of pale-based outer underwing coverts are more expected in Pomarine Jaeger than Parasitic Jaeger, but my references seemed ambiguous about this feature.
