Description of the Bird

Bird was initially observed perched on a cottonwood snag in the far southwest corner of the woods, in an open area of sagebrush understory. I immediately took note of the largely vulture-like posture and carriage while perched, but possessing a strikingly obvious yellow cere and a dark head. (I recognized immediately that this had to be either a Common Black-Hawk or a Zone-tailed Hawk, although I leaned strongly toward it being a ZTHA based on habitat alone). It stayed on the branch for only a moment, and I never even had time to view it through my bins perched before it took off. Fortunately it flew in my direction and I was able to see its tail from the underside, which was long and had thin alternating bands of light and dark all the way to the terminus. As it flew further away I could see its strong resemblance in appearance and flight style to a Turkey Vulture, but it tended to stay at tree-top height or even dip below that, unlike what you'd expect a TUVU to do.


After losing sight of the bird, I re-spotted it circling fairly high overhead, occasionally being joined by a pair of Mississippi Kites. At this point I was able to photograph the bird. I noticed the continuing resemblance to TUVU soaring style, yet possessing distinctly buteo-like wing tips with only somewhat emarginated primaries, and a larger head than any TUVU.

Similar Species Discussion

Turkey Vulture - the yellow legs and the large dark head with prominent yellow cere eliminate TUVU

Common Black Hawk - eliminated because of the comparatively long tail and the thin alternating tail bands, which COBH only possesses as a juvenile but while also having brown streaky plumage

Previous Experience

I've encountered ZTHA in southern Arizona on a couple occasions, and have photographs from one occasion. I've also seen COBH in Costa Rica, at close range, also photographed.
