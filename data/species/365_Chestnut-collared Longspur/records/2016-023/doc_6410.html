Description of the Bird

The bird was foraging along the roadside, the only area not snow-covered, with several dozen Horned Larks. I first noticed the Chestnut-collared Longspur when it flew a few feet and showed its significant white retrices as it landed. Jessica and I were then both on the bird, and noted its nearly all-black belly. In profile, the reddish nape was obvious. It did not yet have well-defined facial markings, either black streaks or yellow patch. We were able to watch it forage for about 20 seconds before it again flushed a short distance and called. It gave the distinctive "cher-dee" flight call of Chestnut-collared Longspur twice from about twelve feet in the air, and then landed again. I took one photograph about this time (included and uncropped). The bird is in flight, pointed away from the camera, and blurry, but the white retrices and part of its black belly are still visible. Just after the photo, two other cars drove by, flushing the entire flock of Horned Larks and the Chestnut-collared Longspur. We did not locate the bird again after that.

Similar Species Discussion

It was almost immediately clear what species we were looking at due to the proximity of the bird, but the extensive white on the tail, black belly, and reddish nape all eliminate Horned Lark. When it first flushed, the tail pattern suggested an upside-down black "T", making McCown's Longspur a possibility. When the bird landed though, the nearly all-black belly and clearly reddish nape eliminated McCown's Longspur as a possibility. Although it is getting late in the winter to encounter Lapland Longspur, and we did not consider Lapland Longspur at the time of the sighting, the observed bird's black belly and extensive white on tail eliminate Lapland Longspur as a possibility.

Resources Used

From my work in eastern Colorado, I am familiar enough with the species that I did not use any resources at the time of the sighting to identify the bird. After the sighting, Jessica referenced her Sibley Field Guide to Birds of Western North America (first edition) to reinforce her identification. 

Previous Experience

I have encountered this species in flocks of dozens to hundreds in eastern Colorado during multiple spring and fall seasons while working on various bird monitoring projects.
