Description of the Bird

The three of us were in Tyler's boat birding the reservoir. We were looking at two Common Loons in breeding plumage. As we began checking else where for birds we noticed another loon nearby that was not in breeding plumage. We motored over to have a closer look and first noted how big it was compared to the Common Loons. We slowly approached the loon for good photos.  Brenda Wright, and Tyler Lausten took many photos while I studied the loon. Large loon, yellow pale bill, long neck and overall uniform gray. Likely a first year or secnd year loon that was uniform gray and no sign of breeding plumage. The bill was held upright at about a 30 degree angle above horizonal it showed little fear of us and the boat. In the next several minutes the loon did not dive or swim away from us even though we were very close. Sex was not determined nor certain age but a non-adult.

At this point we knew the loon could be none other than a Yellow-billed Loon. No calls were given. I am not sure who first saw the loon but it may have been Tyler.  

Similar Species Discussion

Brenda and myself have seen lots of Common Loons in all plumages. Pacific Loons and Arctic Loons are smaller than Common Loons and do not hold bill upward. Red-throated Loons are even smaller. We have seen all loon species but have llittle experience with Arctic Loon.

Our loon was very large with a large bill which eliminated all other loon species. The neck lacked all the neck marking associated with the other loon species. In fact our loon had little neck plumage variation except light gray in front and around the face. 

Previous Experience

This is the first county record for Montezuma. There are five other records of Yellow-billed Loon in Western Colorado and we have seen only one in eastern Colorado. We have seen Yellow-billed Loons in Alaska, however. All other loon species have shown up in western Colorado several times and Brenda and I have studied them in depth except Arctic Loon. We have seen Arctic Loons in Europe.
