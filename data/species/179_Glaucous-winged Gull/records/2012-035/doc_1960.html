Description of the Bird

At about 5pm, Tim Smart and I were scanning the many gulls scattered about Prewitt Reservoir. Far away, I saw a gull with an even pale gray (roughly similar to Herring Gull) mantle circling in a mass of swirling gulls. The lighting was excellent, and though distant, the look was good enough for me to feel confident that it was an adult Glaucous-winged Gull. We saw the bird several times, probably for 3-4 minutes total, but finally it disappeared, and nothing else new came into view.

We headed down towards the dam. Just before the dam started, we climbed atop the dike, and a flock of gulls popped into flight from the shore. One of them was the Glaucous-winged Gull. It was about 100 feet away in excellent light, and it took a couple round trips (giving good looks at upper and underwings), before heading out across the reservoir.

This was a bulky gull that appeared larger than nearby Herring Gulls. The mantle was similar in color to the Herring Gulls, or perhaps, just a wee bit darker (as I?ve often noticed in WA). The trailing edge of the upper wing was white from secondaries through inner primaries, where it then started to narrow. The outer two to three primaries were slightly darker gray distally, with most of darker color seeming to be on outer webs. White ?mirrors? were present on P10 and P9. The rump, tail, and head were completely white (many GWGUs in WA are in full alternate plumage by March), as was the head. The bill was hefty and yellow (I did not look for red gonydeal spot; it presumably was there, but possibly was absent). The legs were pink. They eye color was not noted.

The underwings looked entirely pale. I did not note whether the secondaries or primaries contrasted at all with the wing coverts (in bright light, this modest contrast is often lost), excepting that the distal wingtips showed a shadow of the upperwing pattern, with some dusky seemingly ?showing through? and the mirrors visible: again a similar pattern to the upper wing, but faint.

The lighting was excellent, with the hazy sunlight coming directly from behind us. Some heatwaves were present in initial sighting, but at second sighting, neither heatwaves nor wind sullied our view. I was using a Kowa 88mm scope with a 20-60x eyepiece.

Similar Species Discussion

In my experience, this bird was typical for GWGU adult. Hybrids would show darker wings, particular at tips. Glaucous Gull would lack darker tips, have paler mantle
