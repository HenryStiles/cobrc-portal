Description of the Bird

First, my trip report, including a brief written description of the species, can be found here: http://tiny.cc/OXM2U

A large, pale-dusky gull. About the same size as nearby Herring Gulls, although appearing huskier and chunkier in overall jizz.

The bird was rather uniformly colored overall, paler than nearby first-cycle Herring Gulls. Its tail and primary tips were about the same shade of light brown as the rest of the bird--they were about the color of coffee with a lot of milk stirred in. The wings were relatively short, not extending very much beyond the tail. On the primary tips on the bird at rest, I was able to see slight variation in shading, but it was not substantial.

The bird's bill was mainly or entirely dark, and it was fairly large. Given its distance, I can't swear that the bill was entirely dark, but it was at least mainly dark.
Behaviors: Standing on the ice with many other gulls.
Call: None heard.
Plumage: first-cycle

Similar Species Discussion

1. Herring Gull. First-cycle Herring is usually darker overall, with higher-contrast plumage, especially with regard to the flight feathers of the wings and tail. Herring Gull's bill at that age is a bit longer and thinner, sometimes showing a fair bit of pale coloration, although that is variable. Herring Gull is longer-winged and not as chunky in appearances as Glaucous-winged.

2. Some first-cycle Thayer's can appear quite similar to first-cycle Glaucous-winged. Indeed, plumage can appear practically identical. However, Thayer's is a slighter, daintier bird, with a notably thinner bill. Structural differences between Thayer's and Glaucous-winged are like those between Herring and Glaucous-winged (see above), only more so. This bird was too big, too chunky, too short-tailed, and too large-billed to be a Thayer's.

Glaucous Gull. A dark first-cycle Glaucous Gull would appear pale-dusky overall like a Glaucous-winged, but even a dark individual would be lighter than a normal first-cycle Glaucous-winged Gull; in particular, a first-cycle Glaucous Gull would have practically white primaries, unlike the pale-dusky primaries of this bird. Also, a first-cycle Glaucous Gull would have extensive pale at the base of the bill, unlike this bird. Body structure would be about the same, but a first-cycle Glaucous Gull might be expected to appear somewhat more massive overall.

HYBRIDS. Sorry, but I cannot rule out the possibility that this bird was a hybrid. Frankly, I don't see how any bird with Glaucous-winged Gull parentage can be declared to be "pure"--except with molecular analysis. Since the primaries were concolor with the rest of the mantle, I would say that Western x Glaucous-winged Gull ("Olympic" Gull) is not too likely for this bird--but I certainly wouldn't rule out that possibility. As to the possibility of a Glaucous-winged x Herring Gull hybrid, the bird in question was chunkier than might be expected of that hybrid combination, but, again, I don't see how the possibility can be ruled out.

Previous Experience

I've seen 2 other problematic first-cycle Glaucous-winged Gulls in Boulder County, including one that everyone else declared to be "pure," even though I held out the hybrid possiblity.

I've seen thousands of birds of Glaucous-winged Gull parentage elsewhere, mainly along the Pacific Coast. I've spent a lot of time studying birds of Glaucous-winged Gull parentange in the Great Basin, where apparent hybrids greatly outnumber apparently pure birds.

I am pathologically curious about hybrids, I believe they are far more prevalent than most birders care to realize, and, of course, I immediately wondered if this bird could be a hybrid. Beats me. It was a nice phenotypic match for Glaucous-winged Gull. If that's our standard, then I think it's a pretty good candidate for Glaucous-winged Gull. But if we're realistic about hybrids, I don't see how this bird can definitively be declared to be a non-hybrid. I would say that about any other Colorado Glaucous-winged Gull for which molecular evidence does not exist.
