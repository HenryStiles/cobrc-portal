Description of the Bird

At about 9:30 am (2.5 hours post sunrise), Tim Smart and I were watching gulls feeding on dead fish in a small cove at Prewitt Reservoir. We?d positioned ourselves so that the sun was to our backs, and the lighting excellent. I saw a big gull coming in that was not particularly pale, but one that lacked darker wingtips above, and had completely pale primaries beneath. I yelled to Tim, alerting him that there was a Glaucous-winged Gull like bird coming in. It landed. The hefty bill, the primaries that were of the same darkness as the remainder of the wing, the short wings, and bulkiness led to the ID of Glaucous-winged Gull, unsullied by genes of other species (as far as one can tell by phenotype, anyway). Overall, the bird was in view for about 30 seconds in flight and 3-4 minutes while perched, as close as 100 feet in flight and about 150-200 feet while perched. Viewed through Kowa 88mm scope, with 20-60x eyepiece. No heatwaves or wind to disturb viewing.

This was a large gull. Though its length, when standing, was about the same as the nearby Herring Gulls, its bulk was clearly greater. Its bill was stouter than that of the nearby Herring Gulls, being thicker and with a more bulbous tip. The bill was entirely black. The legs were pink. The back was pale gray, perhaps a shade darker than that of an adult Herring Gull, but distinctly paler than that of a California Gull. The wingcoverts were well worn and brownish. The tertials and folded primaries were of the same darkness as the wing coverts and grayish-brown. The primaries had whitish edgings. The wingtips barely projected beyond the tail. The head and neck were white with moderate brownish smudging on sides of neck and nape and lighter smudging onto crown and auriculars. There was no streaky appearance to this smudging (as is typical of Herring Gull). The underparts were moderately marked smudgy brownish. The tail was grayish-brown (did not look to see if there was a pale tip to tail or not). The upperwings, in flight, were rather evenly grayish brown contrasting slightly with a grayer back. The tail was grayish-brown and the rump white. The undersurface of the primaries were evenly whitish.
Behaviors: See above. Picking at dead fish at lake shore

Similar Species Discussion

Glaucous-winged x Herring Gull would have darker wingtips, typically smaller bill, sometimes paler eye, usually not as bulky in body. GW x Western Gull would be darker backed, darker wing-tipped. This bird appeared like a typical GWGU to me, nothing deviating from that bird's usual appearance in this plumage
