Description of the Bird

At about 8:50 am, with low cloud cover starting to dissipate, I noted a rather chunky gull with near-white wingtips floating high in the water next to a few Herring and Ring-billed Gulls. The flock was about 100 yards in front of me. At that time, the combination of filtered sunlight, the lack of heatwaves, and no wind created optimal viewing conditions. I watched the bird float about, giving me looks from all angles, for about 5 minutes before it took flight. It then circled about for 30-60 seconds and landed. It sat for another minute or two, took to the air again, and then drifted off to the far western edge of the reservoir, landing out of sight. I was clearly looking at a “big gull.” It had short wings, yet was still a bit longer, and taller, on the water than the nearby Herring Gulls. It had a moderately hefty (for GW Gull) bill, which was mostly black, with a bit of pink coming in at the very base. The gull was in “first winter” plumage, being largely brown with a gray back. More specifically, the back and scapulars were dusky gray, a shade or two darker than those of the nearby adult Herring Gulls. The wing coverts were mottled brown and worn. The primaries were nearly white, with pointy tips (often seen on GW Gulls with worn primaries in my experience). The underparts were smudgy medium brown, paler than those of immature Herring Gulls, but darker than most (all?) immature Glaucous Gulls. The dark of the body faded into a slightly paler brown head, which was somewhat smudged and streaked. In flight, the upperwings looked uniform pale brown, with a hint of a darker secondary bar, and primaries that appeared the same color as the rest of the wing. The back was a bit darker and gray. The rump was slightly paler than the evenly brown tail, which was a tad darker than the wings.

Similar Species Discussion

The bird’s appearance is fairly typical for GW Gulls by March/April in my experience. By later in the season, some have nearly entirely white wings that contrast with surprisingly darkish gray scapulars. Adult GW Gulls are slightly darker backed than Herring Gulls. I find that immature Herring and GW Gulls (and Western Gulls, for that matter) often look a bit darker backed than adults, perhaps because of some brown admixed into the back plumage. In any case, a Herring x GW Gull or Western x GW Gull would certainly have had darker primaries. A GW x Glaucous Gull hybrid should have far more pink on the bill, probably a somewhat longer bill, typically a paler rump and tail, and definitely a paler back. I found this bird to rather typical of GW Gulls that I grew used to seeing in w. Washington during this season.
