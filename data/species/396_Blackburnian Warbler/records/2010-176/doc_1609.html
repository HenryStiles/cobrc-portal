Habitat

"Riparian" vegetation consisting mostly of Siberian elms and skunkbush sumacs around a highly organic, treatment pond full of mosquitos and midges. The elms apparently held a population of European Elm Flea Weevils, judging by the telltale holes in most of the extant leaves still on the trees.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  8X42 B&L Elite binocular. 20D Canon with 100-400 lens.
Distance:  Usually about 20 feet away.
Light:  Sunny, warm day. Light good. Seen mostly with the sun behind me to the south while I looked north from the south side of the pond area.

Description of the Bird

This was a very plain warbler, typical warbler size and shape, thin billed. Overall bird was gray above, pale yellowish below with indistinct stripes on the underparts, with two bold white wingbars. Strong, thick whitish-yellow supercillium which extended back onto the sides of the neck but did not appear to connect with the stripe on the other side of the head (i.e., did not form a "collar"). Wide gray stripe on the side of the crown, which gave the bird a gray-capped look (except for the pale stripe in the front of the crown with provided a "part"). Faint yellowish, central forecrown stripe. Back gray with faint tan stripes. Area below the eye marked by a broad pale yellow arc. Lores from the eye to the bill medium gray. Bill longish, somewhat downcurved, and mostly dark with apparently pale inner mandible margins. Auricular area plain gray and set off from the light arc below the eye and pale yellow supercillium. Rear of the auricular slightly darker than the main part. Throat pale yellow. Breast pale yellow with broad, faint stripes (not unlike a purple finch in the lack of sharp definition to their edges). Flanks also faintly striped. Belly whitish, apparently mostly unstriped (not seen all that well). Wings blackish with bold white wingbars, upper wingbar being slightly more conspicuous. Flight feathers appeared mostly black with white edges. Leg color dark.
Behaviors: Very active, not seen all that well in any one spot, moving all around at about 15 feet high above the ground in the exotic Siberian Elms that ring the water hole. Apparently seeking European Elm Flea Weevils (a new exotic insect in CO that appears to be replacing the long-standing "default" insect of elm foliage in CO, the also-exotic Elm Leaf Beetle). Did NOT confirm the food item, however. Disappeared before I could get great looks or decent photos, unfortunately.
Call: Chip note heard, typical warbler, harsh, "-ck" at end, difficult to describe in a way that would be helpful
Plumage: first-winter

Similar Species Discussion

Immediately settled on this bird being one of the pale plumage and sexes of the "Black-throated" Dendroica warblers (dark rump eliminated Yellow-rumped, Magnolia, Cape May, Palm). The primary task seemed to be separating it from Townsend's or Black-throated Green. Pale back-stripes and pale stripe on the front of the crown seemed most helpful in providing proof of the species and the age. Facial pattern of darkish auricular area, pale lower eye arc, not unlike Townsend's and some Black-throated Green individuals. Again, mantle stripes proved most helpful (but unfortunately do not show all that well in the photos provided - in the photos this is more of a suggestion). Young Hermit should show complete yellow eyering, plain unstriped back and lack the crown stripe. Golden-cheeked would show darker, plain back and lack the central crown stripe. To my eye, the bill on this bird looked longer and thicker (and maybe paler in certain views) than typical Townsend's and Black-throated Green (and Hermit, Golden-cheeked, Black-throated Gray)

Resources Used

Standard field guides including Sibley, Dunn warbler guide in the Peterson series, mostly.

Previous Experience

Not a lot with this age and sex of Blackburnian (few seen in the East, none in CO that I know of). Lots of experience with all ages of Townsend's, limited experience in CO with Black-throated Green but lots in the East.
