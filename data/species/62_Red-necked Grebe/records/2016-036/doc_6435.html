Description of the Bird


http://ebird.org/ebird/view/checklist?subID=S30045181

http://ebird.org/ebird/view/checklist?subID=S30048944


My two observation periods are recorded in eBird reports above.  To summarize, in the morning observation period, the RN Grebe was relaxing in the lake, occasionally preening.  The RN Grebe was closer to the male Common Merganser than the female CO Merganser was at first, which helped to compare the two species to bring out the differences.  The RN Grebe's neck was a range of reddish brown, whereas the CO Merganser's was black.  The grebe's bill was more stout and yellowish at the base, and its body was gray, darker on top and lighter on sides, whereas the merganser's bill was red and its body mostly white with black on back.  In the afternoon observation period, the RN Grebe was fishing all alone.  I made two video recordings of the fishing RN Grebe for two reasons.  One was to record the grebe's behavior.  The other was to record what most fascinated me as I watched the bird; namely, how its red neck color changed in relation to the low-angle light from the setting sun, from dark brown to reddish brown to orange to even yellow.  I have uploaded one video to YouTube (https://youtu.be/OOTKzEmNqow).  It is also linked to my eBird report.  Although I do carry a notebook with me, I find myself taking notes by photos and videos increasingly lately.  The advantage is that photos and videos allow me to simultaneously observe and document the birds, whereas note-taking has me looking at my notebook, rather than the birds.    

Similar Species Discussion

My immediate consideration was the female Common Merganser, which had been seen earlier swimming side by side with the breeding-plumaged male Common Merganser.  As the two birds moved around, the differences became obvious.  The grebe's head was black-on-white, the female merganser's was mostly reddish.  The grebe's stout bill with yellow base contrasted with the merganser's thinner, red bill.  Once these features were seen and photographed, I had no need to consider Eared Grebe, which had been recently reported from the nearby Pinon (or Pinyon) lake, or Horned Grebe.  If the bird were in non-breeding plumage, I would have.
