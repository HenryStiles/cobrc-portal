Description of the Bird

On May 30, 2014, while monitoring a Western Grebe colony at Lake John as part of RMBO's ColonyWatch, I observed a pair of Red-necked Grebes building a nest at the northwest edge of the lake in a cove where the Western Grebes regularly nest. I was scanning the cove with a Zeiss 20-60X spotting scope. Based on profile and plumage the birds were recognizable as Red-necked Grebes. Slightly smaller and chunkier appearing than the nearby Western Grebes, the pair had shorter necks. They had dark backs and crowns with conspicuous white throat and cheeks. The front and sides of the neck and the upper breast were reddish. The bill was large. Later, with a closer view we could see that the bills were yellow at the base with darker tips. I asked Paul Slingsby to look at the birds, allowing him to make the identification without prompting. He immediately identified them as Red-necked Grebes. After watching from the spit at the easterly side of the cove, we drove on the main road around the lake to a spot directly opposite the nest. The nest was about 10-15 meters from the road. One of the birds remained near the nest platform while the second swam some distance away and brought back wet vegetation to the nest. The nest was in nearly open water with some smartweed beginning to emerge.

When the grebes began to appear nervous, we drove around to the southwest side of the cove and continued observing with a scope as the birds continued building the nest. I revisited the site again June 2-3. On June 2, the birds had abandoned the original nest site. One of the birds was sitting on a rudimentary new nest toward the middle of the cove in barely emerging smartweed. The second bird, the male, was building a third nest in marsh grasses on the southwest side of the cove near a pull off used by campers. The bird buidling the third nest split his time between adding material around the bird in the middle and adding to the nest in the grass. By the end of that day, the nest in the middle of the cove had been abandoned and one of the birds was on the nest in the grasses. Incubation there was continuing through June 18.

Similar Species Discussion

The birds were noticably smaller than the nearby Western Grebes, ruling out Clark's Grebe as well. The size of the bill alone ruled out any of the other grebes. The contrast between the white cheeks and throat and dark crown and neck along with the reddish foreneck and breast eliminated any of the loons.
