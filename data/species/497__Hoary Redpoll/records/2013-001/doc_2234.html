Description of the Bird

Grayish finch the size of American Goldfinch with small yellow bill and cheery red cap. Underparts mostly white with pale pink suffusion on upper breast, extending slightly into forward portion of flanks. Flanks with diffuse discontinuous pale gray streaking, heaviest streak rearmost, often not visible under wing and stronger on bird’s right side. Undertail coverts white except for gray streak on longest undertail covert. Tail mainly seen from underneath, pale gray. Upper surface of tail darker gray with whitish fringes. Rump very clean, mostly white with some pink tinge towards tail. Wings grayish with wide white bar formed at tip of greater coverts. Back grayish with a faint tinge of beige, overall pale color formed by wide pale fringes on mantle feathers. Head with a thick-necked profile, similar gray-beige as mantle overall. Lores and chin medium gray, separated by pale feathers in-between to create discontinuous subtle mask. Nasal feathering extending down base of maxilla creates a bumped look on forehead. Pale line extends from above eye to forehead, separating mask from red cap. Cherry-red cap well defined, smallish, extends only slightly behind eye.
Behaviors: Feeding on Nyjer seed with Common Redpolls (up to about 20), American Goldfinches, and House Finches.
Call: Not noted
Plumage: Adult male exilipes ssp

Similar Species Discussion

The only similar species to consider here is Common Redpoll. While separating the two can be challenging, here I primarily follow David Sibley’s online guidelines for identifying Hoary Redpoll. Following is his list of suggested Hoary Redpoll traits to look for, with my comments on how this bird matches up in follow in parentheses. He notes that the traits are best compared with Common Redpolls of the same age & sex, which fortunately I was able to do.
- the scapulars should be paler with frosty whitish edges on Hoary
(this bird lines up well, with the pale gray fringes on the scapulars contributing to the overall pale upperparts. See comparison photo with ad. male Common in similar pose, angle, and lighting to evaluate.)
- flank streaking should be sparse and narrow on Hoary
(in comparison to Commons this bird is very lightly streaked, the streaks are pale gray instead of nearly black, and the streaks are discontinuous.)
- undertail coverts should be white or with only narrow streaks on Hoary
(Yes, as noted the bird has a single dark streak on one of the longest undertail coverts, well within the range for Hoary Redpoll.)
- rump should be mostly white on Hoary, whiter on males (but male Commons can also have a noticeably pale rump)
(This bird shows this well, see description.)
- the bill should look short and small, with fluffy nasal bristles covering the base of the upper mandible and making the forehead bulge a bit (but Commons also have tiny bills, you have to study some Commons at close range before you will feel confident using this feature)
(The bill on this bird was distinctly smaller than the ca. 20 Common Redpolls present, and has the forehead bulge created by the fluffy nasal bristles covering base of maxilla. While bill size in redpolls can overlap, (per Pyle and Seutin et al.) one of the things that originally caught my attention with this bird was its smallish bill, though I’ve seen Hoaries with even smaller-looking bills.)
- The pale edges on the wing coverts and secondaries should be broader and whiter on Hoary (but this is variable in both species and, as with all of these clues, it’s important to compare birds of the same sex)
(Yes, in comparison with other ad. male Commons, the wing bar on this bird is a bit wider. Additionally, the dark areas in the wing are distinctly lighter than comparable areas in ad. male Common Redpolls.)
Sibley also says “Other features that have been mentioned and that might be worth watching for and testing (but many of these are very subjective and my impression currently is that these are less useful than the ones mentioned above):”
- a smaller and brighter red “poll” on the forehead of Hoary
(The bird clearly shows this feature.)
- less dark color on the throat and lores on Hoary: more restricted and not as dark (but appearance varies with angle of view)
(The bird clearly shows this feature.)
- Hoary overall larger and fluffier (but note subspecies differences in size) (Not noted, if pressed I’d say the bird looked the same size as Commons.)
- neck appears thicker on Hoary
(I think this bird was subtly more bull-necked than Commons nearby for comparison.)
- fluffy “leggings” on Hoary (Not observed.)
- A tendency for Hoary to raise tail when foraging on the ground (Not observed.)
- relatively longer tail (Not observed.)
- there may be subtle differences in calls, and this deserves more testing, but singling one calling bird out of a flock is usually impossible. The larger “Greenland” subspecies also may have different calls.
(I didn’t note any differences, though Steve Mlodinow reports hearing a standout call note in the flock when they flushed which we compared to Hoary recordings immediately via my iPhone. Steve said the call was a match.)

Of additional interest is a three-dimensional character index for redpoll identification that Sibley summarized online. While not designed to identify redpoll species, the index was made to objectively sort redpolls in a study into dark, intermediate, and light groups. I believe this bird would score at 14 or 15 (out of 18, with 1-6 scores assigned to undertail coverts, flank, and rump. A score of 1 is heavily marked while 6 is extremely clean.) This places this bird into the lower end of the pale group (14-18) for male redpolls (Sibley: Numerically, a male (pink breast) with a score from 14 to 18 was called a Hoary in Troy’s paper, a score from 3 to 7 would be Common, and from 8 to 13 intermediate.)

In summary, I believe that the suite of characters described above indicates that the bird a male exilipes Hoary Redpoll.
My conclusion is supported by the expert opinions of Steve Mlodinow, Tony Leukering, David Sibley, Daniel Gibson, Peder Svingen, and Kim Eckert.

Additional Information

Comments: Colorado and indeed, the northern half of the US is experiencing a very strong irruption of redpolls this winter. If not unprecedented, this is shaping up to be the biggest movement of these birds into the state in decades. Expert comments (see Similar Species section above) and eBird range map comparisons of Nov-Dec. 2012 vs. the same span in 2011 show that Hoary Redpolls are along for the ride into the lower 48 states (see last image uploaded showing comparison maps.)
