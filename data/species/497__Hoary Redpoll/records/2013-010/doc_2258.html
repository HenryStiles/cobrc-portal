Description of the Bird

Bird was small finch body shape with small, short yellow bill. I would estimate that it was only 4-5 inches in body height. The bird was a pale snowy color with faded, minimal streaking on the sides. The crown of the bird had a clear bright red crown patch. The facial markings were pale and whitish with little in the way of markings. His chin had a small black patch. The breast of the bird was snowy white with no other streaking, the undertail coverts were snowy white with one small brown streak. Based on the position of the bird I could not see its rump and flew it flew away before I could examine its back feathering further. Its bill was noticeably shorter than the common redpolls around it from a subjective comparative standpoint.
Behaviors: The bird really did not exhibit any specific behaviors other than to remain perched in group of trees that were parallel to feeders at the Discovery Science Museum in Fort Collins, CO. The bird was in the middle of group of other redpolls numbering around 20. I was able to watch the bird for about 3-4 minutes before it left the area.
Call: NA
Plumage: winter

Similar Species Discussion

The common redpolls that I had viewed leading up to the sighting were considerably more streaked, had a brownish coloring on their sides, head, and face. The bird I viewed was the exact opposite - very pale and snowy in coloring. I also looked at the bill size subjectively and concluded that its small short size was different from the common redpolls. Lastly, I was able to see the undertail coverts and when I saw only one small streak I concluded it was a likely Hoary. I would have liked to have seen its rump, but I was unable to.

Resources Used

For ID and general characteristics I used the National Geographic Field Guide 6th Ed for general comparison. I also discussed some general characters with those birders around me - specifically using the undertail coverts streaking as a tool for ID. I followed up after the sighting by reviewing several discussions on Cobirds about distinguishing Common Redpolls and Hoary Redpolls and determined that it was likely a Hoary Redpoll.
