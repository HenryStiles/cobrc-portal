Description of the Bird

A redpoll species by the red "poll" on the bird's forecrown. Undertail coverts white with one long dark shaft streak on the longest feather. Belly white. Flanks with faint grayish streaks. Face with whitish wash to background and with a few darker feathers but no contrast between ear coverts and face = NOT a brownish face. Bill short, thin and yellow, surrounded above and below by SMALL AREA of BUSHY dark feathers hiding the base of the bill. Rump, when seen, did not have streaks to it (see photo by Nick Moore). Feet and legs black. Amount of red on forecrown smaller in length than COREs. Wings dark with two white wingbars. Back streaked brown and white. Looked for white scapulars but not sure about that feature. Overall frosty or cold appearance and noticeably lighter than even light-colored COREs.
Behaviors: Traveled with large flock of redpolls, most identified as Commons. Came to feaders, thistle, platform, and millet feeder prefering it seemed the platform feeder and the perches above both sets of feeders. Fed voraciously. Perched for up to a minute, sometimes by itself.
Call: flight calls not distinguised from much more abundant Common Redpolls who gave a type of chew chew chew in flight
Plumage: basic - I think a first year male

Similar Species Discussion

Common Redpoll - The many Common Redpolls nearby and those we had been watching for over an hour where much browner-backed, for sure not having any light scapulars or feathers in that area. Even the light-colored COREs when photographed, I could see numerous dark shaft streaks (moslty shorter and wider) on the undertail coverts (photographed for comparison). If patient, the COREs' rumps where seen to be heavily streaked altough seeing the rump on any bird was often impossible. All of the known COREs had short pointed bills, but noticeably longer than this bird's bill. Their (COREs) faces were light brown without the white wash in the background (as seen on HOREs) and even the light-colored COREs had faces where the auriculars were browner, contrasting with their light gray faces. On the adult male COREs, the pink of the breast and bellies was extensive and was more red than pink, unlike the color of very light pink on the HORE. There were some COREs seen here with relatively small bills but nothing approximating the bill of the HORE and the amount of dark above and below the bill on the COREs was not fluffed out obscuring the base of their bills as it was on the HORE. There were some 1st year COREs with indistinct flank steaking but the overall appearance was still obvious for CORE after undertail, rump, face, and back coloration was compared.

Resources Used

I used every resource I own and also the Sibley redpoll comparative webpages and comments from Dan Gibson about the Schmoker redpoll (different age and amount of pink) and what are the 5 most important features to look at.

Previous Experience

I worked 4 summers in Alaska, two where both redpoll species occur and I spent 3 weeks in Barrow in late September but all of my experience was over 20 years ago so no recent experience studying HORE until this winter. I watched the flock in Ft. Collins for two hours and photographed as many likely birds as possible. One of the summers I worked in coastal AK above treeline with Richard Bunn, former chair of the CBRC. At that time we used this short bill feature as seen on this bird as our biggest clue to separate HORE from CORE. We described the AK HOREs then, as birds that looked like someone stepped on theri face pusing in their bills, a feature this bird shows.
