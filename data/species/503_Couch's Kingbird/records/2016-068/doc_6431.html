Description of the Bird

I first saw a medium-sized yellow-bellied flycatcher fly in from the south and land in the top of a mature upright juniper shrub.  It was immediately recognizable as a kingbird.  Since it was my first kingbird of any kind this year, I obviously took note of it.  The first thing that struck me as odd was the lack of white on the outer tail when it flared its tail to land.  The tail appeared unformly brown with narrow pale feather tips.  I next noticed a head pattern that did not fit with the expected Cassin's Kingbird, given the appearance of the tail.  The head was light gray with a slightly darker auricular area (aka "mask").  This pattern maybe contrasted a bit more than would be expected in Western but did not have the heavier mask typical of Cassin's, nor the stark white malar/throat contrast seen in Cassin's.  Looking at the throat, I noticed it was light gray with a greenish yellow (not gray) upper chest.  The rest of the underparts below the greenish-yellow upper chest was bright yellow.  The bill also appeared a bit thicker and perhaps a bit longer than I would expect in Western, but not as thin and long as Tropical Kingbird.  I was beginning to suspect Couch's at this point.  The bird flew to the top of another juniper and caught something large, which it ate rather quickly with its head down and I could never make out.  It might have been a darner dragonfly.  I moved to its location on the north side of the ditch to try for closer observations/photographs.  The bird flew to the south side of the ditch, flew behind a big juniper, and I never saw it again.  Finishing out the description of this yellow kingbird, its back was pale greenish-gray but not as bright green as the field guides and my experience indicate is typical of Tropical/Couch's.  In fact I have seen Western Kingbirds with greener backs than this bird had.  That is the only field mark that is not in keeping with a slam-dunk ID of Couch's.  The vocalizations I heard were infrequent and rather quiet, not at all aggitated or hyper, and were mostly single notes that sounded like "kit" or "kip".  They were not unlike notes I have heard from Western but also would seem to be good for Couch's.

Similar Species Discussion

The lack of white in the outer tail was the major difference between this bird and Western.  The bird also had a distinctly (but not deeply) notched tail which did not seem in keeping with Western.  The voice was similar.  The back was similar.  The throat seemed more yellow-green than I remember with most Westerns that have at least a narrow band of gray across the upper chest.  This bird's bill seemed a bit thicker and longer than typical Western.

The bill did not seem long enough for Tropical and the call note was wrong.

The lack of a bright white throat and too pale a head pattern eliminated Cassin's.  Tail looked pretty similar to Cassin's in terms of the upper feather color and pattern, except for the notch.

Resources Used

Sibley, National Geo and Stokes field guides.

Additional outside information obtained by the CBRC:

I am near 100% sure this is a Couch’s Kingbird.  The bill is not large enough to be a Tropical, but I will circulate this among some experience Texas birders who also see these two taxa regularly and see what I get back. 

You will never be able to prove that one way or the other without a specimen. To my knowledge there is no evidence that they hybridize in areas where they are sympatric in northeastern Mexico and southern Texas, so I generally dismiss that as a real possibility.  I liken it to Hepatic and Western Tanagers in that Western Tanager is known to hybridize with all of Piranga when they come into contact, but has never been documented to hybridize with the one that they share a significant range and habitat overlap with.

Mark Lockwood

Sorry that I'm just getting around to your email. Like you and others, I feel that this bird is a Couch's. The bill length and proportion seems to be fairly typical of the birds that we see in S. Texas. I have some photos of calling birds that I could dig up if necessary that are consistent with the Colorado bird. I am going to send you three photos from two weeks ago of calling Tropicals (there were six birds present) from my backyard at Rancho Viejo. These are at least two birds represented in the three photos and possibly three different individuals. You can see from these photos that the bills are longer and more parallel from base to tip than the Colorado bird. The Colorado bird appears to have both a shorter bill and a more tapered bill (thickest at base to a relatively narrow tip), especially noticeable from one of the photos from below. At this angle, Tropicals can sometimes show a tapered bill, but never as much as the Colorado bird in my opinion. Even relatively "short" billed Tropicals do not show this much tapering in my opinion. You have my permission to forward these images to Mark Peterson if you feel that it would help.

Brad McKinney

Hi Mark and everyone.  I suppose this bird was silent, as they usually are in central Texas.  I wonder if they attempted to stimulate the bird with recordings?  I had one in Temple on July 24 that stimulated nicely to Tropical recordings while flat-out ignoring Couch's.  Fortunately, this bird also had a nice bill shape that was a better fit for Tropical than for Couch's.  While considerable overlap exists, I agree with you that this Colorado bird has a better bill for Couch's--shorter and deeper proportions versus longer and thinner.
