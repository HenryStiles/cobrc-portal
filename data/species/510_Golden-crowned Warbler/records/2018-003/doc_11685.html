Description of the Bird

warbler based on bill - dorsal surface, but not head, a dull gray. Tail often cocked. Ventral surface from chin to undertail coverts fairly bright yellow. Head pattern distinctive - broad, dull yellow median crown stripe with orangish feathers noticeable anteriorally (photo). Median crown stripe surrounded by black stripes extending to nape but not all the way to base of bill where replaced by a gray loral spot. Yellow eye ring is spit into dull yellow eye arcs. Dull gray auricular. The bend of the wing was bright yellow (photo). Wings, dull gray are short as seen in flight photo. Eyes appeared dark brown; legs and feet are sometimes appearing bright pink and other times appearing dull pink.  

Calls sometimes single sharp chip or sometimes double chips especially when feeding in close approximity to HOWA male and Mac warbler but IMO not similar to a wren chip as described in the literature. Sometimes silent for 45 minute stretches but usually chipping when foraging. When I saw it, the bird was always from 10 inches to 2.5 feet above ground level feeing in dense stand of chokecherry shrubs (Prunus virginiana var. melanocarpa) which btw is not on Jennifer Arkerfield's range map in the new Flora of Colorado for Cheyenne County.

Similar Species Discussion

The closest species in appearance is another Basileuterus, Three-banded Warbler, but in that species the gray of head is paler and a black supercilium terminates posteriorally in a black triangle. Three-banded Warbler also has an olive back, not dull gray. Russet-crowned Warbler, another Basileuterus, also looks somewhat similar to GCWA but it has deep rufous median crown stripe and darker gray on the face. It also has both a white-bellied race and a yellow-bellied race.

Golden-crowned Warbler has three or four subspecies groups depending on taxonomy used. The Stripe-headed Warbler group has four subspecies occuring closest to the southern U.S. border with Mexico. As of 1996, it has been determined that all U.S. records of GCWA are of the culicivorus group and the northeastern brasherii subspecies which is described in Wablers by Dunn and Garrett and whose description closely resembles the Colorado bird given that the amount of rufous in the crown stripe is variable. Interesting is the one New Mexico record from May 9 and May 10 is close to the longitude of the CO bird but slightly further west. 

In the U.S., OCWA has the overall color pattern to some extent but it is easy to eliminate based on the amazingly good photos of the GCWA submitted to eBird. (Compare the terrible photos posted from the six or so locations for GCWA in Texas). Also, the Texas sightings are all from fall, winter, and early spring, but none from May.
