Description of the Bird

the bird was a fairly large hawk, about the size of Red-tailed Hawks seen soon before the observation. The shape was fairly typical of a Buteo, with a short neck, fairly large head, broad and long wings, and chunky body. The bird flew with powerful wingbeats, a little faster than Red-tailed Hawk.

The plumage was entirely black, except for the tail, which had a bold and wide white basal tail band, and a wide (but less so than the basal band) black subterminal band, and a narrow white terminal band. The tail was extremely short for a hawk, and was broad, especially when fanned out in flight. The wings were extremely broad, much more so than any other buteo I've seen.

The legs were long for a Buteo, with long tarsi that were bright yellow. The cere was bright yellow, and the bill and eye were black.
Behaviors: On the first day I saw the bird, I observed it for only 3 seconds or so, flying down the river at a distance. I was only able to observe the tail pattern during this observation.

On the second day, we first saw the bird fly up to the exposed perch about 80 yards away. Right after it first landed it lost its balance, tiltling forward and fanning its tail, giving us an excellent look at the tail pattern and uppertail coverts. It only stayed on the perch for about 10 seconds before flying into and through the trees, and then down the line of trees to the west. It flew across the gap created by the road, allowing for excellent if short looks at it in flight at about 50 yards. It was last seen flying to the west outside the trees, but likely dived back into the trees at some point.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Species other than hawks, including Double-crested Cormorant, were ruled out by the Buteo shape of the bird, with short neck, chunky body, different shaped wings, short tail, etc.

Zone-tailed Hawk was ruled out by the shorter tail, bolder white basal tail band and lack of other bold white tail bands, white instead of grayish bands when viewed from above, much broader wings, and behavior and habitat. Harris's Hawk was ruled out by the shorter tail, completely black plumage without rutsy patches, much broader wings, and habitat and behavior. Other dark and black buteos (including dark Broad-winged Hawk and Rough-legged Hawk) were ruled out by the tail shape and pattern, and the very broad wings, as well as the long bright yellow legs, and yellow cere.

Great Black-Hawk was ruled out by the lack of white uppertail coverts, smaller size than would be expected for that species, completely black feathering on the thigs, as well as extreme unliklieness of occurence.

Resources Used

Howell and Webb's Mexico bird guide was consulted to rule out Great Black-Hawk.

Previous Experience

I have seen three adult Black-Hawks in the US, but many in Mexico and Costa Rica. I have seen two Great Black-Hawks in Mexico. I have seen many of all the similar species except for dark-morph Broad-winged Hawk.
