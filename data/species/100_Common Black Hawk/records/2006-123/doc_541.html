Description of the Bird

On approximately September 2, 2006 Rich Miller reported to southern CO birders that he might have seen an adult Common Black-Hawk, describing the tail pattern of this species. He did not see it the next day but saw it again near the MacKenzie Bridge over the Arkansas River on approximately 4 September seeing the same feature which led him to believe that it was indeed a Common Black-Hawk. Rich also found what he thought was this species (also an adult)in September of 2005 upstream near the Reynolds Bridge over the Arkansas River.

SeEtta Moss reported a dark raptor in Canon City between 5 and 8 September along the Arkansas River, not giving specific information about the location but most likely the same bird that Rich Miller had seen.

I decided to try to see the bird and waited until Sunday the 10th of September to do so, thinking that morning traffic over the bridge would be the lightest on a Sunday morning.

Walking along the south shore of Valco Ponds in Canon City I stopped near where the pond bends to the south and looked towards the Arkansas River and immediately saw a large dark bird perched in a small plains cottonwood tree on the south bank of the river. I first thought that it was the target bird or possibly a roosting Turkey Vulture. When I put my spotting scope on the bird, I was thrilled to see that it was an adult Common Black-Hawk. I quickly took one digiscoped photo. During the time between digiscoping and putting my camera back in its pouch, the bird had dissapeared. Here is what I saw through the scope and later revisited in my one photo.

Large blackish raptor perched on a small limb about 20 feet up in a tree that leaned out over, or very close to the water of the South Bank of the Arkansas River. The most noticeable feature was the wide white tail band, which seemed about to be located about mid point of the tail. The next most noticeable feature was the bird's all-black undersides including everything except the wide white tail band and the thin white terminal tip to its tail. I could see longish yellowish tarsi and a yellowish cere. The tail and wings appeared to be about the same length. Separating the white tail band and the terminal whitish band was a dark tail band that was wider than the white terminal band and much narrower than the upper white tail band. Although very distant, I thought that the dark tail band was between 1 and 2 inches wide.

I only saw this bird the first time for about 15 seconds, not including the 10 seconds when I was trying to get my camera lined up with my scope lens. The time was 6:55 AM.

I saw the bird later, 7:20 AM, when I walked downstream along the Valco grass road that parallels the river. I flushed the raptor from about 2/3 of the way from the top of a very large willow on the south bank. Tree was about 75 feet tall. The bird flew away from me to the south and then flew behind the trees lining the south bank, towards the MacKenzie Bridge to the west. Here is what I saw during about 5 seconds of flight through my bins. I could see the wide white tail band and this time I could see very wide dark wings that were rounded at the tips. I concentrated on the shape of the wings but did not notice anything else about the bird in flight including any light areas towards the tips of the wings, but at the time I was trying to get an overall impression of the wing shape and overall coloration. The bird was screened during its flight by the trees along the river. I knew that it was either a Common Black-Hawk or possibly at Great Black-Hawk which I can't totally rule out. The wings were the wrong shape for Zone-tailed Hawk or any dark-phased Buteo.
Behaviors: Perched in a 40-60 foot high cottonwood. Bird faced the river (and me) and appeared to be looking at the shore or water.

In flight bird didn't not make any sound. Banked to the right on very broad wings. Flapped maybe three times and then banked and disappeared. Only studied the shape of the wings and briefly looked at the tail again.
Call: no vocalization heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Zone-tailed Hawk has similar dark body and banded tail, but the wings of a Zone-tail are very long and extend past the tail tips at rest. This bird's wings were about even with the tail tips and this bird was chunkier, in my opinion than a Zone-tail.

Previous Experience

I actually have a lot of experience with Common Black-Hawk. I spent one summer walking most of the upper Gila River and much of the San Fransisco Rivers in SW NM looking for Southwest Willow Flycatcher. Here I found about 8 nests of Common Black-Hawk. Another year I surveyed many areas of AZ for nests of Peregrine Falcons for AZ Maime and Squish or as they call themselves Arizona Game & Fish. While doing surveys I found about 8 more nest of Common Black-Hawks. I have seen fewer perched Zone-tailed Hawks, mostly in AZ while I lived there. I have some experience with Great Black-Hawk, but admit that I have trouble separating the two. I know that Common Black-Hawk is more dependant on water than Great Black-Hawk although I have seen Great Black Hawk perched in a few times in the Pantanal of Brazil. I saw close to a hundred Common Black-Hawks this winter in the mangrove areas around San Blas, MX this winter.

Additional Information

Comments: I am sure that this bird is not a Zone-tailed Hawk, based on wing shape mostly, during the brief time I saw it fly and the tail pattern as described above and in the lousy photo that I am sending. The legs were too long for Zone-tail. However, I am not 100% sure that it is not a Great Black-Hawk, but that species is only hypothetical in the U.S. I used to work with Troy Corman, AZ Game & Fish and he described a bird that he and Dave Kreuper had on the San Pedro River in AZ that they think was a Great Black Hawk, so that species remains a very slight possibility. But, I don't think that the Canon bird is big enough for Great Black-Hawk.

I didn't even consider any dark morph buteo based on the tarsi length and especially the tail pattern as seen from underneath and the shape of the wings in flight.
