Description of the Bird

This was a dark blackish-brown large-sized raptor, longer looking when perched than a RTHA. The cere stood out because it was yellowish and contrasted with the dark coloration of the bird. When perched a broad white subterminal tail band was visible on the ventral surface of the tail. The legs appeared longer than the common CO buteos. The underwing pattern (see photo) was striking when the bird mantled after flying twice. There was much more white at the bases of the individual primaries and secondary feathers. In flight the bird was characteristic of the species. The very broad-based wings were apparent. The short tail gave the overall impression of a raptor that was uncharacteristic compared to other CO raptors. In flight ALL the flight feathers and the tail feathers were worn. (I looked in BNA online and this species in its NA range molts once per year in October which can explain why there feathers were so worn.
The tail tips were thinly white; there was a broad subterminal dark band on the tail and then proximal to that there was a broad white tail band and proximal to that there was more dark on the tail at the base.
Behaviors: Mostly sat up in a tall cottonwood. I saw the bird flush twice on its own and twice more when a photographer insisted it was his right to get closer. It mostly glided and did not travel more than a 100 meters each time it flew, sometimes less.
Call: non heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Zone-tailed Hawk and all of the dark-morphed Buteos are superficially similar but none have the large broad wings contasting with the banded and very short tail of this bird.

Resources Used

I looked at Zone-tailed Hawk in the Big Sibley guide.

Previous Experience

I am very familiar with this species. When I surveyed for Peregrine Falcons on designated streams in AZ, I saw about 20 individuals sometimes very close, and they mostly acted tame until approached closely as this bird did, the AZ birds flying off but not very far. I also have seen about 10 others when I surveyed southern NM for Southwest Willow Flycatcher. Both species seem to prefer similar slow-flowing creeks. I have also seen a few in the Davis Mountains in TX and the bird that appeared along the Arkansas River in CO. They are distinctive, especially in flight.
