Description of the Bird

Mark Peterson, Brandon Percival, and I were joined by Glenn Walbek, Loch Kilpatrick and another individual (sorry, can't remember name) on the afternoon of 1 May. We were watching shorebirds in mid-afternoon at Lake Holbrook. The sun was to our backs, heat waves were not problematic with the closer shorebirds, which were 100 yards away or so. I was using a Kowa 88 mm scope with 20-60x eyepiece at 60x, comfortably.

Description
After about 20 minutes, I noticed a dowitcher (there were about 125 dowitchers present), that had orange only from throat through chest (maybe uppermost belly). The base color of the remainder of the underparts was whitish. It also had rather prominent barring on the sides, turning into diffuse spotting on sides of chest. The orange color was paler than the deeper rufous of the surrounding LB Dowitchers. The orange color extended onto face. The upperparts appeared paler than that of the LB Dows, not with feather tips more contrasting, per se, but with an overall more patterned look. Plate 82.7 of Paulson's shorebird book gives a good representation, except that some of the gray wing coverts were retained from "winter" plumage. The primary projection was visible, whereas it was not in the nearby Long-billed Dowitchers (short projection). This could, however, have been due to incomplete growth of the tertials. Notably, however, the banding in the tertials were fresh, and they were more contrasty, with broader orange-ish bands, than in the LBDOs. The tail pattern was not clearly seen. The bill length was within range of the nearby Long-billeds, but when it held its bill up once, for a relatively prolonged period of time, a small kink (often, but not always visible in SBDO in my experience, occasionally seen in LBDO in my experience) was detectable. No obvious size difference was noted (between this bird and LBDOs), but the bird had a more "flat-backed" and a less "football like" shape when actively feeding.

While I was looking at this bird, Mark had found two other dowitchers of interest. These looked more like typical hendersoni and were in full alternate plumage as far as we could detect. Their underparts were not as deeply colored as the LBDOs, but the orange underparts did extend from throat/face to undertail coverts. The upperparts were very much like Plate 82.7 in Paulson's book. The difference in tertial markings between these birds and the LBDOs was noticeable, and similar to bird 1. The sides were distinctly barred breaking into light spotting on the sides of the chest. The primaries could be seen to project beyond the tertials (short projection). Tail pattern was, again, not quite visible. When feeding, the flatter backed appearance was consistently evident. Both birds looked shortish billed, one perhaps shorter than the range of LBDOs present. Neither of these two birds presented their bill in profile long enough to determine details of shape.

The facial pattern (crown, supercilium) was not scrutinized in any of these birds, excepting that the supercilium was rather broad anteriorly in all three, seemingly more so than most of the LBDOs present.

No vocalizations were heard.

Similar Species Discussion

Identification.
The back color, shade of orange beneath, tertial pattern, and body-habitus-while-feeding should ID all three birds, at least when used in combination. I find that, contrary to the wording in some books, that spring SBDOs have more obvious barring on sides than LBDOs. I believe that some of this is due to the heavy feather edging on fresh spring LBDOs obscuring some of the side barring. I have found this fairly consistently useful. For the bird with only partly orange underparts, subspecies might come into question, but O'Brien (pers comm.) has seen hendersoni on breeding grounds with similar underparts and such is depicted in several guides as well. A LBDO going from basic to alternate should not have orange on neck/chest and have distinct dark (not gray) side barring as bird number 1 did. Also, LBDOs in this state of molt typically have some gray still on face, which bird number one did not. The barred sides without barred sides of chest is also reputed to be a SBDO mark. Bill shape, primary projection are somewhat supportive.

Having dealt with this ID quandary dozens+ times per year in WA, I feel very comfortable with (ie, confident of) the identification of all three birds as SB Dowitchers, and probably all three were hendersoni. Mark and Brandon agreed with the identification. Glenn, Loch, and friend were gone before this point. There opinions are unknown to me.
