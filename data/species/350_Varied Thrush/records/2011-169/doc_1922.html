Description of the Bird

A bird of similar size and shape of an American Robin with a more slender build and longer appearance. The crown was black that extended down the nape to the top of the back. It had an orange supercillium that began above the eye and extended in an arc down the side of the neck. There was a black mask in the auricular area that began at the base of the bill, encompassed the eye and extended down the side of the neck, parallel to the supercillium "stripe". The throat was orange down to the upper breast where it met a fairly broad black band that extended across the breast. The orange breast continued below the band to the belly area where it faded into white, which continued through the vent to the end of the undertail coverts. The flanks were a mottled orange/gray towards the front-end to gray at the back-end. The back was a uniform dark gray all the way down through the upper tail covers to the end of the tail. The lesser and greater wing converts were orange giving the appearance of two orange stripes when the wing was folded. When the bird flew there was a distictive broad orange stripe on the upper wing that extended from the base of the wing to the end of the wing along the greater coverts, contrasting nicely with the dark gray secondary and primary feathers.
Behaviors: The bird flew into a crab apple tree that was approximately fifteen yards and slightly uphill from me. It landed on a branch and kind of bobbed a bit while looking at me. It hopped up and picked at a shriveled crab apple, and then flew back to an apple tree at the other side of the yard.
Call: No vocalizations were heard.
Plumage: Adult male

Similar Species Discussion

The only species that should be reasonably considered is the American Robin. The robin does not have the orange supercillium stripe or the black breast band, and its breast color is a different shade of orange. The distinctive orange and gray pattern of the wings is very diagnostic for only this species.
