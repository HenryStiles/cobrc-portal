Description of the Bird

I was informed of the presence of a Varied Thrush coming to the feeder of a private residence in Roxborough, Douglas County. On the morning of February 7, 2004, Doug Faulkner, Bill Schmoker, Elena Thomas, and myself arrived at the home of Dr. Tom Olkowski to attempt to view the bird. Within roughly 15 minutes, the bird arrived to some Gambels oak on the west side of the backyard. Within a few minutes more, the bird occasionally came to the feeder, but usually stayed back in the oakbrush. The bird was roughly American Robin sized, though seemed more elongate. The upper parts were slate gray. The sides of the face were black, which formed a mask from the lores through the eyes to the rear head. An orangish supercilium extended from the eye rearward. The crown was grayish brown and somewhat scaled. The cheek, throat, and breast were orangish-rust. There was a diagnostic black band that extended across the upper breast. The vent and undertail coverts were mostly whitish. The wings were blackish basally but had two orangish wingbars across the greater and lesser coverts. There was also orange-red edging to the black flight feathers. The tail was a dark, charcoal gray with very small white tips to the rectrices. The legs were pale pinkish.
Behaviors: The bird was rather skulky and chose to usually stay back in the oakbrush. A few times, however, it flew into the feeder and picked at seed.
Call: None heard
Plumage: basic

Similar Species Discussion

American Robin is the most similar species. Robins differ in lacking a broad, black breast band, the orange supercilium, and orange wingbars.
