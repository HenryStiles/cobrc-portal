Habitat

Spruce trees and ground under spruce trees which is covered in spruce cones, needles, and other leaves. The puddle it frequents in our yard is also mostly shaded by the large spruce tree.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Nikon Monarchs 8X42
Distance:  60-80 ft. in our yard, initially seen at 20 ft.
Light:  Bird is almost always seen in shady, conditions under spruce trees. Never seen in full sunlight. Some days have been overcast and a few sunny. Light conditions had no impact on the ID.

Description of the Bird

This bird is American Robin-size with a blue-gray back and orange underparts. The bill is dark and slightly longer than an American Robin robin with a slight downward curve. The bird has a thick, dark "collar" across the top of the breast. The bird has a dark face with an orange supercilium. The upper tail is also blue gray. The throat of the bird is orange. There are two orange wing bars.
Behaviors: This bird has been observed mainly when it flies down to the ground to pick and scratch underneath large spruce trees. It is extremely shy and return into the thick, inner branches of the spruces at the slightest disturbance. Recently it has started eating dried mealworms that we have put out for it under a spruce in our yard. It quickly runs from worm to worm and then usually goes to drink from a small, permanent puddle in the yard. It was observed being aggressive toward a nearby Dark-eyed Junco once, but normally pays the birds feeding around it no attention.
Call: We have not heard any vocalizations from the bird.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

The adult American Robin does not have a dark collar across the upper breast and its back is more of a brown-gray than blue-gray. The bright orange supercilium and all dark bill also seperated this bird from American Robin. Varied Thrush really isn't similar to any other species here.
