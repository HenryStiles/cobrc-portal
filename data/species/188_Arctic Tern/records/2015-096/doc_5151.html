Description of the Bird

I observed the bird for 10 minutes as it stood quietly on a rock in shallow water about 1 foot from the shore. I happened upon it and it was not bothered by my closeness. I had been observing shorebirds when I noticed it. The sun was behind the bird but it was quite distinctive with very white and contrasting black coloration. The head appeared rounded. The black cap was very extensive. Bill was short and dark red in color. Legs were very short, orange in color. He moved around on the rock. The tail seemed longer than the wingtips. I looked away for a few moments and while my back was turned the bird very quietly flew off and I did not see it in flight.


Similar Species Discussion

I am not experienced with terns so I after I had driven home I used my photos and recollection, Sibley's new 2nd Edition, Peterson and Kaufman to determine the bird was either a Common or Arctic tern. I forwarded a photo to one of my mentors, Jeannie Mitchell. Jeannie sent my photo on to Bill Maynard who believed it to be Arctic. I forwarded more photos to Bill and he was kind enough to point me in the direction of Arctic and suggested I make this report. After hearing from Bill, I forwarded the photos to John Rawinski who is the main man in the SLV for his consideration. As there is no valley record, he suggested I also make this report.

Quite frankly it was happenstance that I saw this bird and a magical moment for me as it was magnificent. If I had known better I would have put in a call to John right away. As it was I was on my way home from the valley and was looking for Sabine's Gulls that had been reported. It was my first visit to Smith Reservoir. What can I say?
