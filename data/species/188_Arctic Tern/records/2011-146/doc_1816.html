Description of the Bird

The bird was clearly a Sterna tern -- about half the size of the Ring-billed Gulls on the reservoir, with wingbeats about twice as fast, mostly white plumage, pointed wings and a deeply forked tail. It had a medium-length, thin, pointed, mostly dark bill and a neat black cap that came from the bill through the eyes and around the back of the head, including much of the crown and the nape. Its wings were almost entirely whitish, with only hints of darker markings, like a very thin black tip to the outer primaries visible from below only at close range, and a dark (not quite black) carpal bar on the upperwing with a slight brownish wash around it, also only really visible at close range. The palest part of the upperwing was the central trailing portion, which was silvery-white, a triangular region on the wing roughly in the same place as the white on a Sabine's Gull wing.
Behaviors: The bird flew all around the reservoir, usually within 20 feet of the surface, occasionally wheeling and diving down to the water's surface to pick something off the water. Its flight was fast and its wingbeats rapid and choppy, especially compared to the comparatively lumbering flight of the Ring-billed Gulls around it.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: probably juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

It was easy to rule out gulls by the plumage pattern, size, flight style, and (especially) the deeply forked tail. Non-Sterna terns were ruled out by size, wingbeat speed, and shape. Forster's Tern was ruled out by the head pattern -- Forster's should just have a black eyepatch, not a cap that meets around the back of the head. Common Tern was ruled out by wing pattern, especially the silvery triangle on the trailing edge of the upperwing and the lack of extensive black on the outer primary tips.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Arctic Terns on the breeding grounds in Maine at a couple of different colonies, and also found an adult in Nebraska a few years back (record accepted by the Nebraska Records Committee). But this was the first time I'd studied a juvenile and the first Arctic Tern I'd seen in at least 5 years.
