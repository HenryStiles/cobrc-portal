Description of the Bird

I was birding Windsor Lake around Noon on 26 May. There were a number of Forster's Terns about, some perched, some feeding over the lake. I was hoping to find a Common Terns, and was briefly distracted on a couple occasions by one-year old Forster's Terns. I then noted a tern that was flying both more actively (more dips and swerves, presumably in pursuit of insects) and with more "bounce" than the Forester's Terns. This flight style quickly brought Arctic Tern to mind. I watched the bird for about 10 minutes total over a 30-minute period of time. I can only begin to guess at distance: 100 to 300 yards? Maybe closer at times, most likely more distant at times. I was using a 88mm Kowa telescope with 20-60mm eyepiece. The lighting was watery sunlight/high overcast. There were no heat waves at the distance this bird was observed for most of the time.

Description
The Arctic Tern was a Common/Forster's/Arctic type tern with forked white tail, grayish mantle, black cap. The upperwings and back were uniform pearly gray in color, excepting a narrow modestly darker gray trailing edge on primaries (outer 1/2 to 2/3). The mantle color was well within range of Arctics I've seen before. The sheen present on the primaries of Forster's Terns, even first year birds, was absent. The underwings were white with a narrow medium-to-dark gray border to trailing edge on primaries. The belly, chest, and throat were washed in gray, not the white of the Forster's Terns in the vicinity. The undertail covert/vent color was not noted. The crown was black, forming a nice black cap that seemed complete. The face was whitish, providing a contrasting (but not dramatically so) paler patch between throat and crown. The bill was distinctly red, not the red-orange of a Forster's Tern. The bill also appeared relatively petite. It appeared entirely red, though I cannot exclude a small dark tip. The tail and rump were white. On two occasions, when tail was fanned, I was able to note a narrow dark line on the outer edges of the tail. Structurally, the bird appeared rather stubby-headed compared with the Forster's, fitting well with my experience of Arctic Tern. The tail, however, was not as long as normal for adult Arctic Tern. The flight style was "buoyant" compared with Forster's or Common, as noted above.

Similar Species Discussion

Identification
The gray underparts, proportionately small red bill, stubby looking head, wing pattern, tail pattern... eliminates Forster's Tern. The upper and underwing pattern eliminate Common Tern, as does the small-headed appearance. The flight pattern was also Arctic-like, not like that typical of Common Terns. The shorter tail may indicate that this is a first or second year bird. Apparently, second-year Arctic Terns can look completely adult like, or show some remnants of immature plumage (such as dark bar on forewing, incomplete black cap, presence of dark tip on bill... and shorter tail). I feel confident that this bird was an Arctic Tern
