Description of the Bird

Steve and I had been observing multiple terns feeding over the reservoir as well as a few perched fairly close to us. I noticed a bird begin to fly in towards us and got great looks at it and a common tern in close proximity. I at first took note of the limited dark primary tips of the bird and after viewing multiple COTE I noticted the bird looked somewhat different. The bird made a quick attempt to land on a small sandspit when it outstreached its legs I thought they were noticably small. Also, the black in the primaries was somewhat reduced and I got Steve onto the bird. Unfortunately the bird then began to flay away from us. I then was able to view the bird in my scope for about a minute before it flew into another very distant cove. The most obvious mark in flight was a white flash on the back of the wing extending from the base of the wing and and trailing off after the joint in the wing. The white flash is reminicent of the wing color shown in Forster's Tern and Bonaparte's Gull. The bird flew nearly straight away from us before turning and dissappearing. While hoping the bird would turn around I took note of its flight stlye. While maintaing a constant height the bird appeared to bounce, meaning the body would bob up and down as the bird would beat its wings. This flight stlye is distinct from the much more constant flight stlye I expect from Common Tern. 

Similar Species Discussion

Forster's Terns present with limited black in the primaries like the Arctic also show a wing flash, but on the primaries. Separating Common from Arctic is the main difficulty. The bird first stood out as having much reduced black in the primaries then I had seen that day. I had excellent views as the bird made an attempt to land on a sandspit, while difficult to judge in flight the legs did seem noticably small. My experience with common tern ID is very limited and I focused on taking mental notes on anything the seemed unusual to me. As the bird began to fly away I noticed the flash of white on the secondaries. This was not a mark I had known about before then and got me excited about the bird. Knowing flight style was important I watched the bird until it dissappeared. The buoyant/bouncy flight was quite noticable and after viewing the bird I could compare with the Common Tern nearby. Steve and I watched the bird in flight in scilence and then disccussed our observation afterwards. Both of us had taken the same ID marks down and I learned the white secondaries were a good mark for juvenile Arcitc Terns. I found it a great way to learn about separation of the taxa. Additionally I did not take note of bill structure, however Steve noted a smaller bill and a few other marks I did not know to look for. 

Resources Used
