Description of the Bird

I observed the bird from 9:00 A.M. - 10:30 A.M. (these notes are being written just after returning to my car).

It was a medium-sized tern, with size and shape consistent with Forster's, Common, or Arctic.

The head had a full black cap and nape. On the forehead, a bit of white was visible at times, suggesting that the bird was starting to molt.

The bill was entirely orange, of short or medium length.

It had a full white nape. The underparts were pale gray, somewhat mottled as if it were in molt. The throat and upper breast were whiter than the belly, but even they showed signs of molt.

The upperparts were a smooth, unmarked gray. Its folded wing tips looked slightly darker than the rest of the wings.

It had a very faint carpal bar on its right wing, little or no carpal bar on the left wing.

The wing tips and tail were about equal when it was standing (i.e. no primary projection).

In flight, there was a thin, solid black line on the trailing edges of the undersurface of the primaries. The upper surfaces of the wings showed no obvious black.

When viewed in flight towards the sun, there was bold translucence over most of the wings. I saw this feature on several flights from its resting position.
Behaviors: standing on a sheet of ice in the reservoir, and occasionally making flights out over the water
Call: none heard
Plumage: breeding plumage

Similar Species Discussion

From the overall appearance of the bird, the solid black cap, thin bill of medium length, short legs, long wings, I could narrow the choices to:
Common Tern
Forster's Tern
Roseate Tern
Arctic Tern
The full orange bill (in late November) and the black line under the wings rule out Roseate Tern. The breeding plumage and full orange bill in late November rule out Forster's Tern. Common Tern is the most difficult species to rule out, and I used three field marks. First, the bill was entirely orange. Second, the black trailing edge of the underwing surface was very narrow. Third and most importantly, the wings showed a large area of translucence when backlit.

Previous Experience

I had seen many Common and Forster's Terns, and a few Arctic Terns. Only once or twice before had I observed an Arctic Tern in a situation (pelagic trip) where I could view the wing translucence.
