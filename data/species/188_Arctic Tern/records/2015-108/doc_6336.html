Description of the Bird

This appears to be a worn Juvenile. Distinguishing this bird from the Common Terns I viewed a couple of weeks ago are rounder head, lighter carpal bar & wing pattern (white secondaries & white-tipped greater coverts, & translucent, thin black-tipped primaries.) There was just one Tern. It flew in, stayed around a couple of feet in from the shoreline and mostly preened for 20-30 minutes. It would fly away for a minute and return, then it left for 30 minutes or so and returned to the same approximate spot on the Chatfield marina Sandspit. On October 3, 2015 our group was able to approach the bird to within 50 feet and capture some okay images.

Similar Species Discussion

When I first observed the bird on October 2, 2015, it was in response to a post on the Cobird blog by Glenn Walbeck. Having never seen an Arctic Tern, and having spotted and ID'd Common Terns a couple of weeks ago, I did not have a lot of confidence that his would, in fact, be an Arctic Tern. Upon comparing the shots of the Common from my Flickr page, I noticed that this bird had a much rounder head, a much fainter carpal bar, and that in a resting state, the primaries were a much paler gray than those of the Common Tern. Upon returning home, I examined my pictures more closely and believe that I have an Arctic Tern.
