Description of the Bird

Small Sterna tern, light gray back with scalloped coverts, ends of primaries only pale gray and not black or dark gray, relatively short legs that appeared to be dark red (vs. common or forster's), white forehead with black (almost grayish wash) to top of head and down back of the head, relatively small black bill with orange on base of lower mandible. Very negligible carpel bar, which was really only a few darker gray feathers than the rest of the wing coverts (can be seen in attached photos).

In flight, forked tail was all white with just dark gray or black on outer 1 or 2 rects.  Belly appeared all white and underwings fairly clean white to light gray on the underside of primaries with dark tips to outer 5-6 primaries.  Upper wing, in flight, showed white secondaries and the tips to the greater coverts are white, light gray primaries with the aforementioned grayish tips, and darker feathers on leading edge of the wing (carpel bar spread out), although lighting in various images makes things change appearance and how dark the leading edge is).

I needed photos of this bird since it was my lifer (and I somehow missed several on pelagics off Bodega Bay in Aug 15).  The brid was well documented by others, but apparently never submitted.

Similar Species Discussion

Common and forster's are the obvious most similar species. One noticeable feature to me in comparison to the common's and forster's I've seen was the relatively small bill and short legs that made it very short and low to the ground.  These are not features that clinch an ID for something like this though!! It just looked smaller than common and forster's, althought it really is comparable size.

The bill is too small for the range of forster's.  Forster's is likely eliminated simply on basis that this bird does not have a dark eye stripe but rather has black on back of crown and head/nape. The dark eye line is seen in all ages of forster's in alternate plumage (winter/non-breeding plumage), so this should eliminate forster's.

The lack of dark carpel bar was distinct compared to alt plumage common tern since both ages (HY and AHY) of common have a dark carpel bar in alt plumage, which I've always thought was quite distinct ID feature on a sitting bird. I think I recall the pale carpel bar being discussed when the bird was first found and ID was in question.  Immature plumage (really, this bird appears to be mostly in juvenile plumage) draws similarities to common tern, but the bill is shorter than in a common, and the centers of the coverts and back feathers are more gray than brown (in a fresh juv common).  The shorter legs also lean for arctic.  A juv common would have dark secondaries as well.

This bird was too big to be a Least tern, doesn't have the black eye line as seen in non-breeding leasts, and legs were too long and not yellow.

Previous Experience

This arctic was a lifer, but I've seen many hundreds of commons and forster's (and for that matter least's) across the country.  Although I went to Chatfield to see this reported arctic tern, when you see a species that is similar to species you know well, you notice the small and subtle differences between the species and realize what you are looking at is not the species you know well.  Ok, this happens most of the time anyway. I feel this makes me a better observer in the future too.
