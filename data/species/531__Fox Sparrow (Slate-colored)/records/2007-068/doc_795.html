Description of the Bird

While birding the Russian Olive grove below Standley Lake dam, Tim Smart voiced Fox Sparrow. I looked at him perplexed as both Slate-colored (Passerella iliaca schistacea) or Red Fox Sparrow (P. i. iliaca) would be of unusual occurrence at that location. I soon was able to get on the bird myself, and indeed it was a Slate-colored Fox Sparrow perched in an olive. The bird was large for a sparrow. The bill was short and triangular, with the mandible being gray and the maxilla being dull yellow. The crown was gray, as well as the auricular and malar edge. The rest of the face was also gray. The inner throat was white and contrasted with distinct brown streaking across the upper breast and flanks. The breast streaking coagulated to form a thick dark brown blotch (spot) on the center portion of the breast. The upperparts of the bird were gray overall. The wings were bright rufous, with narrow white edges to the coverts, forming a thin wing bar. The tertials were edged in black. The belly and undertail coverts were white, although the undertail coverts were somewhat buffy and showed indistinct brown streaking. The tail was rufous and the legs were pink.
Behaviors: The bird was perched for minutes along the edge of a Russian Olive and then flew deeper into the grove.
Call: None heard
Plumage: basic

Similar Species Discussion

Nominate Fox Sparrow was eliminated by that groups overall rufous tone to the head, upperparts, and breast streaking. Song Sparrow was not a contender due to the lack of rufous tones. All other rufous colored birds that could occur in Colorado were eliminated based on overall size, shape, tail length, bill shape, and pattern of breast streaking.
