Description of the Bird

Time reported here is only grossly approximate; it could be off by 1-3 hours (I didn't note the time in my field notes, for some damned reason!).  Jason noted swifts overhead while we were in the RMBO back yard setting up for an event that day and he immediately identified them as Black Swifts.  I looked up and agreed with his ID, raced inside the office to get my binocular, and watched them for <1 minute as they drifted out of sight.  The five birds were identical enough that I here describe them as one.  It was a large swift with very long wings and medium-length tail that sported a prominent notch when closed, but showing a square tail when spread.  The wings were fairly broad-based, being nearly centered on the body, with body projection behind wings being only somewhat longer than projection in front of wings.  The hands were fairly broad relative to that of other swifts, lacking the strongly tapered wingtip of the other regularly occurring ABA-area swifts. Wingbeats were almost languid for a swift, without the fluttery appearance of Chaetura.  Coloration appeared entirely dark, with no obvious paler areas, but distance would have precluded detecting anything as subtle as the white fringes of juvenile Black Swift, but would not have precluded detecting anything as obvious as the white patches on White-throated or White-collared swifts.

Similar Species Discussion

White-throated Swift, the most likely species of larger swift at Barr Lake, was easily ruled out by that species's very different structure (medium-length wings, long tail), shape (long, pointed tail creating much larger projection behind wings than forward of wings; long, tapered wingtip), and plumage pattern (obvious white patches on flanks and white extending from chin to belly).  Chimney and Vaux's swifts ruled out by those species' fairly short wings, relatively much larger heads, and very fluttery flight style.  White-collared Swift is even larger, with even-longer wings and a long body extension behind the wings.  Common and Fork-tailed swifts would have a much longer tail with a very deep fork.

Resources Used

Previous Experience

I have seen >300 Black Swifts, 1000s of White-throated Swifts, 10s of 1000s of Chimney Swifts, and 100s of Vaux's Swifts in the US.  In the neotropics, I have fairly extensive experience with White-collared, Lesser Swallow-tailed, Black, Costa Rican, Gray-rumped, and Chestnut-collared swifts, and a little experience with White-chinned and Chapman's swifts.
