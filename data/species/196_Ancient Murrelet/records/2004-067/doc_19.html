Description of the Bird

A tiny gray and white waterbird. Completely dwarfed by the Gadwalls it was hanging out with. Much smaller than even a teal would appear next to the Gadwalls. Very short, bluntly pointed bill that appeared grayish yellow when it caught the sunlight. Forehead, crown, lores, and area immediately behind and under the eye are all nearly black. There were no pale highlights visible in the black portions of the head. The eye was dark and completely surrounded by black plumage. Chin, throat, lower cheeks, and breast almost pure white. Thick spur of white extends up sides of nape and back of head well above the level of the eye. A thin black collar extends down from the lower nape nearly to the waterline but doesnt connect across the breast. The upperparts behind this collar are medium gray, perhaps similar in tone to a typical adult California Gull. The wingtips are also almost black. The flanks of the bird had extensive white, with only minimal gray streaking appearing heavier towards the back half of the bird. These gray streaks trended diagonally upwards and backwards on the flanks of the bird. Overall very compact looking with stocky neck, short tail and short wing projection about even with tail tip.
Behaviors: When I first arrived the bird was in flight, moving out farther towards the central eastern part of the reservoir. Shortly thereafter it made another short flight. In flight it stayed low, with fast choppy wingbeats and a direct style of flight. When it landed it skipped three times or so. At this point it joined a group of resting Gadwalls, where it stayed for the rest of my observation. At times it appeared to be sleeping. It preened some and stretched its wings a couple of times. I observed Ring-billed gulls harassing the bird 3 or 4 times- in each case it dove to avoid the gull, pushing itself underwater with its wings.
Call: not heard
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

The bird was too small to be any N.A. duck, as well as having a bill shape different than any ducks. Bill too short and blunt, and wings too short for any small gull or tern species. Bill shape and color as well as plumage pattern wrong for any small N.A. grebe species. Petrels and Storm-Petrels are longer winged, with dark, pointy, droopy bills, and don't show all of the white where this bird does. Other small tubenoses have different bill shapes and plumage patterns. Among other small diving birds this leaves only alcids to consider. The combination of a very short, pale grayish-yellow bill seems to eliminate other N.A. alcids. Specifically, Long-Billed Murrelet (the other alcid that regularly wanders to inland N.A.) has a dark, much longer bill, as well as lacking the white spur extending behind and above the level of the eye. Other N.A. murrelets have dark bills. Murres and Razorbill are too large, and are dark billed. Dovekie is dark billed. Guillemots are too large and have long dark bills. Puffins bills are much more massive, and they have major plumage differences. Some auklets are similar in size but none have pale yellow or grayish yellow bills and none have the dramatic white spur extending up behind and higher than the eye.
