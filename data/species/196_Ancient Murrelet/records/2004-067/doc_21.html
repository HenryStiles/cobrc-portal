Description of the Bird

The bird was an obvious small alcid (much smaller than Gadwalls -- about half the length and only, at most, 1/4 the bulk -- and Com. Goldeneyes -- about 2/3 length -- in direct comparison; overall dark above, white below, with a very roundish shape). The head was mostly black, except for white chin, throat, and an intrusion of white from the throat up and around the auriculars. The upperparts were all medium gray. The sides were whitish mottled with medium gray and the undertail coverts, as well as the rest of the underparts, were white. The bill was pale, sometimes appearing yellowish, but others looking grayish or horn-colored - depending on the light.

I saw the bird fly a couple times and it did so with very rapid, shallow wing beats. At these times, the outer half of the wing looked dark and contrasted somewhat with the paler inner half. The wing linings appeared to be white, though, due to the shallow and rapid wing beats, it was difficult to be sure. I believe that the remiges were dark from underneath.

When the bird landed after its "long" flight covering half the pond, it made a spectacular three-bounce landing, typical of small alcids.
Behaviors: The bird, for the most part, idled on the water's surface. However, as I got there before most birders, I saw it dive on apparent foraging trips at least four times. When it dove, the bird would open its wings and "fly" down under the surface. I don't know that it captured anything on any dives, as, if it did, the items must have been small, because I could not discern anything in the bill upon its bobbing to the surface.

At a couple of points when it was in the SW corner of the pond near Common Goldeneyes, it threatened a nearby goldeneye with a bird-typical thrusting forward of the head and gaping of the bill.
Call: none heard
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

Plumage pattern and behavior ruled out all regularly-occurring swimming birds. All other small alcids ruled out by combination of head pattern, bill color, upperparts pattern and coloration, and underparts coloration.

Previous Experience

I have seen this species on two trips to Alaska, with the most recent being in July 2004, where I saw ~15 Ancient Murrelets. I have seen >50 Marbled Murrelets, ~10 Long-billed Murrelets, a few Kittlitz's Murrelets (all adults in alternate plumage), and 100s to 100s of 1000s of Cassin's, Parakeet, Least, Whiskered, and Crested auklets.
