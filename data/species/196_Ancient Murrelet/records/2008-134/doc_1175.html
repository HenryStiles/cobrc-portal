Large body of open water. Bird came within ~100 feet of shore (canyon wall) while viewing.

Description of the Bird

The bird had an alcid shape, and was alternately floating and diving. It never flew when in our view. It had a light grey back, short, light-colored bill, and a dark black head with a bright white neck. The most distinctive feature was the bright white patch (hook-shaped) behind its eye, which helped us distinguish it from other possible murrelets and flagged its plumage as nonbreeding.
Behaviors: The bird was in view for at least 10 minutes, diving and floating. It's body was low on the water, including its head. It was a fairly cooperative subject to view, diving when we got too close but surfacing not too far away. I think the closest we may have gotten to it was 50 feet (we were in a canoe on Pueblo Reservoir).
Call: None heard.
Plumage: Nonbreeding

Similar Species Discussion

Other similar alcids include marbled and long-billed murrelet. Neither of these has a light-colored bill or a uniform grey back. Marbled murrelet (nonbreeding) has a bright white neck with white collar, but does not have the white hook shape that this bird showed. Marbled Murrelet's head is not as black as the Ancient Murrelet.

Resources Used

Sibley Guide to Birds. Used at time of sighting (we had it with us in the canoe). Used the photos we took both while in the canoe and later at home where we could zoom in on the computer screen.

Previous Experience

We had zero experience with the Ancient Murrelet or other alcids before this sighting. However, I have been bird watching for probably 30 years and work as a Wildlife Biologist, where part of my work involves bird identification.
