Habitat

Overall landscape was mixed conifer-aspen, but the location is not far (several hundred meters) from pinyon woodlands. The bird was located in a patch of deciduous shrubs in an opening around aspen, Douglas fir, and ponderosa pines. I recognized shrubby cinquefoil as one of the dominant shrub species in the area.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Leica Trinovid binoculars, 8 x 42
Distance:  ~20 ft
Light:  Skies were clear, but the observation occurred during the early morning, and because I was in the foothills, the sun was not visible. The light was good but not bright. I was close enough to this bird that light conditions were not a negative factor.

Description of the Bird

The bird's body was small and warbler-shaped---horizontal oriented body, thin pointed bill, tail not longer than it's body. Its plumage was all black and white---black and white striped crown with white eye stripe; black lores, ear patch, and throat; white underparts with heavy black streaking on flanks; and two distinctive white wing bars. Plumage was similar to Black-throated Gray Warbler; however, this bird did not have a yellow spot in front of its eye as does BTYW.
Behaviors: At first, I did not see the bird but heard it singing spontaneously within some deciduous shrubs near a small aspen stand within a larger mixed-conifer-aspen landscape. The bird then popped up out of the shrubs and landed on a low branch in a nearby Douglas fir tree and continued singing. The bird was about 20 feet from me so I got a fantastic view. I watched the bird for several minutes as it continued singing. It did not interact with any other birds while I observed it. I tried to move closer to the bird and it began to move away from me. I continued to pursue the bird, and it eventually stopped singing and flew out the the general area.
Call: wee-see-wee-see-wee-see (squeaky wheel)
Plumage: Breeding

Similar Species Discussion

Black-throated Gray Warbler (BTYW) is very similar in appearance, but this bird did not have a yellow spot in front of its eye. Also, the song of BTYW is very different, a rising “zee-zee-zee, etc” that is somewhat raspy. The “wee-see-wee-see-wee-see” song I heard was very crisp and high-pitched, not at all raspy. Habitat was not suitable for BTYW, although there are pinyon-dominated woodlands nearby. this observation occurred while surveying this area for the Breeding Bird Atlas, and I did not observe any BTYW in this Atlas block. Further, there have been no BTYW's reported in this general area, according to the Breeding Bird Atlas I and II databases.

Resources Used

Sibley, National Geographic and Peterson's Field Guides

Previous Experience

I spent four field seasons working on bird research projects in the eastern U.S., including one season in Rhode Island, one season in ne. Louisiana, and two seasons in Arkansas. Black-and-white Warblers were relatively common in these study areas and I became very familiar with their vocalizations, appearance, and habits. I have been working in pinyon-juniper habitats in NM, where Black-throated Gray Warblers occur, for the past 8 years and am also very familiar with their appearance, vocalizations, and habits. I have banded both Black-throated Gray Warblers and Black-and-white Warblers.

Additional Information

Comments: When I heard this bird singing, I recognized the song immediately because I have so much experience with this species. Without seeing the bird, I may have hesitated at reporting it, but i got such a great look there was no doubt in my mind what i had observed. I felt that early July was way too late for this to be a spring migrant, but too early for a fall migrant, so I'm not sure what to think about the status of this bird. I hope to return to the area to do more atlasing in 2012, and if I observe the bird again I will try and gather more information.
