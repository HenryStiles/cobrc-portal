Description of the Bird

I saw a bird fly into a low shrub and bog down. I slowly approached this shrub, and as I got within 10 feet or so, a different bird popped up and perched in a Cottonwood behind me.  I immediately recognized it as a Gray Flycatcher, a bird I am familiar with through birding in Central Oregon. It's plumage was consistently gray on the mantle, nape, and crown, lacking any green hues.  The chest and belly were whitish.  Two white wingbars were distinct and noticeable, and the wings were a dark gray.  The bird's bill was medium sized (for an empid), with a orangish upper mandible and a mostly orange lower mandible with a dark tip. It had a noticeable eyering. The primaries appeared rather small in relation to the tail. 


In terms of behavior, I watched the bird display its trademark tail pumping.  This was a smooth, subtle motion, in which the tail pumped downwards and then returned to its original position.  It did not flick its tail, or move it upwards, as some empids will do. I watched the bird pump its tail 2 or 3 times during my time observing it.  


After I took a few pictures of the bird, I returned my attention to the original skulking bird I was looking for and did not see anymore of the Gray Flycatcher.  Had I known this bird was significantly rare to the county, I probably would have spent a little more time observing it.  However, I felt very confident of my identification from the time I did spend studying and documenting it.

Similar Species Discussion

Dusky Flycatcher - has greenish hues in the mantle, a slightly smaller bill, and a less rounded eyering. 
Hammond's Flycatcher - has greenish hues in the mantle, a smaller bill, a longer primary projection, and a less rounded eyering.

A few other empids could be considered candidates.  However, the completely gray mantle, nape, and crown combined with the unique tail pumping behavior eliminate all similiar species.  

Resources Used

I am familiar with this species, and have observed it both on its breeding grounds and out of its normal range.  Therefore, I was able to quickly identify it in the field based on memory and prior experience.


I also noticed on eBird that there was a Gray Flycatcher reported from that location two weeks prior to my visitation.  I had that knowledge in the back of my head when I saw the bird, and I assumed that it was the same bird that had been reported earlier. 

Previous Experience

I have seen this species on several occasions in western and central Oregon over the past few years. I am also familiar with Dusky and Hammond's Flycatchers in the aforementioned area.
