Description of the Bird

Vocalizations were not detected, and not mentioned by any later observers of the bird either. Visual observations from binocular views and photograph inspections afterwards include; solid red head and breast except for small white spot between bill and eye. No black border on the breast, or white markings extending below or above the eyes. The rest of the underparts are fairly non-distinct for sapsuckers; yellowish belly peaking out from a mess of mottled white and grayish feathers covering the sides creating a sort of "zigzag" pattern, and whitish undertail marked with some sparse blackish streaking. The black back displayed two narrow columns of neat pale markings of a dull golden tinge. Wing pattern consisted of tertials tipped in white crescents and primaries relatively unmarked compared to closely related sapsucker taxa, but showing white bars that seemingly became more prevalent towards the outer feathers (towards tenth primary). When feathers from the bird's flanks weren't overlapping with its folded wings, the broad white bar lining the secondary coverts (representing the shoulder area to about halfway down the wing when folded) was visible. Tail feathers were jet black excepting the two large wedge-shaped very inner rectrices, which each had two small white spots on the inner side of the shaft.

Similar Species Discussion

A combination of factors pointed pointed to a Red-breasted Sapsucker, and of the ruber subspecies specifically. First, the lack of any white markings in the head other than the spot limited to just behind the bill and each eye are unique to a pure ruber subspecies over a daggetti subspecies or hybrid with Red-naped/Yellow-bellied ancestry. The other plumage features reminescent of the ruber subspecies of Red-breasted Sapsucker were located in the flight feathers; narrow white tips on the tertials and relatively narrow white bars in the primaries, markings that are typically more prevalent in related taxa. In the two central rectrices, while Yellow-bellied Sapsuckers usually show white bars on both sides of the shaft, and both Red-naped and daggetti Red-breasted Sapsuckers have broad white bands on the inner sides of the shafts, this Colorado sapsucker showed only two shrunken white marks that could be described as spots rather than bands, on the inner sides of the shafts. On the sapsucker's back, two very tidy and narrow columns of pale markings are wrong for the Red-naped/Yellow-bellied Sapsucker taxa, while the faintness and golden tinge to these markings are typical of a ruber Red-breasted Sapsucker rather than a daggetti Red-breasted Sapsucker or one with Red-naped ancestry. Finally, the breast of the Colorado sapsucker was a very extensive red, showing no hints of black (indicative of Red-naped ancestry) through that section.

Resources Used


Cornell Lab or Ornithology's description of ruber ssp Red-breasted Sapsucker;
"Entire head red except for black spot in front of eyes and white line from lore to nostril. Sexes alike. Nominate subspecies (Northern: S. r. ruber- shown above) on average larger; with deep, crimson red extending further down the breast. Auriculars red, with relatively faint malar. Large white wing-patch, back black with variable amounts of white or yellow spots, with yellowish wash on white extending down the belly."
And their description of daggetti ssp Red-breasted Sapsucker;
"S. r. daggetti similar to S. r. ruber, but smaller on average, with duller, more orange red on head and breast, auriculars often with black, and white malar more prominent."

However, ID largely made by previous experience of Red-naped and Red-breasted Sapsuckers and assistance of eBird's macaulay library to determine subspecies.

Previous Experience

My previous experience with Red-breasted Sapsuckers are from 3 other occasions in northern California, where the subspecies was likely of the daggetti subspecies. I have observed a multitude of Red-naped Sapsuckers in Colorado, and Yellow-bellied Sapsuckers both in Colorado and other states further to the east.
