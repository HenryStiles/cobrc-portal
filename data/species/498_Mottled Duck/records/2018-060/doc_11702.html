Description of the Bird

An obvious large, dark dabbling duck in southeast part of reservoir, the bird resembled MALL in overall shape and size.  During our entire observeration, the bird foraged in shallow water, often near an apparent brood of MALL-like ducks. Steve first noted it and got me looking at it. The bill's ground color was a bit hard to determine -- somewhere in the yellowy-orange range -- and had significant dusky markings. The crown was noticeably darker than the face and was separated from a short, dark eyeline by pale brown supercilium.  The rest of the face was notably buffy, but with some vague streaking on the cheek.  I could not discern the presence of a distinct black gape patch due to the distance, though one could certainly have been present undetected.  The nape was only marginally darker than the face, while the foreneck was buffy-gray, contrasting rather sharply with the darker chest.  The upperparts were very dark brown, noticeably much darker than the apparent brood of MALL youngsters (which are typically darker than adult female MALLs in this regard).  The tail was dark, just a bit paler than the upperparts, but obviously with no distinct white bits.  The underparts were of similar coloration to the upperparts, including the under-tail coverts, which were not at all pale.  The bird did not open its wings during our observation, but at one point I could see the speculum somewhat well and noted only a very thin white trailing edge, no other white being visible on the wing.

Similar Species Discussion

The primary considerations are confusion with Mexican Duck, American Black Duck, and various Mallard hybrids involving any of the large, dark dabbler species; the bird's plumage was too dark for any other dabbler species.  The bird's short eyeline (which did not approach the slightly dark nape at all closely), the strong buff aspect to the face, and the relatively pale hindneck rule out both Mexican Duck and American Black Duck (both have long eyelines nearly reaching their quite-dark hindnecks).  The dark upperparts and underparts plumage (including the dark under-tail coverts -- seen well when the bird tipped up facing away from us on multiple occasions) rule out Mallard and most Mallard hybrids, particularly f1 hybrids.  The virtual lack of white to the borders of the bird's dark blue speculum also rule out Mallard and most Mallard hybrids.  The dusky markings on the bill help rule out American Black Duck.  The vague streaking in the buff face helped point our determination of the bird's sex as female, as did the bill color.

Previous Experience

I have seen 100s of 1000s of Mallards, 10s of 1000s of American Black Ducks, 1000s of Mottled Ducks (many 100s each of Florida and "Texas" subspecies, the latter of which was probably the source of the bird in question, at least on geographical grounds), 15+ Mexican Ducks, and 100s of Mallard x Mottled Duck hybrids (all in FL).  I have written on the ID of Mottled Duck (in relation to Mallards and hybrids; https://ebird.org/content/ebird/wp-content/uploads/sites/55/eBird_Muddled_Ducks.pdf) and on  ID of Mexican Duck (https://cobirds.org/CFO/ColoradoBirds/InTheScope/14.pdf).  In addition to field experience, I have studied 1000s of photos (eBird and Flickr) of the various large, dark dabbling duck species while preparing the aforementioned articles and in reviewing photos for eBird.
