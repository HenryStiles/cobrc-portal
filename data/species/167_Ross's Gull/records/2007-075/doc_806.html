Description of the Bird

A small gull seen with Ring-billed Gulls for direct size comparison. The bird was obviously much smaller than a Ring-billed by many inches and far less bulky. The bill was black, fairly short. The eye was dark and looked large within the white, domed head. The only other feature visible on the head was a small dark post-occular spot. The upperparts were uniform pale silvery gray with no darker features within them. The folded wings were long, projecting well beyond the tail. No black was visible in the primaries, either the dorsal surface or the small bit of ventral surface visible on the folded wings. The underparts were essentially unmarked white, with perhaps a very faint pink tinge on the breast only seen as the bird faced the morning sun. The tail was clean white. The legs were blood red. In flight, the tail appeared kind of long, but most importantly, when the bird hovered or flared its tail I could see that it was strongly graduated, with the longest feathers in the middle, almost like a juvenile jaeger. I would characterize the shape of the tail to be somewhat like a spade symbol on a playing card. In flight, the entire underside of the wings were dark gray, with a prominent white trailing edge along the entire length of the wing. The underside contrasted sharply with the dorsal wing surface, which was essentially uniform silvery gray out to the wing tips with a white trailing edge. Again, no black was visible at the wing tip or along either wing surface.
Behaviors: The bird was initially on a sand spit with Ring-billed Gulls but soon began walking out to the water, where it briefly swam before taking flight. In flight, it flew up and down the length of the lake a few times, dropping to the water surface occasionally to pick something up before returning to its foraging flight. The bird returned to the spit where it rested for about 5 minutes before resuming its foraging flights again, before once more returning to the spit. Finally, a Northern Harrier spooked all of the gulls off the roost, and when they returned I could not re-locate the Ross's Gull.
Call: Not Heard
Plumage: Adult Basic

Similar Species Discussion

White and gray colors, structure, and habits rule out non-gulls. The small size and post-occular spot rules out most gull species. Among small gulls with post-occular spots, adult Black-legged Kittiwake ruled out by wrong leg & bill color and lack of black in the primaries. Juvenile Black-legged Kittiwake ruled out by lack of nuchal collar, carpal bar, etc. Adult Red-legged Kittiwake ruled out by lack of black in the primaries, bill color, etc. Juvenile Red-legged Kittiwake ruled out by lack of nuchal collar, lack of black in primaires, etc. Black-headed gull (all ages) ruled out by size, extensive black in primaries, bill color, and structure. Adult Bonaparte's Gull ruled out by lack of black in primaries and wrong pattern on dorsal and ventral wing surfaces. Juvenile Bonaparte's Gull ruled out by lack of black in primaries, lack of carpal bar, and wrong pattern on dorsal and ventral wing surfaces. Juvenile Little Gull ruled out by lack of dark cap, carpal bar, and lack of black in the primaries. Adult Little Gull ruled out by long wing projection, lack of dark cap, and while the bird was small, it wasn't as small as a little gull. Finally, the strongly graduated tail is unique among small gulls, ruling out all of these or other contenders. While jaegers show graduated tails, this bird was far too small and delicate for any jaeger, even presuming that a leucistic individual could show the pale gray and white coloration I saw.
