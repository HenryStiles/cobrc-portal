Description of the Bird

The bird was a decent-sized warbler, average in body size but with a long tail. It was mostly solid black with a sharply demarcated bright crimson belly. Wings had a large white solid covert patch and white linings to the otherwise black flight feathers. Tail when closed was black from above and mostly white from below, with a black line down the center and thin black tips to the outer rectrices. When spread, the tail showed a great deal of white from both above and below. Undertail coverts were dirty white. Two tiny white crescents below and behind each eye were visible at very close range. The bird's bill was small, thin and black. Its head appeared crested, as a result of crown feathers frequently being raised in excitement, and the crest appeared a little peaked, which was unexpected, and made it look like the bird might be missing a few feathers from the rear crown area.
Behaviors: The bird associated with a large flock of other warblers (mostly Yellow-rumpeds) and other passerines (Lazuli Bunting, Black-capped Chickadee, etcetera). It was more active than any other warbler, frequently fanning its tail, foraging along vertical tree trunks, and flying relatively long distances between branches in the same tree. It may have been sallying at times for flying insects.
Call: The bird frequently (about once a minute) gave its call, distinctive and unwarblerlike, a medium-pitched, downslurred, fairly musical, somewhat throaty or nasal (as opposed to whistled), slightly and finely burry note, perhaps best transcribed as "cheer." People have likened PARE's call to a call of Pine Siskin and this time, for the first time, I heard the resemblance.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Slate-throated Redstart doesn't have the big white wing patches. Nothing else comes close.

Previous Experience

I have seen Painted Redstarts perhaps a dozen times in Arizona, West Texas, Sonora, Sinaloa, and San Luis Potosi. In the latter location I recorded their calls at length and used them in the WFO sound quiz last fall, so I became quite familiar with the call at that time--though it seems the eastern subspecies' call, while similar, sounds a little less like Pine Siskin.
