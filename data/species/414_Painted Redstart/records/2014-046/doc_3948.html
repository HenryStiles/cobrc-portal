Description of the Bird

When I arrived near the area seen, I heard it's breading song. It sang this song about two times a minute for most of the observation.

Warbler sized bird seen feeding from the tops of the grove trees. It would fly and catch small flying insects the size of gnats and return to one of about four trees in this area. It almost always staid near or at the top of the trees. While landing and taking off from the trres, it would flash its tail feathers were several white feathers on each side were observed.

It is an all black warbler sized bird, black thin bill, small white crescent below the eye, bold wide white wing patch and an unmistakable bright red belly. The white wing patch and tail feathers were often on display while catching insects.

What a beautiful bird!

Similar Species Discussion

A male American Redstart is similar and more common in this area. It was eliminated due to the bright white wing patch vs none on the AMRE. The lower white cresecent eye ring vs all black on the AMRE. The three white tail feathers on each side vs the lack of white in the tail feathers vs black and burnt orange on the AMRE. The red belly, vent and undertail coverts vs the white on the vent and undertail coverts on the AMRE. The size and behavior are similar. However, the call is different as well as the plumage. Not an American Redstart.

Vermilion Flycatcher male has a bright red cap, throat and all underparts with a dark back and two very thin wing bars. It often feeds in the same way as the Painted Redstart. both share a thin black bill. The differences are still very clear. The bird seen had a black head vs the bright red on the VEFC. The large white wing patch vs the two very thin white wing stripes on the VEFC. The habitat fits the warbler better as the water source was 75 yards away.

American Robin also has bright red underparts. However, it too can be eliminated on song, yellow bill and lack of white on the wings.

Previous Experience

I have seen this bird on at least three occasions in SE Arizona and once in New Mexico. I studied the breeding call of both Redstarts and can tell them apart.
