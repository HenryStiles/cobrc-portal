Description of the Bird

Compact, dusky green hummingbird with short, straight, thickish bill. When relaxed, appeared a bit bull-headed. Critical marks include whitish supercilium that extended above and in front of eye, set off by a strong black lore. There was a total lack of buffy or rufous tones on any of the underparts, rump, or tail, and tail slightly longer than wingtips. Underparts were darkish, with greenish flanks transitioning to grayish belly (belly feathers mostly dark-centered with lighter fringes.) Gorget mostly light with a pattern of rows of darker spots which were largest in the center lowest part of the throat. I think a hint of magenta is visible on one of the gorget feathers in one of the images but with the lighting conditions I experienced I didn't see much of any iridescent color. Sherri Williamson (author of Peterson Field Guide to Hummingbirds of North America) comments that the limited gorget development at this late date can only be attributed to an immature female Anna's.
Behaviors: Bird was feeding on agastache sp. flowers and perching nearby when resting, presumably to guard its flower patch.
Call: single, clean high chip notes heard frequently, sounded a bit different than the various selasphorus sp. I'm more accustomed to in my yard.
Plumage: immature female per Sherri Williamson

Similar Species Discussion

Lack of buffy or rufous tones in underparts, rump, and tail rule out more expected Selasphorus species. Dark-centered belly feathers creating dusky appearance, bill proportions, and supercilium pattern rules out Archilochus species. Size rules out bigger gray-bellied hummingbirds like Blue-throated or Magnificent. Bill is to straight & thick for Costa's, and in Costa's the wings would appear longer, the throat would be more lightly marked, and the underparts would look paler.
