Description of the Bird

Female Anna's hummingbird came to the feeder in our front yard on October 5, 2017. She would perch in the lilac bush and return to feeder repeatedly. We watched her feed for an hour or so, and took pictures. She came back for three days in a row that we observed. She was present in the morning and in the afternoon. We did not observe any sounds. 
 We observed the red spot on her throat, the white spot above the eye,  the lack of destincive rufous female characteristics and realized she was not a rufous female, or black chinned female. We did not observe other hummingbirds at this time. 

Similar Species Discussion

We considered a rufous female hummingbird, but this bird did not have brown sides or greenish back. The black chinned humming bird is common in our area, but does not have the red spot on the throat. The wing length was also consistent with an Anna's Hummingbird.

Resources Used

We took close-up pictures with a Panasonic Lumix camera and compared the images to Sibleys Bird Book. We consulted with a fellow birder who checked with his bird books and agreed with our identification.

Previous Experience

We have watched birds, including 10 species of hummingbirds, for 20 years. We have seen Anna's Hummingbirds in central, southern and southeastern Arizona on many occasions during March and April. Our first sighting was in March of 2003 in Patagonia, AZ. We saw Anna's in March of 2005, 2006, 2008, and 2010 in various places in southern California, and Arizona.
