Habitat

The area is in the floodplain of the Purgatoire River on the inside of a bend of the river. The area is dominated by low brush (greasewood, sand sagebrush), grasses (alkali sacaton, switchgrass, sand dropseed), forbs (annual sunflower, annual buckwheat), and a few pieces of large downed wood (cottonwood logs). The site was about 8 to 10 feet above the level of the river within its banks (the river is entrenched in the area), althought the area that the bird was on was relatively flat.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Leica 10x42
Distance:  approximately 15 yards
Light:  thin overcast, but bright sunlight coming from behind and slightly to the left of the observer. There were no large trees or shrubs to cast shadows that would have obscured the bird. The angle of the light offered very good conditions to observe color.

Description of the Bird

Overall length was slightly shorter than nearby White-crowned Sparrows. The body was rounded, typical of small buntings - more junco-like than sparrow-like. The relative length of the tail was shorter than the sparrows. The head appeared slightly heavier than the sparrows relative to the body. The bill was sparrow-like - short, conical - and was a medium dark gray. The culmen was slightly convex, and the lower mandible was straight. For most of the observation the head was rounded, but there was a slight peak at the top of the head when the bird was alert. Head color was medium to dark blue, changing intensity with the angle of the light. The upper back was somewhat paler blue than the head, but the lower back had brownish-gray patches, and the rump was brownish-gray. The upper breast was about the same shade of blue as the upper back, but there was conspicuous mottling with the medium grayish-brown. Within the brownish-gray areas on the breast were noticeable darker gray, somewhat diffuse, streaks. The lower breast faded into pale gray in the undertail coverts. The tail was darker blue, more similar to the darker blue on the head. Flight feathers were about the same shade of blue. The wing coverts were brownish-gray, slightly darker that that of the breast on back. There were no obvious wingbars. The wings were short and rounded, as seen during short flights among the brush.
Behaviors: The bunting was foraging among low brush and weeds. There were short flights from the ground into brush and among the brush. It was alert on the top of a low branch for a few seconds before disappearing. At that point the slight crest appeared at the center of the head, and the neck was extended, giving the bird a slightly longer appearance. It was among a flock of mixed sparrows including White-crowned, American Tree, Song, and Dark-eyed Junco. Also in the immediate vicinity were Pine Siskins, American Goldfinch, and Mountain Bluebird.
Call: a short, sharp "spik!", heard twice during observation
Plumage: winter adult

Similar Species Discussion

Lazuli Bunting would have a pinker hue to the brownish color of the preast in this plumage. The blue coloration of Lazuli is generally paler than the shade that appers in this plumage of Indigo. Breast streaking is less conspicuous in Lazuli than in the bird seen. Wingbars are typically obvious in Lazuli in this plumage.
An Indigo/Lazuli hybrid would have noticeable wingbars (lacking in this bird). The undertail coverts in the hybrid would likely be clear white (pale gray in this bird). The brownish-gray areas would have a more distinctly pinker tone. Bluish feathers would generally be paler in the hybrid than in this bird.
Blue Grosbeak is a larger and heavier bird, with a rather cowbird-like shape. Fall/winter plumages of the grosbeak are distinctly brown, tending toward reddish. Wingbars are obvious.
Mountain Bluebird is a noticeably larger bird with a very different body shape (thinner, more streamlined) and long, pointed wings. The bill in bluebirds is thin and flat, as opposed to the conical shape of the bird seen. The blue and gray coloration in female bluebirds is also very different from the bird observed (both were in near proximity of each other at the time of the sighting). There is little if any darker gray streaking on the gray breast of female bluebirds.

Resources Used

Sibley (2000) and Peterson (1990) were used to confirm witner range of Indigo and Lazuli Buntings. They also confirmed the prominence of wingbars and amount of streaking on the breast of both species.

Previous Experience

Indigo Bunting - I've seen thousands (never more than 350 in a day on a spring bird count) in various plumages over 30 years of birding in IL, IN, MO, AR, KS, KY, and CO. I have seen several of these in plumages similar to the bird in question.
Since moving to CO eight years ago, I have seen several hundred Lazuli in the state. I've also seen many before moving here in CO, WY, NM, AZ, UT.
In 30+ years of birding, I've seen most plumages of Blue Grosbeak, usually a few dozen per year (especially over the last 20 years).
Hundreds of Mountain Bluebirds over the years.
I have seen a few Indigo/Lazuli hybrids in CO, admittedly none in the plumage of the bird observed).

Additional Information

Comments: Having seen numerous winter plumaged Indigo Buntings, I'm rather comfortable with this bird. The bird was typical of what I have seen before as winter male Indigos. (It's somewhat unfortunate that I do most of my birding on my own, so this is a single-observer report.) My hesitation is with not having seen any Indigo/Lazuli hybrids in this plumage. My impression is that they would have at least a hint of a pinkish hue, especially across the breast, that wingbars would be fairly obvious, and any darker streaking on the breast would faint.
Indigo Bunting is a documentable bird in the midwest (IL, IN, MO) in winter, so I'm guessing that it would be in this part of the country as well.
