Description of the Bird

For size of the bird, I'd estimate that the bird appeared to be slightly larger than a junco, but smaller than a Hermit Thrush. The shape appeared somewhat like a thrush. The bird had an orange crown-patch with wide, lateral crown-stripes of a dark brown (almost black) color. The face was a light, brown color. A wide, white eye-ring was evident. The white throat was set off by malar streaks. The white throat also contrasted with the heavily marked chest. At a distance those markings on the chest appeared as very wide, dark streaks. The belly was white. The upperparts and wings appeared brown in color. There were no wing bars. The bill was very light-colored from underneath.
Behaviors: The bird sang from fairly high perches on his territory. The bird was extremely difficult to find, particularly in this habitat. The habitat was ponderosa forest that provided significant canopy cover.
Call: The bird was initially identified by the song. The song was composed of 4 notes, a slight pause, followed by 4 more notes. All notes were on the same pitch. The 2 sets of 4 notes were all at an even rhythm. Each note rang out.
Plumage: Alternate

Similar Species Discussion

When I heard the bird singing on the initial visit, I immediately thought the singer could be an Ovenbird. My only hesitancy was that I recalled the mneumonic as "teacher, teacher, teacher". (Each note of the song I heard seemed to have just one syllable to me.) Kentucky Warbler was the only other candidate for me at the time. As soon as I returned home (an hour or two later), I listened to recordings of Ovenbird (and Kentucky Warbler) songs. I concluded the bird was in fact an Ovenbird from the song alone. Observations of the bird on 6/3/10 agreed with that ID. The distinct pattern of the crown and head provided a definite identification without the need to consider other possibilities.

Resources Used

I listened to "Bird Songs of the Rocky Mountain States and Provinces" by Robert Righter and Geoffrey A. Keller to ID the bird initially. When I did see the bird (2 days later), the field marks supported the earlier ID. No field guides were necessary.
