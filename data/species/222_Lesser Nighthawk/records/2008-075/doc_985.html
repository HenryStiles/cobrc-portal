Description of the Bird

Brian Gibbons called over to me that he had a Lesser Nighthawk flying with about 10 Common Nighthawks.

The Lesser Nighthawk was very buff-colored underneath. The wings were broad at the wrist and tapered to a rounded, not pointed wingtip. The throat was lighter than the rest of the head, but was buff-colored and not white. Approximately 1/3 of the way from the wingtip to the bend in the wing, on the dorsal side of the wing, there was an buffy oval (narrower towards the trailing edge of the wing) spot that was smallish, but always visible in flight, across the first 5-6 primaries, but not on the inner primaries or secondaries. The undersides of the wings had a rufous-buffy look that was noticeably different from the Common Nighthawks' wings. The underside of the tail had a buffy ground color with about five darker brown bands, but no white. The tail was noticeably notched, but the notch was only visible during some of the bird's aerial maneuvers. The inner primaries were spotted with buff, visible mostly when the bird came as close as 50 feet above my head.
Behaviors: The birds was seen only in flight, but it had some interesting flight characteristics, the most interesting, was a 2-second glide that resembled the V-glide (raised wings) made by Rock Pigeons. Otherwise, the bird was constantly flying, but with different flight than the herky-jerky flight of Common Nighthawks nearby. The Lesser made quick maneuvers of all nighthawks but the downstrokes of the wings were not as fast, or so it appeared. Bird was interested in feeding at the insect hatch taking place directly overhead and nearby.
Call: non heard
Plumage: apparently adult breeding

Similar Species Discussion

Common Nighthawk - luckily the bird was flying with numerous, about 10, Common Nighthawks. Even with "naked" eye, the Lesser was noticeably buffier, with shorter and more rounded wings, with smaller primary spot rounder and located more distally than in the Common Nighthawks. The CONIs were all males, their batlike flight noticeably different from the smoother flight of the LENI. The CONIs wings were grayer brown, very tapered to a point. Again, even without binoculars, the width of the wings at the wing bend, were narrow in the CONIs and more broad in the LENI. I purposely looked away from the birds and then tried to find the LENI without bins and each time it took only seconds to find it. That was how distinctive it was.

Antillean Nighthawk. Although I have seen them fly before, it has been a long time, and they are more close in appearance to CONI than to LENI.

LENI also shows a longer-looking tail than both ANNI and CONI which was noticeble. ANNI is even smaller than LENI.
