Description of the Bird

The bird was slightly smaller to a Common Nighthawk when in direct comparison. The wings were long, but with tips that appeared "blunter" than in the nearby Common Nighthawks, giving the birds the appearance of having comparitively shorter wings.

The undersides of the wings were boldly spotted with buffy spots, including on the inner parts of the primaries, and on the secondaries. The underwing coverts were also spotted with buff. One of the birds had a bold white bar across the central part of the primaries, that appeared to be slightly closer to the tips of the wings than on nearby Common Nighthawks, though this was hard to quantify. The other bird had light buffy bars in place of the white bars of the first bird. The primaries beyond the white bar appeared unspotted.

The underparts were light buffy with darker brown barring. The throat on one of the birds was clean white, on the other appeared buffy. The upperparts appeared to be grayish-buffy color on both birds. The underside of the tail (which was fairly long and slightly but noticeably forked) was dark, with a few paler bars, and with a bold white subterminal bar.
Behaviors: The birds were among a group of at least 15 nighthawks over the sewage lagoons, of which at least 2 were Common Nighthawks, and the others unidentified, though I suspect there may have been one or two more Lessers. The Lessers we saw were flying over the water, both low and high, with a more "fluttery" flight than the Commons, with less gliding and swooping.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

All species other than Nighthawks were ruled out by the distinctive shape with long wings, slim body, by the brownish plumage and white bars on the wings, and by the distinctive behavior of swooping low over water late in the evening.

Common Nighthawk was ruled out by the extensive buff spotting on the wings below, by the shorter and more rounded wings, by the wing bar appearing closer to the wingtip, and by the differing flight styles. Further, I believe these were not juvenal nighthawks, or at least one of the birds, due to the clean white throat, which, when combined with the buff spotting on the underwing would support Lesser rather than juvenal Common, which Sibley shows as the plumage most like Lesser Nighthawk.
