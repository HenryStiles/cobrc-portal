Description of the Bird

One individual, first reported by Coen Dexter and Brenda Wright and observed with them. 

The Lesser Nighthawk (LENI) was not found until the approximately 20 Common Nighthawks (CONI) had been in the air for quite a while. 

The LENI stood out to me immediately.  It was "softer" than all the CONIs, it was very obviously a nighthawk while very obviously not being a CONI.

The LENI appeared bit smaller than the CONIs.  It was warmer toned than the CONIs (browner/buffier). 

The LENI had a less contrasty wingpatch which was situated further out on its wing.  The dark of the LENI's wingtip was a small triangle; whereas the dark of the CONIs' wingtips were far more elongated triangles.  The LENI's wingtip also had a more rounded appearance whereas the CONIs' wingtips came to a very sharp point.

The LENI's wing patch is not the bright white of all the nearby CONIs.  Instead it was a more buffy-like color.

Neither Joey nor I noted any bands on the tail.

As lighting conditions faded, the bird was noticably harder to follow than the CONIs - it would disappear.  I attribute this to the lack of "running lights". The CONIs' larger bright-white wing-patch is quite visible even in the poorest of light conditions making them very easy to find and follow.  The LENI's smaller/duller wing-patch becomes almost invisible as the light fades and thus when the bird flew to the far side of the pond, she disappeared only to be relocated as she came back past us.

Flight pattern not at all like the CONI which has a very stiff and aggressive or "snappy" flight style.  The LENI flight style was far less stiff and softer, not at all aggressive.  The CONIs swooped deeply flying both low and high.  The LENI's flight, while undulating somewhat kept it within a few feet of the water/ground.  None of the birds ever set down on the ground.

Resources Used

Prior experience - I've seen a number of LENIs in Arizona and I've seen a huge number of CONIs over the years.

I utilized several bird books and online resources in my research as to the sex of the bird.  Coen Dexter felt that this bird was a male; however I respectfully disagree based upon the buffy wingpatch and the lack of tail banding.
