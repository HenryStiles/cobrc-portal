Description of the Bird

We watched the nighthawks over the Nucla Sewage Ponds on two different nights, 9/2/06 and 9/4/06. We had at least two LENIs the first night and at least two different LENIs the second night. Bird 1 (9/2/06): had VERY reduced white in the wing (the barest crescent), which allowed us to pick it out of the crowd fairly easily. It also had a distinctly different shape than the Common Nighthawks there: blunter wings that were noticeably shorter and seemingly slightly broader than CONI wings; also, the sharp angle at whcih CONI wings are swept back was much less noticeable on this bird. The bird was buffier than most clear-cut CONIs there, which had a distinctly grayins cast in the same light. Secondaries and at least some primaries of this bird looked spotted. Throat did not contrast with rest of head, so I assume this is a juvenile. In direct comparison with other nighthawks, the slightly smaller size and different structure seemed obvious. Also, the CONIs in the area varied in the extent of white on the wings and also somewhat in size, but no other bird had so little white, nor such a shape. Bird 2 (9/2/06): Andrew started looking for nighthawks with primary molt and found one that was missing P9 (the second-to-outermost primary) from the right wing only. The left wing was not molting. This bird was nearly identical to Bird 1, except, I cannot vouch for the complete lack of throat contrast, although it definitely did not have any white there. This bird was seen in worse light than Bird 1 (later in the evening), so I couldn't tell for sure about its overall color or spotting on the remiges. However, its size and structure and the extent of white on the wing were all the same as Bird 1. The following evening (9/3/06), Tony Leukering saw this bird and identified it as a "definite" Lesser Nighthawk. Bird 3 (9/4/06): This bird had a weird feather misplaced on one wing (I believe it was the left wing), so that it looked like the alula on that wing was sticking straight up. The white on this bird's wings was extremely reduced, like that of birds 1 and 2. The wingtip was EXTREMELY blunt compared to other nighthawks--my impression was that it was actually the 3rd primary that was longest, not the second. This bird also seemed buffy overall compared to other nighthawks (given direct comparison in decent light). I noticed the shallower flapping flight on this bird, with faster beats and glides in a shallower V than most other nighthawks out there. Its overall shape was nearly identical to birds 1 and 2. it had no throat contrast as far as I could tell. Bird 4: This bird had more white on the wings than birds 1-3, but not as much as on the adult male CONIs that were present. Its throat was contrastingly buffy, not white. It had no visible white in the tail. Thus it was probably either an adult female with whiter wings than illustrated in Sibley, or perhaps a young male. It shape and flight style were like those of birds 1-3. Although the light was not terrific when we saw this bird, it was fairly close for some time and my looks strongly suggested that the buffy underwing spotting extended up the wing to just short of the white patch.
Behaviors: All these birds were flying over the sewage ponds for the hour before dusk, in the company of 100 or more other nighthawks. See above for details of flight style.
Call: The birds were silent.
Plumage: juvenile and others

Similar Species Discussion

Common Nighthawk is the confusion species. In extended direct comparative looks, the LENIs never failed to look substantially different. In extended views I found the placement of the white on the wing to be a very difficult mark to see. Aspects of flight style were elusive, but on the second night I began to pick up on some of them. Wingtip shape, extent of white and overall wing length were by far the most obvious marks, with overall size, overall color and patterning on the remiges useful as supporting characteristics.
