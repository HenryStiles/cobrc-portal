Description of the Bird

the birds were nighthawks slighty smaller than abundant nearby Common Nighthawks. The overall shape of the bird appeared a little more compact, mostly due to wings that were shorter and appeared broader. The birds flew with a more fluttery flight, without the occasional very deep wingbeats of a Common Nighthawk, and with less gliding overall. When they did glide they held their wings at much more of a horizontal, and not in the deep "V" of a Common Nighthawk.

The outermost primary on the wings (P10) was slightly but noticeably shorter than P9 on all the birds we identified as Lessers, giving the wings a more blunt tipped and rounded look. The white bars on the wings looked different in shape than those on Commons, with a tapering look towards the trailing edge, and a slight curve, making the marks look more comma-like. It also appeared to be placed closer to the tip of the wings, though this was hard to be sure about. The underwings on all the birds we saw were extensively spotted with buff inwards from the white wing mark, including on the inner part of the primaries and the secondaries.

The underparts of the birds were buffy brown, as was the head. The back had more of a grayish tone to it, though not as much as most of the Commons.

All of the birds identified as Lessers had all of the above characteristics. While the total number of Lessers in the group was unknown, we were able to discern at least 4 different individuals with the following differing charactersitics:

Bird 1, the first bird found on 9/2, was a juvenile bird, with much reduced white in the wings, but otherwise with all the marks listed above. Seen for about 3 minutes around 7:30 PM

Bird 2, the second bird found on 9/2, was also a juvenile bird, but with a missing P9 from the right wing. This bird also had reduced white in the wings, though slightly more than bird 1. This bird was also seen by Tony Leukering on 9/3. Seen for about 7 minutes around 7:45 PM

Bird 3, the first bird on 9/4, was another juvenile, with much reduced white in the wing. Unlike bird 1 from 9/2, it had a misplaced covert or alula feather on the left wing that was fairly easy to see throughout the observation. This is the bird in the submitted photos. Seen for about 8 minutes around 7:25 PM

Bird 4, the second bird on 9/4, had more white than any other definite Lesser seen, though still not as much as adult male Commons. The throat was contrasting buffy-colored, indicating a probably adult female. The white in the wing was distinctly curved and comma shaped. Seen for about 15 minutes around 7:40 PM

In addition to these birds, I believe there were at least a couple of more not definitively pegged as additional birds. Towards the end of the observation time on 9/4 we found a few Lessers that were almost certainly additional birds, but could not confirm this.
Behaviors: the birds were with many Common Nighthawks feeding over two small sewage ponds at dusk.
Call: none heard
Plumage: three juveniles and one adult female

Similar Species Discussion

Common Nighthawk was ruled out by a quite of characteristics, but primarily by features in wing structure, with a distinctly shorter P10 than P9, blunter tipped, shorter, and wider wings, and by flight characteristics, with less gliding flight with shallower wingbeats, habit of not gliding with wings in a deep "V", and overall more fluttery flight. Other marks used include reduced white in the wings, buff spotting on the remiges, placement of the white mark on the wing closer to the tip of the wing, overall buffier color, and slightly smaller size.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted in the field, and information gleaned from Tony Leukering was used to help ID the bird. Later, the BNA account for LENI was consulted to get a better handle on some of the field marks.
