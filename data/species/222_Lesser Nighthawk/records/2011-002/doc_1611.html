Description of the Bird

A least one male and one female were present after 30 May 2010. The male was present until 16 August 2010. I will focus on this last sighting. The male nighthawk was in flight so I gained a close inspection of the wings. The wings were rounded and I could see each individual feather as the nighthawk passed directly overhead. The second feather, from the leading edge, was longer than the first feather. The wing bar was white and somewhat closer to the tip of the wing than in Common Nighthawks. The wing bar was tapered at the back and widest at the front. Overall the colors were light brown, buff and tawny. The tail appeared long with a weak sub-terminal white band. The tail had some barring, dark brown vs. buff. The throat did have a white patch but could be seen only at a close distance directly over-head. From above the nighthawk was very light buff and gray but this field mark was seen only at a distance.
Behaviors: The nighthawk came in to feed at sundown with Common Nighthawks. It also made a few passes overhead hawking insects as well as to sip drinks. Often the nighthawk would fly close to vegetation to collect insects.
Call: none heard

Similar Species Discussion

Common and Lesser Nighthawks are difficult to separate. The difficulty is greatest in the late summer when juvenile Common Nighthawks are flying and feeding. In many ways they look like male Lesser Nighthawks. I based my ID on shape and placement of wing bar, overall color both from below and above, wing shape, tail length and feeding techniques. I did have juvenile Common Nighthawks present so that made the ID significantly easier. I have just written a paper of Lesser Nighthawk for the CFO Journal.

Resources Used

Since 2006 many birders have visited the Lesser Nighthawk site near Nucla. Several photos have been taken of these birds. I have read the BNA account, nearly every available field guide and had at length discussions with many knowledgeable birders on the subject of separation of the two species.
