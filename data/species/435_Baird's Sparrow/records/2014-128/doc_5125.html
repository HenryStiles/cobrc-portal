Description of the Bird

All three birds looked roughly the same. Two were together and the third was a mile down the road, roughly. There was no reason to believe it was one of the original two given the movement of other sparrows at location. I drove down to Hemmingway Road, about 4 miles from 3rd bird, drove back, and then found an adult about 3/4 mile from where I'd found the third bird. Could have been the same, could have been a fourth. It was this latter bird that was photographed. 

These were short-tailed, large-headed and large-billed sparrows roughly the size of a Savannah (body size). All birds looked moderately to rather worn. The auriculars, supercilium and lores wore worn orange buff. The lower and rear borders to the auriculars were marked by a jagged blackish line, the uppermost end (bordering upper posterior auricular) being thicker, forming a "spot." The crown was dark with a vague pale central crown stripe. These birds also had a poorly defined, broad but weak, eyering that was pale. The throat was dull whitish, the chest washed in pale buff. The chest also was streaked to varying degrees with worn blackish streaks, narrower towards middle, broader towards sides. On one bird, some streaking went down sides, on other two, it did not. Remainder of underparts dingy white. Back was streaked rust and black for most part. Bill had "typical Ammodramus" appearance, being rather thick and head looked flat, as those of Ammos often do. No wingbars noted.

Similar Species Discussion

The shape and size were typical for an Ammodramus, bill thicker than one would see in a Savannah and color of face wrong. Grasshopper Sparrow would not have bordered auriculars and would be more buff yellow than buff orange. Also, Grasshopper Sparrows typically appear a bit smaller and more delicate to me.
