Description of the Bird

The bird was a small sparrow, noticeably smaller than the many Vesper Sparrows in the area. It was about the size of the one Grasshopper Sparrow, 3 Savannah Sparrows and many Spizella sparrows (Chipping, Brewer's and Clay-colored) we saw that day. It had the flat head and relatively large bill of an Ammodramus sparrow.  The flight was weak and low, with a zig-zag pattern also shown by Grasshopper Sparrows. To me that flight pattern makes the bird appear to me underconfident about staying airborne.

We were fortunate that John Drummond was with us, for several reasons, including the fact that he had seen juvy Baird's in this area on previous trips during the past week, not to mention his experience and knowledge of other similar species.
Once we found the bird, it conveniently landed right on the gravel road or perched on barbed wire, middle strand usually, close to or just opposite the vehicle and showed both its ventral and dorsal aspects, as well as side views. At one point it landed in front of the car and moved out of view, from inside the car, as the hood blocked the view!

The bird was streaky and the ground color was buff. The unique thing I noticed was that the back and side view of the neck seemed to be lit with tiny highlights of white. This could be seen from far away and helped me get on the bird. This appearance on closer inspection was due to pale feather edgings on the scaly back and wing coverts and also pale areas between streaks on the neck and face. This scaly pattern edged with white or very pale buff feathers seems to be supportive of Baird's Sparrow.

The crown was notable for fine alternating buff and darker brown streaking, rather like an adult Brewer's Sparrow crown I thought. On one bird that I took to be an adult BAIS, the midline crown stripe was buff, surrounded by thick dark brown stripes, different from the white midline stripe of a Grasshopper Sparrow. But I did not see enough of the rest of this bird to be sure it was an adult BAIS.
One of the best ID points on the juvy BAIS was the buffy malar area between the two fine dark lines which were the mustache stripe and the lateral throat stripe. This was striking when the bird was seen on the telephone wire thru the scope, for over a minute, which gave crippling views, just as if one had a field guide open to that page. I could see rufous on the folded wing in two areas - the base of the primaries and patchy rufous on the secondaries. That feature helps rule out juvy Grasshopper Sparrow, but another similar species, Savannah Sparrows do share the rufous wing patches. (More below on comparing similar species).
The face was buffy. The iris black.
There was a small pale eye-ring.
Much is made of BAIS having 2 dark spots in the post-auricular area. Those showed up well in a flight photo I took. (Note that all of my photos are quite lousy. They are compatible with BAIS. Not sure why they are so poor, except that many were taken through the window glass).
I will attach a few despite marked embarrassment. John Drummond's photos on the other hand were crisp and leave no doubt about the ID in my opinion, as they show
every feather, every mark.
The black flight feathers were narrowly edged with warm buff or rufous.

The underparts were white toward the belly and vent, buffy on the chest, with dark streaks on breast and flanks. 

On one flight of the BAIS the outer tail feathers looked pale compared to the rest of the tail, when the light caught it just so. Usual flight views did not show this, in contrast to white lateral tails feathers of every Vesper Sparrow we saw.

The juvy BAIS was not vocal, nor did we sit it foraging as such. It would fly in from the field or the weedy ditch, land on the road or roadside barbed wire fence or utility wire and sit there. 

Similar Species Discussion


LeConte's Sparrow ruled out by largish bill; LeConte's bill tiny. ALso LeConte's lacks dark mustache and lateral throat stripes.

Grasshopper Sparrow: Very similar in many respects. This and Savannah are the big "must rule outs." At any age GRSP lacks the buffy malar area, set off by the lateral throat stripe and mustache stripe of the BAIS we saw. Lacks white edged scaly pattern on back that stood out so well on the bird we saw over and over. Juvy lacks thin black lines around auriculars that this putative BAIS showed.

Savannah Sparrow: Grayish adults not hard to rule out, with cold gray tones, not the buff we saw on this BAIS. Reddish Savannah's are very similar, BUT, the bill is noticeably smaller. Savannah's lack the white edgings of back and wing covert feathers-lacks the scaly look. And on Savannah's streaking usually extends farther down flanks and down from chest toward belly than this bird (photos). BAIS ventral streaking is more like a necklace. Our bird lacked yellow lores as some Savannah's have.

Henslow's Sparrow: No greenish or yellow-green tones to suggest adult and some juvy Henslow's. Henslow's similar in many respects. Adult has less white scaling on back. But vent is brownish, not white, as in BAIS. Streaks on breast of Henslow's are on cold grayish ground color, not buff or white like BAIS and are finer, not prominent, like BAIS.

LeConte's Sparrow: Has no lateral throat or mustache stripe. Spiky tail feathers.
Nelson's Sparrow: Spiky tail. Juvy is orangish. Breast streaks are finer, not bold like our bird. Face pattern different.
Vesper Sparrow: Only superficially similar in the streakiness. We saw scores of VESP for comparison. Larger bird, Prominent white lateral tail feathers, lacked white scaling on back, cold gray ground color on head.

Resources Used

I had studied several field guides (Sibley, Dunn et al, and Rising's "Sparrow Book.." as well as study of online images of BAIS and similare species.
I wanted to be sure I knew what to look for on each species. I consulted 4 birders who had been to the site and seen the BAIS to get clued in.
Whie seeing and photograping the BAIS, Drummond and Gillilan  and I constantly discussed what marks we were seeing and how similar species
differed. After calming down I again referred to those texts and on line photos and studied my lousy photos and the excellent ones taken by Drummond
of the bird we saw together.

Previous Experience

I have only seen one BAIS in my life, and that was an adult singing from a weed stalk in April in Texas, with Jon Dunn.

I have extensive experience from general birding and atlasing with Ghoppers and Savannahs of different ages. Birding since
age 14, active during the mid 70's in CO and again from 1989 til now in CO, other parts of the USA.
I studied Henslow's Sparrows for two summers in central Missouri in the 60's on the TUcker Prairie, a tall grass prairie remnant near Columbia and saw them in the hand during banding ops in Florida about 3 years ago. Experience with multiple sightings of LeConte's Sparrows in CO, in winter and with a couple of Nelson's Sparrows 
in CO, including good photos of the one at Chico Basin Ranch a few years ago.

The striking aspect of this whole BAIS experience in El Paso County this year was the difference in the behavior they showed. We have read and heard that they are very hard to see, skulking in the grass, fly away, only perched up singing on territory. But the Drennan Road birds were often on fences or in middle of road, and as I mentioned on a high utility wire for a minute, viewed by scope! Why not more skulky?
