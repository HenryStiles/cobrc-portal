Description of the Bird

I immediately picked this warbler out of a mostly Yellow-rumped Warbler flock, as it was rather drab colored with two very distinct white wingbars.  The individual appeared heavy bodied with a long tail and stout bill for a warbler.  The upper parts were fairly uniform grayish/olive/brown of a medium tone.  The throat and breast were a light yellow and the belly through the vent was white.  The upper breast had very indistinct darker smudges on the sides.  The wingbars were a clean white and distinct.  The eye was framed by indistinct whitish eye arcs and there was a faint dark eye line through the eye.  The yellow of the breast extended slightly around the sides of the neck.  The under tail feathers were white and extended almost to the end of the rectrices with a dusky gray edging on the outside near the terminal of the tail.  The legs were dusky, but not black.  The bill was dusky colored and lighter at the base.

The entire flock was feeding in a small mixed juniper/ pinyon pine grove and this bird was in a pinyon pine.  It was feeding deliberately about five to eight feet above the ground.  Since I was only about twenty-five feet away I had very good looks at this relatively slow moving bird.  It was much slower moving than the yellow-rumps and looked around carefully before moving on.  It did not sing or call.  I did not notice any tail pumping.

Similar Species Discussion

The ID for this bird would have been more difficult in the fall or if it was a first year female Pine Warbler.  Even though I had never seen a Pine Warbler in southwest Colorado before, I was familiar enough with the species from thirty years of birding in Cincinnati and the east to be relatively confident in my identification.  To me there are few similar species in the spring, but a fall sighting would have been more difficult.  There are several warbler species that are superficially similar, but all were eliminated by the relatively bulky size, generally drab coloration, longish tail with white tail spots, distinct white wingbars, somewhat heavy bill, deliberate movements, and whitish eye arcs which in combination eliminate all other species.  If the bird had been a male Pine Warbler than a Yellow-throated Vireo would have been a similar appearing bird, but this bird was not brightly colored and did not have yellow spectacles

Resources Used

I used my previous field experience to identify this bird.  As I returned to my car I considered other possibilities and couldn't think of any.  At the car I reviewed Sibley.  Later at home I consulted 'Warbers' by Dunn/Garrett and concluded that my field ID was correct as an adult female Pine Warbler.

Previous Experience

I have seen this species many times in thirty years of birding in Cincinnati and the east including the southeast where it is most common.
