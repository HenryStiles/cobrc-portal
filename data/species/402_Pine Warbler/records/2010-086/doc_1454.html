Description of the Bird

A very drab small passerine-shaped bird about the size of a Chipping Sparrow or Yellow-rumped Warbler. The bill was black, short (about 1 cm long), narrow and pointed like that of a warbler. Upperparts and underparts were grayish, but upperparts were uniformly darker gray with an olive wash (including rump), lacking any type of streaking, and underparts were paler with faint darker gray smudges on the breast, and whiter on the lower belly and vent. Some feathers at the side of the breast (or possibly on the edge of the wing near the alula) were pale lemon-yellow.
The wing had two thin but crisp white wingbars. The primaries were blackish. The tail was longish for a warbler, extending >1 inch beyond the undertail coverts. The underside of the outer rectrices (r6) was extensively white on the inner web from the base to the terminus, and gray on the outer web. The eye and legs were black. The face had a dull whitish eyebrow, and dull whitish eye arcs. The auricular was pale gray with pale olive wash.
Behaviors: Flitting about in midstory vegetation, about 10-20' above a stream. Frequently calling; occasionally singing.
Call: call a loud sharp note similar to Yellow Warbler; song a weak attempt at a trill, but notes more spaced out and choppy (i.e. slower frequency) and less monotonous (i.e. unevenly pitched) than expected for adult Pine Warbler.
Plumage: juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

Blackpoll in non-breeding plumage has pink legs and streaked back and less white in tail. Bay-breasted Warbler has shorter tail with less white. Cape May Warbler has pale yellowish rump and finer streaking on breast. Yellow-rumped Warbler has distinct yellow rump. Old-world warblers (family Sylvidae) lack wing-bars and white in tail.
