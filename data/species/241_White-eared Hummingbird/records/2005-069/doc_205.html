Description of the Bird

This hummingbird was of medium size and slightly smaller than a Broadtailed. The bill was straight and medium to short for a hummer. I could not see any red at the base of the bill, although it was always in the shade when I observed it. The head was well rounded and the forehead was quite steep above the bill. The most prominent mark was the wide white supercilium that started just above the eye and widened as it extended back and down the side of the neck. Also, there was a strong blackish earpatch that started from the eye and extended down the neck in front of the white supercilium. The area immediately in front of this dark ear-patch was whitish which then transitioned to the throat that was heavily spotted with green dots. The back and scapulars were bright green. Some of these feathers were faintly tipped with rufous or cimmamon. The flight feathers were quite dark and almost black. The chest and upper belly were dingy gray with some green spotting. The undertail coverts were white. The tail was dark, nearly black, and extended slightly past the ends of the folded wings. When folded the tail showed two rounded lobes, which appeared flat when spread. I did not see any white edging on any tail feathers, nor did I get a good look at the upper side of the tail. The legs appeared black.
Behaviors: Made short and infrequent visits to a sugarwater filled hummingbird feeder located at Susan Allertons house.
Call: Not heard
Plumage: Probably immature female

Similar Species Discussion

While there are several female hummers that are superficially similar, the size, bill, head shape, prominent white supercilium, and earpatch distinguished this bird from other NA hummers.
