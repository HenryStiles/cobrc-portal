Description of the Bird

On the evening of July 29, Doug Faulkner and I stopped at the house of Susan Allerton in Durango, La Plata County, to view the female White-eared Hummingbird coming to her feeder. Many other birders had previously seen this bird and documentation for this bird has already been submitted to the CBRC. During conversations with Susan prior to arriving at her house, she informed us that a SECOND White-eared Hummingbird was also present in La Plata county, and that it had been present since July 20. Susan informed us that the second hummingbird host was probably not too open on letting birders into his house, but she was able to get the two of us permission to attempt to see the bird and document its presence for the state archives.

Early the following morning, July 30, Susan Allerton, her husband Peter, Doug, and I met Richard Brown, the homeowner, at his condominium at Durango Mountain Resort (previously Purgatoire Ski Resort) in extreme northern La Plata county at approximately 8:00 AM. Susan had commented that she had seen the bird present at her feeder that morning and this second bird was approximately 22 miles north of the bird present at her home. The Durango Mountain Resort is less than a mile south of San Juan county. Richard invited us into his home and we set up scopes and camera equipment facing his two hummingbird feeders that faced east toward the morning sun. There were numerous Broad-tailed and Rufous Hummingbirds coming to the feeder, as well as a few Calliope Hummingbirds.

Within roughly 15 minutes, the immature female White-eared Hummingbird came to the southernmost feeder. She was about the same size as nearby Broad-tailed Hummingbirds, but quite obvious with her broad, white postocular stripe. The bill was straight and moderately long, being mostly black except for a lightening toward the basal portion of the maxilla. The white postocular stripe, which extended from just anterior and above the eye rearward to the base of the neck, was bordered dorsally by a greenish brown crown and black face mask, which extended widest from the lores to the lower face. The throat was grayish mostly, though was extensively spotted with greenish gray flecks. Overall head shape was also consistent with White-eared Hummingbird, that typified by a very steep forehead and crown that sloped rearward. Upperpart featheration was mainly green, thought the uppertail coverts seemed to have a hint of cinnamon at times. The wing coverts were also green, but seemingly a shade darker than the mantle. The flight feathers (primaries and secondaries) were a dark gray, roughly the same length as the tail projection. The primaries were broad tipped with a healthy bend. The breast flanks, and undertail coverts basally a grayish white, though much of this area was also heavily spotted with green feathers. The green from beneath was more pronounced along the flanks. The tail was generally dark gray to almost black and was pretty square tipped.

The White-eared Hummingbird would only appear at the feeders about every 20 minutes and would stay for usually less than a minute or so. The frenetic activity of the Broad-tailed and Rufous Hummingbirds seemed to make her keep her distance. At 9:00, after obtaining photographs of this second White-eared Hummingbird, we departed. We were later informed that this second bird disappeared on August 7, the same day Susan Allerton reported her bird missing.
Behaviors: Foraged at hummingbird feeder.

Similar Species Discussion

The broad postocular stripe and black mask eliminate virtually all other North American hummingbirds. Female and immature Broad-billed Hummingbirds will have a narrower postocular stripe and black mask, a forked tail, a mostly red maxilla, and is a bit larger. Female Blue-throated Hummingbirds are much larger, have a less defined postocular stripe, have dark gray undersides, and have a large tail tipped with white. Xantus Hummingbirds are brighter green and either have a bright green or orange throat depending upon sex.
