Description of the Bird

This was a shorebird about the same size or slightly smaller than a Lesser Yellowlegs and considerably smaller than Greater Yellowlegs (see photo) which it feed amongst.

When first seen (by me) the bird was far away but the strong buffy color of the breast stood out from a great distance. When Mark Peterson and I accidentally flushed the bird, I was suprised at how long the wings were. I knew to look for the U-shaped white uppertail coverts which I saw as it passed by in flight. Even at the far distance, > 100 meters, a droop to the bill tip was visible.

Luckily I got to see it again much closer and here is what I noticed while it was foraging and preening.
Head was buff colored with dark bill. The bill was thick based and there was a noticeable droop to its tip. The bill appeared to be slightly longer than the head as measure from back of the head to the base of the bill. At times the light feathering just behind the base of the bill was visible. The crown had noticable dark streaking on a buffy background. The face was buffy brown. The back was noticably scaly in appearance, like that seen in Buff-breasted Sandpiper. At times, I could see the birds scapulars stick up over the humped back. I have a photo of this but will only send if requested.

The legs were mostly not in view because of either water or vegetation but in the far view they appeared to be yellowish. I didn't get a chance to notice their length unfortunately.

The breast was unmarked and dark buffy. The hindneck was buffy brown with darker streaks.
Behaviors: Fed mostly in dense Kochea (sp) and unknown sedge species, usually in areas slightly less deep than where both Greater and Lesser Yellowlegs fed. I never actually saw the bird probe into the water although it spent lots of its time searching the sedges.

No unusual behaviors noted.
Call: none given
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

The only bird that looks like this is the similar but smaller Buff-breasted Sandpiper, which also has yellow legs. The BBSA has a mucher thinner shorter bill. The posture of teh BBSA is more upright usually, and the BBSA has noticable spots on the sides of its neck which this bird did not have. The bill of the bird in question was very stout throughout with a droopy tip, not the delicate little bill of BBSA. This bird was the size of a Lesser Yellowlegs, not smaller as a BBSA would be. This bird was always in wet areas with its legs mostly submerged. BBSA will occasionally feed in water but mostly on drier areas.
