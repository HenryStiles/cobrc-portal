Description of the Bird

As I approached the edge of the lake, the pale, golden color of the shorebird's head first drew my attention.  Then I started to notice other unusual features.  Overall, it was a shorebird of medium size, about the size of the Long-billed Dowitchers in the same area and larger than the Lesser Yellowlegs.  Relative to the size of the body, the head seemed unusually small, and the neck unusually thin.  The bill was also rather short for such a large shorebird.  Then, I noticed the bright orange legs.  Realizing there was some possibility the bird was a Ruff, I started obtaining photos when the view wasn't obstructed.  I lost view of the bird when a Northern Harrier flushed the shorebirds and shuffled their locations.

Photos show the features just described, along with plumage characteristics.  After reading about Ruff in field guides, I understand plumage is quite variable for this species.  A detail perhaps worth mentioning, not shown well in the photos, is dark blotches on the breast and sides of the neck.

The bird was an active feeder, walking around in multiple directions.  It bent way over when probing.

I'm not aware of any calls the bird made.  It would have been hard to hear the call of an individual bird because several hundred shorebirds, many of them vocalizing, were in the area, and two nearby coyotes were barking and howling.

Similar Species Discussion

In the field guides I consulted, the bright orange legs would seem to be unique to Ruff.  Other shorebirds (Spotted and Common Redshank, Ruddy Turnstone, Piping Plover) with somewhat similar leg color are easily eliminated based on size, body structure, and bill characteristics.
