Description of the Bird

The bird was a medium-sized shorebird, probably about the size of a Killdeer (no other shorebirds were present for direct comparison when I was there), with legs about as long as a yellowlegs', an incredibly fat-looking body, medium-short wing/tail projection, small head with a steep forehead and a flat crown, forming a sharp angle on the forecrown, and a dark bill that was wide at the base but quickly grew narrow. The bill was perhaps 10-20% longer than the width of the head and slightly downcurved; the profile of the upper mandible was nearly straight, but the profile of the lower mandible was markedly concave. The neck mostly was pulled in tight to the breast, making the bird look almost neckless, but when alarmed the bird would stretch to its full height, and then it was obvious that the neck was actually quite long. The legs were bright (some said glowing) orange. The bird was strikingly patterned: all back, scapular, and covert feathers were broad but pointed, with dark blackish centers and broad buffy-to-light-brown edgings. The tertials were long and banded about 5 times with these blackish and buffy colors. The tertials were characteristically "floppy" and would flip up in the wind. Neither the tail nor the primaries appeared to project beyond the end of the tertials. The bird's head was buffy-brown with a slightly darker patch about the eye and a whitish patch on the lower forehead just above the bill. The bird's breast was buffy-brown with sparse and incomplete thin horizontal blackish banding, which was not symmetrical across the breast. Indistinct blackish spotting extended on a brownish ground color partway down the sides of the breast to the flanks. The belly and the undertail coverts were clean white.
Behaviors: The bird was nervous for most of the time that I observed it, crouching down in the mud, posturing like it was about to fly, eyeing humans and potential aerial predators with what appeared to be great suspicion. Towards the end of my observation it began to walk up and down the mudflats and forage, picking at the mud at the water's edge.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Ruff is distinctive. No other shorebird has the combination of bright orange legs, fairly short downcurved bill, scaly upperparts, and super-fat body. Buff-breasted Sandpiper is perhaps the most similar bird to a breeding Reeve, but it is much shorter- and yellower-legged, much longer-winged, much buffier, especially below, etc.
