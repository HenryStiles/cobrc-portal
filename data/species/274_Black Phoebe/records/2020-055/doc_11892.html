Description of the Bird

Typical Black Phoebe. Petite all-black flycatcher except for a white belly. Pointed crest. Initially detected by hearing its repetitious, weak primary chirp song given nearby. It moved from a small to a much larger tree shortly after I found it, eventually perching on a very high twig at "11 o'clock' near the top of a 9-10-meter-tall plains cottonwood, along the bank of the rapidly flowing Arkansas River. It stayed there for many minutes. I even stopped back at my car to eat some breakfast, and went back to the tree and the bird was still in the same spot, singing. I thought about photographing or recording it, and would have if I'd known at the time it was that rare here.

Similar Species Discussion

Visually, Eastern Kingbird bears some similarity, but it is larger, more gregarious, has all white underneath and a notable white tip to the tail. Eastern Phoebe is similarly sized but isn't all black but rather just on the cap, with grayish green. Black Phoebe song is unlike either the kingbird or Eastern Phoebe, consisting of only a repetitious "bitt-ew" like chirp, over and over without the burry notes of the Eastern.

Resources Used

None really - I was already very familiar with this species, I recognized it immediately. Although in the past, I've used Sibley as a learning reference, and studied numerous sound recordings I have from Stokes and the Macaulay Library.

Previous Experience

I've encountered them many times over the years in California, several occasions in Colorado in the Boulder in the Front Range and West Slope, and also in South America in Colombia and Ecuador.
