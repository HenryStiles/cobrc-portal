Habitat

Birds were in the upper portions of dense tamarisk understory and in the lower portions of the thick cottonwood overstory along a permanent stream with extensive deciduous riparian habitat. Pinyon-juniper woodlands in the immediate vicinity, away from the creek.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Eagle Optics 8x42 binoculars
Distance:  10 feet at closest approach
Light:  It was overcast and occasionally drizzling rain during our observations, so lighting was rather dim but even.

Description of the Bird

Both birds were more or less the same; Andrew said he could see more red on the head of the male, but I never saw this. They were warblers, with short thin pointed black bills, slender bodies and relatively short tails. Plumage was pearly-gray all over the body, without any distinctive markings; no wingbars. Birds had faint whitish eye-rings. They also had dull rusty-red rumps, but these were difficult to see, as the birds mostly kept their wings folded over them.
Behaviors: The birds were agitated, as though we might be near a nest, but we never could find a nest. They were moving very actively throughout the upper understory and lower canopy of the riparian grove, from 7-18 feet off the ground, occasionally foraging in the branches.
Call: Birds were calling frequently in apparent agitation: a high, thin, very sharp, unmusical chip note.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Blue-gray Gnatcatcher is more bluish above, with a much longer tail and a very different call. Kinglets are a different shape, are more greenish, and have wingbars. No other bird in North America besides Lucy's is all grayish with a reddish rump.
