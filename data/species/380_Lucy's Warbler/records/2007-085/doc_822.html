Description of the Bird

the bird was a small warbler, noticeably smaller than Yellow Warblers also present nearby. The overall shape appeared fairly slim for a warbler, accentuated by a reasonably long tail and thin, pointed bill. The bill and legs were black.

The entire bird, except for the rump, was pale gray. The color gray was darker on the back, wings, and head. The loral area was contrasingly paler than the head, and the underparts were also contrastingly paler than the upperparts. The rump was bright brick red, but only visible the few times I saw the bird from behind and the wings were drooped.
Behaviors: I saw a total of seven birds on the public stretch of Yellow Jacket Canyon; I found one nest attended in a crack just above a fork in a cottonwood about 25 feet up. I saw another female carrying food (it appeard to be a small caterpillar). I found each pair by listening for the singing males, which sang for about 3 hours starting immediately after dawn. I also ran across a pair in a juniper tree at the base of the rimrock above the riparian zone; they were actively foraging in the juniper trees and the female gave an alarm call for an extended period of time. These birds were at least 300 feet from the nearest cottonwood or tamarisk.
Call: The male birds were singing a Yellow Warbler-esque song, three parted but faster, slightly higher pitched, and quieter than that of Yellow Warbler. The one bird I extensively recorded sang in an ABAB pattern. In song type A the three parts got successively slower (though barely so), while in the second the middle part was by far the fastest.

Some birds, usually the males in response to pishing, gave an emphatic, flat "stup" call note. The female of the pair I found in the junipers continuously gave a very high pitched, flat "tink", which appeared to function as an alarm call.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

All other species of Warbler were ruled out by the almost entirely gray plumage; Virginia's Warbler especially was ruled out by the complete lack of yellow as well as the brick-red rump.

Juvenile Verdin was ruled out by the thinner and longer bill, brick red rump, and thinner shape, as well as drastically different song.
