Habitat

Wetland - bird was in some low bushes amidst a cattail marsh when I observed it. It then flew off towards a group of tall trees marking the boundary between the preserve and private property on the northwest.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Athlon Midas 8x42 and Sony RX10 MkIV camera
Distance:  40 feet
Light:  Good. The sun was to the right and behind the bird, but partly cloudy conditions kept it from being too harsh and I got great looks at the bird.

Description of the Bird

Small bird very actively foraging alone in bushes/low tree in and around cattail marsh on northwest side of the preserve. While the bird was solo at all times during my observation, it gave off a distinct impression of smallness - I would guess chickadee-sized or smaller. 

The bird was overall gray, the underparts very plain and slightly paler than upperparts. In some views there was a hint of buffiness at the throat and along the flanks, though a closer view may have revealed more buffiness. The breast, belly, and undertail coverts were pale and plain, with no real contrast between any of them. The bird had a thin, short, pointed beak, a thin, pale eyering, and slighly paler lores on an otherwise gray head. There was an identifiable rusty crown, though not extensive from the views I was able to get. The bird's upperparts were uniformly gray from the head to the tip of the tail, with the exception of a rusty rump that was visible when bird was moving and facing the opposite direction.

Primarily based upon the (seeming) limited extent of the crown, my conjecture is this was a female, but given my lack of experience with the species, my inability for closer study of the crown, and lack of understanding of just how variable the extent of the crown can be on either of the sexes and at what ages, I am electing not to age or sex the bird in this report.

This bird to my knowledge was originally found and reported on eBird by Cole, Beth, and Dave Sage on April 16, 2023, though I did not get a chance to chase it until the afternoon of April 17 after following numerous subsequent reports throughout both days. I looked for the bird for about an hour, and while I was investigating a separate bird noise emanating from the marsh to my west (likely a Song Sparrow, this bird never seemed to make noise while I observed it), this bird sallied from a low, snaggy shrub in the marsh. I got my binoculars up for a good look at it in the bush it had returned to, and then the bird popped up and perched for maybe 15-20 seconds atop the bush where I was able to get good, identifiable photos of it from my vantage. It was clearly gleaning some sort of insects from the bushes/air around the bushes while I observed it. It perused some more low shrubs on the far shore from me, working north and west, before ending up in a short tree for another 30 seconds or so, where I observed it moving through the interior and got a poor but identifiable photo of it's rufous rump. It then took off and flew quickly past a group of birders to it's north to land in some trees that bordered private property on the preserve's northwest boundary.

Similar Species Discussion

Orange-crowned Warbler - The bird in question was gray and had none of olive and yellowish tones of an Orange-crowned. Thin pale eyering was complete as opposed to the thin eye arcs of an Orange-crowned. 

Virginia's Warbler - Thinner eyering than expected on a Virginia's. Lacked yellow anywhere on breast, rump, and undertail coverts.

Tennessee Warbler - Lacked a dark eyeline and contrasting supercilium of a Tennessee. No wingbar, no green to the upperparts, and lacked any yellow cast to the underparts.

Nashville Warbler - Thinner eyering than expected on a Nashville. Lacked greenish upperparts, yellowish underparts.

Colima Warbler - Lacked overall brown cast of a Colima, had a thinner eyering, and had pale undertail coverts consistent with rest of underparts as opposed to orangey-yellow undertail coverts that contrast with rest of underparts on a Colima.

Previous Experience

This was my lifer Lucy's but I have studied the bird in advance of past trips to New Mexico in case I should encounter it in the field there.

I am comfortable with most of the similar species:

I am very experienced with Orange-crowned Warbler, encountering them many times a year.

I am used to observing Virginia's Warbler every year on migration and during the breeding season. 

I have observed Tennessee Warbler and Nashville Warbler multiple times on migration in Colorado over the past few years.

I have never encountered a Colima Warbler
