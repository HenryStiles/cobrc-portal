Description of the Bird

The bird was standing in the shallow pool on the concrete slab/pad, next to a Lesser Yellowlegs.  It was considerably shorter than the yellowlegs, and far more compact.

Overall, it was quite similar to a Semipalmated Plover (I had seen one the evening before), but
1) Its legs were bright orange
2) Its back was a light sandy color

The black breast band was complete, but narrow in front, wider at the sides.

Except for the breast band, the underparts were white.

It had a short bill, white forehead mark, and black on the face.

Similar Species Discussion

Piping Plovers are very distinctive birds, so there is not much risk of confusion with other species.  My comparison with a Semipalmated Plover was mainly to help describe the bird; the observed differences in leg and back color were so dramatic that I ruled out Semipalmated as soon as I saw this bird.

Resources Used

I knew immediately that this was a Piping Plover, but I did look at the National Geographic Guide later in the day just to confirm my impression.

Previous Experience

In Colorado, I had seen Piping Plovers just once before, on their primary nesting ground near John Martin Reservoir.  I was aware that they are restricted almost entirely to Bent and Kiowa Counties in Colorado, and that a sighting in northeastern Colorado was not expected.

I have seen Piping Plovers a few times along the Texas coast.
