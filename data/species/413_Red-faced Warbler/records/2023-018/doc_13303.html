Habitat

Mature plains cottonwoods overtopping shrubby understory that included 3-leaf sumac, mulberry and Siberian elm.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Swarovski 8.5 x 42 binoculars
Distance:  50 feet
Light:  Sunny day, sun low in the sky, bird was viewed from below while it foraged in the tops of large cottonwoods that still had their crowns bathed in sunlight.

Description of the Bird

Warbler that was whitish below, medium gray above.  The head was mostly red with a large black middle portion that extended from the cap down thru the eye, coming to a point at the bottom (see photos).  The initial report by Steve Mlodinow, and a later email from Glenn Walbeck, indicated when they observed it ealier in the day the bird was foraging fairly low in a small group of mid-sized trees just beginning to leaf out.  When Jane, Janeal and I finally found the bird, it was in the tops of plains cottonwood trees.  The aforementioned mostly-leafless trees were mulberries.  We never saw the bird in the mulberries.  I think the reason the bird moved in early evening to the tops of the cottonwoods was because the crowns at that time of day were still bathed in sunlight and insects were still active in the catkins and high-up cottonwood leaves.  I would describe the activity level of the bird as modest (more active than a typical vireo, less hyper than kinglets or some warblers can be).  

 The bird was silent as far as we could tell.

 The redness of the head seemed more than what a female would be.

Similar Species Discussion

There really is no warbler-sized North American bird that looks like an adult male red-faced warbler.

Resources Used

Sibley just to see the difference in red shade between male and female.

Previous Experience

I have seen perhaps a dozen of these in Arizona, none in CO.
