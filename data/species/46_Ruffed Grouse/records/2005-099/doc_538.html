Habitat

I estimated that the mixed-conifer forest here was in seral stage 4 (sub-mature). Average height of the canopy was 12 meters. Overstory cover was 70-80 percent. The trees were about equal parts Douglas-Fir and aspen, with a few Ponderosa Pines thrown in. There was a small amount of subcanopy in the form of young aspens. Understory cover was about 20-30 percent, with an average height of one meter, comprised of roughly equal parts serviceberry, snowberry and common juniper. There was woody downfall in the area; ground cover was roughly 80 percent bare, 20 percent grass, with a little woody growth and no bare rock. Average grass height was less than two inches. We were not far away from a fairly dense tangle of deciduous brush including much alder.

Viewing Conditions

Description of the Bird

Andy Boyce flushed a grouse that flew about 30 feet and came back down to the ground. The bird looked smaller than the Dusky Grouse we'd seen earlier in the day. Also seemed to have quieter wingbeats. Bird flushed to base of Douglas-Firs about 10 meters from me to the southeast or east, pretty much 90 degrees left of the sun, but in full shade. Through a gap in the trees I could see the bird from the neck up. It was nervous, bobbing its head rapidly, a behavior we hadn't seen from Dusky Grouse that day, even ones we'd flushed multiple times. It had a brown cast to its plumage and a long cardinal-like crest, very different from the rounded, scruffy crests of Dusky Grouse seen well over the past two days. Bird was on the ground for about 90 seconds before flushing again without apparent provocation. When the bird flushed again, I marveled at how little noise the wings made; at 12 meters, it was barely audible. By contrast, Dusky Grouse wing flushing noise was audible at 30 meters or more.
Behaviors: See above.
Call: Bird was silent, though see comments below on wing noise.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

Dusky Grouse was the only other galliform expected in the habitat. It was eliminated by the brownish plumage, the thin pointed crest, and the nearly silent flight.

Resources Used

We used Sibley extensively earlier in the day.

Previous Experience

I have not seen many Ruffed Grouse; only a half-dozen or so. On the other hand, I had seen over sixty Dusky Grouse that morning alone, as we had repeatedly flushed members of a huge loose flock during our search of the "1991 grove." In addition, we had exhaustively studied a female Dusky Grouse the day before just down the hill in the hopes that it would be a Ruffed Grouse. We kept trying to make grouse after grouse into a Ruffed; when we finally saw the Ruffed, the difference was obvious.
