Habitat

mixed mature doug fir and aspen woodland, about 50-50 of each. Undergrowth was predominately Snowberry, with some Common Juniper, Serviceberry and Wild Rose.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Bausch and Lomb Elites, 10 x 40
Distance:  30
Light:  the bird was entirely in the deep shade. The sun was behind my back, though obscured by a large doug fir

Description of the Bird

the bird was grouse shaped, with a chicken-like body, and significantly smaller (about 2/3 the size) than the the many (45 or more) Blue Grouse we had seen that day. The bird overall appeared more delicate than the Blue Grouse as well. The overall color was a brownish-gray, grayer than the female Blue Grouse we had seen that day, but still with a distinct brown overtone.

The bill was black, and smaller and thinner compared to the head than the bill of a Blue Grouse. The head had a long, thin, "Pyrrhuloxia-like" crest, that was visible at all times, and especially so when the bird was alarmed (as it was during most the observation). There were bold, black ruffs on both sides of the neck, widest at the lower end. The tail was the same brown-gray color as the body, finely barred, with a wide, bold, black terminal band.

I did not see the underparts long enough to notice the patterning or color, and did not notice the patterning on the back or wings.
Behaviors: The bird was originally flushed off the ground by Andy Boyce and almost immediately landed on the ground again. For the duration of the observation the bird was on the ground, walking nervously and raising its crest. It flushed after 30 seconds, never to be seen again.
Call: none heard. When flushed, the wingbeats could barely be heard, compared to the very loud wingbeats of the many Blue Grouse we had seen that day.
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

All grouse other than Blue Grouse were ruled out by habitat and range, as well as small size, black ruffs on the side of the neck, black terminal tail band, and long, thin crest.

Blue Grouse was ruled out by much smaller size, grayer color, smaller bill, more delicate look, much longer, thiner and more obvious crest, black ruffs on the side of the neck, and finely barred tail with the bold black terminal band.

Resources Used

Sibley was consulted the day before to puzzle out the various plumages of Blue Grouse, and the 1992 CFO article on finding Ruffed Grouse here was consulted after the fact. Both helped us know what to look for, but when we saw the bird we knew instantly what we had.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Ruffed Grouse in the east, including some a very close range for extended periods. I have also seen a few Ruffed Grouse in the Rocky Mountains, in northern Idaho and Montana.

I have seen tons of Blue Grouse in Colorado, California, Idaho, and Montana, and had seen 45 or more Blue Grouse that day on Hoy Mountain, including some excellent chances to study both male and female plumages.
