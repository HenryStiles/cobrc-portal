Habitat

mixed aspen-doug fir, with a thick understory of snowberry, serviceberry, and common juniper. There were many fallen trees in parts of the area.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Baush and Lomb Elites 10x40
Distance:  as close as 15 feet
Light:  For the most part the bird was in deep shade, but occasionally would walk onto sundappled ground.

Description of the Bird

the bird was an obvious grouse, noticeably smaller than the Blue Grouse we had seen that day. The overall color was a grayish-brown, slightly browner on the first bird we saw. The overall shape was more slender than the Blue Grouse we had seen that day, with a smaller head, longer neck, and smaller bill.

Both bird we saw had long, thin crests, that were reminscent of the crest of a Northern Cardinal. This crest was especially visible when the birds were alarmed. When the second bird we saw was just walking around it would lay its crest against its head, and the crest was hard to discern. The color of the head, including the crest, was the same grayish-brown as the overall color, and the crest and sides of the face had tiny white scallop-shaped marks on the feathers. There was a small but bold white spot in front of the eye, which itself was very dark brown.

The back of the neck and upper back of the bird had a large and very bold black ruff, that for the most part was hidden by the grayish-brown color in such a way that the ruff was only visible as verticle black streaks, except for the lower end of the ruff on the back, where the black was unobscured. The front and sides of the neck were grayish-brown with bold white scallop marks.

The chest of the bird was also grayish-brown, with large and bold white edges to the feathers. The flanks were white with very large and bold black vertical bars, especially towards the lower flanks. This was much more noticeable on the second individual we saw.

The back of the bird was grayish-brown, with very thin white edges to the feathers, except for the sides of the back, where the grayish-brown feathers had bold white streaks running through the center of teh feathers along the shaft. The wing coverts were slightly browner than the overall color, and had some blackish markings on them. The flight feathers were black.

The tail of the birds were slightly browner than the overall color, especially on the first bird we saw (whose tail appeared almost rufous). The tail had many very thin black bars across the feathers. There was then a somewhat wider pale gray subterminal band, followed by a very wide and bold black subterminal band, and then by another pale gray band as a terminal band. On the first individual we saw the black subterminal band was unbroked, but on the second this band was broken due to the central tail feathers having this band a medium gray color rather than black. When the tail was folded this made the black band very hard to see.

The undetail coverts were white. The birds had tarsi feathered with light brown feathers. The feet were dark gray. The bill was black, and quite small in relation to the head.

In flight the sound of the wings was almost inaudible.
Behaviors: The first bird we found was drumming on a large log at first, and then when we made noise and approached to see it it sat still on the log for a minute or two. It then hopped onto the ground on the other side of the log, and as we walked closer it flushed up a secondary drainage towards Utah.

The second bird was found walking on the ground, and spent most of its time picking berries and buds off of low growing shrubs. At times when we got to close it would run faster, or sit on a log motionless.

The third bird was only heard drumming, while we were watching the first bird, and from a location a good ways off from where we found the second bird.
Call: the first bird we found was drumming when we found it, which sounded like a grouse-like bird taking off, except that the "wingbeats" accelerated. This sound was so low pitched that it almost was felt rather than heard. I also heard some quiet clucking sounds that may have come from one of the birds
Plumage: adult

Similar Species Discussion

All grouse other than Blue were ruled out by locationa and habitat, as well as small size, black ruff on the neck, and black tail band.

Blue Grouse was ruled out by smaller size, very long and thin crest, bold black tail band, and bold black ruff on the sides of the neck, as well as the almost silent flight.

Previous Experience

I have seen many Ruffed Grouse in Mass., including some very well. I have also seen one Ruffed Grouse in Colorado, at the same location as these individuals.

I have seen many Blue Grouse in Colorado, including some the same day as this record, and at least 45 the last time I was on Hoy Mountain.
