Habitat

Dry canyon with scattered bushes and small trees. Looked like I was driving along a dry riverbed too.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Kowa, TSN-4 spotting scope with a 30x wide angle eyepiece. I don't recall the binoculars I was using at this time
Distance:  Maybe 100 feet or so.
Light:  It was a nice clear and sunny morning. I was driving west when I first saw this bird. It flew to the south of me and landed. I had no problems seeing this bird with the sun light off to my left side when I had my scope on it.

Description of the Bird

I was driving through the Cottonwood Canyon area when I flushed a grayish bird with a long tail from a bush along side the road. It flew a short distance to another small tree and landed in good view. I stopped my car, got out of my car and set up my spotting scope on it. I figured I was about 40 yards away from it. Once I got the bird in view, I knew right away it was a Scissor-tailed Flycatcher. I saw what I felt was a female as its tail wasn't longer as compared to it body, maybe the same length to its body length? Still, it had a long tail that was black with white edges. Head and upper back was pale grayish in color, throat and breast was whitish, it had salmon coloring on the flanks, and I could see the reddish-salmon axillaries but only slightly because this bird had its wings folded against its body. Wings appeared dark with whitish feather edges. The bill was dark. If I had to judge the size of this bird, I'd say its body size was close to that of an Eastern Kingbird, which I have here in Wisconsin, but with a much longer tail.
Behaviors: I only observed this bird for 5 minutes before moving on to do more birding. This bird just sat on a branch in this small tree looking around. I did not see it fly after I first flushed it.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

Really no other species that I could misidentify this bird for. No other normally occurring flycatcher in North America has this long tail.

Resources Used

I'm pretty sure I had along the Field Guide to the Birds of North America, published by the National Geographic Society, and probably by Peterson Field Guide too.
