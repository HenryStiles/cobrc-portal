Documentation of Yellow-crowned Night Heron
2010-183

Observer Information

Reporter:  Laurens Halsey   
E-mail: 
Other Observers:  Original observers were non-birder friends that reported a "bittern" in their neighborhood. These observers knew I was visiting nearby and permitted me to view and photograph the bird. When it was learned that this was potentially a rare sp; species, the property owners requested that bird not be reported until much later.

Habitat

Residential backyard with various plant bed and vegetation surrounded water treatment. The water treatment has a couple of small water falls creating falling water sounds - perhaps this attracted the YCNH (just speculation, I never observed the bird at the water treatment or in the surrounding vegetation)

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  10x50 binocs for initial identification as YCNH. Through Olympus C-730 Ultra Zoom digital camera for photography.
Distance:  estimated, 25-100 feet.
Light:  Full sun, sun behind and to the left of this observer.

Description of the Bird

Small to medium sized heron, thick necked, heavy billed. Plumage: Overall streaked brown. Eye reddish-orange. Bill mostly dark, basal half of lower mandible light (straw colored) with distinct transition to dark distal end. Legs yellowish-green.
Behaviors: Roosting in residential backyard, other (non-birding) observers stated the was attempting to roost under yard furniture and a trampoline. All of my observations/photographs were of the bird sitting on top of fences separating yards, and once on the railing of an upperstory backporch.
Call: none noted
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

American Bittern and Black-crowned Night Heron: AMBI - overall coloration, narrow distinct streaking on throat and underparts versus long strips on throat and underparts of AMBI. BCNH - mostly dark thick bill, long legs, distinct streaking

Resources Used

At time of identification, no resources used. After observation, consulted National Geographic Guide to convince original observers that the bird was a night-heron (they have seen adult BCNH and could not believe juvs looked like bitterns). Referenced Sibley's Guide prior to writing this report when I recently found the photographs.
