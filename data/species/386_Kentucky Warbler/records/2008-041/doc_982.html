Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized warbler, about the same size as a Yellow-rumped Warbler, though with a different shape and posture. The bird was mostly seen hopping close to the ground, and had a horizontal posture. The shape was bulky for a warbler, accentuated by a relatively shorter tail than most Dendroica warblers. The bill was short, straight, and fairly thin, but thicker than usual for a warbler, and was black. The legs were pink.

The bird's crown was black, morphing towards the rear crown into a olive green nape and back. The bird had a bold yellow supercilium, and a yellow crescent to the rear of the eye which connected with the supercilium. The auriculars of the bird were black, and the black continued down the sides of the throat to just about the bend on the wing. The upperparts were the same olive green color as the nape, as were the wings and tail. The throat was the same bright yellow as the supercilium, as was the chest and belly. The flanks were tinged olive green.
Behaviors: The bird was first seen skulking low in dense willow, and occasionally singing. The next day it was also seen skulking low in dense willows, but as never heard to sing.
Call: The bird was singing during the first day of the observation, a rich, rolling, churry-churry-churry, made up of two-syllabled notes rather than three syllabled notes as in Carolina Wren. On the second day it was heard chipping, a loud, heavy chip with a slight mettalic quality.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

All other species of warbler were ruled out by the very distinctive face pattern, shape, and behavior of the bird.
