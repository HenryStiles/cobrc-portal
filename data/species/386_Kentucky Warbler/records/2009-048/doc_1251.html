Description of the Bird

The bird was a rather large, distinctively-shaped warbler, with a little bit of a pot-bellied look (or at least a deep-breasted, deep-bellied look). It was uniformly bright yellow below and dull olive-green above. There were no wingbars. There was a black facial mask on which was superimposed an incomplete pair of yellow spectacles that wrapped partway around behind the eye.
Behaviors: The bird was singing fairly regularly from medium-high (10-20 feet) but not exposed perches, mostly in ponderosa pine trees but also lower down in some of the riparian brush. I saw it fly from tree to tree several times but couldn't get a good look at it for 45 minutes. When I finally did get a good look, the bird had come down near the ground and was hanging out near the path in dense riparian underbrush, still singing occasionally.
Call: Bird was singing the same song over and over: a strict monotone series of ca. 6 disyllabic high clear musical whistles, the second syllable higher-pitched: "cher-WEE cher-WEE cher-WEE cher-WEE" at a rate of ca. 3 syllables/sec. I am attaching a recording of the song that I made.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

No other bird looks like Kentucky Warbler--the entirely yellow belly and yellow spectacles on a black mask are distinctive. In addition, the song helps clinch the ID: a strict monotone series of 2-noted whistles might be matched by Northern Cardinal or Carolina Wren, or confused with Ovenbird, but none of those birds could be visually confused with a KEWA.
