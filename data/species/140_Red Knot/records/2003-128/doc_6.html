Description of the Bird

A chunky, short necked shorebird. On juvenile, Legs are dull yellow. The underparts are pale (almost white), with some dark speckling getting heavier towards the breast. The breast is darker (kind of gray) with good coverage of fine dark spots. The face is dominated by a white supercillium, and pale cheeks, giving the bird a bit of a masked appearance. The crown and nape are gray. The iris appears to be black. The bill is straight, fairly heavy, and fairly short for a Calidris sandpiper. The bill is black. The upperparts of the bird are gray to brownish gray depending on the light angle. These feathers are have thin light edging. (juvenile trait.) The primaries appear dark and project only minimally beyond the tail tip.

The adult basic Red Knot was virtually the same in most regards, but lacked the thin pale edging on the upper feather tracts.

The funny thing is that most of the time we were looking at the adult. When I got a finally got a Red Knot in my viewfinder for digiscoping, Chris noted that I was pointing my scope in a different directions. It turns out that I had inadvertently scanned over to the juvenile bird. Unfortunately I wasn't able to photograph the adult.
Behaviors: The birds were in a mixed flock of shorebirds, feeding actively in shallow water.
Call: n/a
Plumage: 1 adult basic and 1 juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

The yellowish legs rule out most North American shorebirds. Among shorebirds with yellow or yellowish legs, the light-legged plovers can be ruled out on bill length, among other obvious plumage differences. Oystercatchers have yellow legs but completely different bill structure and color plus obvious plumage differences. Tringa sandpipers with yellow legs have much longer legs and different bill structures and shapes, plus plumage differences. Spotted and Upland Sandpipers have yellow legs but completely different bill colors and structures as well as obvious plumage differences. Turnstones, Tattlers, and Surfbirds have yellow legs but major bill structure and plumage differences. Ruffs and Reeves have yellowish legs, but much different plumage coloration and bill shape, color, and structure. Dowitchers have yellowish legs, but much longer bills among other obvious differences. Buff-breasted Sandpiper has yellow legs, but a tiny needly bill among other significant structural and plumage differences. Wilson's Snipe has yellow legs, but a much longer bill among other significant structural and plumage differences. Wilson's Phalarope also has yellow legs but significant bill, plumage, and body structure differences.

The overall shape of the bird suggests a Calidris sandpiper, which mostly have dark legs. Purple Sandpipers show yellow legs, but have a longer, more pointed drooping bill than REKN, as well as wingtips that don't reach the tail and other significant plumage differences. Pectoral and Sharp-tailed Sandpipers have yellow legs, but show light-based bills with thinner tapers than REKN. Additionally, PESA and STSA have more richly colored and higher contrasting plumage on their backs, and appear less chunky and compact than REKN. Stilt Sandpipers have yellowish legs, but much longer, thinner, slightly drooping bills among other significant structural and plumage differences. Among other major bill and plumage differences, the yellow-legged Least Sandpiper is much smaller than REKN.
