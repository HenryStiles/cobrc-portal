Description of the Bird

Mostly gray medium-sized shorebird smaller than Black-bellied Plovers with which it occasionally associated and much larger than Baird's Sandpiper which were seen closer to the road. Bill blackish and of medium length, longer i.e than the Black-bellied Plovers' bills. Very long wings and shortish green gray legs. Whitish supercillium. Breast with faint pinkish tinge (which is found on juveniles, but not always seen, and is different than the red of an adult). Faint medium gray eyestripe just below the supercillium. Wing coverts and scapulars strongly edged with white with a dark subterminal dark band and gray feather centers. A faint dark feather shaft visible when bird was the closest, but not when far away. In flight a white wing stipe was visible.
Behaviors: First seen on shoreline by itself whre it probed in the mud. Later joined up with four Black-bellied Plovers and together foragged the shoreline before flying off together.
Call: none heard
Plumage: junvenile bird with some juvenal plumage

Similar Species Discussion

When seen well, no other species except Terek Sandpiper, Great Knot, and juvenile Surfbird has the gray-centered feathers with the dark subterminal bands and the broad white edges giving a strong scaly appearance. Terek Sandpiper's bill is upturned and easy to separate. Wandering Tattler has the same grayish coloration and is about the same size (but larger) and it feathers are gray centered with white edges but not to the extent of juvenile Red Knots, and their white edge feathers do not have the dark subterminal bands. Surbird is darker gray with shorter bill that is yellow based unlike the thinner, longer bill of Red Knot. Great Knot is similar but with darker contrasting feathers on the scapulars and wing coverts and might be slightly longer billed.

Previous Experience

I have seen quite a few Red Knots on the east coast of Virginia where I worked for 5 summers on Assateague Island National Seashore. In addition I have seen a few Red Knots in NW Alaska where I worked for two summers, including juveniles and adults. I was at first fooled there by the coloration of the juveniles which is so different from the adults. I have seen juvenile Surbirds in Alaska and one Terek Sandpiper on Attu and some in Australia. I have only seen Great Knots, a few, in Australia.
