Viewing Conditions

Light:  Clear and sunny for the duration of the viewing. Initially the sun was from the observers back and shining upon the bird. Last seen with the light coming from the observers right.

Description of the Bird

Medium-sized shorebird, appeared to be ever so slightly shorter that nearby Stilt Sandpipers (a little shorter legged). A medium-brown, streaked cap on the head. Auriculars medium buff. Large, dark brown lores extended back barely past the eyes making the bright whitish supercillium really stand out. Throat white, light buff on front of neck and upper breast. Lower breast, belly and undertail coverts STARK white (seen as the bird faced away from me and fed); Legs, bill and eyes black. Bill longish and evenly decurved the entire length (not straight, then drooping at the tip like the Stilt Sandpipers); back, scapulars, wing coverts and tertials were medium-brown with chrisp white feather edges on scaps and wing coverts (identifying this bird as a juvenile). Thin white wingbar running along the base of the primaries and secondaries starting at the body, running along the center of the wing and extending the length of the wing (seen as the bird preened and twice when the bird flew). Primaries dark brown and slightly longer than the tail; Rump white, tail dark brown or black; Did not notice the leg length when the bird flew.
Behaviors: Witnessed this bird:
1) Feeding "sewing machine" style just like nearby Stilt Sandpiper. While feeding with Stilt Sandpipers and a few Long-billed Dowitchers this bird was almost always on the shore side of the flock and not in deeper water on the backside of the flock.
2) Preening breast, belly and wings giving observers a good look at the all-white rump and wingbar.
3) Flying with Stilt, Baird's and Pectoral Sandpipers and Long-billed Dowitchers allowing a great comparison with these (if you could find it in the flock!) and an excellent view of the wingbar.

Bird flushed several times and almost always stayed in the company of anywhere from five to about forty Stilt Sandpipers.

Last seen when a Peregrine Falcon came in and made several passes at a Lesser Yellowlegs (which it eventually caught and ate) and flush all the birds. May have flown to the far south shore of the reservoir.
Call: None noted
Plumage: juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

Stilt Sandpiper - eliminated because they 1) lack the white wingbar, 2) have yellowish legs, and 3) have relatively straight bills that droop nearer the tip.

Dunlin - eliminated because they 1) have much shorter legs, 2) dark rumps and upper tail coverts, and 3) lack a noticable supercillium.

Red Knot - eliminated because they 1) have a shorter, straight bill, 2) yellowish legs

Broad-billed Sandpiper - eliminated because they 1) are very gray, 2) have black rumps and uppertail coverts, and lack the warm buff neck and upper breast.

Pectoral, Sharp-tailed, and Cox's Sandpipers - eliminated because they 1) are much smaller and plumper, 2) have dark rumps, 3) much shorter, and only slightly decurved bills, have yellowish legs.
