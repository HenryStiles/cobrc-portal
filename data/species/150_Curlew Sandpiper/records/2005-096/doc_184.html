Description of the Bird

the bird was a fairly small sandpiper, about 10-15 percent larger than adjacent Baird's Sandpipers. The body size was slightly smaller than adjacent Stilt Sandpipers, but the significantly shorter legs gave the impression of smaller size. The bird had a fairly fat, "dumpy" shape, with a short, thick neck, and a close to horizontal posture. The wings were longer than the tail. The legs were fairly short and black. The bill was black, and almost half again as long as the head. The bill also has a stong downward curve, fairly even, though a little more pronounced at the end. The bill was thickest at the base and tapered fairly evenly to the tip.

The bird had a medium-brown crown, with a thin and indistinct white stripe extending into the center of the crown. The bird had a very bold white supercilium that helped enhance the crowned look. There was a dark brown stripe through the eye that extended the length of the supercilium. The uppermost throat was white, but the lower throat and chest were light buffy, with some faint and indistinct darker striped towards the edge of the chest. The nape and back were a medium brown, about the same color as the crown. The wing coverts of the bird were also a medium brown, though a little darker than the crown and back. All of the covert feathers had a bold light buffy almost whitish edging to them, giving the bird a bold scalloped appearance. The primaries, when folded, appeared almost black. The belly and undertail coverts of the bird was white, with no discernable markings.

In flight a completely white rump was visible, as was a bold white wing stripe in the wings above, and bright white underwings. The tail appeared brownish with some white markings, but I never saw the tail well.
Behaviors: the first day I saw the bird it hung out with Baird's Sandpipers at the edge of the water, and spent most of it's time foraging, and a little time preening. Twice it was seen to fly, once a short distance, and once a long distance.

the second day I saw it the bird was loosely associated with Stilt Sandpipers, but often by itself, and foraging for most of the time at the edge of the water or in shallow water. It also spent some time preening, flying short distances, and resting with its head tucked into its back.
Call: none heard
Plumage: juvenal

Similar Species Discussion

all other sandpipers except for Dunlin and Stilt Sandpiper were ruled out by the combination of the long, strongly downcurved bill, and small size.

Dunlin was ruled out by the shape of the bill, more strongly downcurved than I would expect for that species, and by the boldly scalloped upperparts. Dunlin was also ruled out by the clean white rump without a central black stripe, and by the bold white supercilium and dark eyeline, creating the capped appearance.

Stilt Sandpiper was ruled out by the more strongly and evenly downcurved bill, by the much shorter and black legs, by the shorter neck and dumpier appearance, by boldly and evenly scalloped back. The buffy color of the back and chest also ruled out Stilt Sandpiper, which should appear grayer.

Pectoral Sandpiper was ruled out by the completely black bill, lack of bold markings on the underparts, black legs, bright white rump, and white wing stripe.

Baird's Sandpiper and all other peeps were ruled out by the larger size and strongly downcurved bill
