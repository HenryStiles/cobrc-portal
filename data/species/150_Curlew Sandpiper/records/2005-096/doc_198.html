Description of the Bird

This bird was a smallish sandpiper, that was just a very little smaller than the Stilt Sandpipers and somewhat larger than the many nearby Baird's Sandpipers. It had a rather noticeable eye stripe that was a little stronger than on Stilt Sandpiper, and the cap was a little rusty color. The sides of the upper breast were also quite rusty; the rest of the underside was whitish. The upperparts were scaly with most feathers having lighter edges. The black bill was not as long as Stilt Sandpiper, but longer than Baird's. It was decurved, but not as strongly as adult Curlew Sandpipers that I've seen. However, it was curved for much of its length, whereas Stilt Sandpiper bills are decurved only near the tip. One of the best fieldmarks was all black legs, which were not as long as the yellowish legs of the Stilt Sandpipers. The bird once stretched its nearer wing for a few seconds revealing a medium sized wing stripe all along its length. This also showed one side of a completely white rump, quite similar to a Stilt Sandpiper.
Behaviors: The bird was actively feeding on the mudflat of a large plaind reservoir. I also saw it resting, when it only stood on only one leg. It fed along the edge of the water and not in it, whereas the Stilt Sandpipers were often feeding in the water.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

Stilt Sandpiper has longer, yellowish legs. Bill is decurved only at the end. The markings on the upper breast of the juvenile go all the way across.
Baird's Sandpiper was somewhat smaller, with much shorter legs, much less of a eye line and buffy, not rusty, markings on upper breast and top of head. Does not have an all white rump.
Dunlin is very rarely seen in juvenile plumage in the US, and winter plumage is much more uniformly brown. Does not have all white rump.
