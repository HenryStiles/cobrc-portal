Description of the Bird

The bird was a white-headed gull smaller than nearby Ring-billed Gulls that I picked out due to the dark nape and distinct scapular crescents. It was about 10% smaller than the Ring-billeds with a short, thin yellow bill lacking a distinct black ring and smaller than those of the Ring-billeds. The white head had extensive brownish streaking, particularly on the nape. The head was rounder and the dark eye (surrounded by dark streaking that made the eye look bigger than it was) stood out, almost, seemingly, in the middle of the head (unlike the Ring-billed's eyes, which were placed more toward the front of the head).

The mantle was distinctly darker than those of the neighboring Ring-billeds, being approximately the shade of a dark California Gull (though there were none immediately apparent nearby). The distinct and somewhat large white scapular crescents contrasted markedly with the mantle and with the vague whitish scapular crescents of the Ring-bills next door. The bird also had distinct and wide white tertial crescents creating a similar contrast to that of the scapular crescents. The black primaries had large white tips. Except for some brownish streaking on the upper sides (extending down from the neck), what I could see of the underparts was unmarked white. I could not see the bird's legs.

Though it was getting dark, the sun having set a bit before I found the bird, I managed to take some (poor) digiscoped photos.
Behaviors: Loafing on land spit with other gulls
Call: none heard
Plumage: Definitive Basic

Similar Species Discussion

Ring-billed Gull ruled out by bill size and pattern, mantle color, obvious scapular and tertial crescents, and dark eyes. California Gull ruled out by bill size and pattern and by the bird's small size. I could not definitively rule out Kamchatka Gull, but the small size and very small bill suggest that it wasn't that taxon. I believe that the bird was too dark-backed for Common Gull, but I cannot definitively rule that taxon out, either.
