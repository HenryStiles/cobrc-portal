Description of the Bird

Tony Leukering and I had heard about an adult Mew Gull that had been seen at Prospect Lake in Colorado Springs and as we were birding in the area that day, decided to stop by and see if we could find it. It took awhile, but we eventually found the bird sleeping on the ice with nearby Ring-billed Gulls. We did observe the bird occasionally lift its head up, but I was unable to capture those brief moments with the camera.

The bird was a smallish gull, roughly the same size as the Ring-billeds, but with a longer primary projection. When sleeping, its head was quite rounded and was muddied by extensive dark brown streaking. This muddied smudging extended onto the neck as well. The breast, belly, and undertail coverts were white. The upperparts were a dark gray, noticeably darker in shade from nearby Ring-billeds. Quite evident was that the white scapular crescent was well wider than that of Ring-billeds. The folded primaries were black with white mirrors to primaries 9 and 10 and white tips to the primaries as well. The white mirrors were slightly larger in size than the adjacent Ring-billeds. The legs were a yellowish-green, slightly greener than the Ring-billeds. From beneath, the white mirror on primary 10 was noticeably larger than that of a Ring-billed. When the bird did awake briefly and look around, it was evident that the eye was dark brown and the bill was a lemon-yellow, short, slender, and slightly tapered with little gonydeal angle expressed.
Behaviors: The bird mainly was observed standing and sleeping with its head tucked in its wing. I did see it twice lift its head momentarily to look around.
Call: None heard
Plumage: Definitive basic

Similar Species Discussion

Based on size and general pattern, Ring-billed Gull is the most similarly appearing species. Ring-billed Gulls do not have the brown smudging across the head and neck, generally have a more peaked head shape, have a slightly longer, thicker bill with a dark smudge or complete ring visible near the tip in winter, and have a yellow eye. The mantle color of Ring-billed Gull is lighter in shade than Mew Gull. Ring-billed also have a thinner scapular crescent, a shorter primary extension, and less extensive white in the folded primaries.
