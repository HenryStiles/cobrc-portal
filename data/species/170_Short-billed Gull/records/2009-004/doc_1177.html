Habitat

The bird was sitting on the water's surface. Because there was absolutely no wind, the water's surface was perfectly calm.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Many optics were used. I used a Leica 32X telescope and Nikon 10X binoculars.
Distance:  I estimated that the bird was initially about 300 feet away; we then got to well within 200 feet.
Light:  It was a ridiculously sunny, warm, and calm afternoon. No clouds at all. Initially, we observed the bird from the south-southwest, with the sun pretty much behind us. As we got closer to the bird, we were southwest, then almost due west, of the bird, such that the sun was to our right.

Description of the Bird

I first saw the bird at a distance of about 250-300 feet. It was sitting on the water fairly close to the shore. Without binoculars, I guessed it was going to be a Ring-billed Gull. With binoculars (10X), I was struck by its relatively dark mantle, and I briefly wondered if it was a California Gull. Through a scope (32X), I could easily tell that it was a Mew Gull.

The bird presented itself for leisurely, close-up viewing, and identification as a definitive basic Mew Gull was straightforward. Key marks seen on the sitting bird included: medium-dark mantle with large white "skirt" of folded secondaries; long, pointy black wings, with bold contrasting long, white spot under outermost primary sometimes visible; small-bodied, attenuated shape enhanced by the bird's tendency to ride high on the water and to appear "corky" and buoyant; white underparts; small, gently rounded head with dove-like aspect; large dark eye; slight, almost entirely yellow bill with just a little gray smudging.

After at least 15 minutes of close-up observation (<175 feet through multiple telescopes), the bird flew to the other side of the reservoir, where it was perhaps 750 feet away and not amenable to further observation.

I was not one of the observers who actually saw the bird in flight. However, the bird sometimes thrashed about on the water (see below) permitting additional observation.
Behaviors: During most of the period of observation, the bird was simply sitting on the water along the west shore of Leggett Reservoir. On 3 or 4 occasions, however, it thrashed about--probably a bathing behavior. When its wings were upraised, it was easy to see the long white spot on the outermost primary (most evident from beneath).

After 15+ minutes, the bird flew to the other side of the reservoir, and then, eventually, we lost track of it.

Interestingly, it was, at first, the only gull on Leggett Reservoir. During the course of observation, 2 Ring-billed Gulls showed up, but none landed near the Mew Gull. One Ring-billed Gull did, however, fly directly over the Mew Gull (about 40 feet above the water), but the comparison was not especially helpful.
Call: The bird was silent during the period of observation.

Similar Species Discussion

I have already discussed this somewhat above. As I mentioned above, my first hunch, just based on size and probability of the bird seen with the naked eye, was Ring-billed Gull. When I saw the medium-dark mantle (with binoculars), I briefly wondered if it was a California Gull (mantle color is similar). Through the scope, the bird quickly established itself as a Mew Gull. Points of separation from similar species included the bird's small size overall; its corky/buoyant build; its long, pointy wings; its small, gently curved head; its large, dark eye; its slight bill, almost entirely yellow with just some dark smudging; and exact plumage details of the secondaries and primaries (see above). In details of wingtip pattern and mantle color/pattern, the bird was separated from the highly improbable Kamchatka and Common subspecies of Mew Gull.
