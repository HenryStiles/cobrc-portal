Description of the Bird

The following is a verbatim excerpt from my original paper writeup, dated March 13, 2003:

Structure:
The bird was chicken-like in structure, clearly a member of the Galliformes. It gave the impression of being large for a grouse. Its head showed a slight crest and its tail was medium-length and squared-off. During the observation, I specifically noted its feathered tarsi.

Plumage:
Caveat: although I took notes immediately after this encounter, I subsequently lost them, and must reconstruct my observation from memory.
The plumage was mottled and fairly varied. Overall it was rather dark gray. On the breast and foreneck there were some patches of the “blue” color characteristic of male Blue Grouse, which is not really blue but a kind of pale slaty gray. However, this color was not widespread on the bird. The upperside of the bird had numerous bright white spots, formed by white patches on parts of the webbing of individual feathers. These white spots are not illustrated in Sibley or any other field guide I have; however, I have had subsequent opportunities to verify their existence on other Blue Grouse I have seen in Colorado. Likewise, Sibley does not show any birds with as little blue in the plumage as I observed; this leads me to surmise that the bird might have been a young male, but that is pure guesswork on my part.
These are the only solid details I can recall at this time.
Behaviors: The following is a verbatim excerpt from my original paper writeup, dated March 13, 2003:

Observation time, distance, lighting and optics:
The bird was discovered by Stan before I arrived. I stopped when I saw him parked in the entrance to the parking lot north of the park road and just west of Kingfisher Bridge, because he was staring into the woods with binoculars. When I asked him what birds he had seen, he informed me that a probable Blue Grouse had just walked across the road and into the adjacent trees. Together we followed the bird into the shrubbery, and sure enough, we found a Blue Grouse pacing along the ground. I was able to observe the bird at 10-15 feet distance for at least a couple of minutes. As is typical of the species, it was only slightly wary, and responded to close approach by walking away rather unhurriedly. In this fashion we followed it back across the park road and lost it in a small patch of brush on the south side. Starting fifteen minutes later, Andrew Spencer and Joey Kellner and I tried to relocate it, but we were ultimately unsuccessful.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: possibly molting into adult

Similar Species Discussion

The following is a verbatim excerpt from my original paper writeup, dated March 13, 2003:

Separation from similar species:
The only other galliform birds which are at all likely to occur at Chatfield State Park are Wild Turkey and Ring-necked Pheasant.
Size and structure completely eliminated turkeys, which have a bizarre, unmistakeable shape, long-necked, long-legged and tiny-headed. Also, this bird had a feathered head and tarsi, which turkeys would not.
Female Ring-necked Pheasant is perhaps a more likely source of ID confusion, although the feathered tarsi eliminate this possibility as well. Furthermore, the tail was many times too short and far too broad. Also, the overall color of the bird was far too dark; female pheasants are quite pale-buffy and more uniform. Finally, the confiding nature of the bird would be totally unexpected in a pheasant.

Resources Used

We checked Sibley at the time of observation (Stan thought it might be a Spruce Grouse, and I felt the need to dissuade him).

Previous Experience

Prior to this sighting, I had not seen a Blue Grouse in two years or so, but I had previously encountered the species on approximately a half-dozen occasions in the Rocky Mountain National Park area, and I had also seen and photographed one in Glacier National Park.
I had also seen hundreds of turkeys and, as a former South Dakotan, literally thousands of pheasants.

Additional Information

Comments: Though it is certainly not a rare bird in Colorado, Blue Grouse is an extraordinary find anywhere outside the mountains, and this sighting represented a first record for Chatfield State Park. It is worth noting that the day of this record (4/11/01) was a day of extreme weather in the mountains and adjacent plains which produced an exceptional fallout of birds at Chatfield. The grouse was merely the third of three first park records that day, one of which (a Gray Jay seen by Andrew Spencer) was also a mountain bird exceedingly rare on the plains. (The day’s other addition to the Chatfield list was a McCown’s Longspur seen by Joey Kellner).
