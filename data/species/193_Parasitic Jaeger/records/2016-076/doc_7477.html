Description of the Bird

Upon arrival at Jumbo Reservoir, I quickly noticed a dark gull-sized bird with a white flash on the wings, chasing gulls.  I was joined later by Bill Kaempfer and John Vanderpoel.  We eventually realized that we were observing 3 Jaegers.  One Jaeger flew very close to us, stealing food from a Franklin's Gull.

All 3 Jaegers had dark caps, indicating that they were adults and/or subadults.  Two were light, with dark gray caps and wings contrasting with a white-ish neck and underparts.  The third had more brown in the wings and a light grayish brown wash on the neck and underparts.  Two of the birds had a pale rump and vent.  The third had a dark rump and vent.  All 3 had a low-contrast breast band.  Some small lateral stripes were also seen just below the wing.

In flight, all 3 Jaegers showed a white flash on the underside of the wing at the base of the primaries, and also white near the base of the primaries on the upperside of the wing.  At least one of the birds also had pale patches on the underwing coverts.  I might speculate that this indicates a subadult rather than an adult, but I don't know for sure.  All 3 birds had short feather projections beyond the tail, appearing pointed when the birds were flying and also when they were resting on the water.

When resting on the water, not chasing gulls, the Jaegers were either by themselves or among Western Grebes.  Body length was comparable to that of the Western Grebes.  At times, all 3 Jaegers were together, and there were no detectable differences in size or structure.

Excellent scope views on September 21, plus photos on September 17, revealed key characteristics for identification.  Each bird had a relatively long and thin bill for a Jaeger, the bills were all dark, and each bird had a pale crescent behind the bill.


Note:  scope views were much better than the digiscope images would suggest.  Also, the photos of the close Jaeger were underexposed owing to poor lighting -- bright background but dark foreground benath a developing thunderstorm.

Similar Species Discussion

Long-tailed Jaeger was eliminated because an adult would have a uniform dark underwing rather than a white flash.

Pomarine Jaeger was eliminated becuase it would have a thick, bicolored bill, and no pale crescent behind the bill.
