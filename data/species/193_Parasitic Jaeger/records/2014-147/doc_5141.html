Description of the Bird

This was a light morph adult with tail streamers. I first saw the bird flying at the northwest end of the lake in a manner suggesting that it had just arrived at the location. It flew back and forth high over the water for several minutes, never chasing anything or showing interest in anything. There were no terns present, and the gulls were mostly loafing on the shore or the water. At times the jaeger was above my line of sight, other times at eye level, and at times lower than my view angle; I was perched atop a slope overlooking the lake. After following it for about 4 minutes I eventually looked away at something else, and when I looked back for the jaeger to try to refind it I could not. I think it departed the area, but I'm not sure. 

I was able to track this bird in the spotting scope for nice looks and viewed with binoculars. Age and plumage made the ID fairly easy. Long pointed central tail feathers without "spoons" but not as extreme in length as LTJA. Complete or nearly complete brown band on breast with white belly and brownish vent region. Whitish throat and the neck showed a bit of cream color. Bold dark cap. Upperparts and wings were even medium brown (not blackish), showing white on several primaries on upperside view (but no white flash on upperside). The underwing underwing showed nice flash of white notably more extensive than LTJA, but less bold than on POJA. General size was like RBGU, but with more pointed wings and slender falcon like flight style. The flight was powerful and steady. It appeared slimmer-bodied and less robust and heavy than POJA.

Similar Species Discussion

The white in the primaries, dark upperparts, long tail streamers, and falcon-like jizz clearly indicated a jaeger, which I was able to discern right off when I first saw it. 

The age (adult) and plumage (light morph with full tail streamers) made the ID relatively straight forward. 

LTJA has longer tail streamers, lacks a breast band, is less robust, lighter brown on wings and upperparts, and shows less white in primaries.

POJA with broad-tipped tail streamers (or if broken off then normally shorter than on this bird, sometimes much shorter), with darker coloration on upperparts and vent, more robust bulked-up body with stronger large falcon-like jizz looking more powerful, and more white in primaries.

Resources Used

I knew what species it was as soon as I had a good look at the jizz, body plumage, tail and degree of white in the wings. 

I did look at Sibley after seeing the bird.

Previous Experience

I lived and birder for 30 years along the central California coast where I saw many individuals of Parasitic and Pomarine Jaeger each year, especially in fall. I saw fewer Long-taileds, as they were generally to be seen from pelagic birding boats, and due to seasickness I didn't make it too often. But I did see some 15-20 Long-taileds over the years. A typical fall of active birding and watching the ocean from shore would yield me sightings of 30-70 Parasitics and 20-30 Pomarines per season, sometimes more (like 2013 when some days had 15-20 Parasitices in view at one time!). I became adept at identifying Parasitic and Pomarine at a distance, and developed a nice feel for their relative jizz.
