Description of the Bird

I knew this bird instantly was a Jaeger, unlike all the other birds at this wildlife area. I have seen this species at sea on pelagics but never in migration where it was so fearless and relaxed. The bird was dark, and sat high in the water daintily picking off insect emergents from the water. It paid little regard to me. I immediatley contacted my wife Lisa who was a few minutes away, and we both watched it for the next 30 minutes and took critical photos. I believe it is a dark morph adult (Alderfer, Second Edition, Complete Birds of North America). 

I had an advantage and knew what to look for. You may recall in 2020, our birding group found a road-killed bird that we documented in great detail. It was a Long-tailed Jaeger and was approved by the committee. So I knew photographs showing the white sharfts of the primaries would be immensely important. The bill tip and length would be important. Underwing and upper wing were photographed in detail. 

Bird was the size of a Ring-billed Gull, about 17-19 inches. It was dark brown all over the back, head, rump and tail. The undertail coverts showed a little barring. The central tail retrices showed two pointed tail feather about an inch long, protruding beyond the other tail feathers. Eye was dark, Bill was pale to a darker decurved tip. (See Kaufman Advanced Birding). Bill most closely approxiamted the Parasitic. 

Upper and under wing showed the white shafts of the primaries. There seemed to be 4 to 6 of them having the white. Belly was dark. The nape had a lighter brown color to it like a collar. There was no double white flash on the underwing, that would be present on a Pomarine Jaeger. 

 

This bird suddenly took off after gulls. I had noticed a nearby group of gulls fascinated by this dark visitor from the north. The Jaeger took off and I was amazed by the swift and rapid flight of the bird as it charged at the fleeing gulls. It was almost like the Jaeger enjoyed the fun of terrorizing the gulls. Then as quickly as it left, it returned to the gentle waters and daintily fed on insects. 

As of 1 week later, the bird has remained on Pond 157. Lisa Rawinski saw and photographed it again. I did not think these birds stuck around! 

Similar Species Discussion

Long-tailed Jaeger: usually shows only 2 white shafts on the outer primaries. Bill is usually shorter, more compact and not as long as Parasitic and Pomarine. 

Pomarine:  This bird is larger and heavier built. Its bill will be heavier too. Also, Pomarines show a double white wing flash on the underwing primary feather coverts and our bird clearly lacks those. 

 

 

Previous Experience

I have seen this species on pelagic trips but never as close as this bird. I have expected Jaegers in the San Luis Valley for 40 years now, and with the numerous sightings each fall on the front range, I was sure they came through the Valley. But too few people to find them. Glad this one did not sneak by!

 

In 2020 we documented the SLV first Long-tailed Jaeger. It was approved after a rigorous description and attention to detail. When this bird appeared last week, I knew what I needed to look for.  I purposely try to catch images of the topside and underside of wings, as well as bill size and shape.
