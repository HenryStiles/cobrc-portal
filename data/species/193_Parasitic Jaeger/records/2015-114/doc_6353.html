Description of the Bird

From the boat ramp on the west side of the reservior, we first observed the jaeger flying fast and low just over horizon near the far eastern shoreline. To me, the bird appeared to be a mix of falcon and gull, with long pointed wings but with a robust body, noticeable head projection, and moderate length tail.  At that distance, the bird looked to be entire dark although later views would prove otherwise.  We watched the jaeger chase a couple gulls along the dam and decided to drive over to that part of the reservoir for a better look.  From the dam, we found the bird flying due north and at least halfway across the reservoir.  The viewing was better, but still distant.  I paid particular attention to the wings and noted a single white flash on the underwing's outer primaries.  It also showed a white flash on the outer primaries of the upperwing, whitish underparts with a hint of yellow on the chest, and a whitish neck collar which gave the bird a dark cap offset from the rest of the body.  Otherwise the bird was dark brown.  The distance was a challenge to see more defining features so I focused on what I could.  The body structure and flight pattern was what I expected for a parasitic.  Having seen many pomarine jaegers in Colorado, this bird differed from them by being less heavy chested with less powerful flight.  It looked streamlined for fast flight, again falcon-like.  I noted moderate lengthed tail streamers that appeared to be pointed.

Similar Species Discussion

Pomarine jaeger was eliminated by the lack of a double white flash on the underwings, less bulky body, and pointed tail streamers.  Long-tailed jaeger was eliminated by the bird's brown coloration, shorter tail streamers, and more powerful flight.  This bird was a tweener between those species - less of a power lifter than a pomarine but more badass than a long-tailed.

Previous Experience

I have documented multiple pomarine and long-tailed jaegers in Colorado, but have observed just a small handful of parasitics.  I am also familiar with these species from west coast pelagics and sea watches.
