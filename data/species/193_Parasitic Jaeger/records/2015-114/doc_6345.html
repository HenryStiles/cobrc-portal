Description of the Bird

On October 3rd, Chris Wood and Jessie Barry reported a juvenile PAJA at Jackson Reservoir.  Norm Erthal and I arrived with hope of possibly re-finding this bird.  The weather was overcast and vaguely foggy when we arrived at the north side of the reservoir (SWA).  We scanned the reservoir repeatedly.  Shortly prior to departing, Norm Erthal announced that he had "the jaeger" and that it disappeared against the trees near the campground to the southwest.

We moved to the more eastern portion of the SWA and scanned and re-found the jaeger.  Light was terrible as we looked into the diffused sun-glare caused by the low clouds. The bird was nominally the same size as the RBGUs it was harassing.  Its flight was strong, it accelerated and moved faster than the gulls it chased and it was highly aerobatic - at times almost nighthawk-like.  Its profile was aerodynamic with long narrow wings.

When it settled on the water amongst the WEGRs, it stood out as it bobbed high in the water with wings and tail pointing upwards.

Deciding the light might be better on the bird, Norm and I headed to the south side of the reservoir and viewed the bird from the eastern end of the Jackson dam.  Shortly after re-finding the jaeger, I enjoyed an extended show as it badgered yet another RBGU.  On the final descent on the gull, the jaeger pirouetted 360 degrees with its wings extended and head down providing the first excellent look at both the top and bottom sides of the bird.

The jaeger had a pale head and neck with a dark cap, a dark brown back, wings and tail.  It showed a complete brown breast-band above the light lower chest and belly.  The vent area was brown, but paler than the dark wings.  The upper wings showed some white in the inner primaries and the underside of the wings showed dark with a very bold white patch on the inner primaries.  It was at this point that I realized this bird was not the bird described by Barry/Wood. 

We returned to the north side of the reservoir and in improving light continued to study the bird.  At this point we were studying the bird to determine Pomerine vs. Parasitic.  Since the two are very similar, I was watching for any additional white in the underwing further in from the large bold "flash".  In the tail, I wanted to determine if the two feathers each came to a sharp point or if they were more stubby in structure.

In the whole time of watching the bird in flight, I could not detect any of that inner "flash" in the wings.

The central tail feathers were narrow and came to a sharp point.

Similar Species Discussion

LTJA: Does not have a breastband, has grayer wings without a large patch of white.

POJA: Should be able to detect that second "flash" of white in the underwings.  Tips of central tail feathers should be broad and blunt, not narrow and pointed.
