Description of the Bird

The bird was shaped more-or-less like a gull, but with a slight protrusion of the central tail feathers about an inch or slightly less past the other tail feathers. From a distance the bird looked dark overall. When sitting on the water, the head, neck and breast of the bird were fairly uniform tawny brown, trending towards honey-colored. The back and wings were significantly darker brown. The underparts of the bird were fairly uniformly dark, except the center of the belly was slightly paler, and the undertail coverts were strongly barred. There was some barring along the flanks of the bird as well, but it was only visible a couple of times when the bird raised its wings while it was sitting on the water. About half the time, depending on the angle of viewing, the bird appeared to have a slightly darker cap, which started at the eye and moved diagonally up and back towards the rear of the crown, then forward from there. However, when looking at the bird head-on or with its head mostly facing me, I couldn't really see this darker cap; instead it looked like the light brown included the whole head except for the eye. In flight, the bird showed a strong white flash at the base of the primaries. This was particularly visible on the underwing and could be seen at a great distance. Careful observation revealed that there was a white flash on the upperwing as well, comprised of a white area that appeared contiguous when the wing was spread. It was markedly more extensive than the white primary shafts visible from above on a Long-tailed Jaeger. The rest of the underwing appeared mottled brownish; I couldn't really see any "double flash" like you'd expect on Pomarine Jaeger; nor did there appear to be white patterning like Sibley shows on the other two species of jaeger.
Behaviors: The bird would sit on the water in the center of the reservoir, restlessly turning its head to follow the actions of the few gulls in the area, even if they were many hundreds of yards away. Every 10 minutes or so it would take flight and pursue a gull at high speed, harassing it constantly until it dropped whatever it was carrying. One one occasion the bird traveled nearly half the width of the reservoir to spend a full minute harassing a Ring-billed Gull. When it finally succeeded in pirating the food, the jaeger flew all the way back to the center of the reservoir before consuming it. Kleptoparasitism was the only foraging strategy we saw the bird use. Occasionally the bird stretched and bathed while sitting on the water.
Call: Bird was silent.
Plumage: intermediate juvenile

Similar Species Discussion

I felt that the key feature eliminating Pomarine Jaeger was size: the bird was always clearly and distinctly smaller than the Ring-billed Gulls it was harassing. In fact it was surprisingly small. The key feature I used to eliminate Long-tailed Jaeger was the distinct white flash at the base of the primaries on the upperside of the wing. Other supporting details include the bird's incessant kleptoparasitic behavior; its fast, direct, almost falconlike flight; its dark cap and tawny nape/auriculars, which appeared to mirror the pattern of Parasitic Jaegers in alternate plumage; the warm brown tones of the entire bird (they were lighter than "cinnamon," but definitely not a cold gray; "tawny" is a good word to describe them). In flight the bird appeared sleek, not "chesty" like a large Pomarine. Its bill was thinner than I'd expect on a Pom as well.

Resources Used

I used Sibley in the field and also discussed the identification of the jaeger with the other birders present. I had studied Nick Komar's photos of the bird before arriving at the reservoir.

Previous Experience

I have seen Parasitic Jaegers on only 2-3 occasions, most recently in Colorado about 5 years ago. I have seen Long-tailed and Parasitic Jaegers about twice as often, but I wouldn't call myself a jaeger expert by any stretch.
