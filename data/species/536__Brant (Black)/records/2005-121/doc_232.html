Description of the Bird

the bird was a small goose, about the size of the neary Richardson's Cackling Geese, but with a shorter, thicker neck, and shorter tail giving the bird a more compact appearance. The bill was black, and longer than on nearby Cackling and Lesser Canada Geese. The tail was much shorter, barely sticking out beyond the upper and undertail coverts.

The head of the bird was entirely black. The neck was also black, except for a conspicuous white marks at the top of the neck that almost met in the back of the neck. The bird's chest was black, contrasting with the dark brown on the sides and back of the bird, which was slightly darker than the brown on the same areas of Canada and Cackling Geese. There were some vertical white markings along the brown flanks of the bird. The flight feathers were blackish, with some white edging on the coverts forming white horizontal stripes on the folded wing.

The birds tail was black, but almost entirely obscured by the very long white uppertail coverts. The undertail coverts were white, contrasting sharply with the dark brown flanks and belly of the bird.
Behaviors: the bird was mostly hanging out with Lesser Canada Geese on the pond, swimming around.
Call: none heard
Plumage: adult black

Similar Species Discussion

Canada and Cackling Geese were ruled out by the black neck with out a cheek patch, white mark on the top of the neck, black chest, darker sides that did not contrast with the back, shorter tail with very long white uppertail coverts, and by white marks in the wing coverts and on the flanks, as well as more compact shape.

Young blue-phase white geese were ruled out by the black neck with white mark, brown plumage contrasting with black chest, white marks on the flanks and wing coverts, and white undertail and uppertail converts.

The bird was identified as a Black Brant by the dark sides that did not contrast as much with the chest as on an Atlantic Brant, and large white mark on the neck. There seemed to be less white on the sides than I would expect for a typical Black Brant, but what this means I can't say.
