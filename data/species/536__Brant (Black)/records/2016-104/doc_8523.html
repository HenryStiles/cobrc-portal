Description of the Bird

I walked up to the lake and was still getting my binoculars around my neck when I started looking at the first group of geese nearest to me on the ice, about 20 birds (out of over a thousand total on the lake). Brant flashed into my head immediately. Lifted the binoculars and confirmed. They were standing when I arrived, so I could see many of its field marks: smaller overall size (even compared to Cackling Goose), brilliant white necklace, all black head, dark wash extending much further down onto its chest and belly than the sharply defined neck-line of white-cheeked goose, a bit darker overall, tail white with a small black edge rather than the black tail of white-cheeked goose.

It and the rest of the geese it was with walked a bit further out onto the ice (5 yards, maybe) when I went to the shoreline to get pictures. I went to look at other birds and when I came back the Brant was asleep in the same location, its head tucked into its back.

I returned 1.5 hours later to look again, and it was gone. It was eventually refound by others about seven days later.

Similar Species Discussion

No other goose has this plumage pattern, and considering the great looks and photos obtained, I believe that all other species are eliminated.

Subspecies identification has been based on the two photos I have uploaded -- "Black" Brant proposed by Steve Mlodinow. To my knowledge that subspecies ID has not been called into question. I'm not expert enough in Brant subspecies to make the call myself in the field. Reviewing photos and online resources, I can't see anything to disprove Steve's assessment.

Resources Used

Binoculars, past experience with Brant.

Previous Experience

According to my eBird records, I have seen Branta bernicla six times prior to this one, once in Colorado (the Chatfield CBC bird in 2013). I've seen them in groups ranging from 1 to 15.

I enjoy studying geese in Colorado in winter and rarely fail to scan through a flock for the "interesting" ones. So I've seen the similar species thousands upon thousands of times.
