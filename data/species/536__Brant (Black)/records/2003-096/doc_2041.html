Description of the Bird

This bird appeared very similar to the Greeley Brant (26 Dec 03). In fact, I think that it is the same bird.
Note the distinctive similarities on similar parts of the collar- some good landmarks include a heavy white spot near the front right side
of the collar, with a smaller but distinctive white spot behind that. These areas create the heaviest, most filled-in region of the entire
collar. On the left side, the heaviest part of the collar is located farther back towards the nape. There is one large, heavy white spot in
particular that seems to have a thin dark line running diagonally through it. These features are visible in photos of the bird from both
localities. None of the poses are identical, and the lighting changed, so the spots appear slightly different in shape and contrast in the
comparison photos. I do think the overall patterns match up well, though.
Features visible on the flank of the bird in both sets of photos match up well, too. For example, on the right flank, the 3rd tertial bar
from the back is the longest in both photos, and the rear tertial bar is the shortest (but very bright where visible.) Even the little gaps
in the tertial bars seem to match up, allowing for slight differences in feather position and a month’s worth of wear. On the left flank,
the front three tertial bars are better developed than the rear tertial bar in both sets of photos. Also, the front three tertial bars are
heavier towards the flanks in both photo sets. They are of more consistent length compared to each other than the same three tertial
bars on the right side in both photo sets as well.
Finally, the white crescent-shaped feather edging on the upper flanks is more prominent on the bird’s left side compared to the right
side in both sets of photos. There only appears to be one or two of these towards the front flank on the right side in both photos, while
both left side shots show at least four crescents more evenly spread out along the top of the flank.
To sum, I think that these similarities are more than coincidence. I believe that the bird re-located itself to Broomfield sometime in
early January, 2004.
Behaviors: The bird was sleeping, preening, and swimming around with a flock of many hundred Canada Geese. Many of these were
smaller forms, the majority of these appearing to be Richardson’s Canada Geese.
Call: not heard
Plumage: Juvenile Black Brant

Similar Species Discussion

I don’t believe that any other duck or goose has these plumage traits. Additionally I see nothing to suggest
hybrid characteristics. The real question may be if this is a Black Brant, an intermediate form, or another subspecies altogether such
as Atlantic Brant. I don’t feel fully qualified to address the subspecies issue here and I’m relying on the opinion of other more
experienced birders when I call this a Black Brant.
