Description of the Bird

Mainly seen grazing with "white-cheeked geese" (Canada, Cackling); also three Ross's Geese present. Seen for 15-30 seconds in flight, too (allowing comparative study of tail pattern, Brant vs. "white-cheeked").

While feeding, the goose was no larger than the smallest "white-cheeked geese" present, and notably smaller than many of the "white-cheeked" geese. The Brant was stubby-billed and with a relatively steep forehead.

The white ring around the neck was classic: white, contrasting sharply with the all-black head, neck, and breast; and marked with narrow, vertical, black bars.

This bird was a "Black" Brant (nigricans) because of the uniform black extending entirely across the breast and well down onto the belly. Back and wings black; part of the belly, plus vent and under-tail coverts white; extensive blotchy white on rear flanks.

Seen flying away with "white-cheeked" geese, the tail was markedly different: black centered, with broad white edges ("white-cheeked" instead with all-black rectrices and white upper-tail coverts).

We did not hear the bird.

This bird was first reported, fide John Rawinsky, on Monday, Mar. 16, I believe, and it continued at the refuge, I believe, for several days after this sighting. (I'm reporting this because Doug Faulkner tells me that nobody else has!)

Similar Species Discussion

See previous field for comparison with the "white-cheeked goose" complex (Cackling, Canada). Extensive black, distinctive white collar with narrow and vertical black bars, and very different tail pattern all rule out "white-cheeked goose."

"Atlantic Brant" (subspecies hrota, sometimes considered a full species) is ruled out by the extent of black, extending well down onto the belly. Also, this bird was darker (basically, black) above than "Atlantic Brant."
