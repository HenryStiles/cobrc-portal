Description of the Bird

Immediately recognizable.  I first noticed the rich brown chest above the black belly, then the gray face with a line of black features from the crown down the back of the back of the neck.  All four birds had distinctly orange bills.  Looking closer I could clearly see the greyish white tip of the bill.  Two of the birds alternately stood and sat on the grass (the other two remained sitting).  When they stood I could see the pink legs.  Although their wings remained folded the whole time, the white patch between the brown (above) and black (below) was easily visible.  I didn't think of focusing on the white eye-ring at the time, but in retrospect believe that their eyes stood out distinctly from the gray face.  Very striking and unmistakable for anything else; I knew what I was looking at right away, as I had seen Black-bellied Whistling-Ducks in Texas and in Florida.

Similar Species Discussion

The four ducks stood next to each other in a line, loosely mixed with a larger number of Canada Geese (probably 15 geese or so) along the bank of the river, remaining a few feet apart from them always, but not appearing concerned with each other's presence.  But there was no issue at all separating the BBWD from the geese.  I didn't consider any other possibilities seriously.  I thought of Fulvous Whistling Duck but never believed these might be Fulvous.

Resources Used

As soon as I got home I checked Sibley's, 2e, which confirmed what I was already sure of.  Then I checked my other guides, National Geographic, Smithsonian (Ted Floyd), and National Wildlife Federation (Brinkley), for the bird's distribution.  All of them supported the sighting (though not the location in CO).

Previous Experience

I began birding in 2007 and have become somewhat obsessive ever since (what a pleasure!).  I've travelled the Texas coast a couple of times and have been to north Florida once (Payne's Prairie and Merritt Island), where I've seen BBWD before (Estero Llano Grande in TX).  I'm comfortable identifying ducks in the US now, especially if they are males in breeding plumage, so I don't normally carry a field guide with me.  I've got 507 North American birds on my lifelist now.
