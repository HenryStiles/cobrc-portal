Description of the Bird

A typical wren profile with a long bill and a relatively short stubby tail that was cocked up at 45 degrees at times. A long decurved bill and a rusty colored cap. A broad white supercillium stripe extending from just in front of the eye all the way back to the neck. It was a dirty whitish color in front of and below the eye with a white lower cheek and throat. It was white with a consistent buffy wash from the chest all the way down through the belly to the vent area. It was a uniform rusty brown from the neck, back, shoulders, upper wing coverts, rump and tail. The tail had faint black barring. The wings were a brownish black with white flecking through out. The undertail coverts and tail from underneath was white with black barring.
Behaviors: The bird was very active and it began singing as the sun was coming up. It would perch up and sing for a while and then fly off only to pop up nearby and sing again.
Call: A loud rolling series of two syllable calls such as "Jim-mey" or "tea-kettle" repeated about five times, a pause, and then another five or so. This continued for lengths of time at a stretch.
Plumage: Adult

Similar Species Discussion

A Cactus Wren has a rusty cap and similar supercillium strip but it is a much larger bird with black spotting on the front and not rusty on the back. A Marsh Wren is similarly appearing on the head but the song sounds completely different and it has a black back with white streaks. A Bewick's Wren has a similar appearance but it's song is sounds like a mechancial Song Sparrow's and not like a Carolina Wren. It also has a longer tail and is white in front and not buffy.
