Habitat

The yard is Kentucky Blue Grass and Perrenial Rye Grass. There are Locust Trees, Pinion Pines, Junipers and small shrubs (Euonymus, Potentilla, Russian Sage, Flax). There is a water feature made with native rock and very slow moving water which seems to attract many birds.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  the camera lens and 10x42 binoculars
Distance:  10 to 50 feet - as it flew through the yard back to the water
Light:  Very good

Description of the Bird

The size of the bird was a little larger than a Lark Sparrow, noticeably smaller than a Yellow-headed Blackbird, and about the same size as a Lark Bunting. I mention these three species because all three were present at the water.

When the bird first flew through the yard away from us, we noticed the striking black wings and white rump and first thought of a woodpecker. However when the bird appeared again at the water, the obvious yellow nape made the identification unmistakably a Bobolink.
The entire front of the head and underneath was all black and bold white markings were at the base (or scapulars) of each wing.
Behaviors: The only behavior we observed was that the bird was flying into the yard obviously for a drink of water.
Call: I did not distinguish the call but my fellow observer did hear
Plumage: Adult breeding male

Similar Species Discussion

A couple of other species that I mentioned were at the water gave a good comparison. There were several Yellow-headed Blackbirds obviously larger. But the similar colors of a yellow head and some white on the wings were definitely different than just the yellow nape and white on the wings and also white on the rump of the Bobolink. A single male Lark Bunting, black with white markings on the wings, was at the water with obviously no yellow nape. The distinctive markings could only be a Bobolink.
