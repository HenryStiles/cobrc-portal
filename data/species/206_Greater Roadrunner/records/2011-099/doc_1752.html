Description of the Bird

On the afternoon of 10 July, Tracy Clark and I pulled off to the side of the road near the ne. corner of Jackson Reservoir to look at a hawk Tracy had found. It was an eastern RTHA, and as she was studying it, I looked down the road. To my surprise, I saw a medium (depending on how one might want to define such among N Amer avifauna), bird walking down the road towards us, about 200 feet away. I lifted my bins, and the bird stopped, cocking its long (not narrow, not broad, held modestly flared) tail up and raising its head and neck up at 45-60 degrees, thus assuming a position that roughly resembled a check mark, with the tail forming the longer part of the check.

This is the classic alert position of a Greater Roadrunner, and the surprise of it all caused me to freeze. The bird had its dark crest raised as it stared back at me, also motionless except for some slow changes in how high crest was and the angle of tail. The bird?s bill was somewhat long, relatively straight, and relatively stout. The neck and chest were densely streaked; the remainder of underparts pale/whitish (down to mid-belly). What I could see of wings looked brown and heavily spotted with white/pale. The stand-off lasted about 20 seconds or so, at which time I called over to Tracy. This caused the bird to lower its tail, assume a position with neck and tail parallel to the ground, and trot a few paces towards me, before dashing off the road into scrub.

Similar Species Discussion

The bird size, shape, and behavior matched those of Greater Roadrunner perfectly in my experience. The markings seen also fit, though limited. The combination of such rule out any fowl; for instance, the proportionately larger head, the long broad (not pointed) tail, long and stout bill are wrong for pheasant or any grouse.
