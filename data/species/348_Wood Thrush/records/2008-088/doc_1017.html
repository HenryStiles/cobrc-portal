Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized thrush, smaller than an American Robin but larger than a Hermit Thrush. The shape was typical of a Thrush, with a "chunky" body shape accentuated by a short neck and "pot belly" look, long legs, a reasonably long tail, and a fairly short, thin bill that never the less was not needle-like and was fairly uniform in thickness. The bill was pink at the base and dusky colored at the tip. The legs were pink.

The crown, nape, back, wings, and tail of the bird were medium brown with a strong rufous tinge, especially on the crown and nape. The cheek of the bird was white with strong black horizontal streaking. The underparts were white, and densely spotted with large, round black spots. The undertail coverts were unmarked white.
Behaviors: Most of the observation involved hearing the bird sing. It briefly hopped down onto the ground and into the open, and then flew to a semi-concealed perch at a mid-elevation in a tree. The rest of the time it was not visible.
Call: The bird was singing regularly during the observation; the song was made up of flute-like phrases that were lower pitched than those of Hermit Thrush; each flute-like phrase was immediately followed by a much higher pitched, short rattling sound.
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

All other thrush species were ruled out by the densely spotted underparts, the larger size than Catharus thrushes but smaller than American Robin, and by the rufous colored upperparts. The lower pitched song that included rattling phrases helped rule out similar singers, especially Hermit Thrush.
