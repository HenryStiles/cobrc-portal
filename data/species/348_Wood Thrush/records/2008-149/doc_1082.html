Description of the Bird

the bird was a medium sized thrush, larger than a Catharus thrush, but smaller than an American Robin. The overall shape was similar to that of a Catharus or Robin, though the pot-bellied was more obvious. The bill was fairly short and thin, even in length until a slightly tapered tip. The basal two-thirds of the bill were flesh pink colored, the tip was dusky. The irides were very dark brown. The legs were dusky pink in color.

The cap, nape, back, wings, and tail were rufous brown, brightest on the nape and upper back, and browner with a much less rufous tinge on the remainder of the upperparts. The face of the bird was blackish, with white horizontal striations. The bird had a thin black malar, and a white throat. The underparts were white with bold, large round black spots that were largest on the flanks, smallest on the upper chest, and absent from the lower belly and undertail coverts.
Behaviors: The bird was observed hopping around on the ground in the campground, and was not shy at all and remained in the open for the entire observation. At times it allowed us to approach within a few feet.
Call: none heard
Plumage: alternate

Similar Species Discussion

Catharus thrushes were ruled out by the larger size, more extensively spotted underparts, different face pattern, and rufous upperparts. All other species were ruled out by the thrush shape, and combination of the spotted underparts and rufous upperparts.
