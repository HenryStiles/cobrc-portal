Habitat

The bird was captured at the banding station located in the riparian forest, consisting of mainly cottonwood and willow tree species, surrounding Barr Lake.

Description of the Bird

The crown was red, and the upperparts were reddish brown with a comparatively dull, olive-brown rump, uppertail coverts, and tail. The underparts were white with large, oval, blackish brown spots extending from the upper breast down to the belly with the largest concentration of spots along the flanks. The broken eye rings were thick and white. The bird's bill was large relative to Catharus sp. The maxilla was black, and the mandible was pink with a blackish gray tip. The wing chord measured 105 mm and the tail 70 mm. The difference between the length of the longest primary covert and primary 10 was 2.1 mm. The difference between the lengths of primaries 8 and 6 was 2.6 mm. These measurements fall within the ranges appropriate for a Wood Thrush, according to published data. This bird was aged as Hatching Year due to the incomplete pneumaticization of its skull.
Behaviors: Normal behaviors of the Wood Thrush were not observed during its capture and banding.
Call: not applicable
Plumage: upperprts reddish brown underprts white

Similar Species Discussion

The large size of this bird was one characteristic that separated it from Catharus thrushes; this bird was more stocky than Catharus thrushes, and its wing chord measured 105 mm, which is either outside of the range or in the upper range of wing chords for Catharus thrushes. Another criterion of separation was the plumage of this bird. Catharus thrushes tend to be brightest on the rump; this Wood Thrush was dullest on its rump. The bird's upperparts were brighter red than the typical plumage of a Veery, and its breast spots were larger, bolder, and more extensive (extending down to the belly) than Catharus thrushes. The wing morphology of this bird, primarily the 2.6 mm difference between primaries 8 and 6, also separated this Wood Thrush from Catharus thrushes.
