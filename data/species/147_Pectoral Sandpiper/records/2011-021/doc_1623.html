Habitat

Open, wet mudflat. The bird was pretty much at water's edge, sometimes venturing into the water to probe, sometimes stepping back on the mud a few bird-lengths to rest or digest.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  32x Leica Scope
Distance:  Initially, at least 500 feet. Eventually, ~150 feet
Light:  Sun was to the east. Bird was generally to the northeast. I'd say the light conditions were generally poor. However, I angled for some good viewing toward the end; plus, the sun went behind the clouds, which greatly reduced glare, and made for better observation.

Description of the Bird

At closest observation (~125 feet through 32x scope), I was able to see the following: medium-size shorebird, somewhat pot-bellied, but generally sleek in proportions; dark above, with complex striations of brown-buff and gray-black; densely streaked breast, mainly brown, but black-brown at lowest edge, contrasting very sharply with belly; slightly decurved, medium-length bill, pale yellow-orange at base; straw-yellow legs; primaries extending to tail tip.
Behaviors: Feeding fairly constantly at water's edge, and sometimes right in the water; probed in water up to at least half the bill length. Not especially skittish. The only things preventing closer approach were (a) an inlet canal and (b) a state parks official who was about to give me the heave-ho.
Call: none heard.
Plumage: alternate adult

Similar Species Discussion

Initially, and at a distance (through binoculars), I assumed it was it going to be a Baird's Sandpiper, surely more expected in late March in the San Luis Valley. But many marks (noted in description above) are wrong for Baird's, especially: bill structure and color; leg color; and, of course, the distinctive pattern of the underparts of Pectoral. Also, the bird's primaries, although long, were not super-long as on a Baird's.

I suppose the much smaller Least should be quickly discounted. Plumage-wise, the two species are similar, but the bold breast pattern of Pectoral, well noted on this bird, is nonetheless distinctive; also, this bird's feeding behavior was wrong for Least, as Least does not usually probe in medium-depth water. And their body shapes are very different: Least is a dumpy little thing, but this bird was a powerful, fairly long-winged bird.

Something I'll note here is that I think the bird was a female. I say so for two reasons: first, size; second the breast pattern. Size-wise, the bird seemed on the lower end of the spectrum for this variably sized species. Plumage-wise, I've seen male Pecs in breeding season that were practically gray-black below, but this bird had the gray-black limited only to the lowest portion of the bib. So I would attribute the (relatively) limited gray-black to a female. Then again, maybe it was a male that hadn't yet acquired full alternate plumage; that might be the case, since it is still pretty early in the year.

Previous Experience

I've seen thousands of Pectoral Sandpipers in eastern North America, including goodly numbers in March and April--so I know the alternate (spring) plumage well. Although I've seen several Pectorals in Colorado, I believe this is the first one I've ever seen in the spring. Of course, being a Colorado Birder, I have seen thousands of both Least and Baird's Sandpipers.
