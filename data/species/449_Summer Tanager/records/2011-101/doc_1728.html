Habitat

Flooded agricultural land with a Hay Field, large Fremont and Narrowleaf Cottonwoods as well as scattered Russian Olive and other riparian trees. It was very wet, so large trees were everywhere on this property.

Viewing Conditions

Optics:  Brunton 8x42
Distance:  100 (est.) originally and later closer
Light:  The sky was not very cloudy (if at all) and the sun was behind me, putting the bird out in the open and in perfect light with which to observe it. I could tell it was a Summer Tanager before I even got binoculars on it the light was so good.

Description of the Bird

White walking across a field, my attention was first drawn to a song reminiscent to that of a slow Blue Grosbeak. I immediately knew that it was a bird not regular in the area and, as I have recently spent a lot of time in Louisiana and Arizona, was under the impression that it could be a Summer Tanager. Upon rounding a corner, I saw the bird perched in the top of a Fremont Cottonwood, actively singing from a stationary perch. The bird was yellowish all over except for red feathers that were coming in scattered over the body and the head, which was already almost completely red. This confirmed my suspicion of it being a Summer Tanager, and after studying the bird a while and seeing the relatively large-billed appearance and 'difference' in song from Louisiana birds I am used to, I concluded that the bird was most likely an individual of the cooperi population. I have seen nominate rubra in Mesa County before, and this bird definitely had a different structure more similar to those in Arizona.
Behaviors: For the entirety of the time of observation, the bird was in the top of a Fremont Cottonwood actively singing. After about four minutes, the bird flew, but could still be heard singing for quite a bit longer as it moved from location to location.
Call: Song was a slow whistled series of phrases, reminiscent of an extremely slow Blue Grosbeak more than that of a slow American Robin.
Plumage: 1st year

Similar Species Discussion

The birds song eliminated all but irregularly sounding American Robins and other similar birds, while visual confirmation of the bird narrowed it down to a Tanager. Being a bird without wing bars and being one of the 'one color' tanagers, this eliminated all but Hepatic and Summer. The shape and structure of the bird was not right for Hepatic, while the bill, head and molting pattern all suggested a first-year male Summer Tanager.

Resources Used

I did not use any literature or consult anyone to confirm the ID of this bird. I am very confident of my abilities with Summer Tanager identification from my travels in the southwest and the east, and later had the bird independently verified by other birders.

Previous Experience

I have birded Southern Arizona many times, having seen cooperi birds almost every time with the opportunity to compare them with Hepatic, Western and Flame-colored Tanagers, and have lived in Louisiana for the past two years, where I see them regularly and can compare them with Scarlets. I also guided on High Island, Texas this spring, where I saw Western, Scarlet and Summers side by side for good comparison.
